Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA/QLoRA)

⏱ 27 min Lesson 6.6 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsFull fine-tuning a billion-parameter model is expensive; LoRA trains under 1% of it by learning a low-rank update, and QLoRA fits it on one GPU. This is how customization happens at scale.
By the end, you can
  • Explain LoRA's low-rank update
  • Compute the parameter savings
  • Describe QLoRA's quantization
  • Know LoRA's benefits (no forgetting, swappable adapters)

Concept Fine-tuning a giant model without paying for all of it

Fine-tuning (Lesson 6.2) means updating a model's weights — but for a model with billions of parameters, updating all of them is expensive. You need memory not just for the weights, but also for their gradients and the optimizer's state (Adam keeps two extra numbers per weight, Lesson 4.4), which together can triple or quadruple the memory. Full fine-tuning a 7-billion-parameter model can require many high-end GPUs. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) sidesteps this by training only a tiny fraction of the parameters while freezing the rest.

The dominant PEFT method is LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation). The insight: when you fine-tune, the change to each weight matrix turns out to be effectively low-rank — it can be well-approximated by the product of two much smaller matrices (recall low-rank structure from PCA, Lesson 3.11). So instead of updating the full weight matrix $W$, LoRA freezes it and learns a small update $\Delta W = BA$, where $B$ and $A$ are skinny matrices of rank $r$ (with $r$ tiny, like 8 or 16).

Because $B$ is $d \times r$ and $A$ is $r \times d$ with $r \ll d$, the trainable part ($2dr$ parameters) is minuscule compared to the full $d^2$. You get almost the quality of full fine-tuning at a tiny fraction of the cost — and since the original weights are frozen, you avoid catastrophic forgetting (Lesson 6.2) and can keep many different LoRA "adapters" for the same base model, swapping them like plugins.

Concept QLoRA: fine-tune a huge model on one GPU

QLoRA pushes this further by also quantizing the frozen base model to 4 bits (storing each weight in 4 bits instead of 16 or 32), which slashes the memory needed to hold it. The small LoRA adapters are still trained in full precision on top. The combination is dramatic: QLoRA made it possible to fine-tune a 65-billion-parameter model on a single consumer GPU — something that previously needed a server rack. This democratized fine-tuning, putting it within reach of individuals and small teams.

Worked example How much does LoRA actually save?

Let's count the trainable parameters for one weight matrix of a large model — full fine-tuning versus LoRA with rank 8. A typical hidden dimension is $d = 4096$.

For this one matrix, full fine-tuning trains 16.8 million parameters; LoRA trains just 65,536 — a 256× reduction, under 0.4% of the weights. Across a whole model, LoRA typically ends up training well under 1% of the parameters while matching full fine-tuning quality on most tasks. The reason this works without hurting quality is the low-rank insight: the adaptation a task requires is simple (low-rank) even though the model is huge, so a small $B$ and $A$ capture it. And because you're training 256× fewer parameters, you need far less memory for gradients and optimizer state — which, combined with QLoRA's 4-bit base, is what collapses "needs a GPU cluster" down to "runs on one GPU." This is the technique that made custom fine-tuned models practical for everyone.

★ Key points to remember
  • Full fine-tuning is memory-hungry (weights + gradients + optimizer state); PEFT trains a tiny fraction instead.
  • LoRA freezes the weight $W$ and learns a low-rank update $\Delta W = BA$ ($r \ll d$) — under 1% of parameters.
  • It works because the change from fine-tuning is effectively low-rank; quality nearly matches full fine-tuning.
  • Frozen base → no catastrophic forgetting, and you can swap many LoRA adapters on one base model.
  • QLoRA 4-bit-quantizes the frozen base too, fitting huge-model fine-tuning on a single GPU.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1For a weight matrix with $d = 8192$ and LoRA rank $r = 16$, compute the full and LoRA trainable parameter counts and the reduction factor.
Show solution & reasoning

Full: $d^2 = 8192^2 = 67{,}108{,}864$ ($\approx$ 67M). LoRA: $2dr = 2 \times 8192 \times 16 = 262{,}144$ ($\approx$ 0.26M). Reduction: $67{,}108{,}864 / 262{,}144 = \mathbf{256}\times$ fewer.

Interestingly the reduction is again 256× — because the ratio is $d^2 / (2dr) = d/(2r) = 8192/32 = 256$. The savings depend only on $d/(2r)$: bigger models (larger $d$) or smaller ranks give more savings. This is why LoRA scales so well — the larger the base model, the more dramatic the relative savings, which is exactly when you need them most.

2You want to serve a customer-support bot, a code assistant, and a creative-writing helper — all based on the same 7B model. Why is LoRA ideal here?
Show solution & reasoning

Train three small LoRA adapters — one per use case — on top of the same frozen 7B base. Each adapter is only a few megabytes (vs ~14 GB for a full model copy), so you store the base once and swap the tiny adapter in for whichever task is active. You could even keep all three in memory and route requests to the right adapter, or merge an adapter into the base when needed.

Full fine-tuning would force you to store and serve three complete 7B models — expensive in storage and GPU memory. LoRA's frozen-base design turns "many specialized models" into "one base + many lightweight plugins," which is a huge operational win. This adapter-swapping pattern is widely used in production to serve many customized behaviors cost-effectively from a single base model — and it's only possible because the base stays frozen.

3How would you choose the LoRA rank $r$? What's the trade-off between small and large $r$?
Show solution & reasoning

The rank $r$ controls the capacity of the adaptation. Small $r$ (4–8): fewest parameters, fastest and cheapest, but limited capacity — fine for simple style/format adaptations. Larger $r$ (32–128): more capacity to capture complex task-specific changes, but more parameters to train and store, and a higher risk of overfitting on small datasets. It's the bias–variance/capacity trade-off from Lesson 3.5, applied to the adapter.

In practice you tune $r$ on a validation set (Lesson 3.12), starting small (8 or 16) and increasing only if the task needs it — surprisingly often, very small ranks work well, which is itself evidence that task adaptations are genuinely low-rank. There's also a related scaling factor (often called $\alpha$) that adjusts how strongly the LoRA update is applied. The practical wisdom: default to a small rank, validate, and only spend more capacity when the data shows it helps — the same disciplined, empirical model-selection mindset from Track 3, now applied to PEFT.

★ Deep dive: merging adapters, why low-rank works, and the PEFT family▸

A practical elegance of LoRA is that the adapter can be merged back into the base weights for deployment: since the effective weight is $W + BA$, you can precompute $W' = W + BA$ once and serve a single matrix with zero extra inference cost — the LoRA structure exists only during training and (optionally) storage. So you get cheap training and no inference penalty, the best of both worlds. Alternatively, you keep adapters separate to swap them, trading a tiny runtime add for flexibility. This merge-or-swap choice is a nice example of an engineering degree of freedom that LoRA's math hands you for free.

Why is the fine-tuning update genuinely low-rank? Intuitively, pretraining already learned rich, general features; a downstream task usually needs only a small, structured nudge — emphasize some directions, suppress others — rather than relearning everything. Empirically, researchers found that the weight change $\Delta W$ from full fine-tuning has most of its "energy" concentrated in a few directions (a low effective rank, measurable via the singular values from the SVD, Lesson 3.11). LoRA exploits this by constraining the update to be exactly low-rank from the start, which also acts as a mild regularizer (limited capacity → less overfitting). It's a beautiful instance of a Track-3 linear-algebra insight (low-rank structure) directly enabling a cutting-edge practical method — the kind of connection that makes the foundations worth knowing.

LoRA is the most popular member of a broader PEFT family. Adapters (the original idea) insert small trainable layers between frozen ones. Prefix/prompt tuning prepends a few trainable "virtual token" vectors to the input and trains only those — even fewer parameters than LoRA, learning a soft, optimized prompt. (IA)³ scales activations with learned vectors. Newer LoRA variants (DoRA, and quantization-aware tweaks) push quality and efficiency further. The shared principle across all of them is the same: freeze the expensive pretrained knowledge, train a tiny number of new parameters to steer behavior. This is transfer learning (Lesson 4.9) refined to its economic extreme, and it's a big reason the modern ecosystem is so vibrant — thousands of specialized, shareable adapters built on a handful of open base models, each one a few megabytes that customizes a giant model for a specific job. Understanding PEFT is understanding how customization actually happens at scale.

We keep saying "bigger is better" — but how much better, and how predictably? Next, in 6.7 Scaling laws & emergent behavior, we see the equations that forecast a model's performance from its size, and the surprising abilities that appear only at scale.
