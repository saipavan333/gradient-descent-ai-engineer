Online evaluation & A/B testing

⏱ 25 min Lesson 11.3 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsOffline results are a proxy that often doesn't transfer. Online evaluation via A/B testing measures a model's true effect on real users — randomized, and trusted only after checking statistical significance, not a raw lift.
By the end, you can
  • Explain online vs offline evaluation
  • Describe an A/B test and randomization
  • Compute lift
  • Know A/B pitfalls (peeking, significance, guardrails)

Concept The only verdict that counts is real users

Offline evaluation (Lesson 11.2) tells you a model looks better on a fixed dataset. Online evaluation tells you whether it actually is better when real users interact with it — and the two often disagree. Online evaluation measures a model's true effect on live traffic, using real user behavior (clicks, purchases, engagement, task success) as the signal. It's the ground truth that offline proxies approximate, because it measures what you actually care about — real outcomes — rather than a stand-in. The catch is that it's slower (you must wait for real traffic and results), riskier (a bad model affects real users), and noisier (real behavior is variable), which is why you do offline evaluation first and reserve online for the final verdict.

The workhorse of online evaluation is the A/B test (a randomized controlled experiment): you randomly split live traffic into a control group that gets the current system (A) and a treatment group that gets the new one (B), run both simultaneously, and compare a chosen metric between them. Randomization is what makes it rigorous — because the two groups differ only in which model they got, any difference in outcomes can be attributed to the model, not to confounding factors. It's the same logic as a medical drug trial, applied to software.

Worked example Reading an A/B test result

Suppose you A/B test a new checkout model. 10,000 users each see the old (control) and new (treatment) versions, and you measure the conversion rate — the fraction who complete a purchase:

control_n, control_conv = 10_000, 500
treat_n,   treat_conv   = 10_000, 560
control_rate = control_conv / control_n
treat_rate   = treat_conv / treat_n
lift = (treat_rate - control_rate) / control_rate
print(f"control   : {control_rate:.1%}")
print(f"treatment : {treat_rate:.1%}")
print(f"lift      : {lift:+.1%}")
Outputcontrol : 5.0% treatment : 5.6% lift : +12.0%

The control converts at 5.0% (500 of 10,000) and the treatment at 5.6% (560 of 10,000), a relative lift of +12% — the new model converted 12% more users. That sounds like a clear win, and it may well be — but here's the crucial discipline of online evaluation: you can't stop there. The difference of 60 conversions could partly be noise — random variation that would appear even between two identical models — so before declaring victory you must check statistical significance: is the difference large enough, given the sample size, to be confident it's real and not chance? With these numbers (10,000 users each, a 12% lift), a significance test would likely confirm it's real, but with fewer users or a smaller lift it might not. This is why A/B tests need enough traffic and time — a small sample can show a big apparent lift that's pure luck. The skill of online evaluation is resisting the temptation to read a raw difference as a result: you compute the lift, then ask whether it's statistically significant, and only then whether it's practically meaningful (a real but tiny lift may not justify the change). The number 5.0% vs 5.6% is the start of the analysis, not the end — real online evaluation is as much about statistics (is this difference trustworthy?) as about the metric itself.

★ Key points to remember
  • Online evaluation measures a model's true effect on live traffic via real user behavior — the ground truth offline only approximates.
  • Offline and online results often disagree: a model can win offline yet lose online (and vice versa).
  • An A/B test randomly splits traffic into control (current) and treatment (new), runs both, and compares a metric.
  • Randomization is what makes it rigorous — the groups differ only in the model, so differences are causal.
  • A raw lift isn't a result: check statistical significance (is it beyond chance?) — which needs enough traffic and time.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A team runs an A/B test, sees the treatment "winning" after 2 hours, stops the test, and ships. List the mistakes and how to run it properly.
Show solution & reasoning

Several mistakes. Stopping early ("peeking"): checking the test repeatedly and stopping the moment it looks good dramatically inflates false positives — with enough peeks, random noise will cross the "winning" line at some point even between identical models. This is the multiple-comparisons problem applied over time, and it's one of the most common A/B testing errors. Too short a duration (2 hours): user behavior varies by time of day and day of week (weekday vs weekend, morning vs night), so a 2-hour window samples an unrepresentative slice — you typically run for at least full days or weeks to average over these cycles. Ignoring significance and sample size: "winning" by some margin isn't meaningful without checking it's statistically significant at an adequate sample size. Novelty effects: a new experience can spike engagement briefly just because it's new, then regress — short tests catch the novelty, not the steady state.

Doing it properly: decide the sample size and duration in advance (a power calculation for the effect you want to detect), run for full weekly cycles to cover behavioral variation, don't peek-and-stop — either wait for the pre-planned end or use proper sequential-testing methods designed for continuous monitoring, and check statistical significance (and practical significance) before concluding. You'd also watch for novelty effects by looking at the trend over time, and guard against other pitfalls like sample-ratio mismatch (the split isn't actually 50/50, signaling a bug). The reasoning skill is recognizing that A/B testing is a statistical experiment with a rigorous protocol, and the intuitive shortcuts — peek, stop when happy, ship — systematically produce false wins. Running experiments honestly (pre-committed sample size and duration, no peeking, real significance testing) is what makes online evaluation trustworthy rather than a way to confirm what you hoped.

2Your new recommendation model improves clicks by 3% in an A/B test. Should you ship it? What else would you check?
Show solution & reasoning

A significant +3% on clicks is encouraging, but "ship it" is premature, because a single metric can improve while the system gets worse in ways that metric doesn't capture (Goodhart, Lesson 11.1). I'd check guardrail metrics — metrics that must not degrade even as the target improves: did the click gain come at the cost of long-term satisfaction, retention, or revenue? Did it increase clickbait or reduce diversity? Did user complaints or unsubscribes rise? A classic failure is a recommender that boosts short-term clicks by pushing sensational content while quietly harming retention and trust. I'd also check segment effects (did clicks rise overall but fall for an important subgroup?), the metric's durability (is the +3% a novelty spike that fades, or stable over the full test?), and secondary/long-term metrics that a short test can't fully capture (which might warrant a longer holdback group).

The reasoning is that a real online experiment tracks a small suite of metrics, not one: the primary metric you hope to move (clicks), plus guardrail metrics that catch harmful side effects, plus long-term/holistic metrics that guard against short-term gaming. Only if clicks rose and the guardrails held and the effect is durable would I ship — and even then, I'd often ramp gradually (Lesson 10.4's canary) while monitoring. This is the online version of Lesson 11.1's core warning: optimizing and testing on a single proxy invites Goodhart failures, so you evaluate against multiple metrics that together approximate the true goal. Recognizing that "+3% clicks" is necessary but not sufficient — and knowing to check guardrails, segments, and durability — is exactly the maturity that separates rigorous online evaluation from metric-chasing that ships harmful changes.

3Sometimes you can't A/B test (e.g., a medical AI where a worse model could harm patients, or a change affecting all users at once). What are the alternatives and trade-offs?
Show solution & reasoning

When a live A/B test is too risky or infeasible, several alternatives trade off rigor against safety and practicality. Shadow mode (Lesson 9.4): run the new model alongside the old on real traffic but don't act on its outputs — just log what it would have done and compare to the old model (and to eventual outcomes). This gets real-data evaluation with zero user risk, though it can't measure the new model's effect on user behavior (since users never see it). Staged/canary rollout: expose the new model to a tiny fraction of users first, monitor closely, and expand only if safe — limiting blast radius, useful when you must eventually go live but want to de-risk. Offline evaluation on rich historical data (including counterfactual/off-policy methods that estimate how a new policy would have performed on logged data) — no user risk but reliant on assumptions. Human expert review: for high-stakes domains, have experts (doctors, for medical AI) evaluate the model's outputs on real cases before any deployment. Rigorous prospective trials: in medicine, a formal clinical trial with oversight, rather than a casual A/B test.

The trade-off is between evaluation rigor and risk/feasibility. A live A/B test is the gold standard for measuring real effect, but it exposes some users to the new model, which is unacceptable when harm is possible or when you can't split traffic. The alternatives reduce or eliminate that exposure — shadow mode and offline methods at the cost of not measuring true user-behavior effects, canary at the cost of speed, expert review at the cost of scale — so you choose based on the stakes: the higher the potential harm, the more you lean on no-risk methods (shadow, expert review, formal trials) before any real exposure, and the more gradually you roll out. The deeper principle, which connects to the whole safety theme of this track, is that evaluation methodology must respect the stakes: you don't experiment on people who could be harmed just to get a cleaner metric, so high-stakes AI demands evaluation approaches that establish safety before exposure, accepting some loss of experimental purity as the price of responsibility. Knowing this menu of alternatives — and when each is appropriate — is what lets you evaluate responsibly when a simple A/B test isn't an option.

★ Deep dive: the statistics of experimentation and building a culture of testing▸

Rigorous A/B testing rests on a foundation of statistics worth understanding at least conceptually. The core question is statistical significance: given natural variation, how confident are we that the observed difference is real and not chance? This is formalized with hypothesis testing — a p-value estimates the probability of seeing a difference this large if the models were actually identical, and a small p-value (conventionally <0.05) suggests the difference is real. Statistical power and sample size determine whether your test can detect an effect of a given size: small effects need large samples, which is why detecting a 0.5% lift can require millions of users. Two error types matter: a false positive (concluding a difference exists when it doesn't — the danger of peeking and multiple comparisons) and a false negative (missing a real difference because the test was underpowered). Modern experimentation platforms handle these with pre-registered sample sizes, correction for multiple tests, sequential testing methods that allow safe continuous monitoring, and variance-reduction techniques — but the intuition to carry is that a difference isn't a result until the statistics say it's unlikely to be noise, and that the common shortcuts (peeking, tiny samples, short windows) manufacture false wins.

Beyond the mechanics, the deeper lesson is cultural. The best product and ML teams build a culture of experimentation: nearly every meaningful change ships behind an A/B test, because intuition about what will work is unreliable — a large fraction of changes that experts are confident will help turn out to be neutral or harmful when actually tested, and only rigorous online experiments reveal which ideas are real. This is humbling and powerful: it replaces "the highest-paid person's opinion" with evidence, catches harmful changes before full launch, and compounds many small validated improvements into large gains over time. It also connects the whole arc of this track — offline evaluation proposes, online experimentation disposes, and the discipline of not trusting a change until real users validate it is the antidote to the metric-goal gap (Lesson 11.1) and the offline/online divergence that plagues ML. For an ML engineer, being fluent in experimentation — designing valid tests, reading them honestly, and respecting the statistics — is as important as model-building itself, because it's how you know whether your models actually help.
