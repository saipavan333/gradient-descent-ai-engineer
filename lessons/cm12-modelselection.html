Track 3 · Classical ML

Model selection & cross-validation

⏱ 26 min Lesson 3.12 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsThe skill that separates trustworthy results from accidental ones: comparing models and tuning hyperparameters without fooling yourself. Cross-validation and pipelines keep every number honest.
By the end, you can
  • Run k-fold cross-validation correctly
  • Tune hyperparameters with grid/random search
  • Prevent leakage with pipelines
  • Use nested CV or a held-out test for honest scores

Concept The problem with a single validation split

You've used a validation set to make choices since Lesson 3.1. But a single split has a weakness: the score depends on which examples happened to land in that one validation set. Get a lucky split and a mediocre model looks great; get an unlucky one and a good model looks weak. With limited data, that randomness can be larger than the real difference between two models you're trying to compare — so you'd pick the wrong one.

Cross-validation fixes this by not relying on a single split. And the broader skill — choosing the best model and its settings without fooling yourself — is model selection, the discipline that separates trustworthy results from accidental ones. It's the capstone of this track because it governs every model in it.

Concept k-fold cross-validation

k-fold cross-validation splits the training data into $k$ equal parts ("folds"). It then trains $k$ times: each time, one fold is held out as validation and the other $k-1$ are used for training, rotating so every fold serves as validation exactly once. Average the $k$ scores for a far more stable estimate — and the spread (standard deviation) of those scores even tells you how reliable the estimate is.

Five or ten folds is typical. The cost is compute — you train $k$ models instead of one — but the payoff is an estimate that barely depends on luck. For classification, stratified k-fold keeps class proportions balanced in every fold (the default for good reason). For time series, you must respect time order and never train on the future.

Concept Searching for the best hyperparameters

Most models have hyperparameters — settings you choose rather than learn (a tree's depth, an SVM's $C$ and $\gamma$, a regularizer's $\lambda$). To tune them you try combinations and keep the best by cross-validation. Grid search tries every combination on a grid you specify; random search samples random combinations.

Counterintuitively, random search is often more efficient: real problems usually have a few hyperparameters that matter and several that don't, and random sampling tries many distinct values of the important ones instead of wasting the budget on a rigid grid. For expensive models, Bayesian optimization goes further, using past results to decide which combination to try next.

Worked example Cross-validation and a hyperparameter search, done right

We evaluate a logistic-regression pipeline on the breast-cancer data with 5-fold cross-validation, then search for the best regularization strength $C$. Crucially, the scaler lives inside a pipeline, so it's refit on each fold's training portion only — no leakage (Lesson 2.5) across folds.

Five folds give five scores from 0.974 to 0.991 — averaging to 0.981 with a small spread (std 0.007). That spread is the gift of cross-validation: a single split might have reported any one of those five numbers, but the average is stable and the tiny std tells us the model is consistent. Now tune $C$:

grid = GridSearchCV(pipe, {"logisticregression__C": [0.01, 0.1, 1, 10]}, cv=5).fit(X, y)
print("best:", grid.best_params_, " CV score:", round(grid.best_score_, 3))
Outputbest: {'logisticregression__C': 1} CV score: 0.981

The search tried four regularization strengths, each evaluated by 5-fold CV, and reports $C = 1$ as best at 0.981. Every number here is a cross-validated average, so our choice doesn't hinge on one lucky split — and because scaling happened inside the pipeline, no fold ever peeked at another's statistics. This is the gold-standard tuning loop, and it's exactly how you'd select among all the models in this track.

★ Key points to remember
  • A single validation split is noisy; k-fold cross-validation averages over k splits for a stable estimate (plus a std).
  • Every example is validation exactly once; use stratified folds for classification, time-ordered for time series.
  • Tune hyperparameters with grid or random search scored by CV; random is often more efficient.
  • Put preprocessing in a pipeline so it refits per fold — otherwise CV leaks and inflates scores.
  • Report the final number on a held-out test set the search never touched (or use nested CV).

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You compare two models with a single validation split: Model A scores 0.91, Model B scores 0.89. With 5-fold CV you get A: 0.90 ± 0.03 and B: 0.905 ± 0.02. Which model do you pick, and what did CV reveal?
Show solution & reasoning

The single split said A wins (0.91 vs 0.89), but CV reveals that gap was noise: A averages 0.90 and B averages 0.905, essentially tied, and A's larger spread (±0.03 vs ±0.02) means its single-split 0.91 was on the lucky end of its range. You'd pick B (marginally higher mean, lower variance) — or, more honestly, conclude they're statistically indistinguishable and choose on other grounds (simplicity, speed, interpretability).

The lesson: a 2-point difference from one split is within the noise, and acting on it would be chasing randomness. CV's standard deviations let you see whether a difference is real before you commit — exactly the protection a single split can't give.

2A teammate runs grid search over 5 hyperparameters with 6 values each, using 10-fold CV, and complains it's impossibly slow. Compute the number of model fits and suggest a fix.
Show solution & reasoning

Number of combinations $= 6^5 = 7{,}776$, and each is evaluated with 10-fold CV, so total fits $= 7{,}776 \times 10 = \mathbf{77{,}760}$ model trainings. No wonder it's slow — grid search's cost explodes exponentially with the number of hyperparameters (the "curse of dimensionality" again).

Fixes: switch to random search with a fixed budget (say 100 combinations × 10 folds = 1,000 fits — 77× cheaper, and usually finds a near-best setting because few of the five parameters truly matter); narrow the grid to the 1–2 parameters that matter most; use fewer folds (5 instead of 10) during the search and reserve 10-fold for the final check; or use Bayesian optimization to spend the budget smartly. Random search is the usual first move.

3Someone reports "my model gets 95% — I tried 200 hyperparameter combinations and report the best one's cross-validation score." What's the subtle problem, and what's the correct procedure?
Show solution & reasoning

The subtle problem: by trying 200 combinations and reporting the best CV score, they've used the CV data to choose — and the maximum over 200 noisy estimates is optimistically biased (some configuration scores high partly by luck). The reported 95% is the score of the winner on the very data used to crown it, so it overstates true performance. It's a mild form of the leakage / "testing on training" problem.

The correct procedure is nested cross-validation (or a held-out test set): an inner CV loop selects hyperparameters, and an outer CV loop (or a final untouched test set) estimates performance of the whole selection procedure. The outer score is honest because the data it uses never participated in choosing the hyperparameters. Simpler version: split off a test set at the very start, do all 200-combination tuning with CV on the rest, and report the chosen model's score on that test set exactly once.

★ Deep dive: nested CV, pipelines, and the discipline that prevents self-deception▸

Nested cross-validation deserves a closer look because it answers a question people constantly get wrong: "how well will my whole pipeline, including the hyperparameter tuning, do on new data?" The outer loop splits into train/test folds; within each outer training fold, an inner loop does the full hyperparameter search; then the chosen model is scored on the outer test fold it never saw. Averaging the outer scores estimates the performance of your entire model-building procedure, not just one fixed model. It's more expensive (folds × folds), but it's the only way to get an unbiased estimate when you're tuning aggressively — and it's how careful papers and competitions report results.

The humble pipeline is the unsung hero of leakage prevention. Any step that learns from data — scaling, imputation, feature selection, PCA, even choosing which features to keep — must be fit on training data only, and refit fresh on each CV fold. Doing this by hand is error-prone; wrapping every such step in a pipeline makes cross-validation automatically refit the whole chain per fold, closing leaks you might not even notice. A shocking fraction of "too good to be true" results in industry trace back to a scaler or feature-selector fit on the full dataset before splitting. The fix is structural: never transform raw data outside a pipeline.

Step back and notice the throughline of this entire track: everything is about not fooling yourself. Train/test splits, held-out validation, regularization, the bias–variance gap, cross-validation, nested CV, pipelines — each is a guard against the same seductive failure of believing a model is better than it is. Models are easy; honest evaluation is hard, and it's the skill that most separates practitioners who ship reliable systems from those whose "95% accurate" model collapses in production. Carry this discipline into every track ahead — it matters just as much for a large language model as for a logistic regression.
