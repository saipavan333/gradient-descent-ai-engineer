Track 7 · LLMs & Generative AI

What is a large language model

⏱ 22 min Lesson 7.1 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsAn LLM is the transformer you built in Track 6, scaled to billions of parameters. Knowing the lifecycle — pretrain, fine-tune, align, deploy — and what scale does and does not fix is the foundation for this whole track.
By the end, you can
  • Define an LLM as a scaled-up transformer
  • Walk the pretrain→finetune→align→deploy lifecycle
  • Explain in-context learning and emergence
  • Recognize failure modes (hallucination, frozen knowledge)

Concept A large language model is a transformer, scaled up

You already built the architecture of an LLM in Track 6: a stack of transformer decoder blocks trained to predict the next token (Lesson 6.3). A large language model (LLM) is exactly that — there's no new architecture — but scaled to an extreme along three axes at once: billions of parameters (weights), trillions of tokens of training data, and enormous compute (thousands of GPUs for months). "Large" is the whole story; the recipe is the transformer you already understand.

Why does mere scale produce such capable systems? Because the next-token objective (Lesson 6.3) implicitly demands grammar, facts, reasoning, and style — and a bigger model trained on more text can capture more of those patterns. The relationship between scale and capability is so reliable it has its own equations (scaling laws, Lesson 7.7), and some abilities appear only past a certain size. The model that does all this is, mechanically, still "predict the next token, very well."

Concept The life of an LLM: pretrain, fine-tune, align, deploy

A deployed assistant like ChatGPT isn't a raw next-token predictor — it goes through several stages, each of which gets its own lesson in this track:

The stages: pretraining on a massive text corpus (the expensive, knowledge-building step), supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction–response examples to teach it to be useful, alignment (RLHF or DPO) to make it follow human preferences, and finally deployment, where prompting (Lesson 7.5), retrieval (Track 8), and tools (Track 9) shape its behavior in production. A "base model" has only been pretrained; a "chat model" has gone through all of it.

Concept What scale buys — and what it doesn't

Scale unlocks remarkable capabilities. The headline one is in-context learning: a large model can learn a new task from a few examples placed in the prompt, with no weight updates — show it three labeled examples and it generalizes the pattern (Lesson 7.5). It also gains broad world knowledge, multi-step reasoning, and cross-domain transfer, all as byproducts of next-token prediction at scale.

But scale doesn't fix everything, and the failure modes matter. LLMs hallucinate — confidently state false things — because they're trained to produce plausible text, not verified truth. Their knowledge is frozen at training time (no awareness of yesterday's news, fixed by retrieval in Track 8). They can be inconsistent, sensitive to prompt wording, and they inherit biases from their training data (Track 12). Understanding both sides — what scale gives and what it doesn't — is what separates someone who uses LLMs well from someone who trusts them blindly.

Worked example Counting the parameters of an LLM

Where do those "billions of parameters" actually live? Let's count them for GPT-2 small, using the transformer structure from Track 6. The parameters are in the token embedding table (vocab × dimension) and in each layer's attention and feed-forward weights (roughly $12 d^2$ per layer: about $4d^2$ for the Q/K/V/output projections and $8d^2$ for the FFN).

That's 124 million parameters — and indeed GPT-2 small is a 124M-parameter model. Notice the quadratic $d^2$: the per-layer cost grows with the square of the dimension, so doubling the model width quadruples each layer's parameters. This is why scaling up gets expensive fast, and why the largest models (GPT-3's 175 billion) are roughly 1,400× this small one — wider, deeper, with bigger vocabularies. Every one of those parameters is a number learned by gradient descent (Track 1) to lower next-token loss. "Large" language model is a very literal name.

★ Key points to remember
  • An LLM is a transformer decoder (Track 6) scaled to billions of parameters, trillions of tokens, and huge compute.
  • The lifecycle: pretrain → supervised fine-tune → align (RLHF/DPO) → deploy.
  • Scale unlocks in-context learning (learn from prompt examples, no weight updates), broad knowledge, and reasoning.
  • Failure modes persist: hallucination, frozen knowledge, prompt sensitivity, inherited bias.
  • It's still just next-token prediction — the capabilities emerge from scale, not a new objective.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A model has dimension $d = 2048$ and $L = 24$ layers, with a vocabulary of 50,000. Roughly how many parameters? (Use embedding $= \text{vocab} \times d$ and $\approx 12 d^2$ per layer.)
Show solution & reasoning

Embedding: $50{,}000 \times 2048 \approx 102$M. Per layer: $12 \times 2048^2 = 12 \times 4.19\text{M} \approx 50.3$M; times 24 layers $\approx 1{,}208$M. Total $\approx 102 + 1{,}208 \approx \mathbf{1.3\ \text{billion}}$ parameters.

Notice the layers now dominate (1.2B vs 0.1B for embeddings) — as models get deeper and wider, the transformer blocks, not the embedding table, hold the vast majority of parameters. This is roughly a GPT-2 XL / small-modern-LLM scale. The same arithmetic, scaled further (d≈12288, L≈96), gives GPT-3's 175B.

2A user asks an LLM "What happened in the news yesterday?" and gets a confident but fabricated answer. Explain why, and name the technique that fixes it.
Show solution & reasoning

Two issues combine. First, the model's knowledge is frozen at training time — it has no information about events after its training cutoff, so it literally cannot know yesterday's news. Second, rather than say "I don't know," it produces a plausible-sounding continuation (hallucination), because that's what next-token prediction optimizes for.

The fix is retrieval-augmented generation (RAG, Track 8): fetch relevant, up-to-date documents (e.g., today's news) and put them in the prompt, so the model answers from provided sources rather than its frozen memory. Grounding the model in retrieved facts is the primary defense against both stale knowledge and hallucination — which is exactly why RAG is one of the most important LLM techniques in production.

3Distinguish a "base model" from a "chat model," and explain why you usually can't just deploy a freshly pretrained base model as an assistant.
Show solution & reasoning

A base model has only been pretrained — it's a raw next-token predictor that completes text. Ask it a question and it might continue with more questions (because that's a plausible continuation of a list of questions in its training data), rather than answering. It's knowledgeable but not helpful. A chat model has additionally gone through supervised fine-tuning and alignment (Lessons 6.2–6.3), which teach it to follow instructions and behave as a helpful assistant.

You usually can't deploy a base model directly because it doesn't reliably do what you ask — it has the knowledge but not the behavior. The SFT and RLHF stages are what convert "a model that knows things" into "an assistant that helps," which is why those stages (the next two lessons) exist and why every deployed chatbot has been through them.

★ Deep dive: emergence, the bitter lesson, and what "understanding" means▸

One of the most surprising findings is emergence (Lesson 7.7): certain abilities — multi-step arithmetic, following complex instructions, in-context learning itself — are essentially absent in small models and then appear, sometimes abruptly, past a certain scale. A 1B model can't do a task at all; a 100B model does it well. Whether these jumps are truly discontinuous or an artifact of how we measure them is debated, but the practical reality stands: some capabilities only show up when you go big, which is a large part of why the field has chased scale so relentlessly.

This connects to what Rich Sutton called the "bitter lesson" of AI: over and over, general methods that leverage more computation (search and learning) have beaten hand-crafted, knowledge-engineered approaches. LLMs are the ultimate expression — almost no task-specific engineering, just a simple objective (predict the next token), a general architecture (the transformer), and staggering scale. Decades of clever linguistic rules were outperformed by "feed it the whole internet and make it big." It's humbling, and it's reshaped how the field allocates effort: toward data, compute, and scalable methods rather than bespoke cleverness.

Finally, the philosophical question this raises: does a next-token predictor understand anything, or is it a "stochastic parrot" remixing its training data? The honest answer is that it's genuinely contested and partly a question about what "understand" means. Empirically, large models build internal representations that track meaning (they can manipulate concepts, not just surface words), yet they also fail in ways no understanding system would (confidently wrong arithmetic, contradictions). A useful stance for an engineer is pragmatic: treat the model as a powerful, fallible pattern-completer — extraordinarily capable within the distribution of its training, unreliable outside it — and design systems (retrieval, verification, guardrails; Tracks 8, 9, 12) that harness the capability while bounding the failures. The rest of this track is about exactly that: how these models are trained, aligned, steered, and made useful and safe.

You know what an LLM is and how its life goes. Next, in 6.2 Pretraining vs fine-tuning, we look closely at the two training stages — the expensive one that builds knowledge, and the cheap one that specializes it — and when to use each versus prompting or retrieval.
