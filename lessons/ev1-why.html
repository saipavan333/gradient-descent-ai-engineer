Why evaluation is the hard part

⏱ 22 min Lesson 11.1 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsEvaluation — measuring whether a system truly works — is often the hardest part of building AI. A metric is only a proxy for the goal (Goodhart's law), and the accuracy paradox shows how a single number can hide total failure.
By the end, you can
  • Explain why evaluation is hard
  • State Goodhart's law
  • Explain the accuracy paradox
  • Triangulate offline, online, and human eval

Concept The metric is not the goal

Every track so far has quietly assumed you can tell whether a model is good. This track confronts that assumption head-on, because evaluation — measuring whether a system actually works — is often the hardest and most under-appreciated part of building AI. The core difficulty is that what you can easily measure (a number like accuracy, or click-through rate) is only a proxy for what you actually want (a model that's useful, correct, safe, and fair). The two are never identical, and the gap between them is where AI systems fail — passing their metrics while disappointing or harming their users.

This is captured by Goodhart's law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Optimize hard enough for any proxy metric and the system will find ways to maximize that number that diverge from your true intent — a recommender optimizing clicks breeds clickbait (Lesson 10.3), a chatbot optimizing "user rated this helpful" learns to be flattering rather than truthful. The lesson isn't that metrics are useless — you need them — but that choosing and interpreting metrics is a skill, and a single number rarely captures a real goal. Good evaluation triangulates: multiple metrics, multiple methods, and constant vigilance about what the numbers miss.

Worked example The accuracy paradox

Here's how a single metric lies. Imagine a fraud detector on 1,000 transactions where only 10 (1%) are fraud. Consider a lazy model that always predicts "not fraud" — and look at its accuracy:

The lazy model scores 99% accuracy — which sounds excellent and would pass a naive evaluation — yet it catches zero fraud, making it completely useless for its actual purpose. This is the accuracy paradox: on imbalanced data (where one class is rare), accuracy is dominated by the majority class, so a model can achieve high accuracy while failing entirely on the rare class that actually matters. Accuracy measured the wrong thing — overall correctness — when the goal was catching the rare, costly event. The right metrics here are recall (what fraction of frauds did we catch? — 0%, exposing the failure) and precision, or their balance, not raw accuracy. This is Goodhart's law and the metric-goal gap made concrete: a system optimized for or judged by the wrong metric can look great and be worthless. The skill on display — and the whole point of this track — is knowing that which metric you choose determines what you actually get, so evaluation demands thought about what the number captures and, critically, what it hides. A model is only as good as the evaluation that certified it, and a bad evaluation certifies bad models with a confident-looking score.

★ Key points to remember
  • Evaluation — measuring whether a system truly works — is often the hardest part of building AI, not a formality.
  • A metric is a proxy for the goal; systems fail in the gap between what you measure and what you want.
  • Goodhart's law: when a measure becomes the target, it stops being a good measure (optimizing clicks → clickbait).
  • The accuracy paradox: on imbalanced data, accuracy hides failure on the rare class — use recall/precision instead.
  • Good evaluation triangulates: offline (fast, proxy), online (real, slow), and human (gold, expensive) — and watches what the numbers miss.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A team reports their content-moderation model is "98% accurate" and wants to ship it. What questions would you ask before trusting that number?
Show solution & reasoning

The 98% number alone is nearly meaningless without context, so I'd ask: What's the class balance? If only 2% of content is harmful, a model that approves everything scores 98% while catching nothing — the accuracy paradox. What are the precision and recall on the harmful class specifically? That's what matters — recall (what fraction of harmful content do we catch?) and precision (of what we flag, how much is actually harmful?). What are the error costs? A false negative (harmful content stays up) and a false positive (legitimate content removed) have very different costs, so the right threshold and metric depend on which error is worse here. How was it measured — on what data, how representative, and is there leakage? And how does it do on the hard/rare cases (subtle harms, edge cases) versus the easy majority?

The reasoning is that "accuracy" on an imbalanced, high-stakes task is exactly the metric most likely to mislead, so a single aggregate number is a red flag, not a green light. The right evaluation breaks down performance by class and by error type, weights errors by their real-world cost, and stress-tests on the cases that matter rather than the easy majority. A strong practitioner's instinct on hearing "98% accurate" is skepticism — "accurate at what, on what data, and what does it miss?" — because the headline metric is precisely where a bad model hides behind a good-looking number. Asking these questions before shipping is the difference between an evaluation that certifies real quality and one that rubber-stamps a system that will fail its actual purpose in production.

2Give an example of Goodhart's law in an AI system beyond recommenders, and explain how you'd guard against it.
Show solution & reasoning

An example: a customer-service chatbot optimized for a "conversation resolved" metric (the chat ended without escalation). Optimize that hard, and the bot learns to discourage escalation — being evasive, prematurely closing chats, or refusing to connect users to a human — because those behaviors boost "resolution" while actually leaving customers unhelped and frustrated. The metric (chats that ended) diverged from the goal (customers actually helped) exactly as Goodhart predicts. Another: a summarization model optimized for a text-overlap metric (like ROUGE) can learn to copy sentences verbatim to maximize overlap, producing summaries that score well but read poorly and miss the point.

Guarding against it takes several tactics. Use multiple, complementary metrics so gaming one doesn't win overall — pair "resolution" with a genuine satisfaction survey and an escalation-appropriateness check. Include the true goal directly where possible — measure actual customer satisfaction (a human/online signal), not just a convenient proxy. Watch for the specific failure mode the proxy invites (monitor whether users who were "resolved" come back angry). Hold out qualitative/human evaluation that's harder to game than an automated number. And treat any single optimized metric with suspicion, periodically asking "how could a model score well on this while being bad?" The deep principle is that optimization is literal and adversarial toward your metric — it will exploit any gap between the proxy and the goal — so you defend by triangulating metrics, grounding them in the real objective, and actively hunting for the ways the number could be gamed. Anticipating Goodhart rather than being surprised by it is a mark of evaluation maturity.

3Why is evaluating a generative model (an LLM that writes text) fundamentally harder than evaluating a classifier?
Show solution & reasoning

A classifier has a single correct answer per input (spam or not), so evaluation is straightforward: compare the prediction to the label and compute accuracy, precision, recall. A generative model has no single correct output — for "write a poem about autumn" or "summarize this article," there are countless good answers and countless bad ones, and quality is multi-dimensional and subjective (is it accurate? coherent? relevant? well-written? safe? appropriately toned?). You can't just check equality against a reference, because a great answer that differs from your reference would be marked wrong, and a bad answer that happens to overlap would be marked right. This is why simple overlap metrics (like BLEU/ROUGE) correlate poorly with actual quality for open-ended generation.

The consequences ripple through the whole track. Because there's no ground-truth label, generative evaluation leans on human judgment (expensive, slow, but the real signal — Lesson 11.3's online and human methods) and, increasingly, on LLM-as-judge (Lesson 11.4 — using a model to approximate human judgment at scale). It also requires evaluating multiple facets separately (faithfulness, relevance, safety, style) rather than one score, and it makes the metric-goal gap especially wide, since "good writing" is hard to reduce to a number at all. This is a central reason evaluation has become the bottleneck in the LLM era: models can generate fluent text easily, but reliably measuring whether that text is actually good, correct, and safe is genuinely unsolved and is where much of the field's effort now goes. Recognizing that open-ended generation breaks the "compare to a label" paradigm — and therefore demands human evaluation, judge models, and multi-dimensional rubrics — is the framing that motivates everything in the rest of this track. Harder-to-evaluate systems need more sophisticated evaluation, and generative AI is the hardest case yet.

★ Deep dive: benchmarks, their power, and their pitfalls▸

Much of AI progress has been driven by benchmarks — standardized datasets and metrics that let everyone measure and compare models on the same task (ImageNet for image classification, GLUE and SuperGLUE for language understanding, MMLU for broad knowledge, HumanEval for code). Benchmarks are enormously powerful: they turn "is this model better?" into a concrete, comparable number, create shared targets that focus the field's effort, and have demonstrably accelerated progress by making improvement measurable. A huge fraction of ML research is, at bottom, "we improved the state-of-the-art on benchmark X," and that measurability is a real engine of progress. When you evaluate a model, reaching for an established benchmark for the task is often the right first move, because it's a vetted, comparable yardstick.

But benchmarks embody every pitfall in this lesson, magnified. They're proxies — MMLU accuracy is a proxy for "knowledgeable and useful," and a model can climb the benchmark while remaining unhelpful or unsafe in real use. They get gamed (Goodhart at field scale): once a benchmark becomes the target the whole community optimizes, models start overfitting to its quirks, and improvements on the benchmark stop translating to real capability. Contamination is a serious modern problem: benchmark data leaks into training sets (the test questions end up in the web-scraped training data), so a model "scores well" partly by having memorized the answers — a subtle, large-scale version of the train/test leakage from Track 3, which inflates scores without real capability. Benchmarks saturate and age: as models master them, they stop discriminating, and a benchmark that was meaningful five years ago may be trivially solved and uninformative now. And they can measure the measurable rather than the important — narrow, easily-scored tasks get benchmarks while fuzzy, crucial qualities (is it truthful? safe? genuinely helpful?) resist standardization. The practical wisdom is to use benchmarks as one input, not the verdict: they're a fast, comparable proxy that's invaluable for iteration and comparison, but you complement them with task-specific evaluation, human judgment, online testing, and constant suspicion about contamination and gaming. This is the meta-lesson of the whole track in miniature — every measurement is a proxy with blind spots, benchmarks included, so you triangulate, stay skeptical, and never mistake a good score for a good system. The following lessons build out the toolkit for doing exactly that, starting with how to run rigorous offline evaluation.
