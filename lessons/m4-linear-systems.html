Track 1 · Math Foundations for ML

Linear systems, span, rank & the inverse

⏱ 24 min Lesson 1.4 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsEvery linear model and least-squares fit is secretly asking: given outputs, which inputs produce them — can we solve a system of linear equations? Knowing when a solution exists, is unique, or is impossible is how you reason about whether a model can even fit your data.
By the end, you can
  • Read Ax = b as intersecting lines and say when it has one, none, or many solutions
  • Define span and linear independence and picture them
  • Find the rank of a matrix and explain why it is the true dimensionality
  • Explain the inverse and why a singular matrix has none

Concept Turning the question around

In Lesson 1.2 we always knew the matrix $A$ and the input $x$, and we computed the output $b = Ax$. Now we flip it: we know the output $b$ and want to find the input $x$. This is called solving a linear system, and it's the quiet workhorse beneath linear regression, "best-fit" lines, and the basic question "can my model even represent this data?"

Let's define the words. An equation is a statement that two things are equal, like $x + y = 5$. It's called a linear equation when the unknowns ($x$, $y$) only appear multiplied by plain numbers and added together — no squares, no $x \times y$, nothing bent. (That word "linear" again means "straight-line.") A system of linear equations is just two or more such equations that must all be true at the same time. To solve the system is to find the values of the unknowns that satisfy (make true) every equation at once.

A single linear equation in two unknowns, like $x + y = 5$, is made true by a whole line of $(x,y)$ points (e.g. $(5,0)$, $(4,1)$, $(3,2)$, …). A second equation, $x - y = 1$, is another line. Solving the system means finding the point(s) that lie on every line at once — geometrically, where the lines cross.

Only three things can happen, and each tells you something real about your data:

PictureNumber of solutionsWhat it means
lines cross onceexactly onethe equations pin down a unique answer
lines are parallel (never meet)nonethe equations contradict each other — no $x$ works
lines lie exactly on top of each otherinfinitely manyone equation is redundant; the answer isn't pinned down

Those last two — "no solution" and "infinitely many" — aren't weird edge cases to ignore. They're exactly the situations where a model can't fit or can't decide, and the tools in this lesson (span, rank, the inverse) are how you diagnose them.

Concept Span: everywhere your vectors can reach

Recall a linear combination from Lesson 1.1: scale some vectors and add the results, like $2u + 3w$. The span of a set of vectors is every point you can possibly reach by taking linear combinations of them — try all multipliers, add them up, collect all the results.

One nonzero vector spans a line (all its stretched and flipped copies). Two vectors that point in genuinely different directions span the whole plane — you can reach any point by mixing them. This directly answers "can my system be solved for any $b$?": yes, exactly when the columns of $A$ span the space that $b$ lives in.

Concept Linear independence: does each vector add something new?

Vectors are linearly independent if none of them can be built as a linear combination of the others — each one points in a direction the rest can't reach. If one of them can be built from the others, the set is linearly dependent, and that redundant vector adds nothing to the span (it's already reachable).

i
Why a data scientist cares

If two feature columns are linearly dependent — say "height in centimeters" and "height in inches" (one is just 2.54× the other) — then one is redundant. The model gains no new information from the second, and, as you'll see with the inverse below, it can even make the math blow up. Spotting dependence is spotting duplicated or redundant features.

Concept Rank: the true number of dimensions

The rank of a matrix is the number of linearly independent columns it has — equivalently, the dimension of the space its columns span. It's the matrix's "true" dimensionality, which can be smaller than how many columns it appears to have.

A matrix is full rank when all its columns are independent (no redundancy). If the rank is less than the number of columns, the matrix is rank-deficient: some column is redundant, the columns don't span the full space, and a system $Ax = b$ may have no solution or infinitely many. Rank, in one number, tells you whether your equations actually constrain the unknowns.

Concept The inverse — and when it doesn't exist

For a square matrix (same number of rows and columns) that is full rank, there's an inverse, written $A^{-1}$ ("A inverse"), that undoes what $A$ does: $A^{-1}A = I$, where $I$ is the identity matrix (the "do nothing" matrix from Lesson 1.2, the matrix version of the number 1). With an inverse, the system $Ax = b$ has the clean solution $x = A^{-1}b$ — just "undo $A$ on both sides."

But if $A$ is rank-deficient, it is singular: it collapses space (the square flattens onto a line, its area becoming 0), and a collapse can't be undone — there is no inverse. The single number that detects this is the determinant (a number computed from a square matrix that measures how much it scales area or volume). A determinant of $0$ means "collapsed to zero area," which means singular, which means no inverse.

★
The one-line summary

Square + full rank + nonzero determinant ⇒ invertible ⇒ $Ax=b$ has exactly one solution $x = A^{-1}b$. Anything else, and you're in "no solution / many solutions" territory — which is exactly where regression's least-squares trick (Track 3) earns its keep.

Worked example A café receipt, solved by computer

A receipt says: 5 items were bought for $14 total. Muffins cost $2, lattes cost $4. How many of each? Let $x$ = muffins, $y$ = lattes. That's a linear system:

$$ \underbrace{x + y = 5}_{\text{count of items}}, \qquad \underbrace{2x + 4y = 14}_{\text{total dollars}}. $$ 
import numpy as np

A = np.array([[1, 1],      # item count:   1·x + 1·y
              [2, 4]])      # dollars:      2·x + 4·y
b = np.array([5, 14])

x = np.linalg.solve(A, b)               # ask NumPy to solve A x = b
print("muffins, lattes:", x)            # [3. 2.]
print("determinant:", np.linalg.det(A)) # 2.0  (nonzero -> invertible, unique solution)
print("rank:", np.linalg.matrix_rank(A))# 2    (full rank for a 2x2)
Outputmuffins, lattes: [3. 2.] determinant: 2.0000000000000004 rank: 2

Three muffins, two lattes — the unique crossing point from the diagram. The nonzero determinant and full rank both confirm a single solution exists, before we even look at the answer. Now watch what happens when the equations are secretly redundant (the second is just the first, doubled):

A_bad = np.array([[1, 2],
                  [2, 4]])              # row 2 = 2 × row 1  -> dependent rows
print("rank:", np.linalg.matrix_rank(A_bad))   # 1  (rank-deficient!)
print("determinant:", np.linalg.det(A_bad))    # 0.0 (singular)
np.linalg.solve(A_bad, np.array([3, 6]))       # raises LinAlgError: Singular matrix
Outputrank: 1 determinant: 0.0 LinAlgError: Singular matrix

Rank 1 instead of 2, determinant 0, and solve flatly refuses — exactly the "collapsed" case. In PyTorch the tools are torch.linalg.solve, torch.linalg.det, and torch.linalg.matrix_rank; in TensorFlow, tf.linalg.solve and tf.linalg.det. Same concepts, same warnings.

★ Key points to remember
  • A linear system $Ax=b$ asks "which input $x$ produces output $b$?" — geometrically, where the equation-lines meet.
  • It has one solution (lines cross), none (parallel), or infinitely many (same line).
  • Span = everything reachable by linear combinations; independent vectors each add a new direction.
  • Rank = number of independent columns = the matrix's true dimensionality. Full rank = no redundancy.
  • A square, full-rank matrix is invertible ($A^{-1}A=I$); a rank-deficient one is singular (determinant 0, no inverse).

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1By substitution, solve the system $x + y = 10$ and $3x - y = 2$. Then say how you'd confirm the answer is unique without solving again.
Show solution & reasoning

From the first equation, $y = 10 - x$. Substitute into the second: $3x - (10 - x) = 2 \Rightarrow 4x - 10 = 2 \Rightarrow 4x = 12 \Rightarrow x = 3$, so $y = 7$.

Uniqueness check: the coefficient matrix is $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\3&-1\end{bmatrix}$, whose determinant is $(1)(-1) - (1)(3) = -4 \neq 0$. A nonzero determinant ⇒ full rank ⇒ exactly one solution. So you can certify "unique" from the determinant alone, before ever finding $(3,7)$.

2Are the vectors $(1,2)$, $(2,4)$, and $(0,1)$ linearly independent? What is the rank of the matrix whose columns are these three vectors, and what does it say about their span?
Show solution & reasoning

$(2,4) = 2\cdot(1,2)$, so the first two are dependent — the second adds nothing. But $(0,1)$ points in a new direction not on the line of $(1,2)$. So there are two genuinely different directions among the three vectors: the rank is 2.

Implication: three vectors, but they span only a 2-D space (the whole plane, since 2 independent vectors already fill 2-D). The "extra" vector is redundant. This is the everyday meaning of rank — count the genuinely different directions, not the vectors.

3You run a linear regression and get the error "singular matrix." In plain terms, what has probably gone wrong with your features, and what's one fix?
Show solution & reasoning

"Singular" means the feature matrix is rank-deficient — some column is a linear combination of others (duplicated/perfectly-correlated features, a one-hot encoding that includes every category plus an intercept, or "dollars" and "cents"). The math that solves for the coefficients needs a full-rank, invertible matrix, and it can't invert a collapsed one.

Fixes: drop the redundant column; remove one category from a one-hot set (the "dummy variable trap"); or add a little regularization (ridge regression), which nudges the matrix back to invertible. Each restores full rank. You'll meet ridge formally in Track 3 — here you understand why it works.

★ Deep dive: the determinant as area, and why ML almost never computes an inverse▸

The determinant is an area (or volume) scale factor. Feed the unit square through a $2\times2$ matrix and it becomes a parallelogram; the determinant is that parallelogram's area (with a sign for orientation). $\det = 2$ doubles area; $\det = 1$ preserves it (like a rotation); $\det = 0$ flattens it to a line — zero area, the collapse that kills invertibility. In 3-D it's the volume of the transformed cube. That's the whole geometric story behind "determinant 0 = singular."

So why do real ML systems almost never compute $A^{-1}$? Two reasons. First, explicitly inverting a big matrix is slow and numerically fragile — solvers like np.linalg.solve are faster and more accurate than forming $A^{-1}$ and multiplying. Second, and more importantly, ML systems are usually overdetermined: more equations (data points) than unknowns (parameters), so there is no exact $x$ with $Ax=b$. Instead we find the $x$ that makes $Ax$ as close to $b$ as possible — the least-squares solution, which projects $b$ onto what the columns can reach (remember projection from Lesson 1.3 — it returns here). And for large models the dominant method isn't linear algebra at all; it's gradient descent, the iterative method you'll meet in Lesson 1.8. A related warning sign even when a matrix is invertible: a tiny (but nonzero) determinant means it's nearly singular, making solutions wildly sensitive to small noise — captured by the condition number. Well-conditioned data is stable; ill-conditioned data amplifies every measurement error.
