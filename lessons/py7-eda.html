Putting it together: an EDA workflow

⏱ 28 min Lesson 2.7 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsEDA is the disciplined first look that separates good projects from doomed ones — checking shape, types, missingness, distributions, relationships, and leaks before any model.
By the end, you can
  • Run a repeatable EDA checklist on a new dataset
  • Find missing values, outliers, and suspicious correlations
  • Spot leakage and duplicates before modeling
  • Turn observations into modeling hypotheses

Concept Look before you model

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the disciplined first look at a dataset, before any modeling. Its purpose is to understand the data and find problems while they're still cheap to fix — wrong types, missing values, outliers, duplicates, suspicious relationships, and leaks. (An outlier is a value far away from the rest — a 200-year-old customer, a negative price — often a data-entry error or something worth investigating.) Skipping EDA is how people spend a week training models on data that was broken from the start. EDA also ties together everything in this track: pandas to inspect, NumPy underneath, plotting to see.

EDA isn't random poking; it's a repeatable checklist you run on every new dataset:

Concept The checklist, step by step

Each step pairs with a pandas command (don't worry about memorizing them — the worked example shows them in action):

  • 1. Load & peek — df.head() (show the first few rows), df.shape (rows, columns). Look at actual rows; do the values make sense?
  • 2. Types & missingness — df.info() (the dtype and non-empty count of each column), df.isna().sum() (how many missing values per column). Is a number stored as text? Where are the holes?
  • 3. Distributions — df.describe() (count, mean, min, max, etc. per column) and histograms. Range, skew, outliers, impossible values (a negative age, a 999 placeholder).
  • 4. Relationships — df.corr() (the correlation between each pair of numeric columns — a number from −1 to +1 saying how strongly they move together) and scatter plots. Which features relate to the thing you're predicting, and to each other?
  • 5. Leakage & duplicates — df.duplicated().sum() (count of exact repeat rows), and a hard look for any feature that's too predictive or wouldn't exist at prediction time.
  • 6. Hypotheses — turn what you saw into concrete plans: which features to use, what to transform, what to predict.
!
The single most useful EDA habit

Actually look at the raw rows — df.head(20), df.sample(10) (10 random rows). Summary statistics average away the weirdness; eyeballing real records surfaces the placeholder "-1" ages, the duplicated entries, and the garbled text that no .describe() will ever show you.

Worked example Running the checklist on a messy dataset

Six customer records with planted problems — watch the checklist surface each one.

import pandas as pd, numpy as np

df = pd.DataFrame({
    "customer_id": [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 5],          # note: 5 appears twice
    "age":         [34, 28, np.nan, 45, 52, 52],# one missing
    "income_k":    [55, 40, 62, 90, 120, 120],
    "channel":     ["web", "web", "app", "app", "web", "web"],
    "spend":       [12, 8, 15, 30, 45, 45],
})

print("shape:", df.shape)
print("\nmissing per column:\n", df.isna().sum())
print("\nduplicate rows:", df.duplicated().sum())
print("\nchannel counts:\n", df["channel"].value_counts())   # how many of each category
print("\ncorrelations with spend:\n", df.corr(numeric_only=True)["spend"].round(2))
Output (abridged)shape: (6, 5) missing per column: age 1 ... duplicate rows: 1 channel counts: web 4 app 2 correlations with spend: income_k 1.00 age 0.99 spend 1.00

In a few commands the checklist exposed three issues: a missing age (impute or drop — Lesson 2.5), a duplicate row (customer 5 appears twice → df.drop_duplicates()), and a near- 1.00 correlation between income_k and spend — extremely strong, and worth a second look: is income a fair predictor, or is spend partly derived from it (leakage)? Each finding becomes an action. That's EDA: not modeling, but the careful look that decides whether modeling will even work.

A fast first pass for any dataset:

df.describe(include="all")        # numeric + categorical summaries at once
df.sample(10)                     # eyeball real rows
df.isna().mean().sort_values()    # fraction missing per column, sorted
# import seaborn as sns; sns.heatmap(df.corr(numeric_only=True), annot=True)  # correlation map
★ Key points to remember
  • EDA is the disciplined look before modeling — understand the data and find problems while they're cheap to fix.
  • Run a checklist: peek at rows → types & missingness → distributions → relationships → leakage & duplicates → hypotheses.
  • Key tools: head, info, describe, isna().sum(), duplicated(), value_counts(), corr(), histograms, scatter.
  • Always look at raw rows — summaries hide placeholders, duplicates, and junk.
  • A correlation that's too perfect is a leakage warning, not a win.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You load a dataset and df.info() shows the "price" column has dtype object (text) instead of a number. What likely happened, and how would you fix it?
Show solution & reasoning

Some values aren't pure numbers — there are dollar signs, commas, or a stray "N/A"/"unknown" string in the column, so pandas typed the whole column as text. Fix: clean the strings (df["price"].str.replace("[$,]", "", regex=True)) and convert with pd.to_numeric(..., errors="coerce"), which turns anything un-parseable into NaN you can then handle.

The reasoning: a numeric column typed as text is a classic early-EDA catch — every later computation (mean, correlation, scaling) would silently fail or mislead. Checking dtypes first, before any math, prevents a cascade of confusing errors downstream.

2List, in order, the first five things you'd run on a brand-new spreadsheet you've never seen.
Show solution & reasoning

(1) df = pd.read_csv(...) then df.shape — how big? (2) df.head() / df.sample(10) — what do real rows look like? (3) df.info() — dtypes and non-empty counts. (4) df.describe(include="all") — ranges, outliers, category counts. (5) df.isna().mean() — fraction missing per column.

The reasoning: this sequence goes from coarse to fine — size, then a real look, then types, then distributions, then missingness — so you build an accurate mental model before touching a model. Having a fixed opening routine means you never skip the step that would have caught the problem.

3A teammate says "the data looks clean, let's skip EDA and train." Give two concrete risks of doing so.
Show solution & reasoning

(1) Hidden leakage: a feature that secretly encodes the answer (or wouldn't exist at prediction time) sails through, producing fantastic offline scores that collapse in production. (2) Silent data problems: duplicates that over-weight certain rows, a numeric column stored as text, placeholder values like -1 or 999, or a train/test split that puts the same entity on both sides — all of which quietly corrupt results.

The reasoning: "looks clean" usually means "I looked at the first five rows." Most damaging data issues are invisible without the deliberate checks EDA forces. Skipping it doesn't save time — it defers the cost to a far more expensive stage (debugging a deployed model, or worse, trusting a wrong one).

★ Deep dive: confirmation bias, the data dictionary, and analyzing the target▸

Guard against confirmation bias. EDA is exploratory, which makes it easy to go hunting for the story you already believe and stop when you find supporting evidence. The discipline is to look for what would disprove your hunch too: slice the data different ways, check whether a relationship holds across subgroups, and be especially skeptical of results you like. The same statistical caution from Track 1 (a pattern in a small sample may be noise) applies — don't over-interpret a wiggle in 50 rows.

Get (or build) a data dictionary. Before trusting a column, know what it actually means: its units, how it was collected, what a missing value signifies, and crucially when it becomes available relative to the prediction. Many "features" turn out to be leaks or nonsense once you learn their provenance. A column named status could be an input or the answer; only the dictionary (or asking whoever produced the data) tells you. This unglamorous metadata work is decisive.

Analyze the target specifically. For supervised problems, study the thing you're predicting first. Is the target imbalanced (1% fraud vs. 99% legit)? Then accuracy is a useless metric and you'll need the precision/recall thinking from Track 3, plus careful sampling. Is it skewed or heavy-tailed (prices, durations)? You may predict its log. Are there impossible target values to clean? Understanding the target's distribution shapes your metric, your loss, and your whole approach — which is why seasoned practitioners plot the target before anything else. EDA, done well, is where a project's biggest decisions are quietly made.
