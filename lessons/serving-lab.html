Where does p99 come from? When replicas can't keep up, requests wait in the queue — and waiting is latency. As utilization climbs past ~80%, the queue (and the tail) grows non-linearly: this is queueing theory, and it's why you never run a service at 100%. Autoscaling adds replicas when the tail crosses your target, then removes them when traffic falls.

Try these

1. Turn autoscaling off on a spiky pattern. Fix the replica count too low and watch a traffic spike send the queue — and p99 — through the roof. Requests aren't lost, but users wait seconds. This is an outage in all but name.

2. Turn autoscaling on. Same spike, but now replicas scale up to absorb it and scale back down after. Watch the cost line rise during the spike and settle after — you pay for peak, not average.

3. Over-provision. Crank replicas/capacity way up with autoscaling off. p99 is beautiful and flat… and utilization sits at 15% while you pay for idle machines. The job is the balance: enough headroom for the tail, not so much that you're lighting money on fire.

What this lab proves
  • Latency is mostly queueing — past ~80% utilization the tail explodes non-linearly, so you provision for the peak, not the average.
  • Autoscaling trades cost for stability — it follows the load so you don't pay for peak capacity 24/7, at the price of some lag and complexity.
  • Throughput ≠ latency — you can have huge throughput and terrible p99 at the same time; they're different problems.
  • Every knob is a trade-off — bigger replicas, more replicas, batching, caching each buy latency or cost, never both for free.
