Evaluation Lab

A model outputs a score, but a decision needs a threshold. Where you set it silently trades false alarms against misses — and every metric you'll be asked about in an interview moves as you slide it. Here they all move together, live.

One classifier, one set of scores. Drag the threshold and watch the confusion matrix reshuffle, precision and recall pull against each other, and the operating point slide along the ROC and PR curves. Then make the problem harder and watch the whole thing degrade — that's what a weak model feels like.

Precision vs recall, in one sentence each. Precision: of everything you flagged positive, how much was right (low precision = crying wolf). Recall: of everything that was actually positive, how much you caught (low recall = missing real cases). You almost never get both — lowering the threshold raises recall and lowers precision. F1 balances them; AUC summarizes the model's ranking ability independent of any threshold.

Try these

1. Chase recall. Drag the threshold low. You catch almost every positive (recall → 100%) but flag a pile of negatives too (precision drops). This is the right call for cancer screening — a miss is far worse than a false alarm.

2. Chase precision. Drag the threshold high. Now everything you flag is correct (precision → 100%) but you miss many real positives (recall drops). Right for, say, auto-blocking content where a wrong block is costly.

3. Weaken the model. Slide difficulty up so the distributions overlap. The ROC curve sags toward the diagonal and AUC falls toward 0.5 — no threshold can save a model that can't separate the classes. Evaluation tells you that, before you ship.

What this lab proves
  • A score isn't a decision. The threshold turns probabilities into labels — and it's a real, tunable choice.
  • Precision and recall trade off. Moving the threshold helps one and hurts the other; pick based on which error is costlier.
  • ROC/PR curves show all thresholds at once. AUC measures ranking quality independent of where you set the line.
  • Accuracy lies on imbalanced data. Always look at the confusion matrix and per-class metrics.
