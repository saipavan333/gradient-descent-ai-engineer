Tokenizer Lab

Before a language model sees a single word, a tokenizer chops your text into pieces. Here's a real Byte-Pair Encoding tokenizer — it learns its vocabulary from a corpus in front of you, then splits whatever you type. No libraries, every merge computed live.

Type your name, a rare word, an emoji-free sentence — and watch how the model would actually read it. Then drag the vocabulary size down to zero and see text dissolve into single characters. That trade-off — few big tokens vs many small ones — is one every LLM makes.

Why not just split on spaces? A word-level vocabulary would need millions of entries and still choke on a word it never saw. Character-level never has that problem but makes sequences painfully long. BPE is the middle path: common words stay whole (the_), rare ones break into reusable subwords (token + ization), and nothing is ever unknown.

Three things to try

1. Watch a word assemble. Set vocabulary to 0 — every character is its own token. Now drag it up slowly. Frequent pairs like t+h merge first, then th+e, until whole common words become single tokens. That's literally how the vocabulary is built.

2. Feed it a rare word. Type something the corpus never saw — your surname, "antidisestablishmentarianism", a UUID. It still tokenizes, just into more pieces. That "graceful degradation" is why models never hit an unknown token.

3. Feel the cost. The chars/token number is why tokenization matters for your bill: models charge per token, so a tokenizer that packs more characters into each token is literally cheaper to run. Non-English text and code usually get more tokens per character — a real fairness and cost issue.

What this lab proves
  • Tokenization is learned, not fixed — BPE greedily merges the most frequent adjacent pair, again and again, to build its vocabulary.
  • Subwords are the sweet spot — common words stay whole, rare words split into reusable pieces, and there are no unknown tokens.
  • Vocabulary size is a dial — more merges → fewer, longer tokens (shorter sequences, bigger embedding table); fewer merges → the opposite.
  • Tokens are the unit of everything — context length, latency and cost are all counted in tokens, not words or characters.
