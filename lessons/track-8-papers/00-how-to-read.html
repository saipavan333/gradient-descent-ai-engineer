How to read a paper (the three-pass method)

Research moves through papers, but nobody reads them front-to-back like a novel. There’s a method — three passes of increasing depth — that lets you triage dozens of papers and truly master the few that matter.

Track 8 · Lesson 0 Method + worked example ~20 min Builds on: Tracks 1–7

You now have the foundations to read modern AI research — but the literature is enormous and uneven, and reading inefficiently will drown you. This track teaches the working researcher’s loop: read, reproduce, critique. We start with reading, using Keshav’s widely taught three-pass method, then apply it to a paper you already know.

💡 Concept: read in passes, not in order

A paper is written to be defended — dense, linear, every claim hedged. That is the worst possible order for learning. Instead, make several quick passes, each adding depth, and stop as soon as you have what you need. Most papers deserve only a glance; a few deserve to be rebuilt.

  • Pass 1 (~10 min) — title, abstract, introduction, section headings, and conclusion. Goal: the five Cs — category, context, correctness (plausible?), contributions, clarity. Decide whether to continue.
  • Pass 2 (~1 hr) — read the figures, tables, and method carefully; skip heavy proofs. Goal: understand what they did and what the evidence shows. Note terms and references you don’t know.
  • Pass 3 (hours) — virtually re-implement the paper: re-derive each step, challenge every assumption, ask “what would I have done?”. Goal: deep enough to reproduce and critique it.

✍️ Worked example: a three-pass read of “Attention Is All You Need”

You built a transformer in Track 5, so let’s see how the passes would have taken you there from the 2017 paper that introduced it.

Pass 1 (skim). Category: a new architecture for sequence transduction. Context: replaces recurrence/convolutions, which it claims are slow and bad at long-range dependencies. Contributions: the Transformer; self-attention; state-of-the-art translation at lower training cost. Verdict: relevant and plausible — continue.

Pass 2 (content). Figure 1 is the architecture (encoder/decoder stacks); the key equation is scaled dot-product attention \(\operatorname{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d_k})V\). Tables show BLEU scores beating prior models. Open questions noted: why divide by \(\sqrt{d_k}\)? why multiple heads? why positional encodings?

Pass 3 (rebuild). Implement attention, multi-head, positional encodings, and a training loop — exactly Track 5. Now the “why \(\sqrt{d_k}\)” answers itself (it keeps the softmax from saturating), and you can critique choices (sinusoidal vs learned positions). You could now extend the paper — the mark of a Pass-3 read.

The passes mirror this curriculum. Pass 1 is the abstract-level intuition each lesson opens with; Pass 2 is the figures-and-method core; Pass 3 is the from-scratch implementation. Reading a paper well and learning a topic well are the same skill at different speeds.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Three passes of increasing depth; stop at the depth the paper warrants. Don’t read linearly.
  • Pass 1 answers relevance (the five Cs) in ten minutes from abstract, intro, headings, conclusion.
  • Pass 2 extracts the method and evidence from figures and tables; flag what you don’t understand.
  • Pass 3 means re-implementing and challenging — the only way to truly own a paper.
  • Read with questions: what problem, what’s new, what’s the evidence, what are the assumptions and limits?

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. The main goal of Pass 1 is to…

  • understand every equation in the paper.
    That’s Pass 2–3 work. Pass 1 is a fast triage, not a deep read.
  • decide, in about ten minutes, whether the paper is relevant and worth deeper reading.
    Correct. From the abstract, intro, headings, and conclusion you assess category, context, contributions and plausibility — enough to triage without sinking an hour into it.
  • reproduce the paper’s experiments.
    Reproduction is the Pass-3 (and next-lesson) goal, far beyond a first skim.
  • memorize the reference list.
    References matter for context, but rote memorization is never the goal of any pass.

Q2. Why read a paper in passes rather than linearly front-to-back?

  • Papers are written in random order.
    They’re carefully ordered — but ordered to defend claims, not to teach them.
  • It is against the rules to read the conclusion first.
    There are no such rules; reading the conclusion early is in fact part of Pass 1.
  • Papers are written to defend results densely; passes let you extract what you need at the right depth and triage efficiently.
    Correct. Most papers warrant only a quick pass; investing deeply in all of them is impossible. Layered reading matches effort to value.
  • Linear reading is faster.
    It’s usually slower and less effective — you spend equal effort on a landmark paper and a forgettable one.

Q3. You finish Pass 2 and can explain the method and results, but a key proof and one design choice still puzzle you. What does the method recommend?

  • Discard the paper as too hard.
    Partial confusion is normal after Pass 2 and not a reason to quit.
  • Cite it without understanding it.
    Citing what you don’t understand is how errors propagate through the literature.
  • Re-read Pass 1 only.
    Going shallower won’t resolve a proof or design question; you need to go deeper, not back to the skim.
  • Do a Pass 3 (or targeted deep read): re-derive the proof and re-implement the design choice to understand it.
    Correct. Pass 3 is exactly for the few papers where you need to resolve every detail — by rebuilding and challenging them, which is the next lesson’s reproduction work.

Q4. During Pass 2, where should most of your attention go?

  • The figures, tables, and the description of the method — the evidence and how it was produced.
    Correct. Figures and tables carry the core results and the method’s shape; reading them carefully (and noting unknowns) is the heart of Pass 2, while heavy proofs can wait.
  • The acknowledgements and funding.
    Occasionally useful for context, but not where understanding comes from.
  • Every proof, line by line.
    Detailed proofs are deferred to Pass 3 for the few papers that warrant it; grinding through them in Pass 2 is inefficient.
  • The formatting and typography.
    Irrelevant to the science; this is never the focus.

✍️ Practice

1. Do a real Pass 1 on the “Adam” optimizer paper (Kingma & Ba, 2014), which you implemented in Track 2. In ten minutes, write the five Cs: category, context, contributions, correctness (plausible?), clarity.

Show solution

A reasonable Pass 1: Category — a first-order stochastic optimization algorithm. Context — builds on momentum and RMSProp; aims to combine their benefits with bias-corrected adaptive per-parameter step sizes. Contributions — the Adam update rule, bias-correction terms, and empirical results across tasks. Correctness — plausible; the math is elementary and you already verified the update works in Track 2. Clarity — high; the algorithm fits in a box. Verdict: a foundational, well-written paper worth a Pass 2 if you want the convergence argument. Notice how much you can extract before reading a single proof.

2. Build a one-page reading-notes template you’ll reuse: what fields capture a paper well enough to recall it months later?

Show solution

A durable template: Citation & link; one-sentence summary; problem being solved; key idea/method (with the central equation); main result (the headline number and on what benchmark); baselines compared against; assumptions & limitations; questions/criticisms; how it connects to my work. The last three fields are what separate a researcher’s notes from a student’s — they capture not just what the paper says but whether you believe it and where it could break. Filling “key equation” and “assumptions” forces a genuine Pass 2.

3. Conceptual: when is it legitimate to stop after Pass 1 and never read further?

Show solution

Very often — and that’s the point. Stop after Pass 1 when the paper is outside your current question, when its contribution is clearly incremental to your needs, when a better/standard reference covers the same ground, or when Pass 1 reveals fatal red flags (next lesson). Reserve Pass 2 for papers whose method or evidence you actually need, and Pass 3 for the handful you will build on or must master. Efficient researchers read many papers at Pass 1 and very few at Pass 3 — the funnel is doing its job.
