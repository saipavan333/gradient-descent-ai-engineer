Eigenvectors, eigenvalues & a first look at PCA

⏱ 26 min Lesson 1.5 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsSome directions a transformation only stretches, never rotates. Those eigenvectors reveal the hidden axes along which your data actually varies — exactly what dimensionality reduction (PCA) exploits.
By the end, you can
  • Define eigenvectors and eigenvalues and recognize them geometrically
  • Connect the covariance matrix to directions of greatest variance
  • Explain PCA as 'rotate to the informative axes, then drop the rest'
  • Read a scree plot to choose how many components to keep

Concept The directions a matrix refuses to rotate

In Lesson 1.2 you learned that a matrix is a transformation — multiply vectors by it and it moves them around, generally rotating them (turning their direction) and stretching them (changing their length). But for almost every matrix there are a few special directions it only stretches, never rotates: a vector pointing that way comes out pointing the exact same way, just longer or shorter. Those special directions are called eigenvectors ("eigen-" is German for "own / characteristic," so an eigenvector is the matrix's "own" direction), and the amount each one is stretched is its eigenvalue, written with the Greek letter $\lambda$ ("lambda").

The defining equation is compact:

$$ A v = \lambda v. $$

Read it in plain English: "applying the matrix $A$ to the eigenvector $v$ (left side) gives the same thing as just scaling $v$ by the number $\lambda$ (right side)." All the rotating that $A$ does to other vectors, it skips for its eigenvectors — they keep their direction and only change length.

Why care about a niche geometric fact? Because eigenvectors are how you find the hidden axes of your data — and that powers the single most useful data-compression technique in classical ML.

Concept Data has directions of more and less spread

Picture a cloud of data points — say, people plotted by height and weight. The cloud isn't a round blob; it's stretched into an oval, because taller people tend to weigh more (the two features move together). There's a direction along which the data spreads out the most, and a direction (at a right angle to it) with the least spread.

"Spread" has a precise name, variance. Before defining it, recall the mean (the average): add the numbers and divide by how many there are. The variance is the average of the squared distances from the mean — a single number saying how far, typically, the points sit from their center (we cover it fully in Lesson 1.10; here just hold "variance = how spread out"). The way different features vary together is captured in a grid called the covariance matrix. And here's the payoff: the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are exactly those directions of most and least spread, with the eigenvalues telling you how much variance lies along each. These special directions are the principal components.

Concept PCA: rotate onto those axes, then drop the boring ones

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is the procedure: find the principal components (the covariance eigenvectors), then describe each data point by its position along the top few components instead of the original features. If most of the spread lives along the first component (PC1), you can project every point onto PC1 (the shadow idea from Lesson 1.3, back again) and represent it with a single number — throwing away the original axes while keeping almost all the information.

Scaled up, this is how you take data with hundreds of correlated features and compress it to a handful of meaningful ones — making models faster, removing noise, and letting you draw 2-D pictures of high-dimensional data you could never otherwise see. (This shrinking of the number of features is called dimensionality reduction.)

Concept How many components to keep? Read the scree plot

Each principal component's eigenvalue is the amount of variance it captures. Divide each by the total and you get the explained variance ratio — the fraction of the data's total spread that component accounts for. Plot these fractions in descending order and you get a scree plot (named because it looks like the rubble — "scree" — at the foot of a cliff). You keep enough components to cover most of the variance, often 90–95%.

Worked example Compressing height & weight into one number

Five people, each described by height (cm) and weight (kg). These two features are highly correlated, so PCA should be able to summarize both with a single component.

import numpy as np

H = np.array([150, 160, 170, 180, 190.])
W = np.array([ 50,  58,  65,  73,  80.])
X  = np.column_stack([H, W])             # stack into a 5x2 matrix (5 people, 2 features)
Xc = X - X.mean(axis=0)                  # center: subtract each column's mean (PCA needs this)

cov = np.cov(Xc.T)                        # the 2x2 covariance matrix
vals, vecs = np.linalg.eigh(cov)          # eigenvalues (vals) and eigenvectors (vecs)
order = np.argsort(vals)[::-1]            # sort largest-eigenvalue first
vals, vecs = vals[order], vecs[:, order]

print("explained variance ratio:", (vals / vals.sum()).round(4))
pc1 = vecs[:, 0]
if pc1[0] < 0: pc1 = -pc1                 # eigenvectors are sign-arbitrary; pin the sign
print("PC1 direction:", pc1.round(2))
print("each person on PC1:", (Xc @ pc1).round(1))
Output (rounded; run the notebook for exact)explained variance ratio: [0.9998 0.0002] PC1 direction: [0.8 0.6] each person on PC1: [-25.1 -12.3 -0.1 12.7 24.9]

PC1 alone explains 99.98% of the variance — so the single column "position along PC1" captures nearly everything two features told us. The direction $[0.8, 0.6]$ says PC1 mixes height and weight in roughly a 4:3 ratio, which is the "overall body size" axis the data is really spread along. We went from 2 numbers per person to 1 with essentially no loss. In a real problem this is 100 features → 10.

In practice you'd reach for scikit-learn, which wraps all of this into three lines:

from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
pca = PCA(n_components=1)
X_1d = pca.fit_transform(X)               # the compressed data
print(pca.explained_variance_ratio_)      # [0.9998]

Under the hood scikit-learn uses the SVD (a close cousin of eigen-decomposition — see the deep dive) for numerical stability, but the idea is exactly what you just did by hand.

★ Key points to remember
  • An eigenvector $v$ of $A$ satisfies $Av = \lambda v$: $A$ only stretches it (by the eigenvalue $\lambda$), never rotates it.
  • The eigenvectors of the covariance matrix are the data's directions of most/least spread — the principal components.
  • PCA = rotate onto those axes, then keep the top few; project the data into fewer dimensions with minimal loss.
  • The explained variance ratio (and its scree plot) tells you how many components to keep.
  • Use it to denoise, speed up models, and visualize high-dimensional data in 2-D.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Verify by hand that $v = (1, 1)$ is an eigenvector of $A = \begin{bmatrix}3 & 1\\ 1 & 3\end{bmatrix}$, and find its eigenvalue.
Show solution & reasoning

Compute $Av$ using the matrix-vector rule from Lesson 1.2: row 1 gives $3(1) + 1(1) = 4$; row 2 gives $1(1) + 3(1) = 4$. So $Av = (4, 4)$.

Is that a scaled copy of $v=(1,1)$? Yes: $(4,4) = 4\cdot(1,1) = 4v$. Since $Av = 4v$, $v$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda = 4$. $A$ left the direction $(1,1)$ untouched and just quadrupled its length — the geometric signature of an eigenvector. (As a bonus, $(1,-1)$ is the other eigenvector here, with $\lambda = 2$.)

2You have 50 features that are mostly correlated. After PCA, the first 5 components explain 92% of the variance. In a sentence or two, explain to a teammate what reducing from 50 to 5 buys you and what it costs.
Show solution & reasoning

Buys: a 10× smaller, faster, less noisy representation — models train quicker, overfit less, and you can finally plot the data. The 5 components are the directions the data truly varies along, so you keep the signal. Costs: you lose 8% of the variance (some real information), and the new components are mixtures of the original features, so they're harder to interpret ("PC1" isn't "age" — it's a blend). The reasoning: dimensionality reduction trades a little information and interpretability for a lot of speed, stability, and visualizability.

3Two features are perfectly correlated (e.g., temperature in °C and °F). What will PCA's explained-variance ratios look like, and why?
Show solution & reasoning

The ratios will be about $[1.0,\ 0.0]$: PC1 captures essentially 100% of the variance and PC2 essentially none. Because °F is an exact linear function of °C, all the data lies on a single line — there is only one real direction of spread. PCA discovers this and gives the second component zero variance (eigenvalue 0, just like the rank-deficient case from Lesson 1.4). This is PCA automatically detecting and removing redundancy — the link between eigenvalues and rank.

★ Deep dive: eigen-decomposition, symmetric matrices, and the SVD▸

When a square matrix has a full set of eigenvectors, you can rewrite it as $A = Q \Lambda Q^{-1}$, where the columns of $Q$ are the eigenvectors and $\Lambda$ ("big lambda") is a diagonal matrix holding the eigenvalues. This eigen-decomposition says: any action of $A$ is "rotate into the eigenvector frame ($Q^{-1}$), stretch each axis by its eigenvalue ($\Lambda$), rotate back ($Q$)." Complicated transformations become simple stretches once you look along the right axes — the deep reason eigenvectors matter far beyond PCA (they drive PageRank, vibration analysis, and the stability of dynamical systems).

Why covariance is special. A covariance matrix is symmetric (the entry in row $i$, column $j$ equals the one in row $j$, column $i$, because the covariance of X with Y equals that of Y with X), and symmetric matrices come with a wonderful guarantee: their eigenvectors are always orthogonal (at right angles) and their eigenvalues are real numbers. That's why PC1 and PC2 come out perpendicular — it's a mathematical certainty, not a coincidence of the drawing. Orthogonal components mean the compressed features are uncorrelated, which is exactly what you want.

The SVD connection. Real PCA implementations (scikit-learn included) don't build the covariance matrix and eigen-decompose it; they apply the Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) directly to the centered data, $X = U \Sigma V^\top$. The columns of $V$ are the principal components, and the "singular values" in $\Sigma$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues. SVD is more numerically stable and works on non-square data, so it's the workhorse — but conceptually it computes the same principal directions you found by eigen-decomposing the covariance. You'll meet SVD again in recommender systems and in how LoRA compresses the update matrices when fine-tuning LLMs (Track 6).
