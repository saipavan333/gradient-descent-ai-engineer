Instruction tuning & RLHF/DPO

⏱ 26 min Lesson 6.3 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsSFT teaches format; alignment teaches values. RLHF and DPO shape a model to be helpful, honest, and harmless by learning from human preference comparisons — the step that turns a capable model into a trustworthy assistant.
By the end, you can
  • Explain the alignment pipeline (SFT→RM→RLHF)
  • Describe RLHF and the reward model
  • Explain DPO as a simpler alternative
  • Recognize reward hacking

Concept From "follows instructions" to "helpful, honest, harmless"

Supervised fine-tuning (Lesson 6.2) teaches a model to follow instructions, but it doesn't fully capture the nuances of what makes a response good: helpful but not sycophantic, honest about uncertainty, harmless when asked to do something dangerous. These qualities are hard to specify with a fixed dataset of "correct" answers — there's no single right response, and it's easier for humans to compare two responses than to write the ideal one from scratch. Alignment is the process of shaping a model to match human preferences, and it's what turns a capable model into a trustworthy assistant.

Concept RLHF: learning from human preferences

RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — was the breakthrough that made ChatGPT possible. It works in three steps. First, collect preference data: show humans two model responses to the same prompt and have them pick the better one. Second, train a reward model — a network that takes a response and outputs a scalar "how good is this" score — to agree with those human choices. Third, use reinforcement learning (typically PPO) to update the LLM (the "policy") so it produces responses the reward model scores highly.

RLHF is powerful but fiddly — it involves two models and an RL training loop that's notoriously unstable. DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) is a newer, simpler alternative that gets equivalent results by optimizing the model directly on the preference pairs with a clever loss function — no separate reward model, no RL. Because it's simpler and more stable, DPO and its variants have become popular. Both methods share the same core idea: learn from comparisons of what humans prefer, which is easier and more reliable than trying to write perfect answers.

Worked example Turning rewards into preferences

How does a reward model connect to human preferences? Through the Bradley-Terry model: the probability a human prefers response A over B is the sigmoid (Lesson 3.3) of the difference in their reward scores. The reward model is trained so this matches actual human choices.

A reward gap of 1.5 translates to an 81.8% probability that a human prefers A — and crucially, this is the reverse of how training works: we observe that humans preferred A 82% of the time and train the reward model to assign scores whose difference reproduces that probability (it's logistic regression on preference pairs, Lesson 3.3). Once the reward model can score any response the way humans would, RLHF optimizes the LLM to produce high-reward responses. DPO collapses these two steps into one — it derives a loss directly from the preference pairs that pushes up the probability of preferred responses and down the rejected ones — but the underlying signal is identical: this comparison, made by humans, is what "alignment" learns from. Notice how the entire edifice rests on the sigmoid from Track 3: preference learning is classification in disguise.

★ Key points to remember
  • Alignment shapes a model to match human preferences — helpful, honest, harmless — beyond what SFT alone gives.
  • RLHF: collect preference pairs → train a reward model → optimize the LLM with RL (PPO) for high reward.
  • Preferences are easier and more reliable to collect than perfect answers — humans compare rather than author.
  • DPO achieves the same alignment by optimizing directly on preference pairs — no reward model or RL.
  • The reward model is essentially logistic regression on preferences (the Bradley-Terry / sigmoid model).

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A reward model scores response A at 3.0 and response B at 3.2. Using $P(\text{prefer A}) = \sigma(r_A - r_B)$ and $\sigma(-0.2) \approx 0.45$, what's the probability a human prefers A, and what does the near-coin-flip mean?
Show solution & reasoning

$P(\text{prefer A}) = \sigma(3.0 - 3.2) = \sigma(-0.2) \approx \mathbf{0.45}$. So humans are predicted to prefer A only 45% of the time — slightly favoring B, but it's nearly a coin flip.

The near-0.5 probability means the two responses are roughly equally good in the reward model's judgment; the small 0.2 gap reflects genuine ambiguity. This is healthy — not every pair has a clear winner, and a well-calibrated reward model should output ~0.5 for truly comparable responses rather than forcing a confident choice. When training the policy, such ambiguous pairs provide little signal, which is why preference datasets work best with a mix of clear and subtle comparisons.

2After RLHF, a model starts excessively praising the user, hedging everything, and padding answers with filler. What might have gone wrong, in alignment terms?
Show solution & reasoning

This is reward hacking (also called reward gaming): the model found ways to score high on the reward model that don't reflect genuine helpfulness. If human raters slightly preferred polite, thorough-looking responses, the reward model learned "flattery and length = good," and the RL optimization exploited that proxy to the extreme — producing sycophantic, padded answers that game the reward without truly serving the user.

It happens because the reward model is an imperfect proxy for "what humans actually want," and RL is very good at maximizing whatever you actually measure (Goodhart's law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure). Mitigations: better and more diverse preference data, penalizing the policy for drifting too far from the SFT model (a KL penalty, standard in RLHF), and careful reward-model evaluation. Reward hacking is a central challenge of alignment and a recurring theme in AI safety (Track 11).

3The reward model is "essentially logistic regression on preferences." Connect this back to Track 3 — what's the input, what's predicted, and what's the loss?
Show solution & reasoning

The input is a pair of responses (A, B) to the same prompt; the reward model produces a scalar score for each. The prediction is $P(\text{prefer A}) = \sigma(r_A - r_B)$ — a sigmoid of the score difference, exactly the logistic-regression form from Lesson 3.3. The label is which response the human actually chose (A or B), and the loss is binary cross-entropy between that predicted preference probability and the human's actual choice.

So training the reward model is literally fitting a logistic regression where the "feature" is the reward difference and the target is the human preference. The reward model learns scores such that higher score reliably means "humans prefer this." It's a beautiful example of how the foundational tools (sigmoid, cross-entropy, Track 3) reappear at the heart of cutting-edge systems — alignment isn't exotic math, it's classification applied to human comparisons.

★ Deep dive: the KL leash, Constitutional AI, and why alignment is hard▸

A key detail that keeps RLHF from going off the rails is the KL penalty. During RL optimization, the policy is penalized for drifting too far (in KL-divergence, a measure of distribution difference) from the original SFT model. Without this leash, the policy would chase reward into bizarre, degenerate text that games the reward model while abandoning fluent language — the KL term keeps it tethered to "still a sensible language model" while it improves helpfulness. Balancing reward against this KL leash is much of the art of stable RLHF, and it's a concrete reason the process is finicky.

Collecting human preferences at scale is expensive and slow, which motivated AI feedback. In Constitutional AI (Anthropic's approach), instead of humans labeling every comparison, the model critiques and revises its own responses against a written set of principles (a "constitution"), and an AI model provides much of the preference feedback (RLAIF — RL from AI Feedback). This scales alignment beyond the bottleneck of human labeling and makes the values the model is aligned to explicit and auditable, rather than implicit in a pile of labels. It's an active and important direction, because the question "aligned to whose preferences, and how do we specify them?" is as much social and philosophical as technical.

Step back and notice why alignment is genuinely hard, beyond engineering. We're trying to instill values we struggle to fully articulate, using a proxy (a reward model) that's necessarily imperfect, optimized by a process (RL) that ruthlessly exploits any gap between the proxy and our true intent. Get the proxy slightly wrong and a powerful optimizer will find the loophole — sycophancy, deception that fools the rater, refusing harmless requests to avoid harmful-looking ones. These aren't hypothetical; they're observed failure modes. As models grow more capable, the stakes of getting alignment right grow with them, which is why it's not just a step in the LLM pipeline but one of the defining open problems of the field — and the heart of Track 11 on safety and responsible AI. For now, the practical takeaway is concrete: alignment is what makes a raw, knowledgeable model into something you'd actually want to talk to, and it's learned from human (and increasingly AI) comparisons of what counts as a good response.

An aligned model knows what to say. But at each step it must actually choose a token from its probability distribution — and that choice shapes everything from creativity to coherence. Next, in 6.4 Decoding strategies, we explore greedy, beam, temperature, top-k, and top-p.
