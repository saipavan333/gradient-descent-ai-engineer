Bias–variance decomposition

The U-shaped test-error curve isn’t magic — it’s the sum of two competing quantities pulling in opposite directions. Splitting error into bias and variance is the single most clarifying lens in all of machine-learning theory.

Track 3 · Lesson 1 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: Lesson 3.0, Track 1 expectation

💡 Concept: two sources of error

Last lesson’s U-curve was the sum of two hidden curves moving in opposite directions. To see them, imagine training your model many times, each on a fresh random sample of data, and asking where its predictions land relative to the truth. Two distinct things can go wrong:

  • Bias — the model is systematically off, in the same direction every time, because its assumptions are too rigid to capture the real pattern. A straight line fit to a curve is biased: no matter the data, it can’t bend. Bias is the error of being too simple (underfitting).
  • Variance — the model’s predictions swing wildly from one training set to the next, because it’s sensitive to the particular noise it happened to see. A degree-15 polynomial is high-variance: tiny changes in the data produce a very different fit. Variance is the error of being too flexible (overfitting).

The classic picture is darts thrown at a target, where the bullseye is the truth:

🧮 The decomposition, derived

This intuition is exactly true, and the proof uses only expectation and variance from Track 1. Set up the problem: the true label at a point \(x\) is \(y = f(x) + \varepsilon\), where \(f\) is the true function and \(\varepsilon\) is noise with mean \(0\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) (irreducible — even a perfect model can’t predict the coin-flip part). Our model \(\hat{f}\) is trained on a random dataset, so \(\hat{f}(x)\) is itself random. We want the expected squared error, averaged over both the noise and the random training set:

$$ \mathbb{E}\!\left[(y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right]. $$

🧮 Splitting the error into three pieces

Write \(\bar{f}(x) = \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)]\) for the model’s average prediction over all possible training sets. Substitute \(y = f + \varepsilon\) and add-and-subtract \(\bar f\):

$$ \mathbb{E}\!\left[(f + \varepsilon - \hat{f})^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\big((f - \bar f) + (\bar f - \hat f) + \varepsilon\big)^2\right]. $$

Expand the square. The three cross-terms all vanish in expectation: \(\varepsilon\) has mean \(0\) and is independent of \(\hat f\); and \(\mathbb{E}[\bar f - \hat f]=0\) by definition of \(\bar f\); and \((f-\bar f)\) is a constant. What survives is three clean squares:

$$ \underbrace{(f - \bar f)^2}_{\textbf{bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat f - \bar f)^2\right]}_{\textbf{variance}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\textbf{noise}}. $$

So the expected test error splits exactly into three non-negative pieces:

$$ \text{Expected test error} \;=\; \text{Bias}^2 \;+\; \text{Variance} \;+\; \sigma^2. $$
(★)

Read off the meaning. Bias² \(=(f-\bar f)^2\) is how far the model’s average prediction is from the truth — the systematic error of the cluster’s center. Variance \(=\mathbb{E}[(\hat f-\bar f)^2]\) is how much predictions scatter around their own average — the spread of the darts. And \(\sigma^2\) is the irreducible noise, a floor no model can beat. The first two are exactly the two failure modes from Lesson 3.0, now quantified.

🎯 The trade-off (and why the curve is U-shaped)

Here is the crux: making a model more complex lowers bias but raises variance, and making it simpler does the reverse. A flexible model can bend to match \(f\) (low bias) but chases noise differently each time (high variance); a rigid model is stable across datasets (low variance) but can’t match \(f\) (high bias). Their sum is the U-curve — bias² dominates on the left, variance on the right, with a minimum in between:

🤖 How practitioners move along the curve

Diagnosing which term dominates tells you what to do. High bias (underfitting: train and test error both high and close)? Add capacity — a bigger model, more features, less regularization. High variance (overfitting: low train error, large gap to test)? Reduce it — more data, regularization (next lesson), simpler model, or ensembling. This single diagnostic — “am I bias-limited or variance-limited?” — guides a huge fraction of real ML decisions.

🛠️ From scratch: measure bias and variance

We can estimate bias and variance directly by training many models on many resampled datasets and watching where their predictions land — turning the abstract decomposition (★) into measured numbers.

code/track-3/bias_variance.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

true_f = lambda x: np.sin(1.3*x) + 0.3*x
sigma = 0.3
grid  = np.linspace(1.0, 4.0, 21)          # average the decomposition over the domain

def predictions(degree, n=25, reps=400):
    P = np.zeros((reps, grid.size))
    for r in range(reps):                  # train on a fresh random dataset each time
        x = rng.uniform(0, 5, n); y = true_f(x) + rng.normal(0, sigma, n)
        P[r] = np.polyval(np.polyfit(x, y, degree), grid)
    return P

for degree in [1, 3, 7]:
    P = predictions(degree)
    bias2    = np.mean((P.mean(axis=0) - true_f(grid))**2)  # (avg model - truth)^2
    variance = np.mean(P.var(axis=0))                       # spread across datasets
    print(f"degree {degree}:  bias^2={bias2:.3f}  variance={variance:.3f}"
          f"  total={bias2+variance+sigma**2:.3f}")
# degree 1: high bias, low variance.  degree 7: low bias, high variance.

Watch the pattern as degree rises: bias² shrinks while variance grows, and their sum (plus \(\sigma^2=0.09\)) traces out the U. You have just measured the decomposition that we proved above.

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. The decomposition: expected test error \(= \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \sigma^2\) (proven, exact).
  2. Bias = systematic error of the average model (too simple); variance = scatter across training sets (too flexible); \(\sigma^2\) = irreducible noise floor.
  3. Trade-off: more complexity ⇒ lower bias, higher variance (and vice versa). Their sum is the U-curve.
  4. Diagnose, then act: high bias → add capacity; high variance → more data / regularization / simpler model / ensembling.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. A model fit on different samples gives wildly different predictions, but on average they center on the truth. This model has…

  • high bias, low variance.
    High bias means the average prediction is off-target — but here it centers on the truth. The problem is the spread.
  • low bias, high variance.
    Correct. Centering on the truth on average = low bias; wildly different predictions across samples = high variance. This is the overfitting corner.
  • low bias, low variance.
    Low variance would mean tight, consistent predictions — but these swing wildly. That’s high variance.
  • high bias, high variance.
    High bias is ruled out since the predictions average to the truth. Only the variance is high here.

Q2. In the decomposition (★), what is \(\sigma^2\)?

  • The variance of the model across training sets.
    That’s the variance term \(\mathbb{E}[(\hat f-\bar f)^2]\), a separate piece. \(\sigma^2\) is the noise in the data itself.
  • The bias of the model.
    Bias is \((f-\bar f)^2\), about the model’s average being off. \(\sigma^2\) is independent of the model entirely.
  • Irreducible noise in the data — a floor no model can beat.
    Correct. \(\sigma^2\) is the variance of the label noise \(\varepsilon\); since it’s unpredictable, even a perfect model incurs it. It sets the best achievable error.
  • The learning rate squared.
    Unrelated — that’s an optimization hyperparameter (Track 2). \(\sigma^2\) is a property of the data-generating process.

Q3. Your model has low training error but a large gap to test error. Which knob most directly helps?

  • Add regularization or get more data (reduce variance).
    Correct. A low-train/high-gap signature is high variance (overfitting); regularization, more data, a simpler model, or ensembling all shrink variance.
  • Add more parameters.
    More parameters raise variance — they’d widen the gap, making overfitting worse, not better.
  • Train longer.
    Longer training on an overfitting model typically deepens the overfit; it doesn’t address variance.
  • Increase the learning rate.
    The learning rate is about optimization speed/stability, not the bias–variance balance. It won’t close a generalization gap.

Q4. Increasing model complexity generally has what effect on bias and variance?

  • Raises both.
    Complexity lowers bias (the model can fit more). It doesn’t raise bias.
  • Lowers both.
    If both fell with complexity there’d be no trade-off and no U-curve. Variance rises with complexity.
  • Raises bias, lowers variance.
    Backwards. More flexibility reduces bias and increases variance, not the other way around.
  • Lowers bias, raises variance.
    Correct. That opposing movement is exactly the bias–variance trade-off, and summing the two produces the U-shaped test-error curve.

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · reasoning

A model that always predicts the constant 0, ignoring its input, has what bias and what variance?

Show solution

Zero variance — it gives the same prediction (0) regardless of the training data, so there’s no scatter at all. But high bias — unless the truth really is 0 everywhere, its average prediction is systematically wrong. This is the extreme “too simple” corner: maximally stable, maximally biased. It’s also the baseline any useful model must beat, and a sanity check worth running.

Problem 2 · derivation

In the derivation, why does the cross-term involving \(\varepsilon\) vanish in expectation? State the two properties of \(\varepsilon\) you need.

Show solution

The cross-terms pairing \(\varepsilon\) with the other factors look like \(2\,\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon\,(\,\cdots)]\). They vanish because (1) the noise has mean zero, \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon]=0\), and (2) the noise is independent of the model \(\hat f\) and of \(f,\bar f\) (it’s fresh randomness in the label, unrelated to how the model was trained). Independence lets the expectation factor, \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon\cdot g] = \mathbb{E}[\varepsilon]\,\mathbb{E}[g] = 0\cdot\mathbb{E}[g]=0\). Without independence (e.g. if the model could peek at the noise), the cross-term wouldn’t vanish and the clean three-way split would fail.

Problem 3 · code

Run bias_variance.py and confirm the pattern: as degree goes 1 → 3 → 7, bias² falls and variance rises. Which degree gives the lowest total?

Show solution

You’ll see something like: degree 1 has high bias² (a line can’t match the sine) and tiny variance; degree 7 has near-zero bias² but larger variance (it chases noise differently each run); degree 3 sits in between with the lowest total. The exact numbers vary with the seed, but the qualitative crossover — bias falling while variance climbs, total minimized in the middle — is robust. You’ve numerically reproduced the U-curve from its two components.

Problem 4 · synthesis

Bagging (training many models on bootstrap resamples and averaging them, as in Random Forests) is famous for reducing variance. Using the decomposition, explain why averaging helps — and why it barely changes bias.

Show solution

Averaging \(M\) models trained on different resamples reduces the variance term: the average of \(M\) noisy, roughly-independent predictions has variance shrunk by up to a factor \(M\) (the same “averaging reduces variance” fact from Track 1’s law of large numbers / variance of a mean). Meanwhile the bias is essentially unchanged: each model has the same systematic tendency, so their average has that same tendency — averaging doesn’t fix a shared blind spot. Hence bagging turns a high-variance, low-bias model (like a deep decision tree) into one with similar low bias but much lower variance, which is exactly why Random Forests work. The decomposition predicts both the win (variance↓) and its limit (bias unchanged).
