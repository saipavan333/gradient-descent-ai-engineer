Track 11 · AI System Design

How to approach an AI system design interview

⏱ 24 min Lesson 11.1 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsSystem design interviews test judgment, not a memorized answer. A five-step framework — clarify requirements, high-level design, data & model, scale, trade-offs — plus back-of-the-envelope estimation lets you drive any AI system design prompt.
By the end, you can
  • Apply the five-step design framework
  • Clarify requirements before designing
  • Estimate scale (QPS, storage) back-of-envelope
  • Reason about trade-offs out loud

Concept System design tests judgment, not a memorized answer

An AI system design interview asks you to architect a complete ML-powered system — "design the recommendation feed for a video app," "design a search engine," "design an LLM assistant." Unlike a coding question, there's no single correct answer. The interviewer is testing your judgment: can you turn a vague prompt into concrete requirements, sketch a sensible architecture, reason about scale and cost, and discuss trade-offs out loud? The single biggest mistake candidates make is jumping straight to a solution ("I'll use a neural network!") before understanding the problem. Strong candidates slow down, clarify, and reason — because that reasoning is the thing being evaluated.

The antidote to flailing is a framework: a repeatable sequence of steps you walk through for any system design prompt. It keeps you organized, ensures you cover what matters, and signals to the interviewer that you approach ambiguous problems methodically. The same five steps work whether you're designing a recommender, a search system, or an LLM platform.

Step by step. (1) Clarify requirements: ask questions to turn the vague prompt into concrete functional requirements (what it must do) and non-functional ones (scale, latency, cost — Lesson 11.2). Never skip this. (2) High-level design: sketch the major components and how data flows between them — a box-and-arrow architecture. (3) Data & model: go deep on the ML — what data, what features, what model, how it's trained and evaluated. (4) Scale it up: make it work at the real traffic — serving, replicas, caching, sharding (Lessons 10.6–10.7). (5) Trade-offs: discuss the choices you made and their alternatives, acknowledging what you'd monitor and what could go wrong. This is also where MLOps (Track 10) comes back — a designed system still has to be deployed and maintained.

Concept Back-of-the-envelope estimation

A skill that runs through every system design interview is back-of-the-envelope estimation: quick, rough calculations of scale — how many requests per second, how much storage, how much compute — using round numbers. You're not expected to be exact; you're expected to reason about orders of magnitude, because the scale determines the architecture. A system serving 100 requests a day and one serving 100,000 per second are completely different designs, and you can't choose sensibly until you've estimated which one you're building. The key quantity is often QPS (queries per second): the request rate the system must handle, including the peak, which is typically a few times the average because traffic clusters at busy hours.

Worked example Estimating the load you must serve

Suppose you're designing a feed for an app with 10 million daily active users, each making about 20 requests a day. A back-of-the-envelope QPS estimate tells you the scale you're architecting for:

Ten million users × 20 requests is 200 million requests a day; divided by the 86,400 seconds in a day, that's about 2,300 requests per second on average. But average isn't what you design for — traffic spikes during busy hours, so applying a 3× peak factor gives roughly 7,000 QPS at peak. That single number now drives every later decision: at 7,000 QPS you clearly can't serve from one machine, so you'll need horizontal scaling (Lesson 11.6); you'll think hard about per-request latency because it determines how many replicas you need (next lesson); and caching becomes attractive to absorb repeated requests. Notice how little arithmetic this took and how much it told you — that's the point of back-of-the-envelope estimation. In an interview you'd say this out loud: "10 million users, ~20 requests each, so ~200 million a day, ~2,300 QPS average, call it 7,000 at peak — so this is a system that needs to scale horizontally." Stating that reasoning shows you can ground an architecture in the actual numbers rather than hand-waving, which is exactly what separates a strong design from a vague one.

★ Key points to remember
  • System design has no single right answer — it tests judgment: requirements, architecture, scale, and trade-offs.
  • The biggest mistake is jumping to a solution before clarifying the problem. Clarify first, always.
  • Use a framework: clarify requirements → high-level design → data & model → scale → trade-offs.
  • Back-of-the-envelope estimation (QPS, storage, compute) sizes the problem and drives the architecture.
  • Peak QPS (a few × average) is what you design for, not the average.
  • Reason out loud — the interviewer is evaluating your thinking, not a memorized diagram.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1An interviewer says "Design a system to detect spam comments." Before designing anything, what clarifying questions would you ask?
Show solution & reasoning

Good clarifying questions split into functional and non-functional. Functional: What counts as spam (ads, scams, abuse, all)? Do we block automatically or flag for human review? Real-time (block before the comment posts) or batch (scan after)? What's the action on a positive — hide, delete, warn? Is there an appeals process? Non-functional / scale: How many comments per day (→ QPS)? What latency is acceptable (real-time blocking needs <100ms; batch can be slow)? What's the cost of a false positive (blocking a legitimate comment) versus a false negative (letting spam through)? What languages? Is there labeled training data, and how do we get ongoing labels?

The reasoning is that each answer materially changes the design. Real-time blocking forces a low-latency online model (Lesson 11.2); batch scanning allows a heavier, more accurate model. A high cost of false positives pushes you toward a conservative threshold and human review; a high cost of false negatives pushes the other way. The scale determines whether one server suffices or you need to design for thousands of QPS. By asking these before designing, you avoid building the wrong system — and you demonstrate the single most important system-design instinct: that the right architecture is a function of the requirements, so you must extract the requirements first. An interviewer watching you ask these questions already knows you think like an engineer, before you've drawn a single box.

2Estimate the storage needed to keep embeddings for 50 million documents, where each embedding is a 768-dimensional vector of 4-byte floats. Show the reasoning.
Show solution & reasoning

Back-of-the-envelope: each embedding is 768 dimensions × 4 bytes = 3,072 bytes ≈ 3 KB per document. For 50 million documents: 50,000,000 × 3 KB = 150,000,000 KB = 150,000 MB = 150 GB. So the raw embedding storage is on the order of 150 gigabytes.

That number immediately informs the design. 150 GB doesn't fit in the RAM of a typical single machine comfortably, which tells you the vector index may need to be sharded across multiple machines (Lesson 11.7), or that you'll use compression (quantizing the vectors to fewer bytes — e.g., 1-byte values would cut it to ~38 GB). It also tells you this is large but not enormous — you're not in petabyte territory, so it's tractable on a modest cluster. The broader skill on display is translating a data description into a storage estimate with one multiplication, then letting that order of magnitude steer architectural choices (single node vs sharded, full-precision vs quantized). Interviewers love this because it shows you connect the ML artifacts (embeddings) to their real infrastructure cost — and in an interview you'd note the assumptions (768-d, fp32) so they can adjust if the real numbers differ. The estimate is rough but the architectural conclusion (sharding or compression for ~150 GB) is sound.

3Why is "discuss trade-offs out loud" the step that most distinguishes a senior candidate, and what kinds of trade-offs come up?
Show solution & reasoning

It distinguishes senior candidates because every real design is a set of trade-offs, and recognizing them is what engineering judgment is. A junior candidate proposes a design as if it's simply correct; a senior one says "I chose X, which gives us A at the cost of B; the alternative Y would trade the other way, and I'd pick X here because our requirements weight A more." That framing shows you understand there's no free lunch — only choices suited (or not) to the requirements. Interviewers deliberately probe for this, because production systems are defined by their trade-offs, and someone who can't see them will make invisible, costly mistakes.

The recurring trade-offs in AI system design include: accuracy vs latency/cost (a bigger model is more accurate but slower and pricier to serve — Lesson 11.2); freshness vs cost (compute predictions live for freshness, or precompute in batch for cheapness — Lesson 11.4); latency vs throughput (batching boosts throughput but adds latency — Lesson 11.6); consistency vs availability (classic distributed-systems tension); build vs buy (train your own model vs call an API); and precision vs recall in the model itself (Track 3). Naming the relevant trade-off for each major decision, explaining which way you lean and why given the requirements, and acknowledging what you'd monitor to catch it going wrong, is the hallmark of a strong design discussion. It connects directly back to step 1: you can only resolve a trade-off if you know the requirements that tell you which side to favor — which is why the framework starts where it does and ends here, closing the loop from requirements to justified choices.

★ Deep dive: what interviewers actually look for, and how AI system design differs from classic system design▸

AI system design interviews evolved from classic (non-ML) system design — "design Twitter," "design a URL shortener" — and they share the same backbone: requirements, high-level architecture, deep dives, scaling, trade-offs, all reasoned out loud. What you're signaling is the same: structured thinking under ambiguity. Interviewers consistently reward a few behaviors. Driving the conversation: leading the interviewer through your framework rather than waiting to be prompted, while staying responsive to their steers (when they say "let's go deeper on the model," you follow). Making assumptions explicit: "I'll assume 10 million users and read-heavy traffic — correct me if not," so your reasoning is transparent and correctable. Managing scope and time: covering the whole system at a high level before diving deep, so you don't spend twenty minutes on one component and never reach scaling. Connecting decisions to requirements: every choice justified by a requirement, never "because it's cool." A candidate who does these turns a vague prompt into an organized, evolving design — which is the entire skill.

What makes AI system design distinctive is the extra layer the ML adds on top of the classic concerns. You must reason about the data and model lifecycle (where does training data come from, how is the model evaluated and retrained — Track 10 returns here), the two-phase structure common to ML systems (an offline training/indexing pipeline plus an online serving path, which you'll see in recommenders, search, and RAG), and ML-specific failure modes (the model degrades via drift, train/serve skew, feedback loops — all from Track 10 — which classic systems don't have). You also weigh distinctly ML trade-offs: model size against latency, the build-vs-buy question of training your own model versus calling a foundation-model API, and the perennial accuracy-vs-cost tension that dominates LLM-era design (Lessons 10.6–10.7). The mental model that ties it together is that an AI system is a classic distributed system with a learned component at its heart — so you bring all the standard system-design discipline and the ML lifecycle thinking from the previous tracks. Everything you've learned — classical ML, deep learning, RAG, agents, MLOps — becomes a building block you assemble here under real constraints. That synthesis, turning a portfolio of techniques into a single coherent system that meets real requirements at scale, is the capstone skill this track builds, and it starts, every time, with nailing the requirements — which is the next lesson.
