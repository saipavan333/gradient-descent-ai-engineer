Gaussian, MLE & the bridge to ML

The capstone of Track 1. The bell curve that nature can’t stop producing, the principle that turns data into parameters, and the single idea — maximum likelihood — that reveals why nearly every loss function in machine learning has the form it does.

💡 The Gaussian: nature’s default distribution

The Gaussian (or normal) distribution is the bell-shaped curve you’ve seen everywhere. It’s fully described by just two numbers from Lesson 11: its mean \(\mu\) (where the peak sits) and its variance \(\sigma^2\) (how wide it is). We write \(X\sim\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\), read “\(X\) is normally distributed with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\).” Its density is

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). $$
(★)

Don’t be intimidated — read it in pieces. The heart is \(\exp\!\big(-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)\big)\): the term \((x-\mu)^2\) is the squared distance from the center, so as \(x\) moves away from \(\mu\) the exponent becomes a large negative number and \(f\) decays fast — that’s the bell shape. The \(2\sigma^2\) in the denominator sets how quickly it decays (the width). The front factor \(1/(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})\) is just the constant that makes the total area equal \(1\) (Lesson 10). The shape obeys the famous 68–95–99.7 rule: about 68% of the mass lies within \(1\sigma\) of the mean, 95% within \(2\sigma\), 99.7% within \(3\sigma\).

Why it’s everywhere: the Central Limit Theorem

The Gaussian isn’t popular by accident. The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says: if you add up (or average) many independent random effects — whatever their own distributions — the result is approximately Gaussian. Measurement noise, the sum of many small genetic and environmental factors behind height, the average of many dice: all bell-shaped, because they’re sums of many little things. 🎯 Maximum likelihood: turning data into parameters

Here is the question that is machine learning: given data, which parameter values best explain it? Suppose we have measurements \(x_1,\dots,x_n\) and believe they’re Gaussian, but we don’t know \(\mu\). The likelihood is the probability (density) of the observed data as a function of the unknown parameter. Assuming the data points are independent, their joint density is the product of the individual densities:

$$ L(\mu) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i) = \prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_i-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right). $$

Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) chooses the \(\mu\) that makes this as large as possible — the parameter under which the data we actually saw is most probable. The figure shows the idea: among candidate Gaussians, MLE picks the one that fits the data cloud best, which is also the peak of the likelihood curve.

🧮 Deriving the MLE for the Gaussian mean

Products are awkward to differentiate, so we maximize the log-likelihood instead — valid because \(\log\) is increasing, so it has the same maximizer. The log turns the product into a sum:

$$ \ell(\mu) = \log L(\mu) = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left[-\log(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}) - \frac{(x_i-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right] = \text{const} - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\mu)^2. $$

To maximize, differentiate with respect to \(\mu\) and set to zero (the constant and the \(1/2\sigma^2\) factor don’t affect where the max is):

$$ \frac{d\ell}{d\mu} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\mu) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = n\mu. $$

$$ \boxed{\ \hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i\ } \quad\text{— the sample mean.} $$

A clean, satisfying result: the most likely \(\mu\) is just the average of the data. But look one step deeper at what we maximized.

🌉 The bridge: why every loss is a likelihood

Maximizing that log-likelihood meant minimizing \(\sum_i (x_i-\mu)^2\) — the sum of squared errors. That is not a coincidence; it is the secret identity of machine learning:

🔑 The unifying idea of Track 1

Minimizing squared error = maximum likelihood under Gaussian noise. If you assume your targets equal a model’s prediction plus Gaussian noise, then the MLE of the model’s parameters is found by minimizing mean squared error. Likewise, minimizing cross-entropy = maximum likelihood for a Categorical (softmax) model (Lesson 10). Training a model is maximum likelihood; a loss function is a negative log-likelihood in disguise.

This single perspective will organize the entire rest of your journey. When you build linear regression (Track 3), its squared-error loss is Gaussian MLE. When you train a classifier or a language model, the cross-entropy loss is the negative log-likelihood of the correct labels under the model’s predicted distribution. When you meet a VAE or diffusion model (Track 6), they are maximizing (a bound on) the likelihood of the data. And it all rests on the pieces you just assembled: vectors and matrices to hold the parameters and data, the dot products inside every layer, eigenvectors behind PCA, and probability to define what “most likely” even means. That is the whole of Track 1, and it is the foundation of everything ahead.

🛠️ From scratch: fit a Gaussian, see the bridge

code/track-1/gaussian_mle.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Generate data from a "true" Gaussian we pretend not to know
data = rng.normal(loc=5.0, scale=2.0, size=10_000)

# MLE for a Gaussian: the estimates ARE the sample mean and variance
mu_hat = data.mean()
var_hat = data.var()                       # MLE variance (divides by n)
print("mu_hat ~", round(mu_hat, 3),        # ~5.0
      " sigma_hat ~", round(np.sqrt(var_hat), 3))   # ~2.0

# Show MLE maximizes the log-likelihood: it should beat any other mu
def log_likelihood(mu, x, sigma=2.0):
    return np.sum(-0.5*np.log(2*np.pi*sigma**2) - (x-mu)**2 / (2*sigma**2))
for mu in [3.0, 4.0, mu_hat, 6.0]:
    print(f"  loglik(mu={mu:>5.2f}) = {log_likelihood(mu, data):.1f}")
# the largest log-likelihood is at mu_hat (the sample mean)

# THE BRIDGE: maximizing Gaussian log-likelihood == minimizing squared error
sse = lambda mu: np.sum((data - mu)**2)
print("argmin SSE matches sample mean:",
      np.isclose(mu_hat, min(np.linspace(4,6,2001), key=sse), atol=1e-3))  # True

# Central Limit Theorem: averages of a skewed variable become Gaussian
skewed_means = np.array([rng.exponential(1.0, size=30).mean() for _ in range(5000)])
print("mean of averages ~", round(skewed_means.mean(), 3),   # ~1.0
      " (looks Gaussian by n=30)")

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\): density \(\propto \exp(-(x-\mu)^2/2\sigma^2)\); \(\mu\) centers, \(\sigma\) widens; 68–95–99.7 rule.
  2. CLT: sums/averages of many independent effects are approximately Gaussian — why noise so often is.
  3. Likelihood = probability of the data as a function of parameters; MLE maximizes it (usually via the log-likelihood).
  4. For a Gaussian, \(\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}}\) = the sample mean. Maximizing its log-likelihood = minimizing squared error.
  5. The bridge: training = MLE. MSE ⇔ Gaussian noise; cross-entropy ⇔ softmax/Categorical. A loss is a negative log-likelihood.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. Heights are \(\mathcal{N}(170, 7^2)\) cm. Roughly what fraction of people are between 156 and 184 cm?

  • About 68%.
    68% is the \(\pm1\sigma\) range, i.e. 163–177 cm. The given range is wider than that.
  • About 50%.
    50% would be one side of the mean. The interval here is symmetric and spans two standard deviations each way.
  • About 95%.
    Correct. \(156 = 170-2(7)\) and \(184 = 170+2(7)\), so this is the \(\pm2\sigma\) range, which holds ~95% of the mass.
  • About 99.7%.
    99.7% is the \(\pm3\sigma\) range (149–191 cm). Two sigma, not three, gives ~95%.

Q2. Why do we maximize the log-likelihood instead of the likelihood itself?

  • \(\log\) is increasing, so it has the same maximizer, and it turns products into easy-to-handle sums.
    Correct. The maximizer is unchanged, and \(\log\) converts the product of densities into a sum, which is far easier to differentiate (and numerically stabler).
  • The log-likelihood gives a different, better answer.
    It gives the same optimal parameters — that’s exactly why the trick is allowed. It’s a convenience, not a change of objective.
  • Because the likelihood can be negative.
    A likelihood (a product of densities) is non-negative. The motivation is products-to-sums, not sign issues.
  • To make the parameters integers.
    Taking a log has nothing to do with making parameters integers; estimates remain real-valued.

Q3. Minimizing mean squared error is equivalent to maximum likelihood under what assumption?

  • Uniformly distributed targets.
    A uniform likelihood is flat, so it wouldn’t single out a squared-error objective. The squared term comes specifically from the Gaussian.
  • No noise at all.
    With zero noise there’s nothing probabilistic to maximize. MSE arises precisely from a noise model.
  • Categorical (softmax) outputs.
    That assumption gives cross-entropy, not squared error. MSE corresponds to a different noise model.
  • Gaussian noise on the targets.
    Correct. If targets = prediction + Gaussian noise, the negative log-likelihood is (up to constants) the sum of squared errors, so MLE = least squares.

Q4. The Central Limit Theorem helps explain why…

  • all data is exactly Gaussian.
    Not all data is Gaussian (incomes, waiting times, and many others are skewed). The CLT is about sums/averages, not raw data.
  • averages of many independent effects tend to be Gaussian.
    Correct. The CLT says sums or averages of many independent contributions approach a Gaussian, which is why measurement noise so often is bell-shaped.
  • the mean always equals the variance.
    Mean and variance are independent parameters of a Gaussian; the CLT says nothing forcing them equal.
  • large datasets need no modeling.
    The CLT is about the shape of averaged quantities, not a claim that modeling becomes unnecessary.

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · reasoning

Test scores are \(\mathcal{N}(75, 10^2)\). Between which scores do about 95% of students fall? What fraction score above 85?

Show solution

95% lie within \(\pm2\sigma = \pm20\) of the mean, i.e. between \(55\) and \(95\). For “above 85”: that’s \(85 = 75 + 1\sigma\). About 68% lie within \(\pm1\sigma\), leaving 32% in the two tails, so ~16% in the upper tail. Hence about 16% score above 85.

Problem 2 · derivation

Find the MLE of the variance \(\sigma^2\) for a Gaussian (treating \(\mu\) as known). Differentiate the log-likelihood with respect to \(\sigma^2\).

Show solution

Write \(v=\sigma^2\). The log-likelihood is \(\ell(v) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi v) - \frac{1}{2v}\sum_i (x_i-\mu)^2\). Differentiate with respect to \(v\):

$$ \frac{d\ell}{dv} = -\frac{n}{2v} + \frac{1}{2v^2}\sum_i (x_i-\mu)^2 = 0. $$

Multiply by \(2v^2\): \(-nv + \sum_i (x_i-\mu)^2 = 0\), so

$$ \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\mu)^2 \quad\text{— the (population) sample variance.} $$

Note it divides by \(n\), not \(n-1\); the \(n-1\) version is the unbiased estimator, a subtlety we’ll revisit in Track 3. The MLE and unbiased estimators can differ.

Problem 3 · code

Empirically demonstrate the CLT: average \(n\) Uniform(0,1) samples many times and show the distribution of the average looks Gaussian for \(n=30\) (e.g., by comparing to the 68% interval).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(7)
n = 30
avgs = np.array([rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n).mean() for _ in range(50_000)])
mu, sd = avgs.mean(), avgs.std()
within_1sd = np.mean(np.abs(avgs - mu) <= sd)
print("mean ~", round(mu, 3),           # ~0.5
      " within 1 sigma ~", round(within_1sd, 3))  # ~0.68  -> Gaussian-like

The averages center near \(0.5\) (the mean of Uniform(0,1)) and ~68% fall within one standard deviation — the Gaussian signature — even though the underlying Uniform is flat, not bell-shaped. That’s the CLT.

Problem 4 · synthesis

In one paragraph, explain to a peer how the pieces of Track 1 — vectors, the dot product, eigenvectors, and probability — come together when training a simple linear model. (This is the “explain it to a peer” test.)

Show solution

A data sample is a vector of features (Lesson 1); a linear model predicts with a dot product of that feature vector with a weight vector (Lesson 3), optionally written as a matrix multiply over a whole batch (Lessons 5–6). We assume the target equals this prediction plus Gaussian noise (Lesson 12), which makes the right training objective the negative log-likelihood — and that objective works out to minimizing squared error (the expectation of a squared deviation, Lesson 11). To understand and precondition the optimization, we look at the eigenvectors of the feature covariance (Lessons 8, 11), and we often reduce dimensions first with PCA. Every concept in this track shows up in that one sentence of training a model — which is exactly why we built them first.
