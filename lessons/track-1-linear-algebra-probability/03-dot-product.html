The dot product: length, angle, projection

One operation eats two vectors and returns a single number — and that number secretly encodes length, angle, and similarity. It is the most important computation in machine learning. Every neural network is, at heart, a pile of dot products.

Track 1 · Lesson 3 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 1–2

💡 Concept: two vectors in, one number out

Addition and scaling gave us new vectors. The dot product is different: it takes two vectors of the same dimension and returns a single scalar. Written \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}\) (say “a dot b”), it’s defined by the simplest rule imaginable — multiply matching components, then add them all up:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} \;=\; a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \cdots + a_n b_n \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i. $$

The big Greek sigma \(\sum_{i=1}^{n}\) is just compact notation for “add up the following as \(i\) runs from \(1\) to \(n\)” — it’s the for-loop of mathematics. For example, \((1,2,3)\cdot(4,5,6) = 1\cdot4 + 2\cdot5 + 3\cdot6 = 4+10+18 = 32\). That’s the entire definition. Everything else in this lesson is discovering what that one number means.

📖 Three properties (each obvious from the sum)

These follow immediately because the dot product is built from ordinary multiplication and addition:

Symmetric: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}\) (since \(a_ib_i=b_ia_i\)).   Linear: \((c\mathbf{a}+d\mathbf{c})\cdot\mathbf{b}=c(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})+d(\mathbf{c}\cdot\mathbf{b})\).   Non-negative on itself: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a}=\sum a_i^2 \ge 0\), and it’s \(0\) only when \(\mathbf{a}=\mathbf{0}\).

📐 Meaning 1: a vector’s length

Take the dot product of a vector with itself: \(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} = v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2\). In 2-D that’s \(v_1^2+v_2^2\) — and by the Pythagorean theorem, that is exactly the squared length of the arrow. So we define the length (or norm, or magnitude) of a vector as

$$ \lVert \mathbf{v} \rVert \;=\; \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}} \;=\; \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}. $$

The double bars \(\lVert\cdot\rVert\) denote length. In two dimensions this is just a right triangle and the theorem you already know:

A vector with length exactly \(1\) is called a unit vector. Any nonzero \(\mathbf{v}\) can be turned into a unit vector pointing the same way by scaling: \(\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v}/\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert\) (the hat \(\hat{\;}\) conventionally marks a unit vector). This is called normalizing, and it’s how you separate a vector’s direction from its size.

📐 Meaning 2: the angle between vectors

Here is the remarkable fact. The same dot product also equals the two lengths multiplied by the cosine of the angle \(\theta\) between the vectors:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} \;=\; \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\,\cos\theta. $$
(★)
💡 Quick reminder: what cosine does here

\(\cos\theta\) measures alignment. It runs from \(\cos 0^\circ = 1\) (vectors point the same way), through \(\cos 90^\circ = 0\) (perpendicular), to \(\cos 180^\circ = -1\) (opposite directions). So equation (★) says the dot product is large-positive when the vectors agree in direction, zero when they’re perpendicular, and negative when they oppose.

Why the two formulas are the same (a real derivation)

Two completely different-looking expressions for one quantity deserve a proof, not faith. We’ll show it in 2-D, where “angle” already has a meaning. Write each vector in terms of its length and the angle it makes with the horizontal axis. If \(\mathbf{a}\) has length \(\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\) and points at angle \(\alpha\), and \(\mathbf{b}\) has length \(\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\) at angle \(\beta\), then

$$ \mathbf{a} = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,(\cos\alpha,\ \sin\alpha), \qquad \mathbf{b} = \lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\,(\cos\beta,\ \sin\beta). $$

Now just compute the dot product from the algebraic definition and factor out the lengths:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\big(\cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta\big). $$

The expression in parentheses is exactly the trigonometric angle-difference identity \(\cos(\alpha-\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta+\sin\alpha\sin\beta\). And \(\alpha-\beta\) is precisely \(\theta\), the angle between the two vectors. Therefore \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta\). \(\blacksquare\)

🧮 What about higher dimensions?

In \(\mathbb{R}^n\) we don’t have a pre-existing notion of “angle” between two vectors floating in 784 dimensions. So mathematicians turn (★) around and use it as the definition of angle: \(\cos\theta \equiv \dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}\). This is legitimate because the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality guarantees \(|\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}| \le \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\), so the ratio always lands in \([-1,1]\) — a valid value for a cosine. (You’ll prove Cauchy–Schwarz in the practice problems.)

🎯 Meaning 3: the sign tells you the relationship

Because lengths are always positive, the sign of \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}\) is the sign of \(\cos\theta\) — so the dot product alone tells you whether two vectors broadly agree, are perpendicular, or oppose:

🔑 Orthogonality test

Two vectors are orthogonal (a fancy word for perpendicular) exactly when their dot product is zero: \(\mathbf{a}\perp\mathbf{b} \iff \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0\). This is the workhorse test for “these two directions are independent / carry unrelated information,” and it underlies everything from PCA to the structure of attention.

📐 Meaning 4: projection — how much of one vector lies along another

Picture shining a light straight down onto vector \(\mathbf{b}\); the shadow that \(\mathbf{a}\) casts on \(\mathbf{b}\)’s line is the projection of \(\mathbf{a}\) onto \(\mathbf{b}\). Its signed length is how much of \(\mathbf{a}\) points along \(\mathbf{b}\).

From the right triangle in the figure, the shadow’s length is \(\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\cos\theta\). Using (★) to replace \(\cos\theta\), the scalar projection of \(\mathbf{a}\) onto \(\mathbf{b}\) is

$$ \text{comp}_{\mathbf{b}}\,\mathbf{a} \;=\; \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\cos\theta \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}. $$

If we want the projection as an actual vector along \(\mathbf{b}\), multiply that length by the unit vector \(\hat{\mathbf{b}}=\mathbf{b}/\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\):

$$ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{b}}\,\mathbf{a} \;=\; \left(\frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2}\right)\mathbf{b}. $$

Sanity check the formula’s shape: it’s a scalar (the parenthesized ratio) times the vector \(\mathbf{b}\) — so the result is a vector pointing along \(\mathbf{b}\), exactly as a shadow on \(\mathbf{b}\)’s line should. Projection is the engine behind least-squares regression, Gram–Schmidt, and PCA later on.

🤖 Why ML lives and breathes dot products

Normalize the dot product by both lengths and you get cosine similarity, a pure measure of directional agreement that ignores magnitude:

$$ \text{cosine similarity}(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert} \;=\; \cos\theta \in [-1,1]. $$

This single number is how modern systems measure “how related are these two things?” Search and retrieval rank documents by cosine similarity to your query’s embedding; recommendation systems find similar users this way; and inside a transformer, attention scores how much one token should listen to another by — you guessed it — a (scaled) dot product of their vectors. When you build a GPT in Track 5, the line that makes it “pay attention” will literally be a batch of dot products. Master this operation and a huge amount of ML stops being mysterious.

🛠️ From scratch: the dot product in code

We’ll compute it three ways — a hand-rolled loop, the explicit sum, and NumPy’s built-in — and confirm they all agree.

code/track-1/dot_product.py
import numpy as np

def dot_from_scratch(a, b):
    """The definition, by hand: sum of products of matching components."""
    assert len(a) == len(b), "vectors must have the same dimension"
    total = 0.0
    for i in range(len(a)):       # i = 0..n-1  (code indexing)
        total += a[i] * b[i]
    return total

a = np.array([1.0, 2.0, 3.0])
b = np.array([4.0, 5.0, 6.0])

print(dot_from_scratch(a, b))     # 32.0
print(np.sum(a * b))              # 32.0   (a*b is component-wise, then sum)
print(a @ b)                      # 32.0   (@ is NumPy's dot-product operator)

# Length / norm
def norm(v):
    return np.sqrt(v @ v)
print(norm(np.array([4.0, 3.0])))         # 5.0

# Angle between vectors (in degrees)
def angle_deg(a, b):
    cos_t = (a @ b) / (norm(a) * norm(b))
    cos_t = np.clip(cos_t, -1.0, 1.0)     # guard tiny float overshoot past +-1
    return np.degrees(np.arccos(cos_t))

print(angle_deg(np.array([1, 0]), np.array([0, 1])))   # 90.0  -> orthogonal
print(angle_deg(np.array([1, 0]), np.array([1, 1])))   # 45.0

# Cosine similarity
def cosine_sim(a, b):
    return (a @ b) / (norm(a) * norm(b))
print(cosine_sim(np.array([1, 1]), np.array([2, 2])))  # 1.0  -> same direction
⚠️ Two traps worth internalizing

(1) a * b is not the dot product — it’s component-wise multiplication, giving a vector. You still have to sum it (or use @). (2) Floating-point rounding can push \(\cos\theta\) a hair past \(\pm1\), and arccos of \(1.0000001\) is nan. Always clip to \([-1,1]\) before arccos — a classic real-world bug.

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. Definition: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\sum_i a_i b_i\) — multiply matching components, add them up. Output is one number.
  2. Length: \(\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert=\sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}\). A unit vector has length 1; normalize via \(\mathbf{v}/\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert\).
  3. Geometric form (★): \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta\).
  4. Orthogonality: \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0 \iff\) the vectors are perpendicular.
  5. Projection: scalar \(=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}\); vector \(=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2}\mathbf{b}\).
  6. Cosine similarity \(=\dfrac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert}=\cos\theta\): ML’s default “how similar?” It powers attention.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. What is \((2, -1, 3)\cdot(0, 4, 1)\)?

  • \((0, -4, 3)\)
    This is component-wise multiplication and stops too early — the dot product then sums these to a single number.
  • \(7\)
    Sign slip: \(2\cdot0 + (-1)\cdot4 + 3\cdot1 = 0-4+3\). The \(-1\cdot4=-4\) term is negative.
  • \(-1\)
    Correct. \(2\cdot0 + (-1)\cdot4 + 3\cdot1 = 0 - 4 + 3 = -1\). A negative result means the angle between them is obtuse.
  • \(15\)
    This looks like a length-squared of something, not the cross-multiplied sum. Recompute term by term: \(0,\,-4,\,3\).

Q2. \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=0\) for two nonzero vectors. What do you know?

  • One of them is the zero vector.
    We’re told both are nonzero. A zero dot product doesn’t require a zero vector.
  • They point in the same direction.
    Same direction means \(\theta=0\), \(\cos\theta=1\), giving a large positive dot product, not zero.
  • They point in opposite directions.
    Opposite means \(\theta=180^\circ\), \(\cos\theta=-1\), a large negative dot product — not zero.
  • They are perpendicular (orthogonal).
    Correct. \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\cos\theta=0\) with nonzero lengths forces \(\cos\theta=0\), i.e. \(\theta=90^\circ\).

Q3. Vectors \(\mathbf{a}\) and \(\mathbf{b}\) have the same direction but \(\mathbf{b}\) is twice as long. Their cosine similarity is…

  • \(1\)
    Correct. Cosine similarity is \(\cos\theta\) and depends only on the angle, not length. Same direction ⇒ \(\theta=0\) ⇒ \(\cos\theta=1\). Magnitude cancels out.
  • \(2\)
    Cosine similarity always lies in \([-1,1]\); it can never be \(2\). You may be thinking of the raw dot product, which does grow with length.
  • \(0.5\)
    The factor-of-two in length is divided out by the norms in the denominator, so it doesn’t appear. Only the angle survives.
  • It depends on the actual lengths.
    It doesn’t — that’s the whole point of normalizing by \(\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\). Length is removed; only direction remains.

Q4. Why clip the cosine to \([-1,1]\) before calling arccos in code?

  • To make the angle come out in degrees.
    Degrees vs radians is handled separately (by np.degrees); clipping is unrelated to units.
  • Floating-point rounding can produce values like \(1.0000001\), and arccos of that is nan.
    Correct. Mathematically \(\cos\theta\in[-1,1]\), but tiny rounding errors can step just outside, where arccos is undefined. Clipping restores the valid range.
  • Because the dot product might be negative.
    Negative cosines are perfectly valid (obtuse angles); arccos(-1) works fine. The problem is only values outside \([-1,1]\).
  • To speed up the computation.
    Clipping is about correctness, not speed; it prevents nan, it doesn’t accelerate anything.

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · compute

For \(\mathbf{a}=(3,4)\) and \(\mathbf{b}=(4,3)\): find \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}\), \(\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\), \(\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\), and the angle \(\theta\) between them.

Show solution

\(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=3\cdot4+4\cdot3=24\). Both lengths are \(\sqrt{3^2+4^2}=5\). Then from (★), \(\cos\theta=\dfrac{24}{5\cdot5}=\dfrac{24}{25}=0.96\), so \(\theta=\arccos(0.96)\approx 16.3^\circ\). They’re nearly aligned — which fits, since \((3,4)\) and \((4,3)\) are mirror images across the diagonal and point in similar directions.

Problem 2 · derivation

Show that \(\lVert\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2 + 2(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}) + \lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2\). (This is the vector version of \((x+y)^2=x^2+2xy+y^2\).)

Show solution

Use \(\lVert\mathbf{v}\rVert^2=\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}\) and expand with linearity and symmetry of the dot product:

$$ \lVert\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = (\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b})\cdot(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{b}. $$

Since \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{a}\), the two middle terms combine to \(2(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\), and \(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a}=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2\), \(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{b}=\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2\). Done. \(\blacksquare\) Notice the immediate corollary: if \(\mathbf{a}\perp\mathbf{b}\) then the cross term vanishes and \(\lVert\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}\rVert^2=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2+\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2\) — the Pythagorean theorem, recovered for vectors.

Problem 3 · stretch (Cauchy–Schwarz)

Prove \(|\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}| \le \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\). (Hint: for any real \(t\), the quantity \(\lVert\mathbf{a}-t\mathbf{b}\rVert^2\) is \(\ge 0\). Treat it as a quadratic in \(t\).)

Show solution

If \(\mathbf{b}=\mathbf{0}\) both sides are \(0\) and the inequality holds, so assume \(\mathbf{b}\neq\mathbf{0}\). For every real \(t\),

$$ 0 \le \lVert\mathbf{a}-t\mathbf{b}\rVert^2 = \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{a} - 2t(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}) + t^2(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{b}). $$

The right side is a quadratic in \(t\) of the form \(At^2 + Bt + C\) with \(A=\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2>0\), \(B=-2(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})\), \(C=\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2\). A parabola opening upward that is never negative can touch zero at most once, so its discriminant is \(\le 0\):

$$ B^2 - 4AC \le 0 \;\Rightarrow\; 4(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})^2 - 4\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2 \le 0 \;\Rightarrow\; (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})^2 \le \lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert^2\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert^2. $$

Take square roots: \(|\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}|\le\lVert\mathbf{a}\rVert\lVert\mathbf{b}\rVert\). \(\blacksquare\) This is what guarantees cosine similarity stays in \([-1,1]\), so “angle in \(n\)-D” is well defined.

Problem 4 · code

Write a function most_similar(query, items) that returns the vector in the list items with the highest cosine similarity to query. Test it with query=(1,1) and items=[(2,0),(1,2),(-1,-1)].

Show solution 
import numpy as np

def cosine_sim(a, b):
    a, b = np.asarray(a, float), np.asarray(b, float)
    return (a @ b) / (np.sqrt(a @ a) * np.sqrt(b @ b))

def most_similar(query, items):
    return max(items, key=lambda x: cosine_sim(query, x))

print(most_similar((1, 1), [(2, 0), (1, 2), (-1, -1)]))   # (1, 2)

Cosine similarities: with \((2,0)\) it’s \(2/(\sqrt2\cdot2)\approx0.707\); with \((1,2)\) it’s \(3/(\sqrt2\cdot\sqrt5)\approx0.949\); with \((-1,-1)\) it’s \(-1\) (opposite). The winner is \((1,2)\). This three-line idea is exactly how a vector search engine retrieves the closest match to a query embedding — just with thousands of dimensions and millions of items.
