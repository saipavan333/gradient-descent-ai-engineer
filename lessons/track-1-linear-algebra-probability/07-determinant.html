The determinant: area, volume, invertibility

A single number, computed from a matrix, that tells you how much it stretches space — and whether it can be undone. The determinant ties together area, orientation, linear independence, and invertibility into one idea.

Track 1 · Lesson 7 Concept + derivation + code ~26 min Builds on: Lessons 4–6

💡 Concept: how much does the map stretch space?

A matrix moves space around (Lesson 5). A natural question: does it expand regions, shrink them, or leave their size unchanged? The determinant of a square matrix \(A\), written \(\det A\) or \(|A|\), answers exactly this. It is the factor by which the transformation multiplies area (in 2-D) or volume (in 3-D and beyond). If \(\det A = 3\), every region comes out three times larger; if \(\det A = 0.5\), everything is halved.

The cleanest way to see it: track what happens to the unit square, whose area is \(1\). The matrix sends it to a parallelogram (the one spanned by \(A\)’s columns), and the area of that parallelogram is \(|\det A|\):

The 2×2 formula, and why it’s the area

For a \(2\times 2\) matrix, the determinant has a short formula. With \(A=\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}\):

$$ \det A = ad - bc. $$

This isn’t a magic incantation — it’s the (signed) area of the parallelogram built from the columns \(\mathbf{a}_1=(a,c)\) and \(\mathbf{a}_2=(b,d)\). Here’s the derivation. The area of a parallelogram spanned by two vectors is base times height, \(\lVert\mathbf{a}_1\rVert\,\lVert\mathbf{a}_2\rVert\sin\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between them. Squaring and using \(\sin^2\theta = 1-\cos^2\theta\) together with the dot product \(\mathbf{a}_1\cdot\mathbf{a}_2 = \lVert\mathbf{a}_1\rVert\lVert\mathbf{a}_2\rVert\cos\theta\) (Lesson 3):

$$ \text{area}^2 = \lVert\mathbf{a}_1\rVert^2\lVert\mathbf{a}_2\rVert^2 - (\mathbf{a}_1\cdot\mathbf{a}_2)^2 = (a^2+c^2)(b^2+d^2) - (ab+cd)^2. $$

Multiply it all out and the cross terms cancel beautifully, leaving a perfect square:

$$ \text{area}^2 = a^2d^2 - 2abcd + b^2c^2 = (ad-bc)^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \text{area} = |ad-bc|. $$

So \(|\det A| = |ad-bc|\) is precisely that area. The determinant keeps a sign as a bonus, and that sign carries real information, which we turn to next.

🎯 The sign means orientation; zero means collapse

Area can’t be negative, so why does \(\det\) keep a sign? Because it also records whether the transformation flips space (like a mirror) or preserves its handedness. A positive determinant preserves orientation; a negative one reverses it (left-handed becomes right-handed). And a determinant of exactly zero is the most important case of all — it means the map squashes space flat, collapsing the square to a line (or a point) of zero area:

🔑 The determinant unifies four ideas

\(\det A = 0\) \(\;\iff\;\) the map collapses dimension \(\;\iff\;\) the columns of \(A\) are linearly dependent (Lesson 4) \(\;\iff\;\) \(A\) is not invertible (you can’t undo a collapse — once two points are squashed onto one, you can’t separate them again). One number detects all four at once.

That last equivalence is why the determinant matters so much in practice. Solving \(A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}\) has a unique solution exactly when \(A\) is invertible, i.e. exactly when \(\det A \neq 0\). A determinant near zero warns you the matrix is nearly singular — solutions will be numerically unstable, a constant hazard in ML.

📐 The one property worth memorizing

Determinants interact with matrix multiplication in the most natural way imaginable — areas multiply when you compose maps:

$$ \det(AB) = \det(A)\,\det(B). $$
(★)

The intuition is immediate from Lesson 6: \(AB\) means “do \(B\), then \(A\).” If \(B\) scales area by \(\det B\) and then \(A\) scales the result by \(\det A\), the total scaling is the product. From (★) two facts drop out for free. The identity does nothing, so \(\det I = 1\). And since \(A A^{-1} = I\), we get \(\det(A)\det(A^{-1}) = 1\), hence \(\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det A\) — confirming once more that \(A\) is invertible only when \(\det A \neq 0\) (you can’t divide by zero).

🧮 Beyond 2×2

For \(n\times n\) matrices the determinant is the signed \(n\)-dimensional volume scaling factor. There’s a recursive “cofactor expansion” formula, but it costs \(O(n!)\) operations and nobody uses it for large \(n\). In practice we compute determinants in \(O(n^3)\) via the same elimination that solves linear systems — and software (np.linalg.det) does it for you. The meaning — volume scaling, zero ⇔ singular — is identical in every dimension.

🤖 Where determinants show up in ML

Three places you’ll meet them. (1) The multivariate Gaussian. Its probability density has a normalizing constant \(1/\sqrt{(2\pi)^n\det\Sigma}\), where \(\Sigma\) is the covariance matrix (Lesson 11–12); the determinant measures the “volume” of the spread. (2) Change of variables. When you transform a random variable, densities pick up a factor of \(|\det J|\), the determinant of the Jacobian matrix — this is the entire engine behind normalizing flows, a class of generative models (Track 6), which are literally designed so this determinant is cheap to compute. (3) Diagnosing matrices. A determinant near zero flags a singular or ill-conditioned matrix, warning that an inversion or a linear solve will be unstable.

🛠️ From scratch: determinant in code

code/track-1/determinant.py
import numpy as np

def det2x2(A):
    """The 2x2 formula: ad - bc."""
    (a, b), (c, d) = A
    return a*d - b*c

A = np.array([[2.0, 1.0],
              [1.0, 2.0]])
print(det2x2(A))            # 3.0
print(np.linalg.det(A))    # 3.0000000000000004  (tiny float error)

# Area scaling: a random region's area is multiplied by |det A|
square = np.array([[0,0],[1,0],[1,1],[0,1]])
def polygon_area(P):                       # shoelace formula
    x, y = P[:,0], P[:,1]
    return 0.5*abs(np.dot(x, np.roll(y,-1)) - np.dot(y, np.roll(x,-1)))
mapped = square @ A.T
print(polygon_area(square), "->", polygon_area(mapped))   # 1.0 -> 3.0

# det(AB) = det(A) det(B)
B = np.array([[1.0, 2.0],[0.0, 1.0]])      # a shear, det = 1
print(np.isclose(np.linalg.det(A@B), np.linalg.det(A)*np.linalg.det(B)))  # True

# det = 0  <=>  singular  <=>  dependent columns  <=>  not invertible
S = np.array([[1.0, 2.0],[2.0, 4.0]])      # column 2 = 2 * column 1
print(round(np.linalg.det(S), 12))         # 0.0
try:
    np.linalg.inv(S)
except np.linalg.LinAlgError as e:
    print("not invertible:", e)            # singular matrix

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. \(\det A\) is the (signed) factor by which \(A\) scales area/volume. \(2\times2\): \(\det = ad-bc\).
  2. Sign = orientation: \(\det<0\) flips space; \(\det>0\) preserves it.
  3. \(\det A = 0\) \(\iff\) columns dependent \(\iff\) collapses dimension \(\iff\) \(A\) not invertible.
  4. \(\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)\); \(\det I = 1\); \(\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det A\).
  5. In ML: Gaussian normalization \(\sqrt{\det\Sigma}\), change-of-variables \(|\det J|\) (normalizing flows), and singularity warnings.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. Compute \(\det\begin{bmatrix}3&1\\2&4\end{bmatrix}\).

  • \(14\)
    This added the products \(3\cdot4 + 2\cdot1\). The determinant subtracts: \(ad-bc\).
  • \(10\)
    Correct. \(ad-bc = 3\cdot4 - 1\cdot2 = 12 - 2 = 10\).
  • \(12\)
    That’s only \(ad = 3\cdot4\); you must subtract \(bc = 1\cdot2\).
  • \(11\)
    Close, but \(bc = 1\cdot 2 = 2\), not \(1\). \(12-2 = 10\).

Q2. A matrix has \(\det A = 0\). Which statement is guaranteed?

  • \(A\) rotates space by some angle.
    Rotations have \(\det = 1\) (they preserve area and orientation). A zero determinant is the opposite of a clean rotation.
  • \(A\) doubles every area.
    Doubling areas would be \(\det = 2\). Zero means areas are crushed to nothing, not doubled.
  • \(A\) is not invertible; its columns are linearly dependent.
    Correct. \(\det=0\) collapses space onto a lower dimension, so the columns are dependent and the map can’t be undone.
  • \(A\) must be the zero matrix.
    Not necessarily — e.g. \(\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\2&4\end{bmatrix}\) has \(\det=0\) but is far from the zero matrix. Any dependent-column matrix qualifies.

Q3. If \(\det A = 2\) and \(\det B = 5\), what is \(\det(AB)\)?

  • \(10\)
    Correct. \(\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B) = 2\cdot 5 = 10\). Composing maps multiplies their area-scaling factors.
  • \(7\)
    Determinants of a product multiply, they don’t add. Adding would give \(7\), but the rule is \(\det A\cdot\det B\).
  • \(2.5\)
    That’s \(\det B / \det A\) inverted — unrelated. The product rule gives \(2\cdot5\).
  • It depends on the sizes of \(A\) and \(B\).
    For square matrices of the same size, \(\det(AB)=\det A\det B\) always — no size dependence.

Q4. A reflection across a line has determinant…

  • \(0\), because it’s degenerate.
    A reflection is perfectly invertible (reflect twice and you’re back), so its determinant can’t be \(0\).
  • \(+1\), because it preserves area.
    It does preserve area in magnitude, but it flips orientation, which the sign must record. \(+1\) would mean no flip.
  • \(+2\), because it doubles something.
    Reflections don’t change size at all; the magnitude of the determinant is \(1\).
  • \(-1\), area preserved but orientation flipped.
    Correct. Magnitude \(1\) (no area change) with a negative sign (orientation reversed) gives \(\det = -1\).

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · compute

Find the determinants of \(\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&3\end{bmatrix}\), \(\begin{bmatrix}1&4\\0&1\end{bmatrix}\), and \(\begin{bmatrix}2&6\\1&3\end{bmatrix}\). Interpret each.

Show solution

First: \(2\cdot3 - 0\cdot0 = 6\) — a pure scaling that multiplies area by 6. Second: \(1\cdot1 - 4\cdot0 = 1\) — a shear, which preserves area (det 1), exactly as Figure 6.2 showed. Third: \(2\cdot3 - 6\cdot1 = 0\) — singular: column 2 \((6,3)\) is \(3\times\) column 1 \((2,1)\), so it collapses the plane to a line and is not invertible.

Problem 2 · derivation

Show that for a \(2\times2\) matrix, multiplying one row by a scalar \(k\) multiplies the determinant by \(k\). (This is one of the “row operations” behind fast determinant computation.)

Show solution

Take \(A=\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}\) with \(\det A = ad-bc\). Scale the first row by \(k\): \(A'=\begin{bmatrix}ka&kb\\c&d\end{bmatrix}\). Then

$$ \det A' = (ka)d - (kb)c = k(ad-bc) = k\det A. $$

Geometrically: stretching one edge of the parallelogram by \(k\) stretches its area by \(k\). \(\blacksquare\) (A corollary: scaling the whole \(n\times n\) matrix by \(k\) multiplies the determinant by \(k^n\), since each of the \(n\) rows contributes a factor of \(k\).)

Problem 3 · code

Write code to verify numerically that for a random \(3\times3\) matrix \(A\), \(\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)\). Then confirm a matrix with two identical rows has determinant \(0\).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
A = np.random.randn(3, 3)
print(np.isclose(np.linalg.det(np.linalg.inv(A)), 1/np.linalg.det(A)))  # True

R = np.array([[1.0, 2.0, 3.0],
              [1.0, 2.0, 3.0],     # identical to row 1
              [4.0, 5.0, 7.0]])
print(round(np.linalg.det(R), 12))   # 0.0  (dependent rows -> singular)

Two identical rows are linearly dependent, so the matrix collapses volume and \(\det=0\). The inverse relation holds because \(\det(A)\det(A^{-1})=\det(I)=1\).

Problem 4 · reasoning

A data scientist computes \(\det(\Sigma) = 1\times10^{-15}\) for a \(50\times50\) covariance matrix and is about to invert it. Why should they be worried?

Show solution

A determinant that tiny signals the matrix is (numerically) singular or nearly so — its columns are almost linearly dependent, meaning some features are almost perfectly correlated. Inverting it (needed for, e.g., the Gaussian density or linear regression’s normal equations) divides by quantities near zero, blowing up rounding errors and producing a wildly unstable inverse. Practical fixes: drop or combine the redundant features, or add a small “ridge” \(\lambda I\) to \(\Sigma\) (regularization — Track 3) so the determinant moves safely away from zero. This is a determinant doing its job as an early-warning system.
