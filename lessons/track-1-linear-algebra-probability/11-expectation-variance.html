Expectation, variance & covariance

A distribution can be complicated, but three numbers capture most of what you need: where it’s centered, how spread out it is, and — for two variables — whether they move together. These summaries are the vocabulary of every loss function and the gateway to PCA.

Track 1 · Lesson 11 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 8, 10

💡 Expectation: the center of a distribution

The expectation (or mean, or expected value) of a random variable \(X\) is its long-run average — the value you’d converge to by sampling forever. We write it \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) or \(\mu\) (the Greek letter “mu”). It’s a weighted average of the possible values, each weighted by its probability:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x} x\,p(x) \quad\text{(discrete)}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,f(x)\,dx \quad\text{(continuous)}. $$

The picture makes it click: \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) is the balance point of the distribution — the spot where the “mass” of the PMF (or the area of the PDF) would balance on a fingertip. A long tail on one side pulls the balance point that way.

Expectation is linear, a property used constantly: for constants \(a,b\) and any variables \(X,Y\), \(\mathbb{E}[aX+b]=a\,\mathbb{E}[X]+b\) and \(\mathbb{E}[X+Y]=\mathbb{E}[X]+\mathbb{E}[Y]\) — the latter holds even if \(X\) and \(Y\) are dependent, which is surprisingly powerful.

📐 Variance: how spread out it is

The mean alone can’t tell two very different distributions apart — \(\{-100, 100\}\) and \(\{0, 0\}\) both average to \(0\). We need a measure of spread. The variance is the expected squared distance from the mean:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}\!\big[(X-\mu)^2\big]. $$
(★)

We square the deviations so that distances on either side of the mean count positively (and don’t cancel). Because variance is in squared units, we often take its square root, the standard deviation \(\sigma = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\), which lives in the same units as \(X\) and is the everyday measure of spread.

🧮 The computational shortcut

Expanding the square in (★) gives a formula that’s easier to compute and appears all over ML. Using linearity of expectation and that \(\mu=\mathbb{E}[X]\) is a constant:

$$ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2\mu\,\mathbb{E}[X] + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2. $$

$$ \boxed{\ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \big(\mathbb{E}[X]\big)^2\ } \qquad\text{“mean of the square minus the square of the mean.”} $$

🎯 Covariance: do two variables move together?

With two variables we can ask a new question: when one is above its mean, is the other tend to be above its mean too? The covariance measures exactly this co-movement:

$$ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}\!\big[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)\big]. $$

Look at the product inside. When \(X\) and \(Y\) are usually above (or usually below) their means together, the two factors share a sign and the product is positive — positive covariance. When one tends to be high while the other is low, the product is negative. When there’s no consistent pattern, positives and negatives cancel to roughly zero:

Covariance’s size depends on the units of \(X\) and \(Y\), which makes it hard to interpret. Dividing out the two standard deviations gives the unit-free correlation \(\rho\), always between \(-1\) and \(1\):

$$ \rho(X,Y) = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\,\sigma_Y} \in [-1, 1]. $$

\(\rho = +1\) is a perfect upward line, \(\rho=-1\) a perfect downward line, \(\rho=0\) no linear relationship. (Note the qualifier: zero correlation rules out a linear trend, not every relationship — \(Y=X^2\) can have \(\rho\approx0\) yet be perfectly determined.) For many variables at once we collect all the pairwise covariances into the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\), whose \((i,j)\) entry is \(\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)\). That matrix is symmetric — and its eigenvectors are the principal components of Lesson 8.

🤖 Why these three numbers run ML

Every loss is an expectation. “Minimize the loss” means minimize \(\mathbb{E}[\text{error}]\) over the data distribution; mean squared error is literally a mean of squares. The bias–variance tradeoff (Track 3) decomposes a model’s expected error into a bias term and a variance term — this exact variance. Covariance drives PCA: the covariance matrix’s top eigenvectors are the directions of greatest variance, the axes you keep when reducing dimensions. And standardization — subtract the mean, divide by the standard deviation — rescales features so optimizers behave; it’s the first line of countless ML pipelines. Master these summaries and a huge amount of practice stops being arbitrary.

🛠️ From scratch: the summaries in code

code/track-1/expectation_variance.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

# Expectation & variance of a discrete distribution, by definition
values = np.array([1, 2, 3, 4])
probs  = np.array([0.4, 0.3, 0.2, 0.1])
mean = np.sum(values * probs)
var  = np.sum((values - mean)**2 * probs)
print("E[X] =", mean, " Var =", round(var, 3))   # E[X] = 2.0  Var = 1.0

# Verify the shortcut: Var(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2
E_X2 = np.sum(values**2 * probs)
print(np.isclose(var, E_X2 - mean**2))            # True

# From samples (how you'll actually do it on data)
X = rng.normal(5, 2, size=100_000)                # mean 5, std 2
print("sample mean ~", round(X.mean(), 2),        # ~5.0
      " sample std ~", round(X.std(), 2))         # ~2.0

# Covariance & correlation of two related variables
Y = 0.8 * X + rng.normal(0, 1, size=X.size)       # Y tracks X plus noise
cov = np.mean((X - X.mean()) * (Y - Y.mean()))
corr = cov / (X.std() * Y.std())
print("Cov ~", round(cov, 2), " corr ~", round(corr, 2))   # positive, near 0.85

# The full covariance matrix (its eigenvectors are the PCA axes, Lesson 8)
Sigma = np.cov(np.vstack([X, Y]))
print(np.round(Sigma, 2))

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. Expectation \(\mathbb{E}[X]=\sum x\,p(x)\) — the balance point. It’s linear: \(\mathbb{E}[aX+b]=a\mathbb{E}[X]+b\), \(\mathbb{E}[X+Y]=\mathbb{E}[X]+\mathbb{E}[Y]\).
  2. Variance \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]=\mathbb{E}[X^2]-(\mathbb{E}[X])^2\); spread. \(\sigma=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}}\) shares \(X\)’s units.
  3. Covariance \(\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)]\); sign = direction of co-movement.
  4. Correlation \(\rho=\operatorname{Cov}/(\sigma_X\sigma_Y)\in[-1,1]\); \(\rho=0\) means no linear relation (not “no relation”).
  5. Losses are expectations; PCA diagonalizes the covariance matrix; standardization uses \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\).

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. \(X\) is \(0\) or \(10\), each with probability \(1/2\). What are \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X)\)?

  • \(\mathbb{E}[X]=5\), \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=5\).
    The mean is right, but variance is the mean squared deviation: \((0-5)^2\) and \((10-5)^2\) are each \(25\), averaging to \(25\), not \(5\).
  • \(\mathbb{E}[X]=5\), \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=25\).
    Correct. Mean \(=5\). Each value is \(5\) away, so squared deviations are \(25\); their average is \(\operatorname{Var}=25\) (and \(\sigma=5\)).
  • \(\mathbb{E}[X]=0\), \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=10\).
    The mean of \(\{0,10\}\) with equal weights is \(5\), not \(0\). Recompute from \(\sum x\,p(x)\).
  • \(\mathbb{E}[X]=5\), \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=50\).
    Close idea but over-counted: variance averages the two squared deviations \((25+25)/2=25\), not their sum \(50\).

Q2. Why do we square the deviations in the definition of variance?

  • To make the units match \(X\).
    Squaring actually changes the units to \(X^2\); we take a square root afterward (the std) to recover \(X\)’s units.
  • Because it’s required for the mean to exist.
    The mean exists independently. Squaring is about how we measure spread, not about the mean’s existence.
  • So deviations on both sides count positively and don’t cancel.
    Correct. Raw deviations \((X-\mu)\) average to zero by definition. Squaring makes every deviation positive so spread doesn’t cancel out.
  • To guarantee the variance is less than 1.
    Variance can be any non-negative number, often far above \(1\). Squaring isn’t a bounding trick.

Q3. Two variables have correlation \(\rho = 0\). Which conclusion is justified?

  • They are independent.
    Not guaranteed. Zero correlation rules out a linear trend, but variables can be dependent in nonlinear ways and still have \(\rho=0\).
  • One causes the other.
    Correlation never implies causation, and here there isn’t even correlation. This is doubly unsupported.
  • They have no relationship at all.
    Too strong. \(Y=X^2\) (with symmetric \(X\)) has \(\rho\approx0\) yet \(Y\) is fully determined by \(X\).
  • There is no linear relationship between them.
    Correct. Correlation measures linear association only, so \(\rho=0\) means no linear trend — nonlinear relationships may still exist.

Q4. In PCA, the directions of greatest variance are found from which object?

  • The eigenvectors of the covariance matrix.
    Correct. The covariance matrix encodes spread in every direction; its top eigenvectors point along maximum variance (Lesson 8).
  • The mean vector of the data.
    The mean is the center (a single point); it says nothing about the directions of spread.
  • The determinant of the data matrix.
    A determinant is one number about volume scaling, not a set of directions. PCA needs eigenvectors.
  • The correlation’s sign.
    A single sign can’t identify high-dimensional axes. PCA diagonalizes the whole covariance matrix.

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · compute

A fair die shows \(X\in\{1,\dots,6\}\). Compute \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X)\) using the shortcut formula.

Show solution

\(\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{6}(1+2+3+4+5+6) = \frac{21}{6} = 3.5\).

\(\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{1}{6}(1+4+9+16+25+36) = \frac{91}{6} \approx 15.167\).

\(\operatorname{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = 15.167 - 3.5^2 = 15.167 - 12.25 = 2.917\) (exactly \(35/12\)). So \(\sigma \approx 1.71\).

Problem 2 · derivation

Prove that \(\operatorname{Var}(aX) = a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)\) for a constant \(a\).

Show solution

Let \(\mu=\mathbb{E}[X]\). By linearity, \(\mathbb{E}[aX] = a\mu\). Then

$$ \operatorname{Var}(aX) = \mathbb{E}\!\big[(aX - a\mu)^2\big] = \mathbb{E}\!\big[a^2(X-\mu)^2\big] = a^2\,\mathbb{E}\!\big[(X-\mu)^2\big] = a^2\operatorname{Var}(X). \ \blacksquare $$

The constant \(a\) pulls out as \(a^2\) because variance is built on squared deviations. Consequence for std: \(\sigma_{aX} = |a|\,\sigma_X\) — scaling data by \(a\) scales its spread by \(|a|\), which is exactly why standardization divides by \(\sigma\).

Problem 3 · code

Generate \(Y = -1.5X + \text{noise}\) for Gaussian \(X\), and confirm the covariance is negative and the correlation is near \(-1\) (when noise is small).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(5)
X = rng.normal(0, 1, size=100_000)
Y = -1.5 * X + rng.normal(0, 0.2, size=X.size)   # small noise
cov  = np.mean((X - X.mean()) * (Y - Y.mean()))
corr = cov / (X.std() * Y.std())
print("Cov ~", round(cov, 2), " corr ~", round(corr, 3))  # Cov<0, corr ~ -0.99

The covariance is negative (as \(X\) rises, \(Y\) falls), and with little noise the correlation sits near \(-1\) — a nearly perfect downward line. Increase the noise std and watch \(\rho\) move toward \(0\).

Problem 4 · reasoning

Mean squared error is \(\mathbb{E}[(\,\hat{Y}-Y)^2]\). Explain, using this lesson, why minimizing MSE pushes the model’s prediction toward the mean of \(Y\) (when the prediction is a single constant).

Show solution

Suppose the model must output one constant \(c\). The objective is \(\mathbb{E}[(c-Y)^2]\). Expanding exactly as in the variance shortcut, \(\mathbb{E}[(c-Y)^2] = \operatorname{Var}(Y) + (c - \mathbb{E}[Y])^2\). The first term doesn’t depend on \(c\); the second is a squared distance minimized when \(c = \mathbb{E}[Y]\). So the best constant predictor under MSE is exactly the mean of \(Y\) — and the leftover error is \(\operatorname{Var}(Y)\). This is why “predict the mean” is the baseline every regression model must beat, and it’s the seed of the bias–variance decomposition in Track 3.
