Probability foundations & Bayes’ rule

Machine learning is reasoning under uncertainty, and probability is its grammar. We build it from three simple axioms up to Bayes’ rule — the formula for updating beliefs when new evidence arrives, and the backbone of countless ML models.

Track 1 · Lesson 9 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: nothing (fresh start)

💡 Concept: measuring uncertainty

Probability puts a number between \(0\) and \(1\) on how likely something is — \(0\) means impossible, \(1\) means certain, \(0.5\) means a coin flip. To be precise we need a little vocabulary, and every term is concrete. The sample space \(\Omega\) (capital omega) is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. A single outcome is written \(\omega\). An event is any subset of \(\Omega\) — a collection of outcomes we care about. And the probability \(P(A)\) is the number we assign to event \(A\).

Example: roll a die. Then \(\Omega=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\), one outcome is \(\omega=4\), the event “even” is the subset \(A=\{2,4,6\}\), and for a fair die \(P(A)=3/6=0.5\).

The three axioms (everything follows from these)

All of probability is built on three rules, due to Kolmogorov:

$$ \text{(1)}\ P(A)\ge 0, \qquad \text{(2)}\ P(\Omega)=1, \qquad \text{(3)}\ P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)\ \text{if }A,B\text{ are disjoint.} $$

In words: probabilities are never negative (1); something in \(\Omega\) must happen, so the total is \(1\) (2); and if two events can’t both occur (“disjoint”), the chance of either is the sum (3). From these, useful facts follow immediately. The complement rule \(P(A^c)=1-P(A)\) (the chance \(A\) does not happen) comes from splitting \(\Omega\) into \(A\) and “not \(A\)”. And the general union rule for events that may overlap is

$$ P(A\cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A\cap B), $$

where we subtract the overlap \(P(A\cap B)\) so it isn’t double-counted — you can see the doubled region in Figure 9.1.

📐 Conditional probability: updating on evidence

The most important idea in the lesson. Once you learn that event \(B\) happened, your world shrinks from all of \(\Omega\) to just \(B\), and probabilities must be recomputed inside that smaller world. The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\) is

$$ P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}, \qquad P(B)>0. $$
(★)

Read the vertical bar “\(\mid\)” as “given.” The formula says: of all the ways \(B\) can happen (the denominator), what fraction also have \(A\) (the numerator)? It’s the proportion of \(B\)’s region that overlaps \(A\). Two events are independent when knowing one tells you nothing about the other, i.e. \(P(A\mid B)=P(A)\), which rearranges via (★) to the clean symmetric form \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B)\).

🎯 Bayes’ rule: flipping the condition

Often you know \(P(B\mid A)\) but want \(P(A\mid B)\) — the reverse. A test tells you \(P(\text{positive}\mid\text{disease})\), but a patient wants \(P(\text{disease}\mid\text{positive})\). Bayes’ rule flips it, and it falls out of (★) in two lines. The overlap \(P(A\cap B)\) can be written two equivalent ways by conditioning on either event:

$$ P(A\cap B) = P(A\mid B)\,P(B) = P(B\mid A)\,P(A). $$

Set the two right-hand expressions equal and divide by \(P(B)\):

$$ P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(B\mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}. $$
(‡)

Each piece has a name worth knowing: \(P(A)\) is the prior (belief before evidence), \(P(B\mid A)\) is the likelihood (how well \(A\) explains the evidence \(B\)), \(P(A\mid B)\) is the posterior (updated belief), and \(P(B)\) is a normalizing constant. Bayes’ rule is learning: it’s the rule for turning a prior belief plus evidence into an updated belief.

The famous example: a positive test

A disease affects \(1\%\) of people. A test is \(99\%\) accurate on the sick (true positive) and has a \(5\%\) false-positive rate on the healthy. You test positive. What’s the chance you’re actually sick? Intuition screams “99%.” Intuition is badly wrong — and the tree shows why:

Plugging into Bayes’ rule, with \(P(+)\) found by adding the two positive branches (the law of total probability):

$$ P(D\mid+) = \frac{P(+\mid D)\,P(D)}{P(+)} = \frac{0.99\times 0.01}{0.99\times 0.01 + 0.05\times 0.99} = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 0.167. $$

Only about \(17\%\)! The reason is the base rate: the disease is so rare that the few false positives from the huge healthy majority swamp the true positives. The same counts as a table make it tangible — imagine \(10{,}000\) people:

Test +Test −Total
Diseased991100
Healthy4959,4059,900
Total5949,40610,000

Of the \(594\) positives, only \(99\) are truly sick: \(99/594 \approx 0.167\). Same answer, no formula needed.

🤖 Why Bayes is everywhere in ML

A classifier’s job is to output \(P(\text{class}\mid\text{data})\) — a posterior. Bayes’ rule is how you get there from a model of \(P(\text{data}\mid\text{class})\) (the likelihood) and a prior over classes; the Naive Bayes classifier does exactly this with an independence assumption. Whole subfields — Bayesian inference, probabilistic graphical models, variational autoencoders (Track 6), and the noise-to-data reasoning of diffusion models — are Bayes’ rule applied at scale. And the base-rate lesson is a permanent guardrail: a “99% accurate” model can still be mostly wrong on a rare class, which is why evaluation on imbalanced data (Track 9) needs more than accuracy.

🛠️ From scratch: simulate to confirm Bayes

When a probability feels counterintuitive, simulate it. We’ll generate a million people and just count — the empirical answer should match \(0.167\).

code/track-1/probability_bayes.py
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
N = 1_000_000

diseased = rng.random(N) < 0.01                      # 1% have the disease
test_pos = np.where(diseased,
                    rng.random(N) < 0.99,            # P(+ | disease) = 0.99
                    rng.random(N) < 0.05)            # P(+ | healthy) = 0.05

# P(disease | positive) = (diseased AND positive) / (positive)
p_d_given_pos = np.sum(diseased & test_pos) / np.sum(test_pos)
print(round(p_d_given_pos, 3))        # ~0.167  -> matches Bayes' rule!

# Law of large numbers: the empirical frequency converges to the true probability
flips = rng.random(100_000) < 0.5
print("running estimate of P(heads):", round(flips.mean(), 4))   # ~0.5
💡 Frequencies become probabilities

The simulation works because of the law of large numbers: as you repeat an experiment, the fraction of times an event occurs converges to its probability. That’s the bridge between the abstract \(P\) and the counts you can actually measure — and the reason any of this applies to real data.

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. Vocabulary: sample space \(\Omega\), outcomes \(\omega\), events = subsets, \(P(A)\in[0,1]\).
  2. Rules: \(P(A^c)=1-P(A)\); \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\).
  3. Conditional: \(P(A\mid B)=P(A\cap B)/P(B)\). Independent \(\iff P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\).
  4. Bayes: \(P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B)}\) = posterior ∝ likelihood × prior.
  5. Base rates matter: a rare condition makes false positives dominate — accuracy alone misleads.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. You roll a fair die. What is \(P(\text{even} \mid \text{the roll is} \ge 4)\)?

  • \(1/2\)
    That’s the unconditional \(P(\text{even})\). Conditioning on “\(\ge 4\)” restricts the world and can change the answer.
  • \(1/6\)
    \(1/6\) is the chance of one specific face, not this conditional. We need the fraction of \(\{4,5,6\}\) that is even.
  • \(1/3\)
    This would be evens among all six faces restricted wrongly. Among \(\{4,5,6\}\) the evens are \(\{4,6\}\) — that’s \(2/3\), not \(1/3\).
  • \(2/3\)
    Correct. Given \(\{4,5,6\}\) (three equally likely outcomes), the even ones are \(\{4,6\}\), so \(P=2/3\). Conditioning shrank the sample space to those three.

Q2. A test is “99% accurate,” yet a positive result implies only a 17% chance of disease. The main reason is…

  • the test is broken.
    No — a genuinely 99%-accurate test produces this. The math, not a fault, drives the low posterior.
  • the likelihood \(P(+\mid D)\) is too low.
    It’s actually high (0.99). The low posterior comes from the prior, not the likelihood.
  • the low base rate makes false positives outnumber true positives.
    Correct. With only 1% sick, the 5% false-positive rate acting on the 99% healthy yields far more false than true positives.
  • the events are independent.
    Test result and disease are clearly dependent (that’s why the test is useful). Independence isn’t the issue; the base rate is.

Q3. In Bayes’ rule \(P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B)}\), the term \(P(A)\) is called the…

  • prior — belief in \(A\) before seeing evidence \(B\).
    Correct. \(P(A)\) encodes what you believed beforehand; Bayes updates it into the posterior \(P(A\mid B)\).
  • posterior.
    The posterior is the output, \(P(A\mid B)\) — belief after evidence. \(P(A)\) is the input belief.
  • likelihood.
    The likelihood is \(P(B\mid A)\) — how well \(A\) explains the evidence. \(P(A)\) doesn’t involve \(B\).
  • evidence.
    The evidence/normalizer is \(P(B)\). \(P(A)\) is your starting belief about \(A\).

Q4. Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent with \(P(A)=0.3\), \(P(B)=0.5\). What is \(P(A\cap B)\)?

  • \(0.8\)
    That’s \(P(A)+P(B)\) (and even then you’d subtract the overlap for a union). Intersection of independent events multiplies.
  • \(0.15\)
    Correct. For independent events \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)=0.3\times0.5=0.15\).
  • \(0.3\)
    That’s just \(P(A)\). Knowing \(B\) (independent) doesn’t change \(A\), but the joint event still requires multiplying the two probabilities.
  • It can’t be determined.
    Independence is exactly the extra fact that lets you compute it: \(P(A\cap B)=P(A)P(B)\).

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · compute

Two fair coins are flipped. What is \(P(\text{two heads} \mid \text{at least one head})\)?

Show solution

Sample space \(\{HH, HT, TH, TT\}\), each with probability \(1/4\). “At least one head” is \(\{HH,HT,TH\}\), probability \(3/4\). “Two heads and at least one head” is just \(\{HH\}\), probability \(1/4\). So

$$ P(HH \mid \text{≥1 head}) = \frac{1/4}{3/4} = \frac{1}{3}. $$

Not \(1/2\) — the condition rules out \(TT\) but leaves three equally likely cases, only one of which is \(HH\). A classic conditioning trap.

Problem 2 · derivation

Derive Bayes’ rule from the definition of conditional probability, stating where each step uses an axiom or definition.

Show solution

By the definition (★) applied two ways to the same overlap \(P(A\cap B)\):

$$ P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} \quad\text{and}\quad P(B\mid A) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(A)}. $$

From the second, \(P(A\cap B) = P(B\mid A)\,P(A)\). Substitute that numerator into the first:

$$ P(A\mid B) = \frac{P(B\mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}. \qquad\blacksquare $$

Every step is just the conditional-probability definition; Bayes’ rule is bookkeeping on a single overlap, which is why it’s so general.

Problem 3 · code

By simulation, estimate \(P(\text{two heads}\mid\text{at least one head})\) from Problem 1 and confirm it’s about \(1/3\).

Show solution 
import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
N = 1_000_000
c1 = rng.random(N) < 0.5      # coin 1 heads?
c2 = rng.random(N) < 0.5      # coin 2 heads?
at_least_one = c1 | c2
two_heads = c1 & c2
print(round(np.sum(two_heads & at_least_one) / np.sum(at_least_one), 3))  # ~0.333

The estimate hovers near \(0.333\), confirming the hand answer. Simulation is your safety net whenever a conditional feels slippery.

Problem 4 · reasoning

A spam filter flags an email. Spam is 40% of all email; the filter flags 95% of spam and 8% of legitimate mail. What is \(P(\text{spam}\mid\text{flagged})\)?

Show solution

Bayes’ rule with prior \(P(S)=0.4\), likelihood \(P(F\mid S)=0.95\), and false alarms \(P(F\mid S^c)=0.08\) on the \(60\%\) legitimate mail. First the evidence (total probability of a flag):

$$ P(F) = 0.95(0.4) + 0.08(0.6) = 0.38 + 0.048 = 0.428. $$

$$ P(S\mid F) = \frac{0.95\times 0.4}{0.428} = \frac{0.38}{0.428} \approx 0.888. $$

About \(89\%\). Unlike the rare-disease case, spam isn’t rare (40% base rate), so a flag is fairly trustworthy here — the base rate cuts both ways.
