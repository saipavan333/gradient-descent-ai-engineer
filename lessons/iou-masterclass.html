IoU & object detection, from scratch

How a computer grades "did I find the object?" — the one number behind every detection benchmark. You'll drag it, compute it, and build it.

A self-driving car's vision system draws a box and says "pedestrian, here." The true pedestrian is in a slightly different box. Question: was the car right? Not yes-or-no — how right? We need a single number that says how well two rectangles agree. Slide one sticky note partly over another: the score is how much they share, divided by how much they cover together. That number is IoU, and it is the referee for every object detector on Earth.

By the end you'll be able to compute the overlap score of any two boxes by hand, build IoU from scratch in code, explain the 0.5 "hit" threshold and why it changes by task, and see how detectors turn this one number into a full accuracy benchmark.

1 · Intuition Shared area over combined area

Object detection means: for every object in a picture, draw a bounding box and name it. To grade a predicted box, we compare it to the ground-truth box — the human-drawn correct answer.

Two rectangles can relate in messy ways: one bigger, shifted, tilted apart. We want a score that is 1 when they match perfectly and 0 when they don't touch at all, smoothly in between. The trick is beautifully simple: measure the area they share, and divide by the total area they cover together. Lots of shared area relative to the total ⇒ score near 1. Little shared area ⇒ score near 0. That ratio is Intersection over Union (IoU) — "how much of the combined footprint is agreement."

✦
The picture: two overlapping boxes. The dark middle sliver they share is the intersection. Everything either box covers is the union. IoU = middle ÷ everything. That's the whole idea.

2 · The formal idea The overlap ratio, exactly

Write each box by its corners: a top-left (x1, y1) and a bottom-right (x2, y2), where x grows rightward and y grows downward (image convention). The area of a box is width × height = (x2−x1) × (y2−y1).

IoU  =  area(intersection)  /  area(union)

The intersection is itself a rectangle — the region inside both boxes. Its left edge is the rightmost of the two left edges, max(x1ᴬ, x1ᴮ); its top edge is the lowest of the two top edges, max(y1ᴬ, y1ᴮ); its right edge is min(x2ᴬ, x2ᴮ); its bottom is min(y2ᴬ, y2ᴮ). If those give a negative width or height, the boxes don't overlap and the intersection area is 0 (this clamp matters — see Common mistakes). The union is "everything either box covers." You get it by adding both areas and subtracting the shared part once (so it isn't double-counted): area(union) = areaᴬ + areaᴮ − area(intersection).

i
The 0.5 rule. IoU is a number from 0 to 1; we still need a line for "close enough to count as a correct detection." The standard line is IoU ≥ 0.5 — predictions at or above 0.5 count as a hit, below as a miss. It's a convention, not a law: strict benchmarks use 0.75, and modern ones average over many thresholds (more in the frontier note).

4 · Worked example Every number, by hand

Ground truth G = (80, 60, 240, 180) and prediction P = (100, 80, 260, 200) — same size, shifted down-right by 20 pixels. Let's grade it.

areas:   G = (240-80)(180-60) = 160 × 120 = 19200
         P = (260-100)(200-80) = 160 × 120 = 19200

intersection corners:
  left   = max(80, 100) = 100      right  = min(240, 260) = 240
  top    = max(60,  80) =  80      bottom = min(180, 200) = 180
  width  = 240 - 100 = 140         height = 180 - 80 = 100
  area(intersection) = 140 × 100 = 14000

union = 19200 + 19200 - 14000 = 24400

IoU = 14000 / 24400 = 0.574

So IoU ≈ 0.574. Because 0.574 ≥ 0.5, this counts as a correct detection — the box is a bit off but close enough. Read the union step carefully: we add both areas (19200 + 19200 = 38400) but that counts the shared 14000 twice, so we subtract it once to get 24400. Forgetting that subtraction is the single most common IoU bug. Everything here is just max, min, multiply, and one divide — you can grade a detector with arithmetic a child could do.

5 · Build it from scratch Run it yourself

The Python is a line-for-line twin (run it in Colab):

def iou(a, b):                       # boxes: (x1, y1, x2, y2)
    ix1, iy1 = max(a[0], b[0]), max(a[1], b[1])
    ix2, iy2 = min(a[2], b[2]), min(a[3], b[3])
    iw, ih = max(0, ix2 - ix1), max(0, iy2 - iy1)
    inter = iw * ih
    union = (a[2]-a[0])*(a[3]-a[1]) + (b[2]-b[0])*(b[3]-b[1]) - inter
    return inter / union

print(round(iou((80,60,240,180), (100,80,260,200)), 3))   # 0.574

6 · Common mistakes Traps people actually hit

❌ Not clamping negative overlap to zero

When boxes don't overlap, ix2 − ix1 comes out negative. Multiply two negatives (width and height) and you get a bogus positive "intersection." Fix: clamp each side with max(0, …) before multiplying, so non-overlapping boxes correctly give intersection 0.

❌ Forgetting to subtract the intersection from the union

Union is not areaA + areaB — that double-counts the shared region. Fix: union = areaA + areaB − intersection. This one silently makes every IoU too small.

❌ Treating IoU ≥ 0.5 as sacred

0.5 is a convention. Fine-grained tasks (medical, robotics) demand 0.75 or higher; the standard COCO benchmark averages over IoU thresholds 0.50–0.95. Fix: pick the threshold your application actually needs, and report which one you used.

❌ Mixing up box formats

Some libraries use (x, y, width, height), others (x1, y1, x2, y2), others center-based (cx, cy, w, h). Feeding one format into a function expecting another gives nonsense IoUs. Fix: convert to one convention first and be explicit about which.

7 · Key results Worth memorizing

★ Lock these in
  • IoU = area(intersection) / area(union) — shared area over combined area; 1 = perfect, 0 = no overlap.
  • Intersection corners: max of the left/top edges, min of the right/bottom edges; clamp width and height to ≥ 0.
  • Union = areaA + areaB − intersection (subtract the overlap once).
  • A detection usually counts as correct at IoU ≥ 0.5 — a convention; stricter tasks use higher thresholds.
  • IoU penalizes both position error and size mismatch, which is why it's the universal detection metric.

9 · Graded practice Warm-up → transfer → stretch

Warm-up Two identical boxes

What is the IoU of a box with an exact copy of itself?

Solution. The intersection is the whole box; the union is also the whole box. So IoU = area / area = 1.0. This is the sanity check every IoU implementation must pass — if iou(b, b) isn't 1, you have a bug (usually a format or clamp error).

Transfer A box half the size, centered inside

Ground truth is 100×100. The prediction is 50×50, sitting exactly in the center of it. What's the IoU — and what does it teach?

Solution. Intersection = the small box = 50×50 = 2500. Union = 100×100 + 50×50 − 2500 = 10000 + 2500 − 2500 = 10000 (the big box entirely contains the small one, so the union is just the big box). IoU = 2500 / 10000 = 0.25. Even though the small box is perfectly positioned (dead center, fully inside), IoU is only 0.25 — a miss at the 0.5 line. The lesson: IoU punishes size mismatch, not just position. A detector that finds the right spot but the wrong scale still fails, which is exactly what you want for "did you box the object correctly?"

Stretch From IoU to a real detector: NMS & mAP

A detector often outputs several overlapping boxes for the same object, each with a confidence score. How is IoU used to (a) clean that up, and (b) turn detections into a single accuracy number?

Solution. (a) Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS). Sort the predicted boxes by confidence, keep the highest, then delete any lower-confidence box whose IoU with a kept box exceeds a threshold (say 0.5) — they're duplicates of the same object. Repeat down the list. IoU is the "are these the same object?" test that removes duplicates, leaving one box per object. (b) mean Average Precision (mAP). To score the whole detector: match each prediction to a ground-truth box using IoU (a match needs IoU ≥ threshold); matched = true positive, unmatched prediction = false positive, missed ground truth = false negative. Sweep the confidence cutoff to trace a precision–recall curve, take the area under it (Average Precision) for each object class, and average across classes → mAP. Modern benchmarks (COCO) go further and average AP over IoU thresholds from 0.50 to 0.95, rewarding tight boxes. The takeaway: IoU is the atom; NMS and mAP are built entirely on top of "how much do these two boxes overlap?" Master IoU and the rest of detection evaluation is assembly.

10 · Frontier note Where this lives in the real world

Every object-detection leaderboard — the COCO benchmark, autonomous-driving datasets, medical-imaging challenges — ranks models by mAP, and mAP is IoU all the way down. IoU also trains detectors: variants like GIoU, DIoU, and CIoU are used directly as loss functions, because plain IoU gives no gradient when boxes don't overlap, and these fixes add a signal that pulls a stray box toward the target.

The frontier is moving past hand-tuned box cleanup: transformer detectors like DETR predict a set of boxes directly and match them to ground truth with a global assignment, sidestepping NMS entirely — but they still evaluate with IoU-based mAP. And the same overlap idea generalizes from boxes to pixel masks (mask IoU) for segmentation, and to 3-D boxes for lidar. Two rectangles' shared area over their combined area turns out to be one of the most reused ideas in all of computer vision.

"To grade a predicted box against the true box, take the area they share and divide by the area they cover together. That's IoU — 1 means perfect, 0 means no overlap, and above 0.5 usually counts as a hit. Every object-detection score is built on it."
