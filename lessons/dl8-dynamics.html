Training dynamics & debugging

⏱ 26 min Lesson 4.8 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsTraining fails in recognizable ways. Reading loss curves and understanding initialization turns mysterious failures into quick diagnoses — the practical craft of making networks actually train.
By the end, you can
  • Diagnose overfit/underfit/high-LR from loss curves
  • Explain how initialization controls gradient scale
  • Use He/Xavier init and normalization
  • Apply a debugging checklist (overfit a tiny batch first)

Concept Most training problems are diagnosable — if you read the signs

Deep networks fail in a handful of recognizable ways, and an experienced practitioner diagnoses them not by guessing but by reading the evidence. The single most informative instrument is the loss curve: a plot of training and validation loss over epochs. Its shape tells you what's wrong.

Read it like an X-ray: a healthy run shows both curves descending and staying close. A widening gap (train low, validation rising) is overfitting — regularize or get more data (Lesson 4.5). Spiky or diverging loss means the learning rate is too high — lower it (Lesson 1.8). Both high and flat means underfitting — train longer, raise capacity, or raise the learning rate. You'll consult this chart constantly.

Concept Initialization: getting the gradient scale right

Before training even starts, the random initial weights matter enormously. If they're too small, activations (and gradients) shrink layer by layer until they vanish — deep layers get no signal. If too large, they explode to huge values or NaN. The goal of good initialization is to keep the scale of activations and gradients roughly constant across depth.

The standard fixes are Xavier (a.k.a. Glorot) initialization for tanh/sigmoid and He initialization for ReLU — both set the random weights' spread based on the number of inputs to a layer (its "fan-in") so the variance is preserved. Frameworks apply sensible defaults automatically, and batch normalization (Lesson 4.5) further stabilizes the scale during training. But knowing why a too-small or too-large init breaks training lets you recognize the symptom.

Concept A debugging checklist

When training won't work, go through this in order. (1) Overfit a tiny batch. Take just 2–4 examples and check the model can drive their loss to ~0; if it can't, you have a bug (wrong shapes, broken gradient flow, label mismatch), not a tuning problem. (2) Check shapes and the loss — print tensor shapes; confirm the loss is the right one for the task and isn't NaN at step 0. (3) Inspect the data — are inputs normalized? labels correct? (4) Tune the learning rate — the highest-leverage hyperparameter. (5) Then worry about architecture and regularization.

Worked example Watching initialization make or break the signal

We pass a random signal through 6 linear layers and track the standard deviation (the "size") of the activations at each layer, for three weight scales: too small, the calibrated $\sqrt{1/n}$ (a He/Xavier-style value), and too big.

The three regimes are stark. With scale 0.02 (too small), the signal's size halves and halves until it's $0.001$ by layer 6 — vanished; gradients flowing back would vanish identically, so early layers can't learn. With scale 0.2 (too big), it explodes from 1 to 914 in six layers — and a real network would overflow to NaN. Only the calibrated scale 0.062 = $\sqrt{1/256}$ keeps the std near 1.0 at every depth, so signal and gradient survive all the way through. That single calibration is the entire idea behind He/Xavier init. In PyTorch you get it almost for free:

import torch.nn as nn
layer = nn.Linear(256, 256)                    # PyTorch already uses a sensible default init
nn.init.kaiming_normal_(layer.weight, nonlinearity="relu")   # explicit He init for ReLU nets

You rarely set this by hand — defaults are good and batch norm helps — but when a deep network won't train at all and the loss is NaN or frozen from step 0, bad scaling (init or missing normalization) is a prime suspect, and now you know exactly what's happening inside.

★ Key points to remember
  • The loss curve is your main diagnostic: gap → overfit; spiky/diverging → LR too high; both high & flat → underfit.
  • Initialization sets activation/gradient scale; too small vanishes, too large explodes.
  • He (ReLU) and Xavier (tanh/sigmoid) init pick the weight spread from layer width to keep scale stable.
  • Debug in order: overfit a tiny batch first, then check shapes/loss, data, and the learning rate.
  • If the loss is NaN or frozen from step 0, suspect bad scaling (init or missing normalization) or a too-high LR.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You plot loss curves and both training and validation loss sit flat at a high value and never improve. What does this indicate, and name two fixes?
Show solution & reasoning

Both high and flat = underfitting (high bias, Lesson 3.5): the model isn't even fitting the training data. Either it lacks capacity or isn't learning. Fixes: (1) increase the learning rate (it may be so small that nothing moves — check this first, it's free), and (2) increase capacity (more layers/units) or train longer.

Also verify there's actually a learnable signal (correct labels, informative features) and that the model isn't accidentally crippled (e.g., a frozen layer or a bug zeroing gradients). The diagnostic logic: a flat high loss means "not learning," so make learning easier/stronger — the opposite of the overfitting remedy.

2In the worked example, scale 0.2 made activation std grow 1 → 914 across 6 layers. Extrapolate: what happens in a 50-layer network, and what would you see in training?
Show solution & reasoning

The growth is roughly geometric (each layer multiplied the std by ~3), so over 50 layers the activations would reach astronomically large values — far beyond what floating-point can represent. You'd see the loss become NaN (or inf) within the first step or two: the forward pass overflows, and the gradients explode in the backward pass.

Fixes that target the cause: use proper He/Xavier initialization (the $\sqrt{1/n}$ scale that kept std ≈ 1), add batch/layer normalization to renormalize activations each layer, and consider residual connections (ResNet, Lesson 4.6) which help signals pass through deep stacks. This is exactly why training very deep nets was impossible before these techniques — and why they were such pivotal advances.

3Why is "overfit a tiny batch first" such a powerful debugging step? What does success and failure each tell you?
Show solution & reasoning

A model with enough capacity should be able to memorize 2–4 examples and drive their loss to nearly 0 in a few hundred steps — that's a trivial task. So it cleanly separates two kinds of problem. If it succeeds, your forward pass, loss, gradient flow, and optimizer all work end to end — the plumbing is correct, so any remaining trouble is about generalization/tuning (LR, regularization, data). If it fails (loss won't drop even on 4 examples), you have a genuine bug: mismatched shapes, labels not aligned with inputs, gradients not flowing (a detached tensor, a frozen layer), or the wrong loss.

It's powerful because it's fast (seconds), unambiguous (a model that can't memorize 4 points is broken), and it isolates "bug" from "tuning" — saving you from endlessly adjusting hyperparameters when the real issue is a one-line plumbing error. Seasoned practitioners run this reflexively before any serious training.

★ Deep dive: normalization, residual connections, and the bugs everyone hits▸

Three structural advances tamed the gradient-scale problems this lesson describes, and they appear in nearly every modern network. Normalization layers (batch norm for CNNs, layer norm for transformers) renormalize activations mid-network so the scale can't drift far, making training robust to imperfect initialization. Residual connections (the "skip connections" of ResNet) add a layer's input to its output, giving gradients a direct path that bypasses the layer — so even 100+ layer networks get clean gradient flow, the same trick the LSTM's cell-state highway uses in time. Careful initialization (He/Xavier) sets the starting scale right. Together these turned "deep networks won't train" from a hard barrier into a non-issue, which is a big part of why the field could go from 8 layers (AlexNet) to hundreds.

It's also worth cataloguing the boring bugs that masquerade as deep problems, because they're far more common than exotic gradient pathologies. Forgetting optimizer.zero_grad() (Lesson 4.4). Forgetting model.eval() at test time, leaving dropout on (Lesson 4.5). A shape mismatch silently broadcasting into a wrong loss. Labels misaligned with inputs after a shuffle. Not normalizing inputs. Applying softmax twice (once in the model, once in the loss, Lesson 4.2). A learning rate off by 100×. The reason the debugging checklist matters is that the symptom ("model won't learn") is the same for a deep theoretical issue and a trivial typo — and the typo is usually the culprit. Disciplined, ordered debugging (overfit a batch → check shapes → check data → tune LR) finds these fast, whereas randomly changing the architecture rarely does. This methodical mindset is exactly what separates productive practitioners from frustrated ones.
