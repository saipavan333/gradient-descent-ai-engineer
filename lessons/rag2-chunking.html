Chunking strategies

⏱ 24 min Lesson 8.2 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsYou can only retrieve what your chunks contain, so how you split documents quietly decides answer quality. Chunk size, overlap, and structure-aware splitting are the first real tuning knobs of RAG.
By the end, you can
  • Explain why documents are chunked
  • Reason about the chunk-size tradeoff
  • Use overlap and metadata
  • Compare fixed, recursive, and semantic chunking

Concept Why you can't retrieve whole documents

RAG retrieves text to put in the prompt, but you can't retrieve and embed an entire 100-page document as one unit. First, it wouldn't fit in the prompt. Second — and more subtly — a single embedding (Lesson 6.2) for a huge document would be a blurry average of everything it covers, so it wouldn't match any specific question well. The solution is chunking: split each document into smaller pieces, embed and store each chunk separately, and retrieve only the chunks relevant to a query.

Chunking seems mundane, but it quietly determines RAG quality, because you can only retrieve what your chunks contain. The central tension is chunk size. Too small (a sentence) and a chunk lacks the surrounding context needed to be useful or to match a question. Too large (a whole section) and its embedding becomes a diluted average that matches everything weakly and nothing strongly, plus you waste prompt space on irrelevant text. There's no universal best size — it depends on your documents and queries, and it's a real hyperparameter to tune (Lesson 3.12).

Concept Overlap, structure, and metadata

Three refinements make chunking work in practice. Overlap: let adjacent chunks share some text (say the last sentence of one is the first of the next), so an idea that spans a boundary isn't split in a way that loses its meaning — both halves get enough context. Structure-aware splitting: cut at natural boundaries (paragraphs, sections, code functions) rather than blindly mid-sentence. And metadata: attach each chunk's source, title, page, and section, so you can cite it, filter by it, and give the model context about where the text came from.

The strategies form a spectrum from blunt to smart. Fixed-size chunking just cuts every $N$ characters/tokens — trivial but ignores meaning. Recursive chunking tries to split on paragraphs first, then sentences, keeping chunks under a size limit while respecting structure — the sensible default for most text. Semantic chunking detects topic shifts (where consecutive sentences' embeddings diverge) and cuts there — the cleanest boundaries, at the cost of more computation. Most production systems use recursive splitting with overlap and good metadata.

Worked example Fixed-size chunking with overlap

Here's the simplest chunker — fixed size with overlap — so you can see exactly how the pieces (and their shared regions) are produced.

Each chunk is 40 characters, and the step is size − overlap = 30, so consecutive chunks share 10 characters — notice "ns from da" ends chunk 0 and "ns from data" begins chunk 1 (the overlap). Without overlap, the phrase "patterns from data" would be split across chunks 0 and 1 with no chunk containing it whole, and a query about "patterns from data" might match neither well; the overlap ensures the complete phrase lives in chunk 1. (The tiny final chunk 3, 'm.', shows a rough edge of naive fixed-size chunking — real chunkers avoid such fragments by splitting on sentence boundaries.) This character-level version is for illustration; in practice you chunk by tokens (Lesson 6.1) and split on natural boundaries, but the mechanics — fixed window, step by size minus overlap — are exactly these. The overlap parameter and chunk size are the two knobs you'll actually tune.

★ Key points to remember
  • Chunking splits documents into pieces so you can embed and retrieve only the relevant parts.
  • Chunk size is a key tradeoff: too small loses context, too large dilutes the embedding match.
  • Overlap keeps ideas that straddle a boundary from being lost.
  • Recursive splitting (on paragraphs/sentences) is the common default; semantic chunking is cleanest but costlier.
  • Attach metadata (source, title, section) for citation, filtering, and context.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You chunk a technical manual into single sentences. Retrieval finds sentences that mention the user's keyword, but the answers are unhelpful and lack context. What went wrong, and how do you fix it?
Show solution & reasoning

The chunks are too small. A lone sentence often can't answer a question — it might say "This is configured in the settings file" without the surrounding steps, or match a keyword while missing the actual procedure that spans several sentences. Tiny chunks also have impoverished embeddings that match shallowly.

Fix: increase the chunk size to capture coherent units (a full paragraph or procedure step), use recursive splitting so chunks respect structure, and add overlap so multi-sentence ideas aren't cut. You'd tune the size on a validation set of real questions (Lesson 8.7), watching whether retrieved chunks actually contain enough to answer. The principle: a chunk should be a self-contained, retrievable unit of meaning — big enough to answer, small enough to stay specific.

2Why is attaching metadata (source, title, section, date) to each chunk valuable in a RAG system? Give two concrete uses.
Show solution & reasoning

(1) Citations and trust: metadata lets you show the user where an answer came from ("from the 2024 HR Handbook, section 3.2"), which is essential for verification and adoption (Lesson 8.1). (2) Filtering: you can restrict retrieval by metadata — only the current version (date), only documents the user is authorized to see (access control), or only a specific product's docs — improving both relevance and security.

Other uses: telling the model context it can't infer from the chunk text alone ("this is from a legal contract"), deduplication, and analytics on which sources get used. Metadata turns a bag of text chunks into a queryable, governable knowledge base — and forgetting it is a common reason a RAG prototype can't graduate to production, where citation, freshness, and access control are non-negotiable.

3For chunking source code, why might fixed-size character chunking be especially bad, and what would you do instead?
Show solution & reasoning

Fixed-size character chunking would slice code mid-function, mid-line, even mid-token — splitting a function's signature from its body, or a comment from the code it describes. The resulting chunks are semantically broken: a chunk with half a function is useless for answering "how does function X work?", and the embedding of a fragment is meaningless. Code has strong structure that character offsets ignore entirely.

Instead, use structure-aware (recursive) splitting that respects code boundaries: chunk by function, class, or method, keeping each logical unit intact, and attach metadata (file path, function name, language). Many toolkits have language-aware splitters that parse the syntax. The general lesson — match the chunking to the document's natural structure — is strongest for highly structured content like code, tables, and markdown, where blind character cuts do the most damage. Always ask "what is the natural unit of meaning here?" and chunk along it.

★ Deep dive: advanced chunking and the small-to-big pattern▸

Chunking has grown into a surprisingly deep area because it's so consequential and so dependent on your data. A clever pattern that resolves the size tradeoff is small-to-big retrieval (also called parent-document retrieval): embed and search on small, specific chunks (so matching is precise), but when one is retrieved, return its larger parent chunk or surrounding window to the model (so it has full context). You get the best of both — specific matching and rich context — by decoupling the retrieval unit from the generation unit. A related idea is sentence-window retrieval: match on a single sentence but feed the model that sentence plus a few neighbors. These patterns acknowledge that the ideal granularity for finding text differs from the ideal granularity for using it.

Other advanced techniques target specific document types and failure modes. Hierarchical chunking builds a tree (document → sections → paragraphs) and can retrieve at multiple levels. Summary indexing stores an LLM-generated summary of each chunk or document to embed and search, then retrieves the full text — helpful when the raw text is verbose or jargon-heavy. For tables and structured data, you might convert rows to sentences or store them in a way that preserves relationships. And there's growing interest in letting the retrieval adapt — re-chunking or expanding context based on the query. The honest takeaway is that there's no universal chunking recipe; the right approach depends on your documents (prose vs code vs tables), your queries (factual lookups vs broad synthesis), and your model's context budget. The practical workflow is empirical, exactly like model selection (Track 3): start with recursive chunking plus overlap and good metadata, build an evaluation set of real question–answer pairs (Lesson 8.7), measure whether retrieval actually surfaces answer-bearing chunks, and iterate on chunk size, overlap, and strategy. Chunking is unglamorous, but because retrieval is the bottleneck of RAG (Lesson 8.1), getting it right is often the single highest-leverage improvement you can make — many "the model is dumb" complaints are really "the chunks don't contain the answer" problems in disguise.

Now that documents are in chunks, we need to find the relevant ones by meaning, not keywords. Next, in 7.3 Embeddings for retrieval, we turn each chunk into a vector and retrieve by semantic similarity.
