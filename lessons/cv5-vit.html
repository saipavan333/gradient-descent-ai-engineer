Track 5 · Computer Vision

Vision Transformers: attention for images

⏱ 40 min Lesson 5.5 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsThe transformer that revolutionized language now processes images by treating patches as tokens. Understanding ViT — and the inductive-bias trade-off that decides CNN vs ViT — is essential to modern vision and to seeing why one architecture now spans every field.
By the end, you can
  • Explain how a ViT splits an image into patch-tokens for a transformer
  • Define inductive bias and contrast a CNN's strong priors with a ViT's weak ones
  • Say when CNNs win (small data) and when ViTs win (scale/pretraining)
  • Explain why patch tokens need a positional embedding

Concept Treat an image as a sequence of patches

For a decade, convolutional neural networks (CNNs, Track 5 lesson 2) were the way to process images. Then the architecture that revolutionized language — the transformer (Track 6) — was pointed at images, and the Vision Transformer (ViT) was born. The core idea is disarmingly simple. A transformer operates on a sequence of tokens (in language, tokens are word-pieces). An image isn't a sequence — it's a 2-D grid of pixels. So ViT makes it into one: it chops the image into a grid of small square patches (say 16×16 pixels each), flattens each patch into a vector, and treats that vector as a token. A 224×224 image becomes a sequence of 196 patch-tokens — and now the exact same transformer machinery from language (self-attention, positional encodings, the whole stack) can process it. "An image is worth 16×16 words" was the title of the paper, and it's the whole idea in one line: patches are the words of an image.

Why is this powerful? Because self-attention lets every patch look at every other patch directly. A CNN sees only a small neighborhood at a time (its filters are local) and must stack many layers before distant parts of the image can influence each other. A ViT, from its very first layer, can relate the top-left patch to the bottom-right one in a single step — global receptive field immediately. This makes ViTs excellent at capturing long-range structure and, crucially, they scale beautifully: given enough data and compute, bigger ViTs keep getting better, following the same scaling story that made large language models so capable. The catch — which the next section makes precise — is that "given enough data" is doing a lot of work.

Concept Inductive bias: why CNNs win small, ViTs win big

The deep difference between a CNN and a ViT is their inductive bias — the assumptions about the world that are built into the architecture before it sees any data. A CNN bakes in two assumptions that happen to be true about images: locality (nearby pixels are related, so look at small neighborhoods) and translation invariance (a cat is a cat whether it's top-left or bottom-right, so slide the same filter everywhere). These are strong, correct priors — a huge head start. It means a CNN doesn't have to learn that images have local structure; it's assumed, so the CNN can learn from relatively modest amounts of data. A ViT assumes almost none of this. Self-attention treats patches as an unordered set (position is added, not built in) and lets any patch talk to any other from the start — so the ViT must learn the very structure of images (that nearby patches matter, that translation shouldn't change the answer) from the data itself.

This trade-off has a clean, memorable resolution. On small-to-medium datasets, CNNs usually win: their built-in assumptions are a gift when data is scarce, so they learn efficiently and generalize well without much data. On large datasets (or with large-scale pretraining), ViTs win: with enough data the ViT learns the right structure — and because it assumed less, it isn't limited by a human's guess about what matters, so it can surpass the CNN and keep improving as data grows. In practice this is why modern vision often uses ViTs pretrained on enormous datasets (then fine-tuned via transfer learning, Track 5 lesson 2) — the pretraining supplies the data the ViT needs to overcome its weak prior. The lesson is general: strong inductive bias helps when data is limited; weak inductive bias helps when data is abundant, because the model can learn a better solution than the one you'd have hard-coded.

Worked example Turning an image into patch-tokens

The one operation that makes a ViT possible is patchify: cut the image into a grid of small squares and flatten each into a vector. Here a tiny 4×4 image is split into four 2×2 patches — each becomes one token (one row):

The 4×4 image becomes four tokens, each a length-4 vector. Read the first row: the top-left 2×2 block is [[0,1],[4,5]], which flattens to [0, 1, 4, 5] — exactly the first token. The four patches are the four corners of the image, each collapsed into a vector. That's the entire bridge from "grid of pixels" to "sequence a transformer can read": each patch is one token, so an image of 16×16-pixel patches over a 224×224 image yields 196 tokens, and from there it is identical to processing a 196-word sentence. In a real ViT, two small things are added on top: each flattened patch is passed through a learned linear layer (a patch embedding) to give it the model's working dimension, and a positional embedding is added so the transformer knows where each patch came from (attention itself is order-blind, exactly as in Track 6). But the essential move — the thing that lets attention process pixels at all — is this patchify step. Once you see that a picture can be sliced into "words," the Vision Transformer stops being mysterious: it is the language transformer you already know, fed image patches instead of text tokens.

★ Key points to remember
  • A Vision Transformer (ViT) splits an image into fixed-size patches, flattens each into a token, and feeds the sequence to a standard transformer — "an image is worth 16×16 words."
  • Self-attention gives a global receptive field immediately: every patch can attend to every other from the first layer (a CNN needs many layers to see globally).
  • Inductive bias is the key difference: CNNs build in locality + translation invariance (strong, correct prior); ViTs build in little and must learn image structure from data.
  • CNNs win on small/medium data; ViTs win at scale (large datasets or large-scale pretraining) — strong prior helps when data is scarce, weak prior helps when data is abundant.
  • ViT adds a learned patch embedding and a positional embedding; otherwise it's the same transformer as in NLP.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A startup has 3,000 labeled X-ray images and wants a classifier. A colleague says "ViTs are state of the art, let's use a big ViT from scratch." What do you advise?
Show solution & reasoning

Training a big ViT from scratch on 3,000 images is likely to underperform badly, and the reason is inductive bias. A ViT builds in almost no assumptions about images, so it must learn that nearby pixels relate, that translation shouldn't change the diagnosis, and so on — and learning all that structure takes a lot of data. Three thousand images is nowhere near enough; the ViT will overfit or fail to learn the right structure, while a CNN — whose locality and translation-invariance priors are already correct for X-rays — would learn far more efficiently from the same small set. So the naive "ViTs are SOTA, use a big one" reasoning ignores that ViTs are SOTA at scale, precisely the regime this startup is not in. The better advice has two parts. First, if you want a CNN, a CNN pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned on the 3,000 X-rays is a strong, data-efficient baseline. Second — and this is the key nuance — you can still use a ViT, but not from scratch: take a ViT that was pretrained on a massive dataset and fine-tune it on your X-rays. The pretraining is what supplies the data the ViT needs to overcome its weak prior; fine-tuning then adapts it cheaply to your small set.

The reasoning skill is recognizing that "which architecture is best" is inseparable from how much data you have. ViTs beat CNNs when data (or pretraining) is abundant; CNNs beat ViTs when data is scarce and there's no pretraining. With only 3,000 images and no pretraining, favor the strong-prior model (CNN) — or, better, use transfer learning so that either architecture starts from features already learned on a large dataset, sidestepping the small -data problem entirely. This is the same transfer-learning wisdom from lesson 2, now sharpened by the inductive-bias lens: the less your architecture assumes, the more data (or pretraining) it demands. Being able to reason from dataset size to architecture choice — rather than chasing whatever is "state of the art" in the abstract — is exactly the practical judgment that separates someone who can ship a working medical classifier from someone who burns a month training a ViT that never converges.

2Why does a ViT need a positional embedding added to its patch tokens, when a CNN needs no such thing?
Show solution & reasoning

The answer comes straight from how each architecture handles space. A CNN processes the image with filters that slide over fixed spatial positions — spatial structure is built into the operation itself. The filter at the top-left literally sees the top-left pixels; position is implicit in where the convolution is applied, so a CNN never needs to be told where anything is. A transformer is different: self-attention treats its input as an unordered set of tokens — it computes relationships between tokens based on their content, with no inherent notion of which came first or where each sits. This is exactly the same property you met in Track 6, where attention over words is order-blind and needs positional encodings so the model can tell "dog bites man" from "man bites dog." For a ViT the identical issue arises in 2-D: without position information, the patch from the top-left and the patch from the bottom-right are just two vectors in a set, and the model has no way to know their spatial arrangement — yet arrangement is everything in an image (a face is eyes-above -nose-above-mouth, not those parts in any order). So a positional embedding is added to each patch token, encoding where it came from, restoring the spatial information that patchifying-into-a-set threw away.

The reasoning principle is that attention buys flexibility (any token can relate to any token) at the cost of losing built-in structure — including spatial order — which must then be reinjected explicitly. A CNN gets spatial structure for free from its sliding filters (that's part of its strong inductive bias); a ViT gives up that free structure in exchange for global, flexible attention, and pays it back with positional embeddings. Understanding this trade — that the same order-blindness of attention that required positional encodings for words requires positional embeddings for patches — shows you grasp why the ViT is assembled the way it is, not just its parts. It also reinforces the unifying theme of the modern era: the transformer is one architecture, and applying it to a new domain (vision) mostly means deciding how to turn that domain's data into tokens and how to tell the model where those tokens belong.

3Self-attention compares every patch with every other patch. If you halve the patch size (making patches smaller), what happens to the number of tokens and the compute cost — and why does this matter?
Show solution & reasoning

Smaller patches mean more patches, and attention cost grows quadratically with the number of tokens — so the cost explodes. Concretely: halving the patch size (say from 16×16 to 8×8) means each patch covers a quarter of the area, so you get 4× as many patches/tokens. Self-attention compares every token with every other, so its cost scales as the number of tokens squared; with 4× the tokens, attention cost rises by roughly 4² = 16×. So a seemingly modest change — smaller patches for finer detail — makes the model about sixteen times more expensive in its attention layers. This is the ViT's central scaling tension: finer patches capture more spatial detail (helpful for small objects or fine textures) but the quadratic cost of attention punishes you hard for having more tokens, in both memory and time. It's the same quadratic-attention bottleneck you met for long sequences in language (Track 6 / Track 7): the cost of "everything attends to everything" grows with the square of the sequence length, whether the sequence is words or image patches.

Why it matters practically: patch size is a knob that trades accuracy for compute, and you must set it deliberately. Large patches (16×16 or 32×32) keep the token count and cost manageable but blur fine detail; small patches see more detail but can become prohibitively expensive on high-resolution images. This tension is exactly why a large body of research designs more efficient vision transformers — hierarchical designs that start with small patches and merge them (e.g., Swin Transformer), windowed or local attention that avoids all-pairs comparison, and hybrids that use convolutions early (cheap, local) and attention later (global). The reasoning skill is connecting an architectural choice (patch size) to its computational consequence (quadratic blow-up in attention) and then to the engineering response (efficient-attention designs). Recognizing that "just use smaller patches for more detail" runs headlong into attention's quadratic cost — and that this single fact drives much of vision-transformer research — demonstrates the systems-level understanding that Track 11's efficiency mindset and Track 6's attention math were building toward.

★ Deep dive: convergence — CNNs and transformers are borrowing from each other▸

The cleanest way to hold the CNN-vs-ViT story is not as a war with a winner but as a convergence. When ViTs first appeared they looked like a clean replacement for CNNs, and on huge datasets they were remarkable. But the field quickly discovered that the two architectures' strengths are complementary — a CNN's locality prior is genuinely useful, and a ViT's global attention is genuinely useful — so the most effective modern designs combine them. Hierarchical vision transformers (like the Swin Transformer) reintroduce a CNN-like structure — local windows of attention and a pyramid that starts fine and coarsens — recovering locality and multi-scale processing while keeping attention's flexibility. Meanwhile, modern CNNs (like ConvNeXt) borrowed training tricks and design choices from transformers and closed much of the gap in the other direction, showing that a well-designed CNN remains extremely competitive. The honest summary for an interview: on massive data ViTs shine and scale best; on modest data CNNs (or pretrained backbones) are safer; and in practice the winning systems are often hybrids that blend convolutional locality with attention's global reach. There is no single "best" — there is a data regime, a compute budget, and a design that fits them.

Step back and the deeper pattern is the one this whole curriculum keeps circling: the transformer has become a near-universal architecture. It started in language (Track 6), it now processes images (this lesson), and it also handles audio, video, protein sequences, and more — because its core operation, attention over a set of tokens, is domain -agnostic. Applying it to a new field is mostly a matter of tokenizing that field's data (words, patches, audio frames, amino acids) and telling the model where the tokens belong (positional information). This is why understanding the transformer deeply pays off across every specialization: the vision-language models of the next lesson, the multimodal systems that fuse images and text, and the diffusion models that generate images all lean on transformer components. The inductive-bias lesson generalizes too — it's a fundamental principle of machine learning, far beyond vision: build in assumptions when data is scarce, and let the model learn from data when data is abundant. That single trade-off explains not just CNNs vs ViTs but much of why the deep-learning era, with its enormous datasets, has favored flexible, weakly-biased architectures that learn structure rather than assume it. Having seen the transformer conquer both language and vision, you now hold the through-line of modern AI — and the next lessons (generating images with diffusion, and joining vision with language) build directly on it.
