ML system design — Q&A

⏱ 26 min Lesson 13.5
Why this existsSystem design is the most decisive round: turn a vague prompt into a justified design while reasoning about scale, cost, and trade-offs out loud. The 2026 shift is that evaluation, cost/latency, and guardrails now weigh as much as the architecture.
By the end, you can
  • Apply the design framework
  • Design a recommender and a RAG assistant
  • Emphasize evaluation, cost, and guardrails
  • Handle scaling curveballs

Concept The round that most separates candidates

ML system design is often the most decisive interview round, because it can't be crammed — it tests whether you can take a vague, open-ended problem and drive it to a coherent, justified design while reasoning about scale, cost, and trade-offs out loud. There's no single right answer; the interviewer evaluates how you think. And the emphasis has shifted: for GenAI system design especially, interviewers now weight evaluation, cost, latency, and guardrails as heavily as the architecture diagram itself — so a candidate who only draws boxes and arrows underperforms one who reasons about how they'd measure, budget, and safeguard the system.

Model answer "How do you approach any system design question?"

"I use the same framework every time so an unfamiliar prompt doesn't throw me. First, clarify requirements — I ask questions to turn the vague prompt into concrete functional requirements (what it must do) and non-functional ones (scale, latency, cost), and I estimate the scale with back-of-the-envelope math (users, QPS, data size), because the scale drives the architecture. Second, high-level design — I sketch the major components and data flow before diving in. Third, deep-dive the ML — data, features, model choice, training, and — critically — evaluation: how I'd measure that it actually works. Fourth, scale it — serving, replicas, caching, sharding to meet the load, with explicit attention to cost and latency. Fifth, trade-offs and safety — the choices I made and their alternatives, plus guardrails, monitoring, and failure modes. I'm driving the conversation through this structure while staying responsive to the interviewer's steers, and reasoning out loud the whole time — because that reasoning is what's being evaluated. And I make sure to give evaluation, cost, and guardrails real airtime, not just the architecture, since that's what interviewers now weight most." Why it lands: a clear framework plus the modern emphasis on eval/cost/safety signals you know both how to structure a design and what today's interviewers prioritize.

Model answer "Design a recommendation feed." (condensed)

"After clarifying scale, latency budget, and what we optimize for, I'd use the two-stage funnel, because we can't score millions of items with a heavy model per request. Candidate generation: fast, cheap, high-recall retrieval narrows millions to a few hundred (embedding similarity via ANN for personalization, plus trending and subscriptions for freshness and cold-start). Ranking: a heavier model scores just those candidates with rich features from a feature store. Re-ranking: adjust for diversity and freshness so the top items aren't near-duplicates. Heavy work — training, embeddings, the ANN index — is done offline; the per-request path is light. I'd justify the funnel with cost math (ranking hundreds not millions is thousands of times cheaper). For evaluation, I'd stress that offline metrics are proxies, so I'd A/B test on real users, watching the primary metric and guardrail metrics — because optimizing clicks alone breeds clickbait and filter bubbles (Goodhart), so I optimize a balanced objective and watch for feedback loops. And I'd cover the operational side: monitor quality, detect drift, retrain. The theme: cheap-wide then expensive-narrow, offline/online split, evaluated honestly and safeguarded — not just an architecture." Why it lands: the funnel plus the explicit evaluation-and-safety reasoning hits exactly what modern recommender design questions reward.

Model answer "Design an LLM-powered assistant over a company's documents." (condensed)

"This is a RAG assistant at scale. Offline: ingest and chunk the docs, embed them, build a vector index — incrementally, since docs update. Online (per query): optional query rewriting → hybrid retrieval (semantic + keyword) → rerank to the best few chunks → assemble a prompt with the context, citations, and 'say I don't know if it's absent' → LLM → post-process. Then — and this is where I'd spend real time, reflecting what interviewers now weight — evaluation: a golden test set of question–answer pairs, retrieval metrics (recall@k) separate from generation metrics (faithfulness — is the answer grounded?), LLM-as-judge validated against humans, plus online feedback. Cost & latency: the retrieved chunks are input tokens, so k is a direct cost/latency knob; I'd rerank to keep k small, cache common and semantically-similar queries (huge for support-style repetition), and consider routing easy queries to a smaller model — with a cost budget per query. Guardrails: input/output filtering, PII redaction, and defense against prompt injection via retrieved content. Monitoring: track faithfulness, retrieval hit rate, hallucination rate, and cost per request; watch for corpus and query drift. The design isn't done at the architecture — it's the architecture plus how I evaluate, budget, safeguard, and monitor it." Why it lands: this is the canonical current GenAI design question, and leading the second half with evaluation, cost, guardrails, and monitoring is exactly the 2026 emphasis.

Model answer "Midway through, the interviewer says 'now assume 100× the traffic.' How do you respond?"

"I'd reason about what scales trivially versus what needs rework. Stateless serving scales horizontally — 100× the replicas behind a load balancer with autoscaling — so that's mostly a cost question, not an architecture change. The data stores are harder: a vector index or database that fit on one machine at 1× may need sharding across many at 100×, which introduces fan-out latency and coordination I'd design for (replication for fault tolerance, hedging for tail latency). Caching becomes even more valuable — more traffic means more repetition to absorb — so I'd invest in a strong caching hierarchy. I'd hunt for bottlenecks — any single point that doesn't scale (a shared database, a rate-limited external API) — and address it. And cost becomes dominant, so efficiency levers (batching, model compression, small-model routing) that were optional at 1× are now essential, and I'd revisit build-vs-buy for any external model API at that volume." Why it lands: calmly separating what scales for free (stateless replicas) from what needs redesign (sharding stateful stores) and what becomes critical (caching, cost, bottlenecks) shows exactly the adaptive, scale-aware thinking these curveballs test.

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • First move? Clarify requirements (functional + non-functional) and estimate scale — never jump to a solution.
  • Recommender/search? Two-stage funnel: cheap candidate generation (recall) → expensive ranking (precision).
  • Recurring pattern? Offline (build indexes/models) + light online (per-request) — reused across recsys, search, RAG.
  • RAG assistant? Offline index; online retrieve → rerank → generate with citations; then eval, cost, guardrails, monitoring.
  • Scale a service? Replicas + load balancer + autoscaling; cache, batch, compress to cut cost; shard stateful stores.
  • Latency SLA? Percentiles (p95/p99), not averages — the tail is what users feel.
  • 2026 emphasis? Evaluation, cost/latency, and guardrails weigh as much as the architecture — reason about all of them.
  • Best design principle? Simplest system that meets the requirements; add complexity only where a requirement forces it.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"Design a system to detect toxic comments on a social platform, at scale." (Full answer.)
Show a strong answer

"Clarify: what counts as toxic (hate, harassment, spam), volume (comments/day → QPS), latency (real-time block before posting, or async review?), and the cost of false positives (censoring legit speech) vs false negatives (letting toxicity through). Architecture: a classifier (likely a fine-tuned transformer) scoring each comment; for real-time, a fast model inline; possibly a cheap first-pass filter escalating uncertain cases to a heavier model or human review (a funnel). Data & model: labeled toxic/non-toxic data (with the labeling challenge that 'toxic' is subjective and context-dependent), handling multiple languages, and continual labeling as norms shift. Evaluation — I'd emphasize this: precision and recall on the toxic class (not accuracy — toxicity is rare), the false-positive rate specifically (over-censoring is a real harm), performance across languages and communities (fairness — the model shouldn't be biased against a dialect or group), and human review of a sample. Serving & scale: horizontal scaling for the comment firehose, caching isn't very applicable (each comment is unique), so model efficiency matters. Guardrails & safety: the tricky trade-off between blocking harm and over-censoring — I'd use graduated responses (flag/review vs hard-block), transparency, and appeals. Monitoring: drift (new slang, evasion tactics — an adversarial cat-and-mouse), the false-positive rate, and community feedback. The theme: this is as much an evaluation, fairness, and safety problem as a modeling one." This lands because it treats toxicity detection as the sociotechnical, evaluation-heavy problem it is — leading with the precision/recall/fairness/false-positive concerns and the censor-vs-allow trade-off, not just 'train a classifier.'

2"What distinguishes a great system design answer from a mediocre one?"
Show a strong answer

"A great answer is driven by requirements and honest about trade-offs; a mediocre one is a pile of components with no justification. Concretely, a great answer: starts from requirements and sizes itself to the actual scale, so it's neither over- nor under-engineered; makes every component traceable to a requirement it satisfies (nothing is there because it's trendy); reasons explicitly about trade-offs (accuracy vs latency vs cost, freshness vs cost, simplicity vs scale) and picks the side the requirements favor; gives real airtime to evaluation, cost, and safety, not just the architecture; and plans for the full lifecycle — monitoring, drift, failure modes — not just launch. A mediocre answer skips clarifying requirements, over-engineers for scale it doesn't have (or ignores scale it does), can't justify its choices, and stops at boxes and arrows. The single deepest marker is simplicity proportional to the requirements: the great designer reaches for the simplest architecture that meets the needs and adds complexity (sharding, agents, custom serving) only where a requirement forces it, because every added component is added cost, latency, and failure surface. And they drive the conversation while staying responsive, reasoning out loud so their judgment is visible. Ultimately it's about demonstrating engineering judgment — that you understand there's no free lunch, only choices suited (or not) to the requirements — which is exactly what the round is designed to reveal." This stands out because it articulates a coherent design philosophy (requirements-driven, trade-off-honest, evaluation-aware, biased toward simplicity) that generalizes beyond any single problem — the mark of someone who has genuinely internalized system design.

3"You're asked to design something you have no domain experience with (say, a fraud system, and you've never built one). How do you handle it?"
Show a strong answer

"I'd lean on the framework and transferable patterns, because system design is the same process applied to new specifics — I don't need prior fraud experience to reason well about it. I'd start by clarifying (what kind of fraud, volume, real-time vs batch, cost of each error), which both gathers the domain specifics I lack and shows I don't bluff. Then I'd map it to patterns I know: fraud detection is a classification problem on imbalanced data (fraud is rare), so precision/recall over accuracy, with recall often prioritized but the false-positive cost (blocking legit transactions) also high — a threshold trade-off. It likely needs real-time scoring (a latency budget, online serving) and rich features (a feature store, with the train/serve skew risk). It's adversarial (fraudsters adapt), so drift and continual retraining matter, and it's high-stakes, so evaluation and monitoring are central. I'd state my assumptions openly ('I'll assume real-time card transactions; correct me if it's something else') and reason from first principles, inviting the interviewer's domain knowledge. The key is that I'm honest about what I'd need to learn while demonstrating that the underlying skills — requirements, imbalanced-data evaluation, real-time serving, adversarial drift, trade-offs — transfer directly. Confident bluffing about a domain I don't know would be a red flag; reasoning soundly from transferable principles while clarifying the specifics is exactly what handling an unfamiliar problem well looks like." This lands because it shows the maturity to handle ambiguity — using the framework and pattern-recognition to reason about a novel domain, stating assumptions, and being honest about gaps — which is precisely what interviewers test by asking about unfamiliar systems.

★ Deep dive: system design as the synthesis of everything▸

ML system design is the interview round where everything converges, which is exactly why it's so revealing. A single design question can touch classical ML (choosing and evaluating a model), deep learning and LLMs (the model itself), RAG (retrieval), agents (tool use), MLOps (serving, monitoring, drift, retraining), scaling (replicas, caching, sharding), and evaluation and safety (metrics, fairness, guardrails) — all assembled under real requirements. This is why it's the hardest round to fake and the one that most separates candidates: you can memorize answers to conceptual questions, but you can't memorize your way through designing a novel system in real time — you have to actually understand how the pieces fit and reason about trade-offs on the fly. The strongest candidates treat every prompt as the same process (clarify → architect → deep-dive → scale → evaluate/safeguard → trade-offs) applied to new specifics, and they recognize the recurring patterns (the two-stage funnel, the offline/online split, retrieve-then-generate) that give most large-scale AI systems a familiar skeleton — so an unfamiliar prompt becomes an adaptation, not a blank page.

The current emphasis makes this even more integrative. The shift toward weighting evaluation, cost, latency, and guardrails as heavily as the architecture means a strong answer must span the full engineering reality: not just 'here's the RAG pipeline' but 'here's how I'd measure its faithfulness, budget its token cost, keep its latency in the SLA, guard it against prompt injection, and monitor it for drift.' That's the difference between someone who can sketch an architecture and someone who can actually ship and operate a system — which is what the round is really probing. For your preparation, the implication is that system design is best learned by practicing the whole flow out loud on many prompts (recsys, search, RAG, LLM platforms, classifiers) until the framework and patterns are automatic, and by always pushing your answers past the architecture into evaluation, cost, and safety. And it rewards genuine breadth: because a design question can pull from any part of the field, the candidates who excel are those who've built real understanding across the whole stack — the fundamentals, the models, the LLM tooling, the operational discipline, and the evaluation-and-safety mindset — and can compose them fluently under the pressure of an open-ended problem. That synthesis, more than any single fact, is what the system design round is designed to find, and it's the culmination of everything this curriculum has taught — which is why it's the centerpiece of modern AI/ML interviews.
