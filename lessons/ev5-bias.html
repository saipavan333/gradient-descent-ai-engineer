Bias, fairness & toxicity

⏱ 25 min Lesson 11.5 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsModels learn and amplify human biases from data. Bias enters at every stage — data, labels, model, feedback. Measure fairness by comparing outcome rates across groups (the four-fifths rule); removing a protected attribute doesn't remove bias (proxies).
By the end, you can
  • Identify where bias enters the pipeline
  • Measure disparate impact (four-fifths rule)
  • Explain proxy features
  • Know fairness definitions conflict

Concept Models learn our biases — and can amplify them

An AI model learns patterns from data, and if that data reflects human biases — historical discrimination, skewed representation, prejudiced labels — the model learns those biases too, and can even amplify them. This isn't hypothetical: hiring models have penalized women's résumés, facial recognition has worked far worse for darker skin, and language models reproduce stereotypes present in their training text. Bias in AI means systematic, unfair differences in how a model treats different groups of people. Because models are deployed at scale and carry an aura of objectivity ("the algorithm decided"), biased AI can entrench discrimination more broadly and less visibly than an individual human would — which is why measuring and mitigating bias is a core responsibility, not an optional extra.

A key insight is that bias is not a single bug you can patch — it enters at multiple stages. The data may be unrepresentative (too few examples of some group) or encode historical bias (past hiring favored one group, so the data teaches the model to as well). The labels may reflect annotator prejudice. The model can amplify subtle patterns. And feedback loops in deployment (Lesson 9.8) can entrench bias over time. So addressing bias requires examining the whole pipeline, not just blaming "the algorithm."

Concept Measuring fairness

To manage bias you must measure it, which means defining fairness — but fairness has multiple, sometimes conflicting mathematical definitions. Two common ones: demographic parity asks whether the positive-outcome rate is equal across groups (e.g., loan approval rates are the same for all groups); equal opportunity asks whether the model is equally good for those who deserve the positive outcome (equal recall across groups). A widely-used practical check is the four-fifths rule (or 80% rule): if one group's selection rate is less than 80% of the highest group's rate, that's a legal/practical red flag for disparate impact — a policy that disproportionately harms a protected group even without explicit intent to discriminate.

Worked example Detecting disparate impact

Fairness starts with comparing outcome rates across groups. Here a model's loan-approval decisions are broken down by group, and we check each group's rate against the best group's using the four-fifths rule:

groups = {"A": (600, 1000), "B": (420, 1000)}   # (approved, total) per group
rates = {g: a / t for g, (a, t) in groups.items()}
best = max(rates.values())
for g, r in rates.items():
    ratio = r / best
    flag = "OK" if ratio >= 0.80 else "DISPARATE IMPACT"
    print(f"group {g}: rate {r:.0%}  ratio {ratio:.2f}  [{flag}]")
Outputgroup A: rate 60% ratio 1.00 [OK] group B: rate 42% ratio 0.70 [DISPARATE IMPACT]

Group A is approved 60% of the time, Group B only 42%. Dividing each group's rate by the highest (A's 60%), Group B's ratio is 0.70 — below the 0.80 four-fifths threshold — so the model is flagged for disparate impact against Group B. This simple calculation is how fairness becomes concrete and actionable: instead of vaguely worrying "is this model fair?", you measure the outcome rate for each group and compare, and a ratio under 0.80 is a well-established red flag that demands investigation. Notice what the metric does and doesn't tell you. It reveals a disparity worth investigating — the model approves one group far less often — but it doesn't by itself prove illegal discrimination or tell you the cause (the disparity might reflect a real difference in the data, or a biased feature, or historical bias baked into the labels). It's a detector, not a verdict. And critically, which fairness definition you use matters: demographic parity (equal approval rates) and equal opportunity (equal accuracy for the deserving) can conflict, and you sometimes cannot satisfy all fairness criteria at once — a proven impossibility result. So measuring fairness is the necessary first step (you can't fix what you don't measure), but choosing the right fairness definition for the context, and then diagnosing and mitigating a flagged disparity, is the harder work this detector kicks off. The skill is knowing that fairness must be quantified per-group to be managed, and that the metric is where the analysis begins, not ends.

★ Key points to remember
  • Models learn biases from data and can amplify them — deployed at scale with false objectivity, biased AI entrenches discrimination.
  • Bias enters at every stage: data (unrepresentative/historical), labels (annotator bias), model (amplification), feedback loops — not one patchable bug.
  • Fairness has multiple, conflicting definitions: demographic parity (equal outcome rates) vs equal opportunity (equal recall for the deserving).
  • The four-fifths rule: a group's rate under 80% of the best group's flags disparate impact.
  • Fairness metrics detect disparities to investigate — they're a starting point, not a verdict, and you can't satisfy all definitions at once.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A hiring model shows disparate impact against women, even though gender was never a feature. How is that possible, and how would you address it?
Show solution & reasoning

This is the crucial insight that removing the protected attribute doesn't remove the bias, because of proxy features — other features that correlate with gender and let the model discriminate indirectly. Even without "gender" as an input, the model can pick up gender-correlated signals: attendance at a women's college, membership in women's organizations, gaps in employment history (correlated with parental leave), certain hobbies, or even patterns in word choice on a résumé. The model learns from historical hiring data that favored men (the labels encode past bias), so it finds and exploits whatever proxies predict the historically-favored outcome — reproducing the discrimination through the back door. "Fairness through unawareness" (just deleting the sensitive attribute) is therefore insufficient and a common naive mistake.

Addressing it requires more than dropping a column. First, measure the disparate impact (as in the worked example) — you can only manage what you measure, and you may need the protected attribute available for auditing even if it's not used for prediction. Then diagnose the proxies: find which features drive the disparity (feature-importance analysis by group). Then mitigate with techniques like re-weighting or re-sampling the training data to correct historical imbalance, removing or transforming strong proxy features, adding fairness constraints to the training objective, or post-processing predictions to equalize outcomes — each with trade-offs (often some accuracy cost, and a choice of which fairness definition to enforce). Critically, you'd also question the labels: if the historical hiring decisions were themselves biased, the model is learning to replicate injustice, so the real fix may be rethinking what "good hire" means rather than just tweaking the model. The reasoning skill is understanding that bias lives in correlations and history, not just in an explicit attribute, so genuine fairness work means measuring outcomes, hunting proxies, correcting the data/labels, and accepting trade-offs — not the false comfort of "we didn't use gender."

2Two fairness definitions — equal approval rates (demographic parity) and equal accuracy for qualified applicants (equal opportunity) — conflict for your model. How do you decide which to prioritize?
Show solution & reasoning

You decide based on the context, the harm, and the values at stake — because this is fundamentally an ethical and policy choice, not a purely technical one, and the math can't make it for you. Key questions: What's the cost of each type of error, for whom? In lending, denying a qualified applicant (a false negative) and approving an unqualified one (a false positive) have different costs to the individual and the institution, and those costs may fall differently across groups. Is there historical injustice to correct? If a group was systematically denied opportunity, demographic parity (equalizing outcomes) may be the appropriate corrective; if the concern is purely that the model be equally accurate, equal opportunity fits. What do law and domain norms require? Some domains have legal standards (disparate impact doctrine) that point toward particular definitions. Who is affected and what do they consider fair? — ideally, stakeholders including the affected groups have input.

The deeper reasoning is that the impossibility of satisfying all fairness definitions simultaneously forces an explicit value judgment about what fairness means here, and the responsible move is to make that choice deliberately and transparently rather than letting it happen by default. You pick the definition that best matches the ethical goal in this context, document why, measure against it, and accept the trade-offs (including that you're not satisfying the other definitions, and possibly some accuracy cost). What you must not do is pretend the choice is neutral or purely technical — that's how consequential value decisions get made implicitly and unaccountably by whoever set the default. This is where AI engineering becomes genuinely interdisciplinary: the technical fairness metrics are tools, but choosing among them is an ethical, legal, and social decision that engineers must engage with thoughtfully and in collaboration with domain experts, ethicists, and affected communities — not resolve alone with a formula. Recognizing that the conflict forces a values choice, and that the choice should be explicit, contextual, and accountable, is the mark of mature thinking about AI fairness.

3Beyond fairness, generative models can produce toxic or harmful content. How is evaluating and controlling toxicity different from measuring classification fairness?
Show solution & reasoning

Toxicity — harmful, hateful, offensive, or dangerous generated content — is a different kind of problem from classification fairness, though both are safety concerns. Fairness is about systematic differences in outcomes across groups, measured with statistics over many decisions (approval rates by group). Toxicity is about the content of individual outputs — this specific response is hateful, or gives dangerous instructions, or demeans someone — which is evaluated per-output, often with a classifier or human/LLM judge rating harmfulness, and controlled at generation time. So the evaluation differs: fairness needs group-level outcome statistics and protected- attribute data; toxicity needs per-output harm detection (a toxicity classifier, red-teaming to find harmful outputs, human review of samples) and content policies defining what "harmful" means.

The control methods differ too. Fairness is mitigated by fixing data, features, or the training objective; toxicity is mitigated by safety training (RLHF to make the model refuse or avoid harmful content — Track 6), guardrails that filter inputs and outputs (the next lesson), and red-teaming to proactively find failures. They also share deep challenges: both require defining the harm (what counts as "toxic" or "unfair" is contested and context-dependent — a medical discussion of self-harm differs from encouragement of it), both face the difficulty that harm is often subtle and subjective, and both can conflict with other goals (over- aggressive toxicity filtering makes a model refuse legitimate requests; fairness constraints can cost accuracy). The connecting theme is that responsible AI means evaluating and controlling for harms, not just for quality or accuracy — and different harms (unfair outcomes, toxic content, privacy violations, security failures) need different, specialized evaluation and mitigation, all beyond the accuracy metrics of earlier tracks. Recognizing that "is it good?" (quality) and "is it safe/fair?" (responsibility) are distinct evaluation axes requiring distinct tools — group statistics for fairness, per-output detection and safety training for toxicity — is the framing that organizes the safety half of this track, and it motivates the guardrails and governance lessons that follow.

★ Deep dive: the sociotechnical nature of bias and the limits of technical fixes▸

The most important lesson about AI bias is that it's a sociotechnical problem — it lives at the intersection of technology and society, and cannot be fully solved by technical means alone. The bias in a model usually originates in the world: historical data reflects real historical discrimination, so a model trained to predict "who was hired/promoted/approved" faithfully learns a biased world and reproduces it. You can apply technical mitigations — re-weighting data, fairness constraints, proxy removal, post-processing — and you should, but they're treating a symptom whose cause is societal, and they involve genuine trade-offs (fairness definitions conflict, and enforcing fairness often costs some accuracy). More fundamentally, the technical fixes can't answer the questions that matter most: what should fairness mean here? whose values decide? is the historical label even a legitimate target? A model that perfectly predicts biased historical decisions is "accurate" and "unbiased" by some technical measures while perpetuating injustice — which reveals that the choice of what to predict, and on what data, is itself where much of the ethics lives, upstream of any algorithm.

This is why responsible AI has become an interdisciplinary field, and why engineers can't treat bias as just another metric to optimize. Genuine progress requires diverse teams (people who will notice biases others miss), collaboration with domain experts, ethicists, social scientists, and affected communities, careful thought about whether an AI system should be built for a task at all, and ongoing auditing and accountability rather than a one-time fairness check. It also requires humility about the limits of measurement: fairness metrics detect some disparities but can miss others, "harm" resists complete formalization, and gaming a fairness metric is as possible as gaming any other (Goodhart, Lesson 11.1). The practical stance for an ML engineer is to take bias seriously as a first-class concern — measure it rigorously, hunt for proxies, correct data and labels where possible, choose fairness definitions deliberately and transparently, and accept trade-offs — while recognizing that you're one part of a broader effort that includes non-technical judgment and governance. The engineers who handle this well are those who pair technical fairness skills with the wisdom to know when a problem is beyond a technical fix, to bring in the right people, and to question whether the system should exist. That combination — technical rigor plus ethical and social awareness — is what "responsible AI" actually demands, and it's the disposition the rest of this track is trying to instill, turning next to the concrete defenses of guardrails and red-teaming.
