Derivatives & gradients: slope as a signal to improve

⏱ 27 min Lesson 1.6 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsA model learns by answering one question millions of times: if I nudge this number a little, does my error go up or down? The derivative is that answer, and the gradient is it for every knob at once.
By the end, you can
  • Define the derivative as a rate of change / tangent slope
  • Read it as 'output change per unit input change'
  • Assemble partial derivatives into the gradient vector
  • Explain why the gradient points in the direction of steepest increase

Concept The one question a model asks over and over

Everything so far has been about representing data. Now we start improving a model, and "improving" comes down to one question the model asks millions of times during training: "If I nudge this number a little, does my error get bigger or smaller — and by how much?" Answer that for every adjustable number, and you know exactly how to make the model better. The mathematical tool that answers it is the derivative.

Let's build the idea from scratch. A function is just a rule that takes a number in and gives a number out (for example, "square it"). The derivative of a function at some point is its rate of change there: how fast the output moves when you wiggle the input a tiny bit. If you've ever heard "slope," that's the same thing — the slope measures steepness, how much you rise for each step you take sideways. Picture the function as a curve; at any point you can lay a straight line that just touches the curve there without crossing it — the tangent line — and the derivative is that tangent line's slope (its steepness).

We write the derivative as $f'(x)$ ("f-prime of x") or $\dfrac{df}{dx}$ ("d-f-d-x"). A big slope means the output is very sensitive to the input (steep); a slope of zero means you're on a flat spot — a peak, a valley, or a plateau. As a concrete example, the function $f(x) = x^2$ (square the input) has derivative $f'(x) = 2x$ — so at $x = 3$ its slope is $2 \times 3 = 6$. That tells you: near $x=3$, nudging $x$ upward increases $f$ about 6 times as fast as the nudge.

Concept Zoom in far enough and everything looks like a straight line

Here's the intuition that makes derivatives useful rather than just formal. Take any smooth curve and zoom in on one point until it fills your screen — it looks like a straight line, and that line's slope is the derivative. This gives the single most practical fact about derivatives, the local linear approximation. Writing $\Delta$ (the Greek letter "delta") to mean "a small change in," it says:

$$ \Delta y \;\approx\; f'(x)\,\Delta x. $$

In words: "the change in the output ($\Delta y$) is approximately the slope ($f'(x)$) times the change you made to the input ($\Delta x$)." ($\approx$ means "approximately," because this is exact only for tiny nudges.)

That is precisely the lever a learning algorithm pulls: it knows the slope, so it knows which way and how far to nudge each number to reduce error. You only need a couple of rules to start computing derivatives. The most common is the power rule: the derivative of $x^n$ (x raised to the power $n$) is $n\,x^{n-1}$ — bring the exponent down in front and lower it by one. So $x^2 \to 2x$, and $x^3 \to 3x^2$. Two helpers: "the derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives" (handle each piece separately), and "a constant — a fixed number with no $x$ — has slope $0$" (a flat line doesn't change).

Concept Many knobs at once: partial derivatives and the gradient

A real model has not one input but thousands or billions of adjustable numbers (its parameters). So we need the derivative with respect to each one. A partial derivative, written $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ (the rounded $\partial$ is just "d" for "partial," signaling there are other variables held still), is the derivative with respect to one variable while keeping all the others frozen — "if I wiggle only $x$ and touch nothing else, how does $f$ respond?"

Stack all the partial derivatives into a single vector and you get the gradient, written $\nabla f$ (the upside-down triangle $\nabla$ is read "grad" or "del"):

$$ \nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\ \ldots\right). $$

The gradient is simply "the list of slopes, one per knob."

Concept The gradient points uphill — so step the other way

The gradient isn't just a bag of slopes; as a vector it has a direction, and that direction is special: the gradient points in the direction of steepest increase of the function. Walk along $\nabla f$ and the output rises as fast as possible; walk along $-\nabla f$ (the opposite direction) and it drops as fast as possible. (The diagram below uses a contour map — like a hiking map, where each ring connects points of equal height.)

★
This is the whole idea of training

A model's error is a function of its parameters. Compute the gradient of that error, then nudge every parameter a little in the $-\nabla f$ direction, and the error goes down. Repeat. That loop — gradient descent — is Lesson 1.8, and it's how essentially every model you've heard of is trained. You are one lesson of plumbing (the chain rule) away from it.

Worked example Computing gradients three ways

Take the simple, error-shaped function $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ (a bowl with its lowest point at the origin). Its gradient is $\nabla f = (2x, 2y)$ (apply the power rule to each variable separately). Let's confirm that by hand, then numerically, then with PyTorch's automatic differentiation — and watch a downhill step lower the value.

import numpy as np

def f(x, y):
    return x**2 + y**2

# 1) Estimate the gradient at (1, 2) by literally nudging each input a tiny bit
h = 1e-5                                       # a tiny nudge
dfdx = (f(1+h, 2) - f(1-h, 2)) / (2*h)         # change in f per nudge in x
dfdy = (f(1, 2+h) - f(1, 2-h)) / (2*h)         # change in f per nudge in y
print("numerical gradient:", round(dfdx, 3), round(dfdy, 3))   # 2.0 4.0

# 2) Analytic gradient from the power rule: (2x, 2y) = (2*1, 2*2) = (2, 4) -- matches!

# 3) A downhill step lowers f
g = np.array([2.0, 4.0]); p = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
lr = 0.1
p_new = p - lr * g                             # step opposite the gradient
print("f before:", f(*p), " f after:", round(f(*p_new), 3))    # 5.0 -> 3.2
Outputnumerical gradient: 2.0 4.0 f before: 5.0 f after: 3.2

The "nudge it and measure" estimate $(2.0, 4.0)$ matches the formula $(2x, 2y) = (2, 4)$ exactly, and stepping opposite that gradient dropped $f$ from $5.0$ to $3.2$ — downhill, as promised. Now the magic that makes deep learning possible: PyTorch computes exact gradients automatically, so you never differentiate by hand:

import torch
x = torch.tensor(1.0, requires_grad=True)      # "track operations on x so I can get its gradient"
y = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True)
out = x**2 + y**2
out.backward()                # walk the computation backward and fill in the gradients
print(x.grad, y.grad)         # tensor(2.)  tensor(4.)
Outputtensor(2.) tensor(4.)

You never wrote the derivative — backward() figured out $(2, 4)$ on its own. That feature, automatic differentiation (or "autograd"), is how frameworks get gradients for networks with millions of parameters. The engine that makes it work is the chain rule — the very next lesson. (TensorFlow does the same with tf.GradientTape().)

★ Key points to remember
  • The derivative $f'(x)$ is the rate of change / slope of the tangent — "how the output moves when you nudge the input."
  • Locally, $\Delta y \approx f'(x)\,\Delta x$: the derivative turns an input nudge into a predicted output change.
  • A partial derivative wiggles one variable with the rest frozen; the gradient $\nabla f$ stacks them all into one vector.
  • The gradient points in the direction of steepest increase; $-\nabla f$ is steepest decrease.
  • Autograd (PyTorch's backward()) computes exact gradients automatically — the basis of training.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Find the derivative of $f(x) = 3x^2 + 5x - 7$, then evaluate it at $x = 2$. What does that number mean in plain words?
Show solution & reasoning

Differentiate term by term: $\frac{d}{dx}(3x^2) = 3 \cdot 2x = 6x$; $\frac{d}{dx}(5x) = 5$; $\frac{d}{dx}(-7) = 0$ (a constant has no slope). So $f'(x) = 6x + 5$. At $x=2$: $f'(2) = 12 + 5 = 17$.

In plain words: at $x = 2$ the function is rising steeply — for a tiny increase in $x$, $f$ increases about $17$ times as much. The $-7$ vanished because shifting a function up or down doesn't change its steepness.

2At a minimum of a smooth function, what is the value of the derivative (in 1-D) or the gradient (in many dimensions)? Why does that fact matter for training?
Show solution & reasoning

At a minimum the derivative is $0$ (the gradient is the zero vector): the tangent is flat, so a tiny nudge doesn't change the output.

Why it matters: gradient descent moves by $-\nabla f$, so when the gradient reaches (near) zero, the steps shrink to nothing and training naturally stops moving — it has found a flat spot, ideally the lowest loss. "Gradient ≈ 0" is the signal that learning has converged. (Subtlety for later: a zero gradient also happens at maxima and saddle points, which is why optimization has more to say in Lesson 1.8.)

3You estimate a derivative by the "nudge it" formula $\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ and try to improve accuracy by shrinking $h$ toward $0$. Why can making $h$ too small actually make the answer worse on a computer?
Show solution & reasoning

Mathematically the estimate improves as $h \to 0$. But computers store numbers with limited precision. When $h$ is tiny, $f(x+h)$ and $f(x)$ are almost identical, and subtracting two nearly-equal numbers throws away most of the meaningful digits (this is called "catastrophic cancellation"). Then dividing that noisy near-zero difference by a tiny $h$ blows the error up.

The reasoning: there's a sweet spot for $h$ (often around $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-8}$), below which round-off dominates. This is exactly why frameworks use automatic differentiation instead of the nudge formula — autograd computes the exact derivative through the chain rule, with no $h$ to tune and no cancellation. It's both faster and more accurate, which is the subject of the next lesson.

★ Deep dive: where the derivative comes from, and the rules worth knowing▸

The derivative is defined as a limit — the slope of the line through two points on the curve as those points slide together until they're (almost) the same point: $$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}. $$ That fraction is "rise over run" for a step of size $h$ (the symbol $\lim_{h\to 0}$ means "the value this approaches as $h$ shrinks to zero"); shrinking $h$ turns the line through two points into the tangent line at one point. Our "nudge it" estimate above is just this formula with a small-but-nonzero $h$ — which is why it's close but not exact.

A handful of rules covers most of what you'll meet in ML:

FunctionIts derivativeNote
$x^n$$n\,x^{n-1}$the power rule (the workhorse)
$c$ (a constant)$0$flat line, no slope
$e^x$$e^x$the exponential — its own derivative
$\ln x$$1/x$the natural logarithm — appears in cross-entropy loss
$\sigma(x)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$$\sigma(x)\,(1-\sigma(x))$the sigmoid; you'll use this in logistic regression

Plus three combination rules: the derivative of a sum is the sum of derivatives; a constant multiple pulls straight out ($\frac{d}{dx}(cf) = c\,f'$); and for compositions — a function inside a function, like $f(g(x))$ — you need the chain rule, which is so central to deep learning that it gets its own lesson next. Master the chain rule and you can differentiate an entire neural network, which is exactly what backward() does for you.
