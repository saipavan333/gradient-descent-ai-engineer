Capstone 1: image classifier (transfer learning)

⏱ 90 min Lesson 13.2 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsBuild a custom image classifier with transfer learning: reuse a pretrained backbone (frozen) and train only a small new head on your labels — strong accuracy from little data. The default approach for real image tasks.
By the end, you can
  • Explain transfer learning
  • Freeze a backbone, train a head
  • Plan the full image-classifier project
  • Reason about fine-tuning vs freezing

Concept The project: a custom image classifier from little data

The first capstone is a classic, high-impact project: build an image classifier for a custom task (say, classifying types of flowers, or defective vs good parts, or your own categories) using transfer learning. It's an ideal first project because it's genuinely useful, achievable, and demonstrates a technique used everywhere in practice — and it produces a working model you can show. The magic that makes it achievable is transfer learning: instead of training a deep network from scratch (which needs millions of images and huge compute), you reuse a model already trained on a massive dataset and adapt it to your task with a small amount of your own data.

How transfer learning works: a network pretrained on a huge image dataset has learned, in its early and middle layers, general visual features — edges, textures, shapes, object parts — that are useful for almost any image task. So you take that pretrained network as a backbone (feature extractor), freeze it (don't update those learned features), and attach a small new head (a few new layers) that you train on your labels. Only the head learns your specific classes; the backbone provides rich features for free. The result is strong accuracy from a few hundred or thousand images instead of millions — because the expensive part (learning to see) was already done. This is the single most practical deep-learning technique for real projects, and it's why you rarely train from scratch.

Worked example The transfer-learning principle: train only the head

The core idea — frozen features feeding a small trained head — is easy to demonstrate. Here the "backbone" has already produced features for each example (fixed), and we train only a small linear head (via gradient descent) on our labels. (In the real project the backbone is a pretrained CNN and the head a few layers, but the principle is identical.)

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
X = rng.standard_normal((200, 4))                 # features from a FROZEN backbone
true_w = np.array([1.5, -2.0, 0.5, 1.0])
y = (X @ true_w + rng.standard_normal(200)*0.1 > 0).astype(int)   # our labels

w = np.zeros(4)                                    # train ONLY a small linear head
for _ in range(3000):
    p = 1 / (1 + np.exp(-(X @ w)))                 # logistic head
    w -= 0.5 * (X.T @ (p - y) / len(y))            # gradient descent on the head only
acc = ((X @ w > 0).astype(int) == y).mean()
print(f"trained head on frozen features -> accuracy: {acc:.2f}")
Outputtrained head on frozen features -> accuracy: 0.99

Training only the small head on the frozen backbone's features reaches 0.99 accuracy — the head learned to map the pretrained features to our classes, without touching the expensive-to-learn features themselves. That's transfer learning in miniature: the backbone (here, fixed features; in the real project, a pretrained CNN) does the hard work of representing the input, and a tiny trainable head adapts that representation to your specific task, so you get strong results from little data and little compute. In the actual project you'd use a real pretrained vision model — the standard workflow is: load a pretrained backbone (e.g., a ResNet or a vision transformer from a model library), replace its final classification layer with a new head sized to your number of classes, freeze the backbone, and train the head on your images with the reproducibility foundation from Capstone 0 (fixed seed, versioned data, logged config). A typical implementation in PyTorch loads torchvision.models.resnet18(weights='DEFAULT'), freezes its parameters, swaps model.fc for a new linear layer, and trains only that — a dozen lines. The principle you're demonstrating is the same one this toy shows: reuse learned features, train only what's specific to your task, which is why a few hundred images can yield a strong classifier and why transfer learning is the default approach for real image tasks.

★ Key points to remember
  • The project: a custom image classifier via transfer learning — a classic, achievable, portfolio-worthy build.
  • Transfer learning reuses a pretrained backbone (feature extractor) and trains only a small new head on your labels.
  • You freeze the backbone (keep its learned general features) and train only the head — strong accuracy from little data/compute.
  • Real workflow: load a pretrained model, replace the final layer with your-class head, freeze the backbone, train the head.
  • It's the default for real image tasks — you rarely train from scratch.

The reasoning skill is sequencing it correctly (data and setup first, then model, then honest evaluation and iteration) and knowing the key decisions at each step (augmentation to fight overfitting on small data, freeze-then-optionally-fine-tune, class-sliced evaluation to find weaknesses). This is exactly the kind of end-to-end project you can deep-dive in an interview, explaining the decisions and trade-offs — which is what makes it valuable both as a learning exercise and as portfolio evidence that you can take a problem from raw data to a working, evaluated model.

2When would you fine-tune the backbone (not just the head), and what's the trade-off?
Show reasoning

You'd fine-tune some backbone layers (unfreeze them and train at a low learning rate) when your task differs enough from the pretraining data that the frozen general features aren't quite sufficient, and you have enough data to train more parameters without overfitting. The spectrum: frozen backbone + trained head (fastest, least data, when your task is similar to pretraining — e.g., classifying natural objects with an ImageNet-pretrained model); fine-tune the top backbone layers (when your task is somewhat different — e.g., medical or satellite images unlike everyday photos — so the higher-level features need adapting); fine-tune more or all of it (when your task is quite different and you have substantial data). The rule of thumb is: the more your data resembles the pretraining data and the less data you have, the more you freeze; the more different your task and the more data you have, the more you fine-tune.

The trade-off is adaptation vs overfitting and cost. Fine-tuning more layers lets the model adapt its features to your task (higher potential accuracy), but training more parameters needs more data (or you overfit), more compute, and a careful low learning rate (so you don't destroy the useful pretrained features — a common mistake). Freezing more is faster, safer on small data, and often sufficient, but caps how much the model can specialize. The practical approach is usually to start frozen (train the head, get a strong baseline fast), then, if you need more accuracy and have the data, unfreeze the top layers and fine-tune gently. The reasoning skill is matching the amount of fine-tuning to your task's similarity to pretraining and your data size — a judgment that shows you understand transfer learning as a spectrum, not a single recipe, which is exactly the kind of nuance that makes a project discussion impressive.

3Your classifier does well on the test set but poorly on real photos users upload. What likely went wrong, and how would you address it?
Show reasoning

This is distribution shift between your test set and real-world usage — the model generalizes to data like its training/test data but not to the genuinely different distribution of user photos. Likely causes: your training data was too clean or narrow (professional photos, consistent lighting/angles/backgrounds) while users upload messy, varied images (bad lighting, odd angles, cluttered backgrounds, phone-camera artifacts); or your classes as users use them differ subtly from how you labeled them; or there are conditions in the wild absent from your data. The test set said the model works on data like your training data, but real usage is a different distribution, so the good test score was an over-optimistic proxy for real-world performance (echoing the offline/online gap from Track 11).

To address it: make the training data more representative of real usage — collect actual user-style photos (varied conditions), and use aggressive, realistic augmentation (lighting, blur, crops, rotations) to simulate the variability the model will face. Evaluate on real-world data, not just your clean test set — ideally a held-out set of genuine user photos, which gives an honest estimate. Monitor in production (Track 9) for the classes and conditions where it fails, and retrain on the failures. The reasoning is that a test set only certifies performance on its distribution, so if that distribution doesn't match reality, the model will disappoint in the wild — a classic and important lesson. The fix is to close the gap between your data and real usage (representative data, realistic augmentation, real-world evaluation, and monitoring), which is exactly the production-minded thinking that distinguishes someone who's only done tutorials from someone who understands deploying models to real users. Recognizing this as distribution shift, and addressing it by making training and evaluation reflect reality, is a strong, mature answer.

★ Deep dive: why transfer learning works, and what this project demonstrates▸

Transfer learning works because of a deep property of how neural networks learn: they build hierarchical representations, where early layers learn general, low-level features (edges, colors, textures), middle layers learn mid-level patterns (shapes, parts), and later layers learn task-specific concepts (specific object categories). The general features in the early and middle layers are useful for almost any visual task — an edge detector helps whether you're classifying cats, cars, or cells — so they transfer across tasks with little or no modification. Only the task-specific top needs to be replaced and retrained for your classes. This is why a model pretrained on everyday photos transfers well even to quite different domains, and why the pretraining, done once on a massive dataset at great cost, provides value to countless downstream tasks that could never afford that scale themselves. Transfer learning is, in a sense, how the field amortizes the enormous cost of learning to see (or to read, in NLP) across everyone — the same principle that underlies using pretrained LLMs, which is the next capstone.

What this project demonstrates for your portfolio goes beyond "I trained a classifier." It shows you understand and can apply the dominant practical paradigm of modern deep learning: leveraging pretrained models rather than training from scratch, which is how real image (and language) systems are actually built. It exercises the full workflow — data preparation and augmentation, the freeze-and-train-head technique, honest evaluation with the metrics of Track 11, and iteration — on a real, self-defined task, all on the reproducible foundation of Capstone 0. And it sets up the key judgments (how much to freeze vs fine-tune, how to fight overfitting on small data, how to close the gap between your test set and real usage) that make for a rich interview deep-dive. The through-line to the rest of the capstones is that reusing large pretrained models and adapting them efficiently is the central skill of applied modern AI — you'll do the language analog next (fine-tuning an LLM with LoRA, which is transfer learning for language), then build systems around pretrained models (RAG, agents), then serve and operate them. Mastering transfer learning here, and understanding why it works (hierarchical, transferable representations) rather than just following the recipe, gives you the foundation for all of it — and a genuinely useful, portfolio-worthy project that proves you can turn a pretrained model into a custom solution, which is exactly what most applied ML work asks for.
