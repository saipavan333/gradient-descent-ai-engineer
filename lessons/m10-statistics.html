Statistics for ML: expectation, variance, MLE & the normal

⏱ 27 min Lesson 1.10 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsStatistics turns a finite, noisy sample into trustworthy conclusions — why a test set estimates real performance and why most loss functions look the way they do. Maximum likelihood justifies cross-entropy and MSE.
By the end, you can
  • Define expectation and variance as center and spread
  • Explain sampling and why a held-out set estimates performance
  • State maximum likelihood and connect it to common losses
  • Describe the normal distribution and the Central Limit Theorem

Concept From "what could happen" to "what the data says"

Probability (Lesson 1.9) reasons forward: given a model, what outcomes are likely? Statistics reasons backward: given a finite, noisy pile of data, what can we conclude? That backward direction is the everyday job of an ML engineer — estimate a quantity, judge how much to trust it, and pick the model that the data supports. Two simple summaries do most of the work: the center of a bunch of numbers and how spread out they are.

The expectation (also called the mean or average), written $E[X]$ ("E of X"), is the balance point of a distribution — the place it would balance if the probabilities were weights on a seesaw. For a list of equally-likely numbers it's just "add them up and divide by how many." For a distribution where each value $x$ has probability $P(x)$, it's each value weighted by its probability: $E[X] = \sum_x x\,P(x)$ (recall $\sum$ means "add up all the terms").

Concept Variance and standard deviation: how spread out

The mean alone hides a lot — two datasets can share the exact same mean yet look completely different. The variance measures spread: the average of the squared distances from the mean. (Why squared? So that being below the mean and above it both count as "far" instead of cancelling out, and so big misses count extra.) Its square root, the standard deviation $\sigma$, is the spread expressed back in the original units (dollars, centimeters), which is why $\sigma$ is the one people usually report.

You've already met variance in disguise: PCA (Lesson 1.5) finds the directions of maximum variance, and squared-error loss is an average squared distance. Variance's fingerprints are all over ML.

Concept Sampling: why a test set tells the truth

You never have the whole population (every possible data point) — only a sample (the finite bunch you actually collected). The sample mean (the average of your sample) is an estimate of the true mean, and — as the coin simulation in Lesson 1.9 hinted — the more data you average, the closer and steadier that estimate becomes. This guarantee has a name: the Law of Large Numbers. It's the entire justification for evaluating a model on a held-out test set: a large enough sample of unseen data gives a reliable estimate of real-world performance. Small test sets give noisy, untrustworthy numbers — a mistake to avoid.

Concept Maximum likelihood: where loss functions come from

Here's the idea that quietly defines almost every loss you'll minimize. Given some data, maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) chooses the model parameters that make the observed data most probable. Imagine trying every possible parameter value, asking "how likely is my actual data under this value?", and keeping the value at the peak.

(The "hat" in $\hat\theta$ — "theta-hat" — is standard notation for "our estimate of the parameter $\theta$.")

★
Why this matters more than it looks

The famous loss functions are MLE in disguise. If you assume your data is the truth plus bell-curve (Gaussian) noise, then "maximize the likelihood" turns out to be exactly "minimize the mean squared error." If you assume a categorical distribution over classes, it becomes "minimize cross-entropy." So when you train with MSE or cross-entropy in Track 3, you're doing maximum likelihood. The losses aren't arbitrary — they fall out of one principle.

Concept The normal distribution and the Central Limit Theorem

Why is the Gaussian bell curve everywhere in statistics and ML? The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is the reason: when you add up or average many independent random influences, the result tends toward a bell curve — no matter what shape the original data had.

Because measurement noise, accumulated errors, and sample means are all "sums of many small effects," they end up approximately Gaussian — which is why the normal distribution underlies so many models, confidence intervals, and the MSE-from-Gaussian-noise story above.

Worked example Mean, spread, a stabilizing average, and an MLE

Start with the basic summaries on a small sample, then watch a sample mean settle down, then compute a maximum-likelihood estimate by hand.

Quick check on the variance: the numbers $[2,4,4,4,5,5,7,9]$ have mean 5; their distances from 5 are $[-3,-1,-1,-1,0,0,2,4]$; squared, $[9,1,1,1,0,0,4,16]$; the average of those is $32/8 = 4$; and $\sqrt{4} = 2$ is the standard deviation. The die's sample mean homes in on the true $3.5$ as $n$ grows — the Law of Large Numbers, and the reason big test sets are trustworthy. Now maximum likelihood for a coin: we saw 7 heads in 10 flips and ask which bias $p$ makes that most probable.

from math import comb
heads, n = 7, 10
ps = np.linspace(0, 1, 101)                       # try every p from 0 to 1
likelihood = comb(n, heads) * ps**heads * (1 - ps)**(n - heads)
p_hat = ps[np.argmax(likelihood)]                 # the p with the highest likelihood
print("maximum-likelihood estimate of p:", round(p_hat, 2))   # 0.70
Outputmaximum-likelihood estimate of p: 0.7

The likelihood peaks at $\hat p = 0.7$ — exactly the observed fraction of heads. That's MLE confirming common sense: the best estimate of the coin's bias is the proportion you actually saw. Scale this principle up and it's how a logistic regression learns its probabilities and how a language model learns next-word distributions — each is maximizing the likelihood of its training data.

★ Key points to remember
  • Expectation $E[X]$ is the mean — the distribution's balance point.
  • Variance / standard deviation measure spread (average squared distance from the mean, and its square root).
  • The sample mean estimates the true mean and stabilizes as data grows (Law of Large Numbers) — why test sets work.
  • Maximum likelihood picks parameters that make the data most probable; MSE and cross-entropy are MLE in disguise.
  • The Central Limit Theorem makes sums/averages Gaussian, which is why the normal distribution is everywhere.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Compute the mean and variance of the dataset $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$ by hand, showing each step.
Show solution & reasoning

Mean: $(1+3+5+7)/4 = 16/4 = 4$.

Squared distances from the mean 4: $(1-4)^2=9$, $(3-4)^2=1$, $(5-4)^2=1$, $(7-4)^2=9$. Average them: $(9+1+1+9)/4 = 20/4 = 5$. So variance $= 5$, and standard deviation $= \sqrt5 \approx 2.24$.

The procedure to internalize: center (subtract the mean), square (so distances don't cancel), average. That "average of squared deviations" pattern is exactly the mean-squared-error loss you'll minimize when training regressors — variance and MSE are the same computation.

2You flip a coin 100 times and get 58 heads. What's the maximum-likelihood estimate of the coin's probability of heads, and why might you still hesitate to call the coin "biased"?
Show solution & reasoning

The MLE is the observed fraction: $\hat p = 58/100 = 0.58$.

Why hesitate: even a perfectly fair coin ($p=0.5$) produces 58+ heads fairly often by chance. The typical wobble (standard deviation) of the count for 100 fair flips is $\sqrt{100 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 0.5} = 5$, so 58 is only $1.6$ wobbles above the expected 50 — well within normal random variation. The reasoning: a point estimate ($0.58$) isn't the whole story; you also need its uncertainty before claiming an effect is real. This is the seed of statistical significance and why ML results need confidence intervals, not just single numbers (Track 12).

3Explain in a sentence or two how "minimizing mean squared error" and "maximum likelihood" are connected.
Show solution & reasoning

If you assume each target equals the model's prediction plus independent Gaussian (bell-curve) noise, then the likelihood of the data is largest exactly when the squared differences between predictions and targets are smallest. So maximizing that Gaussian likelihood is mathematically the same as minimizing mean squared error.

The takeaway: MSE isn't an arbitrary choice — it's the maximum-likelihood loss under a Gaussian-noise assumption. Change the assumption (to categorical outcomes) and MLE hands you cross-entropy instead. Knowing this tells you which loss to use for a problem, instead of guessing.

★ Deep dive: deriving MSE from MLE, and the n−1 in sample variance▸

MSE falls out of Gaussian MLE. Suppose each observed target is $y_i = \hat y_i + \varepsilon_i$, where the noise $\varepsilon_i$ is Gaussian with mean 0 (the $i$ subscript just indexes the data points). The probability of one observation is proportional to $\exp\!\big(-(y_i - \hat y_i)^2 / 2\sigma^2\big)$. The likelihood of all the data is the product of these, and — using the log trick from Lesson 1.9 — maximizing the log-likelihood turns that product into a sum: $\sum_i -(y_i - \hat y_i)^2 / 2\sigma^2 + \text{const}$. Maximizing this is the same as minimizing $\sum_i (y_i - \hat y_i)^2$ — mean squared error. Run the identical argument with a categorical distribution and the log-likelihood becomes the negative cross-entropy. Two of the most important losses in ML, both from one principle. That's the kind of unifying insight that separates someone who memorizes formulas from someone who understands them.

Why sample variance divides by $n-1$. You may notice np.var divides by $n$ by default, but statisticians often divide by $n-1$ (called Bessel's correction). The reason: when you estimate variance using the sample mean (rather than the unknown true mean), the data sits slightly closer to its own average than to the true center, so dividing by $n$ under-estimates the true variance. Dividing by $n-1$ corrects that bias. It's a small but real example of a deep theme — an estimator can be systematically off (we say it's biased), and good statistics is partly about understanding and correcting such biases. This same bias-versus-spread thinking about estimators is the cousin of the bias–variance tradeoff for models that anchors all of Track 3.
