Plotting with Matplotlib

⏱ 22 min Lesson 2.6 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsYou cannot understand data you cannot see. A good plot reveals skew, an outlier, or a leak in seconds — the fastest debugging and discovery tool you have, and how you communicate findings.
By the end, you can
  • Use the Figure/Axes model of Matplotlib
  • Make histograms, scatters, lines, and bars
  • Choose the right chart for the question
  • Label a chart so it communicates clearly

Concept You can't understand data you can't see

A table of summary numbers can hide a lot: two datasets can share the exact same average and still look completely different. A single picture reveals a lopsided distribution, a stray outlier, a broken sensor, or a data leak in seconds — things a wall of numbers conceals. Plotting (drawing charts of your data) is the fastest exploration and debugging tool you have, and it's how you communicate findings to people who don't read code. Matplotlib is the foundational plotting library in Python; almost everything else builds on it.

Concept The Figure / Axes object model

Matplotlib has one mental model worth learning properly. A Figure is the whole canvas — the blank page. It contains one or more Axes — and here "Axes" means the actual plot area (the box where the data is drawn), each with its own horizontal and vertical axis, title, and content. (Yes, the naming is confusing: "an Axes" is a whole plot, not a single axis line.) You create both at once with fig, ax = plt.subplots(), then call methods on ax to draw and label.

The core verbs are few: ax.plot(x, y) draws lines, ax.scatter(x, y) draws points, ax.hist(values) draws a distribution, ax.bar(labels, heights) draws bars, and the labelers ax.set_xlabel(), ax.set_ylabel(), ax.set_title(), ax.legend() add the words.

Concept Which chart for which question?

Choosing the chart is really choosing the question. Four cover most of what you need:

Two of those words: a distribution is the overall shape of one variable's values — where they cluster, how spread out they are, whether there are outliers — and a histogram shows it by chopping the range into bins and drawing a bar for how many values fall in each bin.

★
A chart should make one point clearly

Every chart needs axis labels and a title — an unlabeled plot is a riddle. Beyond that, less is more: drop gridlines, 3-D effects, and decoration that carries no information ("chart junk"). Ask "what is the one thing a viewer should take away?" and design toward that.

Worked example A histogram and a scatter, properly labeled

Two of the four core plots. A histogram shows the shape of one variable; a scatter shows the relationship between two. (Run the companion notebook to see the figures render — code can't draw on this page.)

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
size  = rng.normal(1500, 400, 200).clip(500)       # 200 house sizes
price = size * 0.2 + rng.normal(0, 30, 200) + 50    # price related to size, plus noise

# 1) Distribution of one variable -> histogram
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5, 3))
ax.hist(size, bins=20, color="#6366f1", edgecolor="white")
ax.set_xlabel("house size (sq ft)")
ax.set_ylabel("count")
ax.set_title("Distribution of house sizes")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# 2) Relationship between two variables -> scatter
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5, 3))
ax.scatter(size, price, s=12, alpha=0.6, color="#10b981")   # alpha = see-through dots
ax.set_xlabel("size (sq ft)")
ax.set_ylabel("price ($k)")
ax.set_title("Price vs. size")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

The histogram would show whether sizes are bell-shaped or lopsided (and expose outliers as stray bars off to one side); the scatter would show the clear upward trend we built in — bigger houses cost more — plus the scatter of points around it. In five lines each you've turned 200 numbers into a picture that tells you the shape and the relationship at a glance. Notice every plot has both axes labeled and a title; make that automatic.

For quick exploration, pandas wraps Matplotlib so you can plot straight off a DataFrame:

df["size"].plot(kind="hist", bins=20, title="sizes")   # one-liner during EDA
df.plot.scatter(x="size", y="price")

Same engine underneath — convenient for the rapid look-see of an EDA session (Lesson 2.7).

★ Key points to remember
  • Plotting reveals what summary numbers hide — skew, outliers, leaks — and communicates findings fast.
  • Matplotlib model: a Figure holds Axes (plot areas); fig, ax = plt.subplots(), then draw and label on ax.
  • Four core charts: histogram (distribution), scatter (relationship), line (trend), bar (comparison).
  • Always add axis labels and a title; cut decoration that carries no information.
  • df.plot(...) is the quick pandas shortcut during exploration.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1For each goal, name the chart: (a) compare total sales across five regions; (b) see whether study hours relate to exam scores; (c) check whether incomes are lopsided; (d) show website traffic by month.
Show solution & reasoning

(a) Bar chart — comparing a measure across categories. (b) Scatter — relationship between two numeric variables. (c) Histogram — the distribution/shape of one variable. (d) Line chart — a trend over time.

The reasoning: pick the chart by the question's type — comparison, relationship, distribution, or trend — rather than by habit. Matching the four core charts to those four questions covers the vast majority of EDA.

2You make a histogram of incomes and almost all the bars are crammed at the far left with a long thin tail to the right. What does this tell you, and what's one fix to read it better?
Show solution & reasoning

The data is right-skewed ("skewed" = lopsided, with a long tail on one side): most people earn modest amounts, while a few earn enormous amounts that stretch the axis and squash everyone else into the first bars.

One fix: plot on a log scale (ax.set_xscale("log")) or histogram np.log(income), which spreads the bunched values out and makes the shape readable. The reasoning: skew is extremely common in real data (incomes, web traffic, word counts), and recognizing it from a histogram tells you both how to visualize it and that you may want to log-transform the feature before modeling — a small EDA insight with real downstream impact.

3A colleague's bar chart of "average response time" makes a tiny improvement look huge. You notice the vertical axis starts at 95, not 0. Why is that misleading?
Show solution & reasoning

Starting the vertical axis at 95 instead of 0 visually exaggerates small differences: a change from 100 to 98 fills a large fraction of the truncated axis, looking like a dramatic drop, when it's only a 2% change. The bar lengths no longer represent the values honestly.

The reasoning: for bar charts, the bar length encodes the quantity, so the axis should generally start at 0; truncating it distorts the comparison. This is a classic "how to lie with charts" trap — and spotting it is part of being a trustworthy analyst, a theme that returns in responsible-AI communication (Track 11). Honest visualization is as much an ethical skill as a technical one.

★ Deep dive: beyond the basics — seaborn, small multiples, and choosing scales▸

seaborn is a library that sits on top of Matplotlib and makes statistical plots a one-liner with attractive defaults: sns.histplot, sns.scatterplot with automatic color-by-category, and especially sns.pairplot(df), which draws every pairwise scatter and each variable's histogram in a grid — a superb first look at a new dataset. sns.heatmap(df.corr()) visualizes the correlation matrix (which features move together), instantly flagging the perfectly-correlated pairs that signal redundancy or leakage (Lessons 1.4 and 2.5).

Small multiples. When comparing groups, resist cramming everything onto one busy chart. A grid of small, identical sub-plots — one per group, sharing axes — is often far clearer than ten overlapping lines. Create them with fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 3) and index axes[i, j]. The eye compares shapes across a tidy grid much better than it untangles a spaghetti plot.

Scales carry meaning. Choosing a scale is a modeling decision in miniature. A log scale turns multiplicative relationships and heavy-tailed data into something linear and readable (practice 2). A shared axis across sub-plots makes them honestly comparable. And the axis-origin question (practice 3) is really about which visual feature — length or position — encodes your value. The throughline: a plot is an argument made of ink, and every choice (chart type, scale, axis range, what to leave out) shapes the conclusion a viewer draws. Make those choices deliberately and honestly, and plotting becomes not just exploration but clear, trustworthy communication.
