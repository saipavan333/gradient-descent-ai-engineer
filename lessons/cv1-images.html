Track 5 · Computer Vision

How computers see: images as tensors

⏱ 35 min Lesson 5.1 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsA model never sees a 'picture' — it sees a grid of numbers. Every vision technique, from convolution to Vision Transformers, is just math on that tensor of pixels, so this is the foundation the whole track stands on.
By the end, you can
  • Explain an image as a height x width x channels tensor of pixel numbers
  • Read a pixel as its [R,G,B] values and convert an image to grayscale
  • Say why vision needs its own tools (spatial structure, huge input size)
  • Describe data augmentation and why it improves generalization for free

Concept To a computer, an image is just numbers

This track is a specialization on computer vision — teaching machines to see — and it builds directly on the neural networks and CNNs of Track 4. The first thing to internalize is that a computer has no notion of a "picture." An image, to a model, is a tensor: a grid of numbers. A color image is a 3D tensor of shape height × width × 3 — for every pixel, three numbers giving its red, green, and blue intensities (each 0–255). A 1000×1000 photo is three million numbers. Every vision model — from a simple classifier to a diffusion image generator — operates on exactly this: arrays of pixel values, never a "photo."

Why does vision get its own tools? Because image data has spatial structure a plain neural network would waste. A 1000×1000 image flattened into one long vector is a million inputs — a fully-connected layer on that is astronomically large and, worse, throws away the fact that nearby pixels are related and that a cat is a cat wherever it appears. This is exactly why CNNs (Track 4) exist: they exploit locality (small filters over neighborhoods) and translation invariance (the same filter slides everywhere), making vision tractable. The whole field is built on representing images as tensors and processing them with architectures that respect their spatial structure.

Worked example An image is a tensor you can compute on

Here's a tiny 2×2 color image as a tensor, and a real operation on it — converting to grayscale by averaging the three color channels, which is exactly the kind of pixel math vision runs on:

The image is a (2, 2, 3) tensor — 2 pixels tall, 2 wide, 3 channels — and the top-left pixel is [255, 0, 0], pure red. Converting to grayscale just averages each pixel's three channels: pure red (255+0+0)/3 = 85, and yellow (255+255+0)/3 = 170. That's it — an image operation is array math on pixel values. This tiny example is the whole mental model scaled down: a real photo is the same thing at 1000×1000×3, and every vision technique (a convolution, a resize, a color adjustment, a neural network forward pass) is arithmetic on this grid of numbers. Notice what the model does not get: any built-in sense that these numbers form a "cat" or a "sunset" — that meaning is what it must learn, from many labeled examples, by finding patterns in the pixel tensors. Grasping that an image is just a numeric tensor — with spatial structure worth respecting — is the foundation the entire field of computer vision is built on, and it's why the CNN (which processes that grid with sliding filters) was such a pivotal idea.

Concept Data augmentation: more data for free

Vision models are hungry for data, and labeled images are expensive to collect. Data augmentation is the trick that stretches a small dataset: apply random, label-preserving transformations to each training image — flips, rotations, crops, brightness/color shifts, zooms — so the model sees many varied versions of every example. A photo of a cat flipped horizontally is still a cat; cropped or slightly rotated, still a cat. By training on these variations, the model learns to recognize the object regardless of orientation, position, or lighting, which improves generalization and fights overfitting — essentially free extra data.

★ Key points to remember
  • An image is a tensor of shape height × width × 3 — three numbers (RGB, 0–255) per pixel.
  • A model sees only this grid of numbers; the meaning (what's in the image) must be learned from labeled examples.
  • Vision needs special tools (CNNs) because images have spatial structure — nearby pixels relate, and objects can appear anywhere.
  • Data augmentation (flips, rotations, crops, color shifts) creates varied label-preserving copies — free extra data that fights overfitting.
  • Every vision operation — convolution, resize, a network's forward pass — is arithmetic on the pixel tensor.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You have only 500 labeled images for a classifier and it's overfitting badly. How does augmentation help, and which augmentations would you choose for photos of everyday objects?
Show solution & reasoning

With only 500 images, the model can easily memorize them (overfit), and augmentation is one of the best fixes because it effectively enlarges and varies the dataset without new labeling. Each epoch, every image is randomly transformed, so the model almost never sees the exact same pixels twice — it can't just memorize, and it's forced to learn the underlying object rather than surface details. For everyday-object photos, good choices: horizontal flip (most objects look valid mirrored), random crop/resize (teaches the object can be at different positions and scales), small rotations (robustness to tilt), and brightness/contrast/color jitter (robustness to lighting). These preserve the label — a flipped, cropped, slightly-brighter cat is still a cat.

The key reasoning is choosing label-preserving transforms appropriate to the domain. Some augmentations are wrong for certain data: a vertical flip is fine for satellite images but wrong for photos of people (an upside-down person is unusual) or for digit recognition (a flipped 6 becomes something else); heavy color shifts would ruin a task where color is the signal (classifying ripe vs unripe fruit). So the skill is matching augmentations to what should and shouldn't change the label. Done well, augmentation can dramatically improve generalization from small data — it's often the highest-leverage, lowest-cost intervention in a vision project, which is why it's standard practice. The deeper point connects to Track 3: augmentation reduces variance (overfitting) by exposing the model to the natural variation it'll face at test time, so it learns invariances instead of memorizing your particular 500 shots.

2Why would flattening a 224×224×3 image into one long vector and feeding it to a fully-connected network be a bad idea?
Show solution & reasoning

Two big problems. Size/parameters: 224×224×3 = 150,528 inputs; a single fully-connected layer mapping that to even 1,000 units has over 150 million weights — enormous, slow, memory-hungry, and prone to overfitting. Stack a few such layers and it's unmanageable. Lost structure: flattening destroys the spatial arrangement — pixel (0,0) and pixel (0,1) are neighbors in the image but become just two arbitrary positions in a long vector, so the network has no built-in notion that nearby pixels relate or that a pattern (an edge, an eye) means the same thing wherever it appears. It would have to relearn, separately, that an object in the top-left and the same object in the bottom-right are the same — wasting capacity and data.

This is exactly why CNNs exist and dominate vision. A convolutional layer uses small filters (say 3×3) that slide across the image, so (a) it has few parameters (the filter is tiny and shared across all positions — weight sharing), and (b) it respects locality (each output depends on a small neighborhood) and gives translation invariance (the same filter detects a pattern anywhere). The result is vastly fewer parameters and a strong, correct inductive bias for images (Track 4). The reasoning skill is recognizing that the architecture should match the data's structure: images have local, translation-invariant spatial structure, so you use an architecture (CNN) that builds those assumptions in, rather than a generic dense network that ignores them. This is the same "inductive bias matched to data" principle from deep learning, and it's why the CNN was the breakthrough that made image recognition work — and why understanding images-as-structured-tensors is the right foundation before diving into the vision tasks that follow.

3A grayscale image, an RGB image, and a batch of 32 RGB images — what are their tensor shapes, and why does batching matter?
Show solution & reasoning

Shapes: a grayscale image is 2D — height × width (one number per pixel, its intensity), sometimes written H × W × 1. An RGB image is 3D — H × W × 3 (three channels). A batch of 32 RGB images is 4D — 32 × H × W × 3 (or, in some frameworks, 32 × 3 × H × W with channels first); the leading dimension is the batch. The pattern: each added axis is another kind of index — pixel column, row, channel, and which image in the batch.

Batching matters for the same reason it does everywhere in deep learning (Tracks 4, 11): you process many images at once as a single tensor so the GPU's parallelism is used efficiently. A GPU can run the same operation over all 32 images in a batch nearly as fast as over one, so batching massively increases training and inference throughput. It's also how mini-batch gradient descent works — you compute the loss and gradient over a batch of examples for a stable, efficient update (Track 1). So virtually all real vision code works with 4D tensors (batch × height × width × channels), and getting comfortable reading these shapes — and spotting when a shape is wrong, the most common bug in vision code — is a core practical skill. The reasoning takeaway is that the tensor shape encodes exactly what's being represented (pixels → channels → images in a batch), and fluency with these shapes is how you reason about, and debug, any vision pipeline. It's the concrete, everyday form of the "an image is a tensor" idea this lesson is built on.

★ Deep dive: color, resolution, and the preprocessing that vision models need▸

A few practical realities flow from "an image is a tensor." Channels aren't only RGB. Grayscale has one channel; RGB has three; but medical and scientific imaging can have many more (satellite images with infrared and other bands, depth maps, medical scans with multiple modalities) — the model just sees more numbers per pixel. Resolution is a real trade-off: more pixels means more detail but far more computation and memory (cost grows with the pixel count), so vision pipelines resize images to a fixed size (commonly 224×224 for classification) that balances detail against cost — and choosing that size is a genuine design decision, echoing the latency/quality trade-offs of Track 11. Preprocessing matters: before a model sees an image, pixels are typically normalized (scaled from 0–255 to a standard range, often using the mean and standard deviation of the training data), because neural networks train far better on well-scaled inputs (Track 4) — and, importantly, the exact same preprocessing must be applied at training and at inference, or you get the train/serve skew problem from Track 10, here in visual form (a model fed differently-scaled pixels at serving time silently degrades).

The deeper theme is that vision is the same machine learning you already know, applied to structured numeric tensors — which is empowering, because everything from the earlier tracks transfers. An image classifier is supervised learning (Track 3) with images as X; it's trained by gradient descent (Track 1) with backprop (Track 4); it can overfit and needs regularization and honest evaluation (Tracks 3, 12); it must be served and monitored in production (Track 10); and it's designed against real requirements (Track 11). What's special about vision is the data's spatial structure and the architectures (CNNs, and later Vision Transformers) that exploit it — but the surrounding discipline is identical. So as you go through this track — classification, detection, segmentation, generation, multimodal — you're not learning a separate field so much as applying your ML foundation to a new, richly-structured data type. Keeping that in mind turns computer vision from intimidating into familiar: it's tensors of pixels, processed by structure-aware networks, trained and evaluated with the tools you already have.
