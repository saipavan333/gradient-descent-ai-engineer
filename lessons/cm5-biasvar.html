Bias–variance tradeoff & overfitting

⏱ 25 min Lesson 3.5 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsEvery model that disappoints is either too simple or too complex. Bias-variance is the one framework that names which, explains why, and points to the fix — the most useful diagnostic in applied ML.
By the end, you can
  • Tell underfitting (bias) from overfitting (variance)
  • Read the U-shaped test-error curve
  • Use the train-test gap as a diagnostic
  • Name the bias-variance-noise decomposition

Concept Two ways to fail: underfitting and overfitting

Every model that disappoints does so in one of two ways, and telling them apart is the most useful diagnostic skill in machine learning. A model can be underfitting — too simple to capture the real pattern, so it's wrong even on the training data (think: a straight line through an obviously curved trend). Or it can be overfitting — so flexible that it memorizes the training data's noise, acing the training set but flopping on anything new (the trap we first met in Lesson 3.1).

To name the cause precisely we use two words. Bias is error from wrong assumptions — the model is too rigid to represent the truth (high bias = underfitting). Variance is error from sensitivity to the particular training sample — the model changes wildly if you reshuffle the data (high variance = overfitting). A straight line has high bias and low variance; a degree-15 polynomial has low bias and high variance. The art is balancing them.

Concept The tradeoff: test error is U-shaped in complexity

Here's the central picture of the whole field. As you make a model more complex, its training error falls steadily — more flexibility always fits the training data better. But its test error (on unseen data) follows a U-shape: it drops while added complexity is capturing real signal, bottoms out at the sweet spot, then climbs as the model starts fitting noise instead. You want the bottom of that U.

This is why the gap between training and test error is so diagnostic. A small gap with high error on both means underfitting (high bias) — the model is too simple, so add complexity. A large gap (great training score, poor test score) means overfitting (high variance) — the model is too complex, so simplify it or get more data. You read these two numbers like a doctor reads a pulse.

Concept The decomposition that explains it all

There's a clean equation underneath. For squared-error problems, the expected test error of a model splits into exactly three pieces:

$$\text{Expected error} = \underbrace{\text{Bias}^2}_{\text{too rigid}} + \underbrace{\text{Variance}}_{\text{too sensitive}} + \underbrace{\text{Irreducible noise}}_{\text{unbeatable}}$$

Bias² and variance are the two we can trade against each other by changing model complexity — push one down and the other tends to rise, which is why the total has a minimum in the middle. The third term, irreducible noise, is the randomness inherent in the data (measurement error, genuine unpredictability); no model, however clever, can beat it. Recognizing that floor keeps you from chasing a perfect score that doesn't exist (recall the linear-regression worked example, where MSE bottomed out near the noise variance we'd baked in).

Worked example Watching overfitting happen, live

We'll fit polynomials of increasing degree to noisy data from a true sine curve, and watch training and test error diverge. Degree is our complexity dial: degree 1 is a line, degree 15 is a wildly flexible curve. We hold out 40% as a test set and report mean squared error on each.

Read the two columns like the U-curve. Training MSE only ever falls (0.237 → 0.059) — more flexibility always fits the training points better. But test MSE bottoms out at degree 3 (0.106) and then climbs, gently at first (degree 5) and catastrophically by degree 15, where the test error of 499 is the model violently wiggling through noise and exploding between points. Degree 1 underfits (both errors high-ish and close); degree 15 overfits (tiny train, enormous test). Degree 3 is the sweet spot — and notice you can find it just by watching the held-out error, no theory required.

★ Key points to remember
  • Underfitting (high bias) = too simple, wrong even on training data; overfitting (high variance) = memorizes noise, fails on new data.
  • As complexity rises, training error keeps falling but test error is U-shaped — aim for the bottom.
  • A small train–test gap with high error = underfit; a large gap = overfit.
  • Expected error = bias² + variance + irreducible noise; the first two trade off, the third is a hard floor.
  • You can locate the sweet spot empirically by watching held-out (validation) error as you vary complexity.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1In the worked example, why does training MSE keep dropping (0.237 → 0.059) while test MSE turns around and climbs after degree 3?
Show solution & reasoning

Training MSE drops because a higher-degree polynomial has more freedom to bend toward every training point — with enough degrees it can pass arbitrarily close to all of them, so the error it sees only shrinks.

But past degree 3, that extra freedom is spent fitting the noise in the training sample rather than the underlying sine. Noise is different in the test set, so a curve contorted to match training noise predicts wildly between and beyond the training points — hence test MSE rises, then explodes (499 at degree 15). The model got better at the training data and worse at the actual task: the definition of overfitting.

2You're overfitting. List three different levers that would reduce variance, and briefly say why each works.
Show solution & reasoning

(1) Simplify the model (fewer features, lower polynomial degree, shallower tree): less flexibility means less room to chase noise. (2) Get more training data: with more examples, the noise averages out and the model can't memorize its way through — variance shrinks toward zero as data grows. (3) Add regularization (the next lesson): a penalty on large weights discourages the contortions that fitting noise requires.

Others include early stopping, dropout (for neural nets), and ensembling (averaging many models cancels their individual variance — exactly why random forests work, Lesson 3.8). They all attack the same target: sensitivity to the particular training sample.

3A colleague says "my model is at 100% training accuracy, so it's done — ship it." What's the flaw, and what would you ask to see?
Show solution & reasoning

The flaw: 100% training accuracy is evidence of nothing good on its own — a sufficiently flexible model can memorize any training set, and 100% is exactly what overfitting produces. Training accuracy measures memorization, not generalization (Lesson 3.1).

Ask to see the validation/test score and the train–test gap. If test accuracy is also high and close to training, great. If test is much lower (a large gap), the 100% is a red flag for overfitting, and you'd dial back complexity or regularize. The single most informative habit in ML is reporting train and held-out numbers side by side.

★ Deep dive: learning curves, double descent, and where the framework bends▸

A practical tool for diagnosis is the learning curve: plot training and validation error as you feed the model more data (rather than more complexity). If both curves have converged to a high error and sit close together, you're bias-limited — more data won't help, you need a better model. If there's still a wide gap with validation error falling, you're variance-limited — more data likely will help. This tells you whether to spend your next week collecting data or engineering features, which is exactly the kind of decision the framework is for.

A close cousin is the validation curve (vary one hyperparameter, like tree depth or regularization strength, and watch validation error) — it's literally the U-curve from this lesson, drawn for the dial you're tuning, and reading off its minimum is how you set that hyperparameter (formalized with cross-validation in Lesson 3.12).

Finally, a humbling frontier note. The clean U-curve is the classical story and it governs essentially all of the models in this track. But very large modern models (deep nets with far more parameters than data points) exhibit double descent: test error follows the U, spikes right at the point where the model can exactly fit the training data, and then — surprisingly — descends again as the model grows even larger. Why hugely over-parameterized networks generalize well despite memorizing the training set is an active research question, and a reminder that the bias–variance picture is a powerful guide, not an unbreakable law. For classical ML, though, trust the U.

You can now diagnose overfitting. The most common cure is to penalize complexity directly. Next, in 3.6 Regularization (L1 / L2), we add a term to the loss that keeps weights small — and discover why one flavor (lasso) sets some of them to exactly zero.
