Transfer learning & fine-tuning

⏱ 25 min Lesson 4.9 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsMost projects cannot train from scratch. Transfer learning reuses a pretrained model's features, reaching strong accuracy with little data — the principle behind every foundation model.
By the end, you can
  • Explain transfer learning, backbones, and heads
  • Choose feature extraction vs fine-tuning
  • Freeze layers with requires_grad=False
  • Know when transfer helps vs hurts

Concept Don't start from scratch — stand on a pretrained model

Training a strong image or language model from scratch takes millions of examples and days of GPU time — resources most projects don't have. Transfer learning sidesteps this: take a model already trained on a huge dataset, and reuse what it learned for your (smaller) task. A network pretrained on ImageNet has already learned, in its early layers, to detect edges, textures, and shapes — features useful for almost any image task. Why relearn them from your 500 photos when someone already learned them from 14 million?

The recipe: take the pretrained backbone (all layers except the final classifier), attach a fresh head (a new output layer sized for your classes), and train. Because the backbone's features are already good, you need far less data and time to reach strong accuracy — often a few hundred examples and minutes instead of millions and days.

Concept Two modes: feature extraction vs fine-tuning

There are two ways to do it, differing in how much of the backbone you change. Feature extraction freezes the backbone entirely (its weights don't update) and trains only the new head — fast, cheap, and great when your data is small or very similar to the pretraining data. Fine-tuning unfreezes some or all of the backbone and trains it too, at a low learning rate, so the features adapt to your domain — better when you have more data or a domain that differs from the original.

A reliable rule of thumb: small/similar data → feature extraction (freeze, train head); more or different data → fine-tune (unfreeze, low learning rate). Use a low learning rate when fine-tuning so you gently adapt the precious pretrained weights rather than destroying them with big updates — a freshly-initialized head produces large early gradients that would otherwise wreck the backbone.

Worked example Train only a head on frozen features

To see the mechanism, we'll freeze a feature extractor and train only a linear head on top of its outputs. (Here the "extractor" is a fixed random nonlinear map; in real transfer learning it would be a pretrained network with far richer features — but the point is identical: the head learns on frozen features.)

We never touched the feature extractor — only the small head was trained — yet the model fits well and generalizes (test MSE 0.0091 ≈ train MSE 0.0089, both near the 0.1²=0.01 noise floor). That's the whole promise of transfer learning: the frozen features do the heavy lifting, and a tiny trainable head adapts them to your target with very little data. In PyTorch the real version loads a pretrained network and swaps its head:

import torchvision.models as models
import torch.nn as nn

model = models.resnet18(weights="IMAGENET1K_V1")   # pretrained backbone
for p in model.parameters():
    p.requires_grad = False                         # freeze the backbone
model.fc = nn.Linear(model.fc.in_features, 3)       # new head: 3 of YOUR classes (trainable)
# only model.fc has requires_grad=True, so only the head trains

Setting requires_grad = False freezes a layer (no gradients, no updates — exactly our frozen extractor); replacing model.fc gives a fresh trainable head. To fine-tune instead, you'd unfreeze some backbone layers and use a small learning rate. This pattern — load pretrained, freeze, swap head, train — is how most real-world image and text models get built today.

★ Key points to remember
  • Transfer learning reuses a model pretrained on a huge dataset, so you need far less data and compute.
  • Keep the pretrained backbone (general features), attach a fresh head for your classes.
  • Feature extraction: freeze the backbone, train only the head (small/similar data).
  • Fine-tuning: unfreeze some/all of the backbone and train at a low learning rate (more/different data).
  • In PyTorch, requires_grad = False freezes layers; replace the final layer for your task.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You must classify 10 types of skin lesion from 800 dermatology images. Outline a transfer-learning plan and justify each choice.
Show solution & reasoning

Start from an ImageNet-pretrained CNN (e.g., ResNet) — its early layers already detect edges and textures useful for any image. Replace the head with a new 10-class output layer. Because 800 images is modest, begin with feature extraction (freeze the backbone, train only the head) to get a fast, low-risk baseline. Then, if accuracy plateaus, fine-tune the last few backbone blocks at a low learning rate, since medical images differ somewhat from ImageNet photos and some adaptation helps. Throughout, use heavy data augmentation (flips, rotations, crops — Lesson 4.5) to stretch the small dataset, and a held-out test set for honest evaluation.

The reasoning chain: small data → lean on pretraining and freeze first; somewhat different domain → consider light fine-tuning; tiny dataset → augment aggressively. This is essentially the plan for Capstone 1.

2Your task is classifying audio spectrograms, and a colleague suggests starting from an ImageNet-pretrained model. When might this help, and when might transfer hurt?
Show solution & reasoning

It can help because spectrograms are images, and low-level features (edges, blobs, textures) pretrained on natural images often transfer to any 2-D signal — giving a better starting point than random weights, especially with limited audio data. Many speech/audio systems do exactly this.

It can hurt (or simply not help) when the domains are too dissimilar — the high-level ImageNet features (detecting dogs, cars) are irrelevant to audio, so deep-layer features transfer poorly, and if you fine-tune carelessly on little data you can overfit. The safe approach: transfer the early layers (general), freeze them, and train a new head; if it underperforms, you've lost little. Transfer is a strong prior, not a guarantee — its value scales with how related the source and target domains are.

3Concretely, what does p.requires_grad = False do, and why does that implement "freezing" a layer?
Show solution & reasoning

It tells PyTorch's autograd not to compute or store a gradient for that parameter. During backward(), no .grad is produced for it, so optimizer.step() has nothing to update — the weight stays exactly at its pretrained value. That's precisely "freezing": the layer still participates in the forward pass (it computes features), but it never changes.

This mirrors the worked example, where the feature extractor W_features was simply never included in the optimization — only head was fit. Freezing also saves computation and memory (no gradients to track for frozen layers), which is partly why feature extraction is so fast. To later fine-tune, you'd set requires_grad = True on the layers you want to adapt and use a small learning rate.

★ Deep dive: why features transfer, and the rise of foundation models▸

Why do pretrained features transfer at all? Because the low-level structure of a domain is shared across tasks. Every natural image — whatever its label — is built from edges, corners, colors, and textures, so a network that learned to see those for ImageNet classification has learned something genuinely reusable. Researchers confirmed this by visualizing the layers: early layers learn near-universal Gabor-like edge filters (much like our hand-made convolution in Lesson 4.6), middle layers learn textures and parts, and only the final layers become task-specific. That's exactly why you freeze the general early layers and replace the specific final ones. The same logic holds for language: a model that learned grammar and word meaning from a trillion words of text has learned structure useful for sentiment, summarization, or question-answering alike.

This insight has scaled into the dominant paradigm of modern AI: foundation models. Instead of training task-specific models, the field now pretrains enormous general models on massive data once — at great expense — and everyone else adapts them. A large language model (Track 7) is a foundation model you transfer from: you prompt it, or fine-tune it with LoRA (Track 7) for your task, exactly the freeze-most/adapt-a-little principle from this lesson, scaled to billions of parameters. The economics are transformative: pretraining costs millions and happens rarely; adaptation costs little and happens everywhere. Transfer learning, which started as a clever trick for small image datasets, turned out to be the organizing principle of the whole modern AI stack — and understanding it here, in its simplest form (freeze a backbone, train a head), is understanding the engine of Tracks 6 through 9.
