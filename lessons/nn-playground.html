What am I looking at? The background color is the network's prediction at every point — blue where it says "class A", red where it says "class B". The line between them is the decision boundary. At the start it's random. Each training step, backprop measures how each weight contributed to the error and nudges it; the boundary bends to fit. With too few neurons the boundary stays too simple to fit the data.

Try these three things

1. Watch depth matter. Put the dataset on Circle and set hidden neurons to 1. Train. It gets stuck around 75% — one neuron can only draw a straight line, and no straight line separates an inner disk from an outer ring. Now raise it to 6 and retrain: the boundary curves into a loop and accuracy jumps to ~100%. That leap is the whole reason we use hidden layers.

2. Break it with the learning rate. On Spiral, push the learning rate to the maximum. Watch the loss curve thrash instead of settling — too big a step overshoots the minimum every time. Drop it low and it crawls but converges. This is the same trade-off you met in gradient descent.

3. Find the minimum capacity. On XOR, start at 1 neuron (fails), then 2, then 3. Find the fewest neurons that reach 100%. That's a hands-on feel for model capacity: just enough to fit the pattern, no more.

The five things this lab proves
  • A neuron is a line; a layer is a shape. Stacking neurons lets a network draw curved, non-linear boundaries.
  • Backprop is just the chain rule at scale — it assigns blame to every weight, and gradient descent nudges each one downhill.
  • Capacity must match the problem — too few neurons underfit (can't bend enough); the boundary literally can't reach the data.
  • The learning rate is the master dial — too high and training diverges, too low and it crawls.
  • Training is iterative — the boundary is random at first and improves one small step at a time, which is exactly what the loss curve shows.
