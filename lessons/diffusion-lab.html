Diffusion Lab

Diffusion models generate images by learning to undo noise. Here's the whole idea in one control: slide right to destroy an image with Gaussian noise, then press Denoise and watch it get rebuilt one step at a time — exactly the process a model like Stable Diffusion learns to run.

Training is the forward direction: take a real image, add a known amount of noise, and ask a network to predict that noise. Generation is the reverse: start from pure static and remove predicted noise step by step until a picture appears. Drag the slider to feel the forward process, then hit Denoise to watch the reverse.

What the network actually learns. At every noise level t, the model is shown the noisy image and trained to output the noise that was added. Subtract that predicted noise and you get a cleaner image; repeat from pure static and a brand-new image emerges. The forward "noising" needs no learning at all — it's just adding Gaussian noise on a fixed schedule. All the intelligence is in the reverse.

Try these

1. Watch signal die. Drag the slider right slowly. The green/red bar shows the mix: early on it's mostly signal with a little noise; by the end it's almost pure noise and the image is gone. That schedule — how fast signal gives way to noise — is a real design choice (this one is a cosine schedule).

2. Generate from static. Push the slider all the way right (pure noise), then press Denoise. The image reassembles from what looks like random static — that's generation. A real model does this without ever seeing the original; it only predicts noise.

3. Notice it's gradual. Denoising takes many small steps, not one. Trying to jump from pure noise to a finished image in a single step is far too hard — taking dozens of small, easy steps is what makes diffusion work (and also why it's slower than a GAN).

What this lab proves
  • Forward = add noise (no learning). x_t = √ā·x₀ + √(1−ā)·ε on a fixed schedule — trivial to compute.
  • Reverse = predict & remove noise (all the learning). The network's only job is to estimate ε at each step.
  • Generation is denoising from static. Start at pure noise and run the reverse process to sample a new image.
  • Many small steps beat one big one. Each step is an easy denoise; stacking them turns noise into structure.
