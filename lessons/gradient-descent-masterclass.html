Gradient descent: how models actually learn

One idea powers nearly all of machine learning: to improve, feel which way is downhill and take a small step. You'll picture it, compute it by hand, run real Python on it, and build it from scratch — until you could teach it.

Imagine you're standing on a foggy hillside at night, and you want to reach the bottom of the valley. You can't see anything. But you can feel the ground under your feet — which way it slopes. So you do the only sensible thing: feel the downhill direction, take one small step that way, and repeat. Step by step, you descend, even blind. That is the entire idea behind how a neural network learns to recognise a face, translate a sentence, or drive a car. The "hill" is how wrong the model is, and the model keeps stepping downhill until it's as right as it can be. This one move is called gradient descent — and this course is named after it for a reason.

By the end you'll be able to explain gradient descent with a picture, work through the steps by hand with real numbers, run and tweak it in real Python right here in the page, build it from scratch to fit an actual line to data, and know exactly why the learning rate makes or breaks it.

1 · Intuition Feel the slope, step downhill

Every model has knobs — numbers it can adjust — called parameters. And it has a score for how badly it's doing, called the loss (think "how much it's losing by"). Turning the knobs changes the loss. If you plot the loss against a knob, you get a landscape of hills and valleys. Learning means finding the knob settings at the bottom of a valley, where the loss is smallest.

Here's the trick. At any point on that landscape you can measure the slope — how steeply the loss goes up or down as you nudge the knob. The slope points uphill (toward more loss). So to reduce loss, step in the opposite direction — downhill. Take a small step, measure the slope again, step again. Repeat until the ground is flat (slope ≈ 0), which means you've reached the bottom. That's gradient descent: repeatedly step opposite the slope.

✦
The one-sentence picture: loss is a hill, the slope tells you which way is up, so step the other way — a little at a time — until the ground is flat.

2 · The formal idea The update rule, symbol by symbol

Let's name things in plain words. Call the knob x and the loss f(x) — a function that takes the knob setting and returns how bad it is. The slope of f at x is its gradient, written f′(x) (read "f-prime of x"; the little mark means "the slope of"). A positive slope means loss rises as x grows; negative means it falls.

The whole algorithm is one line, repeated:

x  ←  x  −  η · f′(x)

Reading it as a sentence: "the new x is the old x, minus a small fraction of the slope." The arrow ← means "becomes" (update the knob to this new value). The Greek letter η (pronounced "eta") is the learning rate — a small positive number (like 0.1) that sets how big each step is. And the crucial part is the minus sign: f′(x) points uphill, so subtracting it moves us downhill. If the slope is steep, the step is big; as we near the bottom the slope shrinks toward zero, so the steps automatically get gentler and we settle in. Multiply the slope by η, subtract, repeat — that is gradient descent, complete.

i
Why "gradient," not "slope"? With one knob the slope is a single number. Real models have millions of knobs, and then the slope becomes a list of numbers — one slope per knob — called the gradient. But the rule is identical: subtract a small multiple of the gradient from every knob at once. Master the one-knob case here and the million-knob case is the same sentence.

Notice three things. Near the top the slope is steep, so the steps are big; near the bottom the slope is gentle, so the steps shrink and it eases in — the algorithm self-adjusts. A tiny learning rate is safe but painfully slow. And there's a ceiling: past a certain learning rate each step overshoots the bottom by more than it started, so it climbs the far wall higher and higher and diverges. That single knob, the learning rate, is the most important dial in all of deep learning.

4 · Worked example Three steps, every number by hand

Let's do it with a pen. Loss f(x) = x², so the slope is f′(x) = 2x (the derivative of x-squared). Start at x = 4.2 with learning rate η = 0.1. Apply x ← x − 0.1·(2x) three times.

start:  x = 4.2

step 1: slope = 2·4.2 = 8.4
        x = 4.2 − 0.1·8.4 = 4.2 − 0.84 = 3.36     f(x) = 11.29

step 2: slope = 2·3.36 = 6.72
        x = 3.36 − 0.1·6.72 = 3.36 − 0.672 = 2.688  f(x) = 7.23

step 3: slope = 2·2.688 = 5.376
        x = 2.688 − 0.1·5.376 = 2.688 − 0.538 = 2.150  f(x) = 4.62

Watch the loss fall: 17.64 → 11.29 → 7.23 → 4.62. We're sliding down the bowl toward 0. And notice the steps shrink (0.84, then 0.672, then 0.538) even though η never changed — because the slope itself shrinks as we approach the flat bottom. That automatic gentling is why gradient descent lands softly instead of rattling around. Everything here is one multiplication and one subtraction, repeated. 7 · Common mistakes The traps that actually bite

❌ Learning rate too big → it explodes

If η is too large, each step overshoots the bottom by more than it started, so the loss grows every step and shoots to infinity (you'll see nan — "not a number"). Fix: lower the learning rate (try 10× smaller). If the loss is rising, the rate is almost always too high — you saw this in the widget past η ≈ 1.

❌ Learning rate too small → it crawls

Too tiny and it takes forever, or stalls before reaching the bottom in the time you allow. Fix: increase it until progress is quick but still stable. Good rates are usually found by trying a few (0.001, 0.01, 0.1) and watching the loss curve.

❌ Forgetting the minus sign → it climbs

Write x = x + η·f′(x) and you do gradient ascent — you climb the hill, maximising the loss. Fix: to minimise, always subtract the gradient. (Sometimes you do want to ascend — that's literally the sister site, Gradient Ascent.)

❌ Expecting the global bottom every time

Simple bowls have one bottom. Complex loss landscapes have many dips (local minima) and flat spots, so plain gradient descent can settle in a merely-good valley, not the best one. Fix in practice: randomness (stochastic gradient descent), momentum, and good starting points — the frontier note has more.

8 · Key points Worth memorising

★ Lock these in
  • Gradient descent = repeatedly step opposite the slope to reduce the loss: x ← x − η·f′(x).
  • The gradient (slope) points uphill; the minus sign makes you go downhill. Flat ground (slope ≈ 0) means you've arrived.
  • The learning rate η is the make-or-break dial: too big diverges, too small crawls.
  • Steps shrink automatically near the bottom because the slope shrinks — so it lands softly.
  • The same loop scales from one knob to millions: it's how essentially every neural network is trained.

10 · Graded practice Warm-up → transfer → stretch

Warm-up One step by hand

Loss f(x)=x², so f′(x)=2x. You're at x = 5 with η = 0.2. Where do you land after one step?

Solution. Slope = 2·5 = 10. Update: x = 5 − 0.2·10 = 5 − 2 = 3. You moved from 5 to 3, toward the bottom at 0 — good. Note the step size was 2; with a smaller η it'd be gentler, with η = 0.5 it'd be 5 − 0.5·10 = 0 (landing exactly at the bottom in one step — lucky for this simple bowl), and with η = 1.0 it'd be 5 − 10 = −5 (overshooting to the same height on the other side — the edge of divergence).

Transfer Diagnose a training curve

You train a model and print the loss each step. Case A: 8.0, 7.1, 6.5, 6.0, 5.7 … slowly dropping. Case B: 8.0, 40, 900, nan. Case C: 8.0, 7.99, 7.985, 7.982 … barely moving. What's happening, and what do you change?

Solution. Case A is healthy — loss falling steadily; leave it (maybe nudge the rate up to finish faster). Case B is diverging: the loss blows up to nan, the signature of a learning rate that's too high — each step overshoots and climbs. Lower η by 10× and rerun. Case C is crawling: loss drops but agonisingly slowly, the signature of a rate that's too low. Raise η. The skill is reading the loss curve like a dashboard: falling nicely = good; exploding = rate too high; barely moving = rate too low. This single diagnostic will serve you for the rest of your ML career.

Stretch Why the same loop trains a billion-parameter model

You just fit 2 parameters (m, b) by gradient descent. A large language model has billions. Conceptually, what changes and what stays exactly the same?

Solution. What stays the same: the update rule, verbatim. Every parameter θ is nudged by θ ← θ − η·(gradient of the loss w.r.t. θ), all at once, over and over. The loss (how wrong the model is) and the "step opposite the slope" logic are identical. What changes is only scale and machinery: (1) the gradient is now a list of a billion slopes, computed efficiently by backpropagation (a later lesson); (2) you can't afford to use all the data for every step, so you use a small random batch — that's stochastic gradient descent (SGD); (3) smarter variants (momentum, Adam) adapt the step per-parameter to go faster and dodge bad spots. But strip all that away and the beating heart is the one line you mastered here: subtract a small multiple of the slope. That's why this lesson is the foundation the entire course — and the whole field — stands on.

11 · Frontier note Where this goes next

Real training rarely uses plain gradient descent. Because computing the slope over millions of examples every step is too slow, models use stochastic gradient descent (SGD): estimate the slope from a small random batch each step — noisier, but far faster, and the noise even helps escape bad valleys. On top of that sit momentum (keep some velocity from past steps, like a ball that builds speed rolling downhill) and adaptive optimisers like Adam (give each parameter its own learning rate). And the gradients themselves, for deep networks, are computed by backpropagation — the chain rule applied at scale, which is the very next thing worth learning. But every one of these is a refinement of the sentence you now own: step opposite the slope.

12 · Recap Say it to a friend

"To make a model less wrong, treat its error as a hill, measure the slope, and take a small step downhill — then repeat. That step is new = old − learning_rate × slope. Do it millions of times over a million knobs and you've trained a neural network."
