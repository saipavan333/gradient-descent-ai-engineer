Diffusion models & image generation

⏱ 28 min Lesson 7.8 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsImage generation works by learning to reverse a noising process. Diffusion — predict the noise, then remove it step by step — is the technology behind Stable Diffusion and DALL·E.
By the end, you can
  • Explain the forward and reverse diffusion processes
  • State the denoising training objective
  • Describe text conditioning and latent diffusion
  • Know why diffusion overtook GANs

Concept Making images by removing noise

Transformers generate text by predicting the next token. Image generation works completely differently, and the dominant approach is the diffusion model — the technology behind Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, and Midjourney. The core idea is almost paradoxical: teach a model to turn noise into images by first teaching it to turn images into noise, then running that backward.

The forward process is fixed and simple: take a training image and add a little Gaussian noise (Lesson 1.10), then a little more, over many steps, until nothing remains but random static. The reverse process is what's learned: a neural network is trained to take a noisy image and predict a slightly less noisy version — to undo one step of noising. Chain the learned reverse steps and you can start from pure random noise and gradually denoise it into a brand-new, coherent image that never existed.

▶
Take it to the lab

Open the Diffusion Lab — slide an image into pure noise, then press Denoise and watch the reverse process rebuild it one step at a time, exactly as a generator learns to.

Concept The training objective: predict the noise

How do you train the reverse step? With a clever, simple objective: take a clean image, add a known amount of noise, and ask the model to predict the noise that was added. Since you generated the noise yourself, you have a perfect label — it's self-supervised, like next-token prediction. The loss is just the difference (MSE, Lesson 3.2) between the predicted and actual noise.

To make it useful, generation is conditioned on a text prompt: the model denoises toward an image matching "a cat astronaut in watercolor." This text-to-image conditioning (via the embeddings of Lesson 5.2 and attention of Lesson 6.4) is what lets you steer the generation. And to make it efficient, latent diffusion (Stable Diffusion) runs the whole process in a compressed latent space (Lesson 7.9's VAE) rather than on full-resolution pixels — far cheaper, which is why image generation can run on a single GPU.

Worked example The forward noising schedule

In the forward process, a noisy image at step $t$ is a blend: $x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon$, where $x_0$ is the clean image, $\epsilon$ is noise, and $\bar\alpha_t$ shrinks from 1 toward 0 as $t$ grows. Let's watch the signal and noise weights trade off over time.

At $t=0$ the image is pure signal (weight 1.00, no noise). By $t=250$ it's a blend (0.84 signal, 0.55 noise — still recognizable). By $t=500$ noise dominates (0.77 vs 0.63). By $t=1000$ it's almost entirely noise (0.98) with a faint trace of signal (0.20). This smooth schedule is the forward process — completely fixed, no learning. The model's only job is to learn the reverse: given a noisy $x_t$, estimate the noise so you can step back toward a cleaner image. Notice the elegance — because $x_t$ is a simple closed-form blend of the clean image and noise, you can jump directly to any noise level during training (no need to simulate all 1000 steps), and the noise you added is the exact label the model learns to predict. To generate a new image, you start at $t=1000$ (pure random noise) and run the learned reverse steps down to $t=0$, watching a coherent image condense out of the static.

★ Key points to remember
  • Diffusion models generate images by learning to reverse a noising process.
  • Forward (fixed): gradually add noise to an image until it's pure static. Reverse (learned): denoise step by step.
  • Training objective: predict the noise added to an image (self-supervised, MSE loss).
  • To generate: start from random noise and run the learned denoising steps to produce a new image.
  • Text conditioning steers generation; latent diffusion runs in a compressed space for efficiency.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Using $x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\epsilon$, at $\bar\alpha_t = 0.25$ what are the signal and noise weights, and is the image more signal or more noise?
Show solution & reasoning

Signal weight $= \sqrt{0.25} = 0.5$; noise weight $= \sqrt{1 - 0.25} = \sqrt{0.75} \approx 0.87$. So the image is mostly noise (0.87 vs 0.5) — recognizable structure is fading but a trace of the original remains.

Notice the weights satisfy $(\text{signal})^2 + (\text{noise})^2 = \bar\alpha_t + (1-\bar\alpha_t) = 1$ — they're the two legs of a right triangle, so the total "energy" is preserved as the mix rotates from all-signal to all-noise. This is the schedule the model learns to reverse: at this noise level it must estimate that 0.87-weight noise component well enough to step back toward a cleaner image. Early steps (low noise) are easy; the hard, creative work happens at high noise levels where the model must hallucinate plausible structure.

2Diffusion generation is slow — it can take dozens of denoising steps per image, while a GAN (Lesson 7.9) generates in one pass. Given that, why did diffusion still overtake GANs for image generation?
Show solution & reasoning

Despite being slower, diffusion won on quality, diversity, and training stability. GANs are notoriously hard to train (the generator/discriminator balance is delicate and prone to "mode collapse," where the generator produces only a few image types — Lesson 7.9) and tend to cover less of the data distribution. Diffusion's training objective (predict noise, a simple MSE regression) is stable and well-behaved, it produces more diverse and higher-fidelity images, and it scales gracefully with data and compute. The slowness is also being addressed — newer methods (distillation, improved samplers) cut generation to a handful of steps or even one.

The trade-off illustrates a recurring theme: the "best" method isn't the fastest or simplest, but the one that reliably produces the best results and is trainable at scale. Diffusion's stable, scalable training mirrored why the transformer beat RNNs (Track 6) — a method that trains reliably and improves with scale tends to win, even at some runtime cost, because compute keeps getting cheaper while quality and stability are hard-won. It's the bitter lesson (Lesson 7.1) again.

3When you type a prompt like "a corgi surfing," how does a diffusion model use it to steer generation toward that specific image?
Show solution & reasoning

The prompt is conditioning. The text is embedded (Lesson 6.2) by a text encoder, and those embeddings are fed into the denoising network — usually via cross-attention (Lesson 6.8), so at each denoising step the model attends to the prompt and nudges the image toward matching it. Instead of denoising toward any plausible image, it denoises toward a plausible image that matches "a corgi surfing."

A key technique here is classifier-free guidance: the model is run both with and without the prompt, and the difference is amplified to push the result more strongly toward the text — a knob (the "guidance scale") trading prompt-adherence against diversity/realism (turn it too high and images get oversaturated and rigid). So generation is the reverse diffusion process from this lesson, plus attention-based text conditioning from Track 6, plus a guidance knob — a nice synthesis showing how the pieces across the curriculum combine into a real system. The same conditioning idea extends to image-to-image, inpainting, and control (depth maps, poses), all variations on "denoise toward something that satisfies this extra signal."

★ Deep dive: the math behind denoising, and why it works▸

Why does "predict the noise and subtract it" actually produce coherent images, rather than just removing static? The deep reason is that the model, to predict the noise well at a given noise level, must implicitly understand what a real image looks like. Consider a half-noised picture of a face: to separate the signal from the noise, the model must "know" that faces have two eyes, symmetric structure, plausible lighting — so estimating the noise requires learning the structure of natural images. When you then run the reverse process from pure noise, at each step the model asks "if this static were a noisy real image, what would the underlying image be?" and nudges toward it; over many steps those nudges accumulate into a coherent image drawn from the learned distribution. Formally, diffusion is connected to score matching — the model learns the gradient of the data distribution (the "score"), i.e. the direction toward higher-probability (more realistic) images, and denoising is gradient ascent on image realism. So in a satisfying sense, generation is the same "follow the gradient" idea from Track 1, now ascending the probability landscape of images rather than descending a loss.

A few connections place diffusion in the broader generative landscape. It's one of several ways to model a data distribution well enough to sample from it — the others (autoregressive, VAE, GAN) are Lesson 7.9, and each makes different trade-offs in quality, speed, diversity, and training stability. Diffusion currently leads for images, audio, and increasingly video, largely because of its stable training and high sample quality. It also unifies surprisingly with the transformer world: modern image generators often use a transformer (not just a U-Net) as the denoising network, and there's active work on diffusion language models that generate text by denoising rather than left-to-right — blurring the line between the text and image paradigms. The frontier is multimodal generation: single systems that produce and reason over text, images, audio, and video together, combining the architectures across this track. Diffusion is a major pillar of that frontier, and understanding it — add noise, learn to remove it, steer with conditioning — gives you the conceptual key to the entire wave of generative media tools reshaping creative work. The last lesson rounds out the picture with the other generative families, so you can place every one of these systems in a single map.

Diffusion is one way to generate; there are others, each with its own trade-offs. Next, in 6.9 VAEs & GANs in context, we meet the other great generative families and build a single map of where each one fits.
