VAEs & GANs in context

⏱ 22 min Lesson 6.9 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsDiffusion is one of four generative families. VAEs (latent spaces) and GANs (adversarial training) round out the map, with foundational ideas — reparameterization, adversarial games — used throughout modern AI.
By the end, you can
  • Map the four generative families
  • Explain VAEs and the reparameterization trick
  • Explain GANs and mode collapse
  • Match a generative family to a task

Concept A map of how machines generate

Diffusion (Lesson 6.8) is one way to generate data, but it's worth seeing the whole family, because each approach makes different trade-offs and you'll meet them all. Every generative model has the same goal — learn a data distribution well enough to sample new examples from it — but they get there differently.

You've already mastered the most important one: autoregressive models (GPT) generate by predicting the next element, one at a time (Lesson 5.3) — dominant for text and code. This lesson covers the two classic families that preceded diffusion for images: VAEs and GANs. They're less state-of-the-art for image quality now, but their ideas (latent spaces, adversarial training) are foundational and still widely used.

Concept VAEs: a smooth latent space

A Variational Autoencoder (VAE) has two halves: an encoder that compresses an input into a small latent vector, and a decoder that reconstructs the input from it (the dimensionality-reduction idea of Lesson 3.11, made generative). The twist that makes it generate: instead of encoding to a single point, the encoder outputs a distribution (a mean $\mu$ and spread $\sigma$), and you sample a latent from it. This forces the latent space to be smooth and continuous, so you can sample new latents and decode them into new, plausible data.

VAEs tend to produce slightly blurry images (the averaging in their reconstruction loss smooths detail), so they lost the image-quality crown — but their smooth, structured latent space is invaluable: it's used inside latent diffusion (Lesson 6.8 — Stable Diffusion's VAE compresses images so diffusion runs cheaply), for anomaly detection, and wherever you want a meaningful, navigable representation.

Concept GANs: a forger and a detective

A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) trains two networks in competition. The generator turns random noise into fake data; the discriminator tries to tell real data from the generator's fakes. They improve together — the generator gets better at fooling, the discriminator better at catching — until the fakes are realistic. It's a forger-versus-detective arms race.

GANs produced the first truly photorealistic faces and dominated image generation in the late 2010s. But they're notoriously hard to train: the two-network balance is delicate, and they suffer mode collapse (the generator finds a few outputs that fool the discriminator and produces only those, ignoring the data's diversity). Their training instability is a big part of why diffusion — with its stable, simple objective — overtook them for high-quality generation.

Worked example The VAE reparameterization trick

The clever part of a VAE is the reparameterization trick: to sample a latent $z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma)$ in a way that gradients can flow through (for backprop, Lesson 4.3), write it as $z = \mu + \sigma \cdot \epsilon$ with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. The randomness lives in $\epsilon$ (which has no parameters), so gradients pass cleanly through $\mu$ and $\sigma$.

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)

mu, sigma = np.array([0.5, -0.2]), np.array([0.3, 0.4])   # encoder output for one input

# reparameterization: z = mu + sigma * eps,  eps ~ N(0, 1)
samples = np.array([mu + sigma * rng.standard_normal(2) for _ in range(5)])
print("encoder gives  mu =", mu, " sigma =", sigma)
print("5 sampled latents z:\n", samples.round(2))
Outputencoder gives mu = [ 0.5 -0.2] sigma = [0.3 0.4] 5 sampled latents z: [[ 0.54 -0.25] [ 0.69 -0.16] [ 0.34 -0.06] [ 0.89 0.18] [ 0.29 -0.71]]

Each sampled latent $z$ scatters around the mean $\mu = [0.5, -0.2]$ with spread set by $\sigma = [0.3, 0.4]$ — so they cluster near $[0.5, -0.2]$ but vary (one reached $[0.89, 0.18]$, another $[0.29, -0.71]$). Decoding each would give a slightly different but related output — this is how a VAE generates variety. The reparameterization trick is what makes it trainable: by pulling the randomness out into $\epsilon$, the path from the loss back to $\mu$ and $\sigma$ is differentiable (it's just $z = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$, a smooth function of $\mu, \sigma$), so backprop works. Without this trick you'd be trying to backpropagate through a random sampling step, which has no well-defined gradient. It's a small, elegant idea that unlocked training VAEs — and the same reparameterization shows up across machine learning whenever you need gradients through a sampling operation.

★ Key points to remember
  • All generative models learn a data distribution to sample new examples; families differ in how.
  • Autoregressive (GPT): predict next element — dominant for text. Diffusion: denoise — dominant for images.
  • VAE: encode to a latent distribution, decode — smooth latent space, slightly blurry; used inside latent diffusion.
  • GAN: generator vs discriminator — sharp images but unstable training and mode collapse.
  • The reparameterization trick ($z = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$) makes sampling differentiable so VAEs can train.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Match each task to the most fitting generative family and say why: (a) generate fluent text; (b) state-of-the-art photorealistic images; (c) a smooth latent space to interpolate between faces and detect anomalies.
Show solution & reasoning

(a) Text → autoregressive (GPT-style). Text is naturally sequential, and next-token prediction is the proven, dominant approach (Track 5). (b) Photorealistic images → diffusion. Diffusion currently leads on image quality, diversity, and training stability (Lesson 6.8). (c) Smooth latent space / interpolation / anomaly detection → VAE. Its encoder produces a structured, continuous latent space ideal for interpolating ("morph face A into face B") and for flagging inputs that reconstruct poorly (anomalies).

The lesson is that there's no single "best" generative model — you match the family to the task's structure and needs. This is the same model-selection judgment from Track 3, applied to generation: text's sequential structure suits autoregression, image fidelity suits diffusion, and a navigable representation suits VAEs. Knowing the families' strengths lets you pick well, and modern systems often combine them (latent diffusion literally uses a VAE inside a diffusion model).

2GANs generate an image in a single forward pass, while diffusion needs many denoising steps. Yet diffusion won for quality. What does this trade-off teach about choosing methods?
Show solution & reasoning

It teaches that training reliability and output quality usually trump raw inference speed when compute is getting cheaper. GANs are fast at generation but painful to train (unstable, mode collapse), capping their quality and diversity. Diffusion trades slower generation for a stable, simple training objective (predict noise) that scales and produces superior, more diverse results — and the speed gap is being closed by distillation and better samplers.

This mirrors the transformer-over-RNN story (Track 5) and is another face of the "bitter lesson" (Lesson 6.1): methods that train stably and improve predictably with scale tend to win, even at some runtime cost, because you can buy back speed with engineering and cheaper hardware, whereas unstable training is a fundamental ceiling. When evaluating a method, weigh trainability and quality-at-scale heavily — a beautiful demo that's impossible to train reliably loses to a slower method that just works.

3The reparameterization trick "pulls the randomness out" into ε. Explain concretely why you can't just backpropagate through a direct sample $z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma)$.
Show solution & reasoning

Backpropagation needs a deterministic, differentiable path from the loss back to each parameter (Lesson 4.3). If you sample $z$ directly from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma)$, the sampling operation is a black box of randomness — there's no smooth function relating a small change in $\mu$ to the resulting $z$, because $z$ was drawn randomly. The gradient $\partial z / \partial \mu$ isn't well-defined through a raw sampling step, so backprop can't flow to the encoder.

The trick fixes this by rewriting $z = \mu + \sigma \cdot \epsilon$ with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ fixed for that forward pass. Now $z$ is a plain arithmetic function of $\mu$ and $\sigma$ (the randomness sits in $\epsilon$, which has no parameters and needs no gradient), so $\partial z/\partial \mu = 1$ and $\partial z/\partial \sigma = \epsilon$ are perfectly well-defined. The stochasticity is still there (different $\epsilon$ each pass), but it's been moved off the gradient path. This is a recurring pattern in ML — whenever you need gradients through randomness, find a way to express the sample as a deterministic function of the parameters plus parameter-free noise — and it's a small gem of an idea worth remembering.

★ Deep dive: the unifying view, and the multimodal frontier▸

Step back and there's a unifying way to see all four families: each is a different answer to "how do I represent and sample from a complex probability distribution I can't write down?" Autoregressive models factor the distribution into a product of conditionals ($P(\text{token}_t \mid \text{earlier tokens})$) and sample one piece at a time — exact likelihoods, great for discrete sequences, but inherently sequential. VAEs learn a smooth latent space and a decoder, optimizing a bound on the likelihood — structured representations, but blurrier samples. GANs sidestep likelihood entirely and learn to generate via the adversarial game — sharp samples, but unstable and no likelihood. Diffusion learns the gradient (score) of the distribution and samples by iterative denoising — stable, high-quality, but slower. They trade off the same axes: sample quality, diversity (mode coverage), training stability, sampling speed, and whether you get a usable likelihood. No method dominates on all axes, which is why all four persist, each in its niche — and why hybrid approaches (VAE + diffusion in latent diffusion; transformer denoisers; autoregressive models over diffusion latents) are common.

The frontier these families are converging toward is multimodal generation: single systems that generate and reason across text, images, audio, and video together. The transformer (Track 5) turned out to be a near-universal backbone — it works for text tokens, image patches, audio frames, and video — so modern systems increasingly use one architecture with different "heads" or conditioning for different modalities, often combining the generative paradigms (a transformer that does autoregressive text and conditions a diffusion image generator, say). The implications are sweeping: models that watch a video and narrate it, generate an image from a description and then edit it by conversation, or produce synchronized audio and video. Understanding the generative families individually — their mechanisms and trade-offs — is what lets you understand these combined systems, because they're built from exactly these pieces, wired together. You've now seen all of them: the autoregressive transformer you built across Track 5, and here the VAE, GAN, and diffusion. That completes the generative-AI picture, and with it the core of how modern AI creates — text, images, and increasingly everything.
