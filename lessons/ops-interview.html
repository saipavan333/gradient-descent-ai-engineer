Track 9 · MLOps & Serving

Interview check: MLOps

⏱ 15 min Lesson 9.10
Why this existsMLOps interviews test whether you can run a model, not just train one: the lifecycle, train/serve skew, drift, serving and scaling trade-offs, monitoring, CI/CD. This checkpoint rehearses those with model answers.
By the end, you can
  • Walk through notebook-to-production
  • Debug offline-good-but-prod-bad
  • Explain drift and retraining
  • Reason about serving, scaling, and CI/CD

Concept What an MLOps interview tests

MLOps questions separate people who can train a model from people who can run one. Interviewers probe whether you understand that deploying a model is the start of the hard part, not the end — whether you grasp the lifecycle loop, can reason about serving and scaling trade-offs, and know why models decay and how to keep them healthy. The signal they're listening for is production maturity: an instinct that the system will fail in subtle ways and a vocabulary of practices for catching and fixing those failures. The model answers below show that reasoning.

Model answer "Walk me through taking a model from notebook to production."

"I think of it as the lifecycle loop. Train stage: I run experiments with tracking (MLflow or similar) so every run's params, metrics, and artifacts are logged and the winner is reproducible — same data, code, environment, and seed. Data stage: features come from a pipeline — ideally a feature store — so they're computed one way and served identically at training and inference, avoiding train/serve skew. Deploy stage: the chosen model goes into a registry, versioned and staged staging → production, then gets packaged in a container with its exact dependencies and served behind an endpoint, with rollback available because old versions are archived. Serve stage: I scale horizontally with replicas behind a load balancer and autoscaling, and optimize cost with caching, batching, and maybe quantization. Monitor stage: I watch both operational metrics (latency, errors) and model quality (drift, prediction distribution, accuracy when labels arrive), and wire drift detection to trigger retraining — and I automate the whole thing with CI/CD/CT so changes are tested, validated against the baseline, and deployed reliably. The thread through all of it: deploying the model is the middle of the story; keeping it healthy as the world changes is the job."

Strong close: emphasize that an ML system is never "done" — it decays and must be continuously maintained.

Model answer "A model performs great offline but poorly in production. How do you debug it?"

"This pattern — strong offline, weak in production, model unchanged — points at the data, not the model, so I'd check two things first. Train/serve skew: are the features computed in production identical to those at training? I'd log the actual served features and compare them, for the same entities, against the training pipeline's output; a mismatch (different defaults, windows, rounding, or a feature computed differently in the serving code) is the classic culprit, and the fix is to unify the computation through a shared feature definition or store. Drift: has the live data moved away from the training distribution since the model was built? I'd compare input distributions and the prediction distribution against training references. I'd also sanity-check that offline evaluation wasn't optimistic — data leakage or a non-representative test set can inflate offline metrics. The discipline is to not assume the model is the problem: the model is identical, so something about the data it's fed or the world it's seeing changed, and the job is to localize which. That instinct — offline-good-but-prod-bad means look at the data plumbing — is what experience teaches."

Model answer "Why do models degrade in production, and what do you do about it?"

"They degrade because of drift — the model was trained on a snapshot of the world, and the world moves away from it. Two kinds: data drift, where the input distribution shifts (you start seeing different inputs than training), and concept drift, where the relationship between inputs and the correct output changes (the same inputs now imply a different answer) — the second is subtler because inputs can look unchanged. The model itself is fine; the assumption that the future resembles the training past has eroded. What I do: monitor for it — data drift I can often catch label-free by comparing input distributions; concept drift usually needs outcomes — and retrain on fresh data when drift crosses a threshold, either on a schedule or triggered by detection. Retraining isn't a failure, it's normal maintenance. The deeper point I'd make is that drift is inherent to deploying ML in a changing world, not a flaw to eliminate — which is exactly why MLOps treats the model as a living system with a monitor-and-retrain loop, rather than a finished artifact."

Model answer "How would you serve a model to millions of users affordably?"

"Two prongs: scale out, and make each prediction cheaper. Scale out with horizontal scaling — many identical replicas behind a load balancer — plus autoscaling so capacity tracks traffic, giving me enough at peak without paying for idle servers off-peak. Cheaper per prediction with caching (reuse results for repeated inputs — huge when inputs recur), batching (group requests into one pass, which GPUs love), quantization or distillation (a smaller, faster model at a small accuracy cost), and the right hardware. I'd also choose the serving pattern by need: online endpoints for real-time, batch for bulk scoring where nobody's waiting. And I'd hold the latency budget in mind — the model is only one slice of the user's total response time, so sometimes a slightly less accurate but much faster model is the correct production choice, because the best offline model isn't always the best model to serve. The framing I'd stress is that a model is trained once but served forever, so inference cost dominates and optimizing it is where much of the real engineering — and money — lives, especially for LLMs."

Model answer "How is CI/CD for ML different from CI/CD for regular software?"

"Regular CI/CD ships code, so it tests and deploys code. ML systems depend on code plus data plus a trained model, so the pipeline has to handle all three — which adds CT, continuous training: the pipeline can retrain on new data, validate the result, and deploy it automatically. The key difference is the deployment gate. In software, 'tests pass' is enough to ship. In ML, there's an extra gate: the new model must actually be as good as or better than the production baseline on a validation set — because retraining on fresh data doesn't guarantee a better model, and you must never silently ship a regression. So ML testing is broader: unit tests for code, plus data validation (schema, ranges, quality) and model validation (quality vs baseline, and often fairness across slices). That baseline gate is precisely what makes automated retraining safe — a bad retrain gets caught and rejected instead of going live. The principle I'd articulate: automation multiplies your discipline, so CT without strong validation gates is a machine for shipping bad models fast, while CT with good gates lets you safely deploy improvements often."

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • What is MLOps? The engineering of deploying, serving, monitoring, and maintaining ML in production — running the lifecycle loop.
  • Train/serve skew? Features computed differently at training vs serving; fix with one shared feature definition (feature store).
  • Data vs concept drift? Input distribution shifts vs the input→output relationship changes; both need retraining.
  • Why a model registry? Versioned models, explicit promotion, and instant rollback (old versions archived, not deleted).
  • Online vs batch serving? Real-time low-latency endpoint vs scheduled high-throughput bulk scoring.
  • Scaling levers? Replicas + load balancer + autoscaling; caching, batching, quantization, better hardware.
  • Two monitoring layers? Operational (latency, errors) and model quality (drift, accuracy) — green ops ≠ good model.
  • Reproducibility needs? Pin data, params, code, environment, and the random seed.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"Design the end-to-end MLOps system for a recommendation model at a large e-commerce company." (3-minute answer.)
Show a strong answer

"Data & features: a feature store computing user/item features consistently for training and serving, fed by batch pipelines for slow features and streaming for fresh ones (recent activity), with point-in-time correctness to avoid leakage. Training: tracked experiments for reproducibility, with a pipeline that can retrain on recent data. Registry & deploy: versioned models, promotion staging → production with canary rollout (send 5% of traffic to the new model, watch metrics, ramp if healthy) and instant rollback. Serving: a hybrid — batch-precompute candidate recommendations cheaply, then re-rank online with live context for freshness; scaled horizontally with autoscaling for traffic spikes (think Black Friday), with caching for popular items. Monitoring: operational metrics plus model quality — recommendation distribution, input drift, and business metrics like click-through; delayed labels (did they buy?) feed slower accuracy tracking. Automation: CI/CD/CT retrains on a schedule plus drift triggers, validates each new model against the baseline and on key segments before deploying. The themes I'm hitting — consistent features, safe gradual deployment, scale for spikes, two-layer monitoring, and automated-but-gated retraining — are exactly the production concerns this system lives or dies on."

This shows you can assemble the whole track into a coherent system tuned to a domain's realities (spiky traffic, delayed labels, freshness needs) — the system-design judgment senior roles want.

2"Your company has one model in production today but plans to have fifty within a year. What MLOps investments do you prioritize, and why?
Show a strong answer

"The shift from one model to fifty is fundamentally about automation and standardization — what you can do by hand for one becomes impossible for fifty. I'd prioritize, roughly in order: (1) Reproducible training with experiment tracking, so every model is rebuildable and comparable — the foundation everything else needs. (2) A model registry for versioning, deployment, and rollback across many models, so you always know what's live and can revert safely. (3) Automated deployment pipelines (CI/CD/CT) with validation gates, because hand-deploying fifty models is untenable and ungated automation is dangerous — the gates let you deploy often and safely. (4) Standardized monitoring across all models — a common framework for operational and model-quality metrics plus drift, so you're not building bespoke monitoring per model and you catch decay everywhere. (5) Shared feature infrastructure if the models share data, for consistency and reuse. The reasoning is that scaling the number of models is a different problem from improving one model: it's about building platforms and automation so each new model rides on shared, reliable infrastructure rather than a hand-crafted pipeline. I'd sequence by what hurts most first and what unblocks the rest — reproducibility and the registry are foundational, then automation, then standardized monitoring."

Recognizing that 'one model vs fifty' is a platform/automation problem — not just more of the same — and sequencing the investments by leverage is exactly the strategic MLOps thinking that distinguishes a senior candidate.

3"Explain to a skeptical engineering manager why MLOps deserves real investment, when 'the model already works.'"
Show a strong answer

"'The model works' is true today and temporary. The single most important fact about ML systems is that they decay on their own — the world drifts away from the training data, so a model that's accurate now will quietly get worse, with no crash to warn us, until it's costing us real money in bad decisions. Without MLOps, we won't even know it's happening, because operational dashboards stay green while quality rots. MLOps is the investment that catches and fixes that decay: monitoring to detect it, retraining pipelines to fix it, and the versioning and deployment machinery to do so safely. Beyond decay, it's also what lets us move fast and safely — reproducibility so we can debug and rebuild, registries and rollback so a bad deploy is a non-event instead of an outage, and automation so we can ship improvements frequently without manual risk. The cost of skipping it isn't visible at launch; it shows up months later as a silently degraded model, an unreproducible result we can't fix, or a bad deployment we can't roll back — and as the inability to scale beyond a handful of models. The framing I'd leave him with: we're not shipping a model, we're committing to operating one, and MLOps is the difference between a model that works at launch and a system that keeps working. The ROI is avoided incidents, faster iteration, and the ability to scale — which is exactly what turns ML from a demo into a durable business capability."

Framing MLOps' value in terms the manager cares about — silent decay as a hidden cost, plus speed, safety, and scale — and positioning it as 'operating a system, not shipping a model' is precisely the business-level communication that lands this argument.

★ Deep dive: thinking like an ML engineer, end to end▸

The strongest MLOps answers share one mental model: a deployed model is a living system in a changing world, not a finished artifact. Everything in the track flows from that. Because the world changes, models drift, so you must monitor and retrain — the lifecycle loop. Because the model depends on data and code together, you must version and validate all three, not just code — reproducibility, feature stores, registries, CI/CD/CT. Because a model is trained once but served forever, inference cost and reliability dominate — serving patterns, scaling, optimization. Because deployment is risky and offline metrics are an incomplete read on reality, you make every change reversible and gradually validated — staging, canary, rollback, baseline gates. When you reason about any MLOps problem, the expert move is to locate it on the loop and ask the production question: how will this fail silently, what signal would warn me, and what's the reversible, automated response? Candidates who frame answers this way — "I'd look at the data first because the model is unchanged," "I'd validate against the baseline before deploying," "green ops doesn't mean a good model" — immediately signal they've operated real systems, because those instincts only come from having watched models decay and deployments go wrong.

Three habits sharpen MLOps reasoning. First, distrust the happy path: assume the model will degrade, the data will break, and the deployment will sometimes be worse, and build the machinery (monitoring, validation, rollback) to catch each — production maturity is fundamentally this defensive instinct. Second, separate the layers: when something's wrong, is it operational (system) or model (quality)? is it the data (skew, drift) or the model itself? is it offline evaluation being optimistic or production genuinely different? — localizing the failure is most of the battle, and the track has given you the map. Third, connect to the foundations: drift is the i.i.d. assumption (Track 3) decaying over time; serving constraints are why the best offline model isn't always the right one; monitoring and gates are the same defense-in-depth instinct as agent guardrails (Track 8). Seeing MLOps not as a bag of tools but as the disciplined practice of keeping a model's generalization valid as the world moves — with the lifecycle loop as the organizing idea and automation as the way to scale it — is the system-level understanding that the best teams hire for. You've now built that understanding end to end: you can take a model from a notebook to a reliably-served, monitored, self-maintaining production system, and reason about every stage. That's the difference between someone who trains models and someone who ships them — and it's exactly what the final tracks build on as they turn to evaluating and governing these systems responsibly."
