The training loop, loss & optimizers

⏱ 27 min Lesson 4.4 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsThe training loop — zero_grad, forward, loss, backward, step — is the heartbeat of every trained model, plus the optimizers (SGD, momentum, Adam) that make it converge.
By the end, you can
  • Write the canonical training loop
  • Explain SGD, momentum, and Adam
  • Train a network to learn XOR from scratch
  • Diagnose learning-rate problems

Concept The loop that turns random weights into a trained model

You now have all three pieces: a forward pass (compute a prediction), a loss (measure how wrong it is, Lesson 3.4), and backprop (get every gradient). Training is just repeating these in a loop, with one more step — using the gradients to update the weights:

One pass over the entire dataset is an epoch; you usually train for many. Within an epoch you process the data in mini-batches (Lesson 1.8) — a few dozen examples at a time — so each step is fast and the gradient is a good-enough estimate. In PyTorch the loop is four lines you'll write for every model you ever train:

for X_batch, y_batch in loader:        # one mini-batch at a time
    optimizer.zero_grad()              # clear old gradients (they accumulate!)
    loss = loss_fn(model(X_batch), y_batch)   # forward + measure error
    loss.backward()                    # backprop: fill every .grad
    optimizer.step()                   # update weights: w <- w - lr * grad

Memorize that rhythm — zero_grad → forward/loss → backward → step — because it is the literal heartbeat of training every network in this course and beyond. The optimizer is the object that performs the weight update; loss_fn is your loss (MSE for regression, cross-entropy for classification).

Concept Optimizers: smarter ways to step downhill

The simplest optimizer is plain SGD (stochastic gradient descent): $w \leftarrow w - \eta\, \nabla L$, step opposite the gradient (Lesson 1.8). It works, but it can zig-zag down narrow valleys and crawl across flat regions. Three upgrades fix this, and they're all one-line swaps in code:

  • Momentum accumulates a running "velocity" of recent gradients, so it powers through flat spots and damps the zig-zag — like a ball rolling downhill (Lesson 1.8's deep dive).
  • RMSProp gives each weight its own step size based on the recent size of its gradients, so steady directions move faster and noisy ones slower.
  • Adam combines both ideas (momentum + per-weight scaling). It's the default for most deep learning: robust, fast, and forgiving of the learning-rate choice. You'll write torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3) constantly.

The learning rate $\eta$ remains the most important dial (Lesson 1.8): too small crawls, too big diverges. Often you also use a schedule that lowers it over training — big steps early, fine steps late — to settle precisely into the minimum.

Worked example Training a network to learn XOR, from scratch

XOR (output 1 when exactly one input is 1) is the classic problem no linear model can solve (Lesson 4.1). We'll train a tiny network — 2 inputs → 8 hidden (tanh) → 1 output (sigmoid) — with a hand-written training loop: forward, MSE loss, manual backprop, SGD step. Watch it go from random to correct.

From a random start (MSE 0.26, predictions near 0.5 — coin flips) the network drove its error to almost zero and learned XOR exactly: $[0.02, 0.98, 0.98, 0.03] \approx [0, 1, 1, 0]$. Nothing here is magic — it's the loop running 4,000 times, each iteration nudging the weights downhill by their gradients. This is the same machinery, scaled up, that trains GPT. The PyTorch version replaces the hand-written backward and step with two calls:

import torch, torch.nn as nn
model = nn.Sequential(nn.Linear(2,8), nn.Tanh(), nn.Linear(8,1), nn.Sigmoid())
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=0.05)
loss_fn = nn.MSELoss()

for epoch in range(2000):
    optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss = loss_fn(model(Xt), Yt)
    loss.backward()       # autograd does the manual backprop above
    optimizer.step()      # Adam does the weight update

Same four-beat loop, but backward() replaces the calculus we wrote by hand and Adam replaces plain SGD — usually converging in fewer epochs. That's the whole workflow you'll reuse for every network from here on.

★ Key points to remember
  • The training loop repeats: zero_grad → forward → loss → backward → optimizer.step.
  • An epoch is one pass over the data; you train in mini-batches over many epochs.
  • SGD steps opposite the gradient; momentum smooths it; Adam (momentum + per-weight scaling) is the robust default.
  • The learning rate is the key dial; a schedule that lowers it over time often helps.
  • In PyTorch, backward() + optimizer.step() replace hand-written backprop and updates.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A beginner writes a training loop but forgets optimizer.zero_grad(). What happens to the gradients, and how would the symptoms look?
Show solution & reasoning

PyTorch accumulates gradients into .grad each time backward() is called (Lesson 4.3). Without zeroing, each step's gradient piles onto all previous steps' gradients, so the effective gradient grows every iteration. The symptoms: the loss behaves erratically — often exploding or oscillating wildly — because the updates are using a corrupted, ever-growing gradient rather than the current batch's.

The fix is the one line: call optimizer.zero_grad() at the top of each iteration. It's the single most common beginner bug, and recognizing the "loss explodes / training is nonsense" symptom and checking for a missing zero_grad() will save you many times.

2Your training loss decreases nicely but extremely slowly — after 50 epochs it's barely moved. List two things you'd try, and why.
Show solution & reasoning

(1) Raise the learning rate (e.g., 10×). A too-small $\eta$ makes tiny steps, so the loss creeps down — the classic "slow and smooth" signature (Lesson 1.8). (2) Switch to Adam (if on plain SGD), which adapts per-parameter step sizes and usually makes much faster progress with little tuning.

Other moves: check that inputs are scaled/normalized (Lesson 2.5) — unscaled features make the loss landscape lopsided and slow; and ensure the data and labels are correct. The diagnostic logic is the mirror of "loss explodes": slow-and-smooth means be more aggressive (higher LR, better optimizer); jagged-and-exploding means be gentler (lower LR).

3In the worked example, the manual code computed dW2 = A1.T @ dZ2 and so on. In the PyTorch version, which single line replaces all of that hand-written backprop, and what replaces the manual W -= lr*dW updates?
Show solution & reasoning

loss.backward() replaces all the manual gradient computation (dZ2, dW2, dZ1, dW1, and the biases) — autograd derives and computes them for you (Lesson 4.3). And optimizer.step() replaces the manual W1 -= lr*dW1; ... updates — the optimizer applies the update rule to every parameter.

That's the payoff of the framework: you write only the forward computation (the model) and the loss, and the two lines backward() / step() handle the calculus and the bookkeeping. The hand-written version exists in this lesson so you know exactly what those two lines do — they're not magic, just the loop you wrote by hand.

★ Deep dive: inside Adam, batch size, and learning-rate schedules▸

Adam maintains two running averages per parameter: the mean of recent gradients (momentum, "first moment") and the mean of recent squared gradients ("second moment"). The update divides the momentum term by the square root of the second-moment term, so a parameter with small, consistent gradients takes big steps while one with large, noisy gradients takes small steps — each parameter gets its own effective learning rate. A bias-correction factor keeps early steps sane. This adaptivity is why Adam "just works" with lr=1e-3 on a huge range of problems, and why it's the default in nearly every modern training script. (Plain SGD with momentum, well-tuned, can generalize slightly better on some vision tasks — a known trade-off — but Adam is the safer first choice.)

Batch size trades noise for speed. Tiny batches give noisy gradients (which can help escape bad spots but wobble), huge batches give precise gradients and use hardware efficiently but can generalize slightly worse and need learning-rate adjustment. Typical values are 32–256; the rule of thumb "scale the learning rate roughly with the batch size" exists because a bigger batch's gradient is less noisy and can support a bigger step.

Schedules are nearly universal in serious training. Cosine annealing smoothly decays the learning rate to near zero over training; step decay drops it at milestones; and a warmup — ramping the learning rate up for the first few hundred steps before decaying — is essential for training transformers (Track 5), because the early, near-random model would otherwise take destructive giant steps. The pattern "big steps to make progress, small steps to settle" is the through-line, and it's why a constant learning rate, while fine for our XOR toy, is rarely optimal for real models.

Your network can learn. But a network with enough capacity will happily memorize its training data. Next, in 4.5 Regularization: dropout, weight decay, batchnorm, we add the techniques that keep deep networks generalizing instead of overfitting.
