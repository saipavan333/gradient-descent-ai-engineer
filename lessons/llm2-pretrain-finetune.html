Track 6 · LLMs & Generative AI

Pretraining vs fine-tuning

⏱ 25 min Lesson 6.2 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsLLMs are made in two phases: expensive pretraining that builds knowledge, and cheap fine-tuning that specializes behavior. Knowing which — versus prompting or retrieval — to reach for is core practical judgment.
By the end, you can
  • Contrast pretraining and fine-tuning
  • Explain SFT / instruction tuning
  • Decide between prompt, retrieve, and fine-tune
  • Recognize catastrophic forgetting

Concept Two training stages: build knowledge, then specialize

An LLM is created in two distinct training phases, and they could not be more different in scale and purpose. This is exactly the transfer-learning idea from Lesson 4.9 — pretrain a general model once, then adapt it cheaply — applied at massive scale.

Pretraining is the self-supervised phase from Lesson 5.3: predict the next token across a gigantic corpus (much of the web, books, code). It needs no human labels — the text labels itself — and it's where the model learns grammar, facts, and reasoning. It is extraordinarily expensive (millions of dollars, thousands of GPUs, months) and done once per base model. The result is a knowledgeable but not-yet-helpful base model.

Fine-tuning takes that pretrained base and continues training it on a much smaller, labeled dataset for a specific purpose. The most important kind is supervised fine-tuning (SFT), also called instruction tuning: train on thousands of high-quality (instruction → response) examples so the model learns to follow instructions and behave like an assistant rather than just autocomplete. Fine-tuning is cheap (hours on a few GPUs, or even one with the tricks in Lesson 6.6) because the model already knows language — you're just steering it.

Concept Fine-tune, retrieve, or just prompt?

Fine-tuning isn't always the answer. In production you usually choose among three ways to get an LLM to do what you want, in roughly increasing order of effort:

The guidance: prompt first — it's free, instant, and handles a huge range of tasks via instructions and examples. If the model needs knowledge it doesn't have (private documents, fresh data), add retrieval (RAG) rather than fine-tuning, because you can't easily fine-tune in new facts reliably. Reach for fine-tuning when you need a consistent behavior: a specific output format, a particular style or tone, a specialized skill, or to make a smaller/cheaper model reliably do one thing. A useful mantra: prompting changes what you ask, retrieval changes what it knows, fine-tuning changes how it behaves.

Worked example What supervised fine-tuning changes

SFT teaches the model to concentrate its probability on helpful responses. To see the effect concretely, imagine the model choosing among three completions to a question — a helpful answer, a curt one, and an off-topic one — and look at the probabilities a base model versus a fine-tuned model assigns (toy logits for illustration; the softmax is real).

The base model spreads its probability almost evenly (0.37 / 0.33 / 0.30) — it might autocomplete with any of the three, including the unhelpful ones, because all are plausible continuations of text. After SFT on demonstrations of good responses, the model concentrates 88% of its probability on the helpful completion. The knowledge didn't change — pretraining already taught it language — but its behavior did: it now reliably prefers the helpful answer. That shift from "knows things" to "does the helpful thing" is the entire point of fine-tuning, and it's why a base model and a chat model feel so different despite sharing the same weights underneath the fine-tune.

★ Key points to remember
  • Pretraining: self-supervised next-token on a huge corpus → broad knowledge. Expensive, done once.
  • Fine-tuning: supervised, on a small labeled set → specialized behavior. Cheap, done often.
  • SFT / instruction tuning trains on (instruction → response) pairs so the model follows instructions.
  • It's transfer learning (Lesson 4.9) at scale: reuse the pretrained model, adapt cheaply.
  • Choose the tool: prompt (change the ask) → retrieve (change the knowledge) → fine-tune (change the behavior).

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You want an LLM to always reply in a strict JSON format with specific fields, no matter the input. Would you prompt, retrieve, or fine-tune — and why?
Show solution & reasoning

Start by prompting (instructing "respond only in this JSON schema" with an example) — often that's enough and costs nothing. If the model still occasionally breaks format on tricky inputs and you need it reliably, fine-tune on many (input → correct-JSON) examples. This is a "consistent behavior/ format" need, which is exactly fine-tuning's sweet spot — you're not adding knowledge, you're enforcing a behavior.

You would not use retrieval, since the need is about output format, not missing knowledge. Fine-tuning for format/style also lets a smaller, cheaper model do the job reliably, which matters at scale. The general lesson: format and style consistency → fine-tune; missing facts → retrieve; one-off or flexible needs → prompt.

2Pretraining a frontier model costs millions of dollars and months, but a company can fine-tune one in an afternoon. Why is there such an enormous gap?
Show solution & reasoning

Pretraining must learn everything — language, facts, reasoning — from scratch, which requires processing trillions of tokens with billions of parameters across thousands of GPUs, many times over. It's building the entire knowledge base. Fine-tuning starts from a model that already knows all that; it only needs to nudge the weights to adjust behavior, using a tiny dataset (thousands of examples) for a short time. You're editing, not building.

This gap is precisely the economic engine of modern AI and the transfer-learning principle (Lesson 4.9) at planetary scale: a few well-resourced labs pay the enormous one-time pretraining cost, and everyone else cheaply adapts the result. It's why "fine-tune an existing model" — not "train your own" — is the right default for almost every application, and why techniques to make fine-tuning even cheaper (Lesson 6.6) matter so much.

3A team fine-tunes a general chat model heavily on legal documents and finds it now writes great legal text but has gotten worse at everyday conversation and basic math. What happened, and how might they avoid it?
Show solution & reasoning

This is catastrophic forgetting: aggressively fine-tuning on a narrow domain shifted the weights so far toward legal text that the model degraded on capabilities it previously had. Fine-tuning overwrites, not just adds — push too hard on one distribution and you erode others.

Mitigations: use a lower learning rate and fewer epochs (gentler updates preserve more); mix in general data alongside the legal data so the model rehearses its old skills; or use parameter-efficient fine-tuning like LoRA (Lesson 6.6), which trains a small add-on and leaves the original weights frozen, structurally preventing the base capabilities from being overwritten. The LoRA approach is popular precisely because it adapts behavior while protecting the model's general competence — and it's the subject of a later lesson in this track.

★ Deep dive: data quality, the data wall, and what pretraining really learns▸

A striking finding of recent years is that data quality often matters more than quantity for fine-tuning. The influential "LIMA" result showed that a base model fine-tuned on just ~1,000 carefully curated instruction examples could rival models tuned on hundreds of thousands of mediocre ones — suggesting the base model already "knows" how to be helpful, and SFT mostly needs to surface that behavior with a few clean demonstrations, not teach it from scratch. The implication: for fine-tuning, invest in a small, high-quality, diverse dataset rather than scraping a huge noisy one. (Pretraining is different — there, scale of data is paramount, though quality filtering still helps a lot.)

Pretraining itself faces a looming constraint sometimes called the data wall: the largest models are now trained on a substantial fraction of all the high-quality text humans have ever written, and we may be running out of fresh, high-quality data to keep scaling. Responses include training on synthetic data (text generated by other models), multimodal data (images, audio, video), and squeezing more learning out of existing data with better methods. How the field navigates the data wall is one of the open questions determining whether the scaling trend continues.

Conceptually, it's worth appreciating what pretraining accomplishes that fine-tuning can't. Pretraining is where the model builds its world model — its compressed representation of how language, facts, and reasoning work — by being forced to predict text about everything. Fine-tuning can access and shape that world model but largely can't expand it: you can't reliably teach a model significant new facts by fine-tuning (it tends to either ignore them or hallucinate around them), which is the deep reason retrieval, not fine-tuning, is the right tool for injecting knowledge. Understanding this division of labor — pretraining builds the mind, fine-tuning shapes its behavior, retrieval supplies fresh facts, prompting directs it in the moment — is the mental model that makes you effective with LLMs, and it organizes the rest of this track and the two that follow.

Fine-tuning makes a model follow instructions — but how do we make it genuinely helpful, honest, and harmless, matching human preferences? Next, in 6.3 Instruction tuning & RLHF/DPO, we meet the alignment step that turns a capable model into a trustworthy assistant.
