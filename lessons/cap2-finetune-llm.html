Capstone 2: fine-tune a small LLM (LoRA)

⏱ 120 min Lesson 13.3 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsFine-tune a small LLM with LoRA — transfer learning for language. Freeze the model and train a tiny low-rank adapter (<1% of params), so customization fits on one GPU. How modern LLM fine-tuning actually happens.
By the end, you can
  • Explain LoRA and the parameter savings
  • Choose fine-tuning vs RAG (behavior vs knowledge)
  • Plan and evaluate the fine-tune project
  • Connect LoRA to transfer learning

Concept The project: adapt an LLM to your task, cheaply

This capstone is transfer learning's language analog: take a pretrained language model and fine-tune it to your specific task or style — but efficiently, using LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation). Full fine-tuning of a modern LLM means updating billions of parameters, which needs enormous memory and compute — out of reach for most. LoRA makes fine-tuning affordable: instead of updating the whole model, you freeze it and train a tiny add-on adapter (well under 1% of the parameters), getting most of the benefit at a fraction of the cost — often on a single GPU. Building this demonstrates that you understand modern, practical LLM customization, a highly sought skill.

How LoRA works: fine-tuning a weight matrix means learning an update to it. LoRA's insight is that this update can be well-approximated by a low-rank matrix — the product of two much smaller matrices — so instead of learning the full (huge) update, you learn those two small matrices (the adapter), which have far fewer parameters. You freeze the original weights and train only the adapter; at inference, the adapter's contribution is added to the frozen weights. Because the adapter is tiny, training needs little memory and compute, and you can keep many task-specific adapters and swap them on the same base model. LoRA (and its quantized variant QLoRA) is how customization of large models happens in practice — you almost never full-fine-tune a big LLM anymore.

Worked example Why LoRA is so cheap: the parameter math

The whole point of LoRA is the parameter reduction, and the math is striking. For one weight matrix of size d×d, full fine-tuning updates d² parameters; LoRA replaces that with two small matrices of size d×r and r×d (rank r), training only their parameters:

d, r = 1000, 8
full = d * d                      # full fine-tune updates d x d weights
lora = d * r + r * d              # LoRA: two small low-rank matrices (rank r)
print(f"full fine-tune params : {full:,}")
print(f"LoRA (rank {r}) params : {lora:,}")
print(f"trainable: {lora/full:.1%} of full  ({full//lora}x fewer)")
Outputfull fine-tune params : 1,000,000 LoRA (rank 8) params : 16,000 trainable: 1.6% of full (62x fewer)

For a single 1000×1000 weight matrix, full fine-tuning trains 1,000,000 parameters; LoRA with rank 8 trains just 16,000 — 1.6% of the full count, 62× fewer. Scaled across a billion-parameter model, that's the difference between needing a cluster of high-memory GPUs and fitting on a single one, because you only store and update gradients/optimizer state for the tiny adapter, not the whole model. And you lose surprisingly little quality: the low-rank update captures most of what fine-tuning would achieve for adapting behavior. This is why LoRA democratized LLM customization — it turned "only big labs can fine-tune" into "anyone with one GPU can." In the real project you'd use a library (like Hugging Face's PEFT) to apply LoRA to a small open LLM: load a base model, wrap it with a LoRA config (specifying the rank and which layers to adapt), and train on your task data — the library handles freezing the base and training only the adapters. QLoRA goes further by also quantizing the frozen base model to 4-bit, shrinking memory even more so you can fine-tune a fairly large model on a single consumer GPU. The parameter math you just computed is the core reason it all works: a low-rank adapter is a tiny fraction of the parameters, so training it is cheap — which is the insight that makes modern LLM fine-tuning accessible, and exactly what this project demonstrates you understand.

★ Key points to remember
  • The project: fine-tune a small LLM to your task/style using LoRA — modern, affordable LLM customization.
  • Full fine-tuning updates billions of parameters (huge cost); LoRA freezes the model and trains a tiny low-rank adapter (<1%).
  • The adapter approximates the weight update as a product of two small matrices — far fewer parameters, little quality loss.
  • Benefits: trains on a single GPU, and you can keep and swap adapters on one base model; QLoRA adds 4-bit quantization for even less memory.
  • It's transfer learning for language — you almost never full-fine-tune a large LLM in practice.

2Someone wants to "fine-tune an LLM so it knows our company's product documentation." Is fine-tuning the right approach?
Show reasoning

"Probably not — this is a knowledge problem, and fine-tuning is the wrong tool for injecting specific facts; RAG is almost certainly the better choice (Capstone 3, Lesson 12.4). Here's the reasoning: fine-tuning instills behavior and patterns, but it's an unreliable way to teach specific facts — the model may not memorize them accurately, will blur details, can't cite sources, and can't be updated without retraining every time the docs change. Product documentation is exactly the kind of specific, frequently-updated knowledge that RAG handles far better: retrieve the relevant doc at query time and put it in the context, so the model answers grounded in the actual current text, can cite it, and updates instantly when you change the document store. So I'd push back and recommend RAG for 'knowing the documentation.'

When would fine-tuning help here? If the goal were also to shape how the assistant responds — a specific support tone, format, or the ability to follow a particular interaction style — fine-tuning could complement RAG (a fine-tuned model with RAG). But for the core need ('answer questions from our docs'), RAG is the right primary tool, and fine-tuning alone would be expensive, unreliable, and hard to keep current. The reasoning skill — and why this is a great interview-style question — is diagnosing the real need (knowledge vs behavior) and matching the tool to it, rather than reaching for fine-tuning because it sounds sophisticated. Recognizing that 'the model should know our docs' is a RAG problem, not a fine-tuning problem, and being able to explain why (facts vs behavior, updatability, citations), demonstrates exactly the practical LLM judgment that distinguishes someone who understands these tools from someone who's just heard of them. It also naturally motivates the next capstone — RAG — as the right approach for this extremely common need."

3Connect LoRA to the transfer learning of Capstone 1 — what's the shared principle, and why does it matter so much?
Show reasoning

The shared principle is efficiently adapting a large pretrained model to a new task by training only a small part of it, rather than training from scratch or updating everything. In Capstone 1, transfer learning freezes a pretrained vision backbone and trains only a small new head; in this capstone, LoRA freezes a pretrained language model and trains only a small adapter. Both exploit the same insight: a big model pretrained on massive data has already learned rich, general-purpose representations (of images, or of language), so adapting it to your task requires learning only a small task-specific delta, not relearning everything. This is parameter-efficient transfer learning — the image and language versions of one idea — and recognizing them as the same principle is a sign of real understanding.

It matters enormously because it's how modern applied AI actually works. The dominant paradigm across the field is: large models are pretrained once, at great cost, by those who can afford it, and then everyone else adapts them efficiently to specific tasks with a fraction of the data and compute — transfer learning for vision, LoRA/QLoRA and prompting for language. This democratizes AI: you don't need Google-scale resources to build a strong custom model; you need a pretrained model and a smart, cheap adaptation technique. It's also why the skills these capstones teach are so valuable — the bottleneck for most applied AI work isn't training giant models (few do that), it's adapting them well to real problems, which is exactly what transfer learning and LoRA are. The deeper connection running through the whole curriculum is that pretrained models are the foundation and efficient adaptation is the craft: you saw it with fine-tuning vs RAG vs prompting for LLMs (Track 6), and you're now building it hands-on. Understanding that Capstones 1 and 2 are the same principle in two modalities — reuse the expensive pretrained representation, train only the cheap task-specific part — gives you a unifying mental model for how nearly all practical AI systems are built on top of large pretrained models, which is the single most important paradigm to internalize for real-world AI engineering."

★ Deep dive: parameter-efficient fine-tuning and the pretrain-then-adapt paradigm▸

LoRA is the best-known of a family called parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods — techniques that adapt a large pretrained model by training only a small number of parameters. Beyond LoRA, the family includes approaches like adapter layers (small trainable modules inserted between frozen layers) and prompt/prefix tuning (learning a small set of "soft prompt" vectors that steer the frozen model). They all share LoRA's goal: capture the adaptation in far fewer parameters than full fine-tuning, so customization is cheap. LoRA's particular strengths — no added inference latency (the adapter can be merged into the weights), strong quality, and swappable adapters (keep dozens of task-specific adapters for one base model, loading the right one per task) — have made it the default. QLoRA's addition of quantizing the frozen base to 4-bit was a further breakthrough, bringing fine-tuning of quite large models within reach of a single consumer GPU, which is why so much community LLM customization now happens this way. Understanding this family, and why low-rank adaptation works (fine-tuning updates tend to be low-rank — they don't need the full expressive capacity of the weight matrix), is exactly the kind of current, practical LLM knowledge that's in high demand.

Zooming out, this capstone crystallizes the paradigm that defines modern AI: pretrain once, adapt many times. A handful of organizations pretrain foundation models on internet-scale data at enormous cost, producing general-purpose models with broad capability. Then the entire rest of the field — companies, researchers, individuals — adapts those models to specific needs using efficient, accessible techniques: prompting (no training), RAG (external knowledge), and parameter-efficient fine-tuning like LoRA (cheap behavioral customization). This division of labor is what makes today's AI ecosystem work: the expensive, capital-intensive pretraining is amortized across countless downstream applications, and the valuable, widely-needed skill is adaptation — knowing which technique fits which need (behavior → LoRA, knowledge → RAG, quick steering → prompting) and executing it well. That's precisely what the LLM track and these capstones teach, and it's why they map so directly to real, in-demand jobs: most applied AI work is building on and adapting pretrained models, not creating them from scratch. Having hands-on experience fine-tuning an LLM with LoRA — understanding the parameter math that makes it cheap, choosing it appropriately over RAG or full fine-tuning, and evaluating the result — puts you squarely in the center of how modern AI is actually practiced. The next capstone builds the other great adaptation technique into a full system: RAG, for giving a model your knowledge.

Fine-tuning shapes behavior; for knowledge, you retrieve. Next, Capstone 3: a RAG assistant over your documents — build a system that answers questions grounded in your own knowledge base, the most-deployed LLM pattern in production.
