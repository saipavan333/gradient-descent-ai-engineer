Optimizer & Loss-Landscape Lab

Training a network is a ball rolling down a hill in a million dimensions. Here's that hill in two, and four different balls — SGD, Momentum, RMSProp and Adam — racing down it from the same spot. Pick a landscape and press play.

Why so many optimizers? Because loss surfaces are nasty — long narrow ravines, flat saddle points, dead ends. Plain SGD zig-zags and stalls; momentum builds speed; adaptive methods (RMSProp, Adam) rescale each direction. Choose a mean surface below and watch which optimizer actually reaches the bottom.

▶ Four optimizers, one landscape

Darker = lower loss. Click anywhere to drop the starting point. Then Run and watch the paths. Turn the learning rate up until someone diverges.

The four in one line each. SGD steps straight downhill — simple, but zig-zags across narrow valleys. Momentum accumulates a velocity, so it powers through and damps the zig-zag. RMSProp divides each step by a running size of that direction's gradients, so steep and shallow directions move at similar speeds. Adam combines momentum and that per-direction scaling — which is why it's the default for most deep learning.

Try these

1. The ravine. Pick Ravine (steep one way, shallow the other). Watch SGD (red) bounce between the walls while barely moving forward, while Adam and RMSProp cruise straight down the valley. This ill-conditioning is why plain SGD is rarely used alone.

2. The saddle. Pick Saddle and start near the centre. SGD can crawl on the flat plateau; momentum and Adam carry speed through it and escape faster. Saddles, not local minima, are the real slow-down in high dimensions.

3. Break it. On any surface, crank the learning rate. Watch an optimizer overshoot and spiral outward — divergence. Every optimizer has a stability limit, and adaptive ones often tolerate a higher rate.

What this lab proves
  • The landscape decides the winner. No optimizer is best everywhere; ravines and saddles favour adaptive methods.
  • Momentum fights zig-zag. A velocity term smooths the path and speeds narrow valleys.
  • Adaptive methods rescale directions. RMSProp/Adam move steep and shallow axes at comparable speed.
  • Learning rate is the stability knob. Too high and any of them diverges.
