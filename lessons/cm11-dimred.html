Dimensionality reduction (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP)

⏱ 24 min Lesson 3.11 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsHigh-dimensional data is hard to see, slow to model, and noisy. Dimensionality reduction compresses it while keeping the signal — PCA for modeling and compression, t-SNE/UMAP for visualization.
By the end, you can
  • Explain PCA as rotating to max-variance axes
  • Read the explained-variance ratio
  • Use t-SNE/UMAP for visualization, with caution
  • Know why to standardize before PCA

Concept Too many features is its own problem

Real datasets often have dozens, hundreds, or thousands of features. That causes three headaches: you can't visualize more than 3 dimensions; distances become less meaningful in high dimensions (the "curse of dimensionality" — everything ends up roughly equidistant); and many features are redundant or noisy, slowing models and inviting overfitting. Dimensionality reduction squeezes many features down to a few new ones that keep most of the information — for plotting, for speed, and for removing noise.

It's unsupervised (no labels needed) and comes in two flavors: linear methods like PCA that are fast, reversible, and great for compression, and nonlinear methods like t-SNE and UMAP built specifically to make a revealing 2-D picture.

Concept PCA: rotate to the most informative axes

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) finds the directions along which the data varies the most and uses them as new axes. The first principal component (PC1) is the single direction capturing the greatest spread; PC2 is the next-most-spread direction perpendicular to it; and so on. These are exactly the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix from Track 1 — PCA is that idea put to work.

To reduce dimensions, you keep the top few components and drop the rest — projecting the data onto them. Because the discarded directions had little variance (mostly noise), you lose little real information. How much you keep is the explained variance ratio: the fraction of total variance each component captures. Plotting it (a scree plot) tells you how many components you need — e.g., "10 components keep 95% of the variance," letting you replace 100 features with 10.

Concept t-SNE and UMAP: maps made for the eye

PCA is linear, so it can miss curved structure. t-SNE and UMAP are nonlinear methods designed for one job: produce a 2-D scatter where similar points sit together, so clusters pop out visually. They're wonderful for seeing structure in high-dimensional data (like embeddings from Track 6).

The crucial caveat: these maps preserve local neighborhoods, not global geometry. So you may read which points group together, but cluster sizes, between-cluster distances, and densities are largely artifacts of the algorithm and its settings — not real. They're visualization tools, not inputs to a downstream model (use PCA for that).

Worked example Compressing 30 features with PCA

The breast-cancer data has 30 features. We standardize them (PCA is variance-based, so scale matters — Lesson 2.5), run PCA, and ask how much of the total variation the top components capture.

The first component alone captures 44.3% of all the variation across 30 features, and the second adds another 19% — so just two components hold 63% of the information, enough to make a revealing 2-D scatter plot of all 30 features. Want to keep almost everything? Ten components retain 95.2% — meaning you can replace 30 features with 10 and lose under 5% of the variance. That's a 3× compression with negligible loss, which speeds up downstream models and strips noise. The numbers also show the characteristic decay (44% → 19% → 9% → …): the first few directions carry most of the signal, the tail is mostly noise worth dropping.

★ Key points to remember
  • Dimensionality reduction compresses many features into a few, for visualization, speed, and noise removal.
  • PCA rotates to the directions of greatest variance (principal components = covariance eigenvectors) and keeps the top few.
  • The explained variance ratio tells you how many components to keep (e.g., "10 PCs = 95%").
  • t-SNE / UMAP make 2-D maps where similar points cluster — great for seeing structure.
  • In t-SNE/UMAP, trust which points group, not cluster sizes or between-cluster distances; standardize before PCA.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1In the worked example, 10 of 30 components captured 95.2% of the variance. Concretely, what are two benefits of using those 10 components instead of all 30 features?
Show solution & reasoning

(1) Speed and size: downstream models train and predict faster with 10 inputs than 30, and the data takes less memory — a 3× reduction here. (2) Noise removal / less overfitting: the 20 dropped components held only ~4.8% of the variance, much of it noise; removing them can actually improve generalization by giving the model fewer spurious directions to latch onto.

A third benefit is decorrelation: the principal components are uncorrelated with each other by construction, which helps models that struggle with correlated inputs (recall multicollinearity in Lesson 3.2). The cost is interpretability — each component is a blend of original features, so "PC3" doesn't have a simple real-world name.

2Why must you standardize features before PCA? What goes wrong if one feature is in dollars (range 0–1,000,000) and another is a ratio (range 0–1)?
Show solution & reasoning

PCA finds directions of maximum variance, and variance depends on scale. The dollar feature, ranging over a million, has astronomically larger variance than the 0–1 ratio — so PC1 would point almost entirely along the dollar axis simply because its numbers are big, not because it's more informative. The ratio feature would be effectively invisible.

Standardizing (each feature to mean 0, standard deviation 1) puts every feature on equal footing, so PCA discovers the directions of greatest genuine co-variation rather than the directions of biggest units. This is the same scale-fairness issue we saw with regularization (Lesson 3.6) and k-means (Lesson 3.10) — any variance- or distance-based method needs it.

3A colleague runs t-SNE, sees three clusters, and concludes "there are exactly three customer types, and type A is twice as common as type B because its blob is twice as big." Critique this.
Show solution & reasoning

The "three clusters" observation may be a useful hypothesis, but the size conclusion is wrong. t-SNE deliberately distorts cluster sizes and spacing to make local neighborhoods readable — a blob's area reflects how the algorithm spread those points out, not how many points it contains or how common the type is. You can't read counts off the picture.

The fix: to claim "A is twice as common," count the actual points assigned to each group (e.g., cluster the data with k-means or check labels) rather than eyeballing blob area. Treat the t-SNE map as a qualitative guide for generating hypotheses ("there seem to be distinct groups — let me verify and quantify"), never as a source of quantitative facts about sizes or distances.

★ Deep dive: PCA as eigen-decomposition, and when linear reduction fails▸

PCA is the direct payoff of Track 1's eigenvectors. You compute the data's covariance matrix (how each pair of features varies together), then find its eigenvectors — those are the principal components, and each eigenvector's eigenvalue is the variance captured along it. Sorting components by eigenvalue and keeping the largest is exactly "keep the directions of most variance." (In practice libraries use the singular value decomposition, SVD, which is numerically nicer but mathematically equivalent.) So the abstract "directions a transformation only stretches" from Lesson 1.5 turns out to be the engine of the most-used compression method in data science — a satisfying closing of the loop.

PCA's limitation is that it's linear: it can only rotate and project, so it captures structure that lies along straight directions. If your data lives on a curved surface — points wrapped on a spiral or a Swiss-roll shape — PCA flattens it badly, because no straight projection untangles a curl. That's the gap nonlinear methods fill: t-SNE and UMAP (and "manifold learning" broadly) can follow curved structure. UMAP has largely overtaken t-SNE in practice because it's much faster, scales to millions of points, and preserves a bit more of the global structure — though the same "don't trust sizes and distances" caution still applies.

One forward link: in modern ML you'll constantly reduce and visualize high-dimensional embeddings — the learned vector representations of words, images, or users (Tracks 6–8). PCA for a quick variance check and a preprocessing step, UMAP for a 2-D picture of whether your embeddings have learned meaningful structure, is a workflow you'll use for the rest of the course. Dimensionality reduction isn't just a classical-ML topic; it's how you keep your bearings in the high-dimensional spaces all of deep learning lives in.
