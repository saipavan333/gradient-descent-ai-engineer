LLM / GenAI & RAG — Q&A

⏱ 26 min Lesson 13.4
Why this existsLLM, GenAI, and RAG questions now dominate ML interviews. How LLMs are trained (pretraining then RLHF), RAG vs fine-tuning, hallucination, prompting, and agents are the topics teams build with — and interviewers test whether you can engineer around a flawed model.
By the end, you can
  • Explain LLM training stages
  • Choose RAG vs fine-tuning (knowledge vs behavior)
  • Explain and reduce hallucination
  • Reason about prompting and agents

Concept The questions that dominate current interviews

LLM, GenAI, and RAG questions are now standard across most ML interviews, because these systems are what teams are building. They test whether you understand how large language models are trained and used, and how to build reliable applications on top of them — prompting, retrieval, agents, and evaluation. As the whole field has shifted here, so have interviews. These questions cluster into a few groups.

Model answer "How is an LLM trained, from raw text to a helpful assistant?"

"Three stages. Pretraining: the model learns language by predicting the next token on a massive corpus of internet-scale text — a self-supervised objective needing no labels, just raw text. This is where it acquires grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and world knowledge, and it's by far the most expensive stage (huge data and compute). The result is a 'base model' that can continue text but isn't yet a helpful assistant. Supervised fine-tuning (instruction tuning): the base model is fine-tuned on curated examples of instructions and good responses, teaching it to follow instructions and respond helpfully rather than just continue text. Alignment via RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback): humans rank model outputs, a reward model is trained to predict those preferences, and the LLM is optimized to produce responses the reward model scores highly — aligning it with human preferences for helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty. So: pretraining gives raw capability from predicting text, instruction tuning makes it follow instructions, and RLHF aligns its behavior with what humans actually want. Newer variants like DPO simplify the alignment step, but the pretraining-then-alignment structure is the key thing to know." Why it lands: naming all three stages and what each contributes (capability → instruction-following → alignment) shows you understand how a next-token predictor becomes ChatGPT.

Model answer "What is RAG, and when would you use it instead of fine-tuning?"

"RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) gives an LLM access to external knowledge at query time: you retrieve relevant documents from a knowledge base (usually via embedding similarity search over a vector database) and put them in the prompt as context, so the model answers grounded in that retrieved information rather than only its trained-in knowledge. It's the dominant pattern for building LLM apps over private or current data. I'd use RAG over fine-tuning when the need is knowledge: the model must answer from specific, current, or private documents (a company's internal docs, today's data, a user's files). RAG's advantages: knowledge updates instantly by changing the document store (no retraining), it can cite sources (verifiable, less hallucination), and it's cheaper and more controllable than fine-tuning. I'd use fine-tuning instead when the need is behavior or style — teaching the model a format, tone, or skill — since fine-tuning instills patterns of behavior but is a poor way to inject specific facts (which it may not memorize reliably and can't update without retraining). The crisp rule: RAG for knowledge, fine-tuning for behavior — and they compose (a fine-tuned model with RAG). Most 'the model doesn't know our data' problems are RAG problems, not fine-tuning problems." Why it lands: the knowledge-vs-behavior distinction is exactly the judgment interviewers probe, and it's a very common real-world decision.

Model answer "An LLM hallucinates. Why does it happen and how do you reduce it?"

"Hallucination — the model confidently generating false information — happens because an LLM is fundamentally a next-token predictor trained to produce plausible, fluent text, not to be truthful. It has no built-in notion of what it does or doesn't know; when it lacks the knowledge, it generates something that sounds right because that's what it was trained to do. Its knowledge is also frozen at training time and compressed lossily into weights, so it fills gaps with confident guesses. To reduce it: ground it with RAG — give it the actual relevant documents and instruct it to answer only from that context (the single most effective fix, since it replaces the model's fuzzy memory with real sources). Instruct it to say 'I don't know' when the context lacks the answer, and to cite sources so claims are verifiable. Lower the temperature for factual tasks (less random). Verify outputs for high-stakes uses (a checking step, or human review). And evaluate faithfulness — measure whether answers are grounded in the provided context. The core insight is that hallucination is inherent to how LLMs work — they optimize plausibility, not truth — so the reliable fix is external grounding and verification, not expecting the model to just 'know' it's wrong." Why it lands: explaining the root cause (plausibility over truth) and leading with grounding shows you understand LLMs' nature, not just tricks.

Model answer "Explain temperature and a couple of prompting techniques."

"Temperature controls the randomness of generation. The model outputs a probability distribution over the next token; temperature scales it. Low temperature (near 0) makes it nearly deterministic — always pick the most likely token — good for factual, precise tasks. High temperature flattens the distribution, making less-likely tokens more probable — more varied and creative, but more error-prone. So you turn it down for accuracy, up for creativity. (Top-p/nucleus sampling is a related knob that samples from the smallest set of tokens covering probability p.) For prompting: few-shot prompting includes a few examples of the task in the prompt, letting the model infer the pattern without any training (in-context learning). Chain-of-thought prompting asks the model to reason step by step ('think through this before answering'), which dramatically improves performance on reasoning tasks because it gives the model space to work through intermediate steps rather than jumping to an answer. These are powerful because they steer a fixed model's behavior at inference time, with no retraining — prompting is often the fastest, cheapest lever to improve LLM output." Follow-up: "Why does chain-of-thought help? — It lets the model externalize and build on intermediate reasoning, effectively giving it more computation and structure for hard problems, rather than forcing a single-step answer." Knowing both the decoding knobs and the prompting patterns, with the why, covers the practical LLM toolkit.

Model answer "What is an AI agent, and what makes them hard to build reliably?"

"An agent is an LLM in a loop that can act — it reasons about a goal, chooses an action (usually calling a tool: search, code, an API), observes the result, and repeats until done. The two essentials are tool use (it can do things, not just talk) and the feedback loop (it adapts based on results), which together let it handle multi-step tasks a single LLM call can't. What makes them hard to build reliably: compounding errors — an agent chains many steps, and even a small per-step failure rate multiplies over a long task into a high chance of overall failure, so long-horizon reliability is genuinely difficult. Error recovery — it must handle tool failures and unexpected results gracefully. And the big one, safety: because it takes real actions, mistakes have real consequences, and it's vulnerable to prompt injection (malicious instructions in content it reads hijacking it) — which has no clean fix. So reliable agents need guardrails (permission limits, budgets, human approval for high-stakes actions), scoping to constrain what can go wrong, and self-correction/reflection to catch errors. The honest framing is that agents are powerful but reliability and safety are their hard, partly-unsolved problems — so you constrain what a misbehaving agent can do rather than assuming it will behave." Why it lands: defining the loop crisply and naming the real difficulties (compounding errors, prompt injection) with the mitigation (guardrails, defense in depth) shows balanced, current understanding rather than hype.

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • How are LLMs trained? Pretraining (next-token prediction) → instruction tuning → RLHF alignment.
  • RAG vs fine-tuning? RAG for knowledge (current/private facts, citable); fine-tuning for behavior/style.
  • Why hallucination? LLMs predict plausible text, not truth; fix with RAG grounding, "say I don't know," citations, verification.
  • Temperature? Randomness knob — low for factual/precise, high for creative.
  • Chain-of-thought? "Think step by step" — improves reasoning by giving the model space for intermediate steps.
  • Few-shot / in-context learning? Examples in the prompt let a fixed model learn the task at inference time.
  • Agent? LLM + tools + a loop; hard parts are compounding errors and safety (prompt injection) — needs guardrails.
  • Context window? The max tokens the model can attend to at once — a hard limit on how much input/history fits.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"How would you evaluate a RAG system that's giving some wrong answers? Where do you look first?"
Show a strong answer

"I'd separate retrieval from generation, because in RAG the failure is usually retrieval, and I'd look there first. For each wrong answer, I'd inspect the retrieved chunks: did the relevant chunk even get retrieved? If not, it's a retrieval failure — I'd check chunking (is the answer split badly or diluted?), the embedding model (is it matching meaning?), and consider hybrid search (adding keyword matching for exact terms it's missing) and reranking; I'd quantify with recall@k on a labeled test set. If the relevant chunk was retrieved but the answer is still wrong, it's a generation/faithfulness problem — the model ignored or misread the context — and I'd strengthen the prompt ('answer only from the context; say you don't know if it's absent'), rerank so the best chunk is prominent, reduce distracting chunks, or use a stronger model, and measure faithfulness (is the answer grounded in the retrieved text?). The discipline is to never guess: look at what was actually retrieved, and let recall@k versus faithfulness tell you which half to fix. Most RAG problems are retrieval problems, so 'inspect the retrieved chunks first' is the highest-value move." This lands because it demonstrates the retrieval-vs-generation debugging methodology and the specific metrics (recall@k, faithfulness) that signal you've actually built and debugged RAG systems — exactly what a current LLM interview wants.

2"Prompting, RAG, and fine-tuning are three ways to adapt an LLM to a task. Compare them and give a rule of thumb for choosing."
Show a strong answer

"They're three levers with increasing cost and commitment. Prompting (including few-shot and chain-of-thought) steers a fixed model at inference time — no training, instant to iterate, cheapest; it's the first thing to try, and often enough. Its limit is that everything must fit in the context window and you're bounded by the base model's capabilities. RAG adds external knowledge at query time — the right tool when the model needs specific, current, or private facts it doesn't have; it's citable and updates instantly, at the cost of building and maintaining a retrieval pipeline. Fine-tuning updates the model's weights on task examples — the right tool for teaching behavior, format, or style, or specializing the model; it's the most powerful for shaping how the model responds but the most expensive and slowest to iterate, and it's a poor way to inject facts (which RAG does better). Rule of thumb: start with prompting (cheapest, fastest); add RAG when the problem is missing knowledge; use fine-tuning when the problem is behavior/style that prompting can't achieve, or when you need to specialize at scale. And they compose — a fine-tuned model with RAG and good prompting. The key discriminators are knowledge vs behavior (RAG vs fine-tuning) and cost/iteration speed (prompting cheapest, fine-tuning priciest)." This answer stands out because it maps the three techniques to what problem each solves (steering, knowledge, behavior) with a clear, practical decision rule — the exact judgment that distinguishes someone who has built LLM applications from someone who just knows the terms.

3"What is the context window, and how does its size shape what you can build with an LLM?"
Show a strong answer

"The context window is the maximum number of tokens the model can attend to at once — everything it 'sees' for a given call: the system prompt, the conversation history, any retrieved documents, and the user's query all have to fit within it. Its size is a fundamental constraint that shapes what you can build in several ways. It limits how much retrieved context you can include in RAG (more room means more or larger chunks, but also more cost and potential distraction). It limits conversation memory — once a chat exceeds the window, you must summarize or truncate older turns, which is why agents and chatbots need memory-management strategies. It bounds document processing — you can't feed a whole book if it exceeds the window, so you chunk and retrieve. And it directly affects cost and latency, since you pay per token and larger contexts are slower. Larger context windows (which have grown dramatically) relax these constraints — enabling longer documents, more history, and more retrieved context in one call — but they don't eliminate the trade-offs: more context costs more, adds latency, and can actually degrade quality if it includes distracting or irrelevant information (the model can lose the signal in a huge context). So the context window is both an enabler and a budget to manage — a bigger one gives you more room, but you still design around fitting the right information in, not just the most. This is why techniques like retrieval, reranking, and summarization matter: they're about getting the most relevant content into a bounded (even if large) window." This lands because it explains not just what the context window is but how it concretely constrains RAG, memory, and cost — showing you understand the practical engineering realities of building on LLMs.

★ Deep dive: the applied-LLM mindset interviewers look for▸

Current LLM interviews reward a specific mindset: treating the LLM as a powerful but flawed component to be engineered around, not a magic oracle. The strongest candidates consistently show three things. First, they understand the model's nature and limits — that it predicts plausible text (hence hallucination), has frozen and lossy knowledge (hence RAG), a bounded context (hence chunking and memory management), and no inherent safety (hence guardrails) — and they design around these limits rather than wishing them away. Second, they reason about the right tool for the problem: prompting for steering, RAG for knowledge, fine-tuning for behavior, agents for multi-step action — matching technique to need, with cost and reliability in mind, rather than reaching for the fanciest option. Third, and increasingly central, they think about evaluation, cost, latency, and safety as first-class concerns, because — as the field and its interviews have shifted — building an LLM demo is easy, but building one that's reliable, affordable, evaluable, and safe is the actual engineering, and that's what interviewers now probe hardest.

This mindset connects everything in the curriculum's LLM, RAG, and agent tracks into a coherent applied practice. The throughline is that an LLM application is a system, not a prompt: it's the model plus retrieval, plus prompting, plus guardrails, plus evaluation, plus serving and monitoring — all engineered to turn a flawed, general model into a reliable, specific product. When an interviewer asks about RAG, hallucination, agents, or evaluation, they're really checking whether you see this whole system and can reason about its pieces and trade-offs, or whether you only know the buzzwords. The candidates who stand out pair genuine understanding of how LLMs work (the training stages, why they hallucinate, what attention does) with the practical judgment to build reliable applications on them (RAG vs fine-tuning, grounding to reduce hallucination, guardrails for agents, evaluation to know it works) — and they consistently surface the cost-quality-latency-safety trade-offs that define real LLM engineering. That combination of foundational understanding and applied, trade-off-aware judgment is exactly what makes someone hireable for the LLM and GenAI roles that increasingly dominate the field — and it's what this curriculum has been building.
