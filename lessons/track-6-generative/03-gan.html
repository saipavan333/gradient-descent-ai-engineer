Generative adversarial networks

Throw away likelihood entirely. Instead, pit a forger against a detective: the generator learns to produce data so convincing that a discriminator cannot tell it from real. When the detective is reduced to guessing, the forgeries are good.

Track 6 · Lesson 3 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 0, 2, Track 4

The VAE gave fast sampling but blurry results, hampered by squared-error reconstruction. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) take a radical turn: define no likelihood at all, and learn to sample by playing a game. The result is famously sharp samples — and famously tricky training. We build both players from scratch and watch the game reach equilibrium.

💡 Concept: a forger and a detective

Two networks with opposing goals. The generator \(G\) maps a random noise vector \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) to a fake sample \(G(z)\); it wants its fakes to pass as real. The discriminator \(D\) takes a sample and outputs a single number \(D(x)\in(0,1)\), its estimated probability that \(x\) is real; it wants to correctly flag fakes. Train them together and each pressures the other: a sharper detective forces a better forger, whose better fakes force a sharper detective. There is no encoder and no likelihood — just this contest.

📐 The minimax objective

The contest is written as a single value function \(V(D,G)\) that \(D\) maximises and \(G\) minimises:

\[ \min_{G}\ \max_{D}\ V(D,G) = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\text{data}}\big[\log D(x)\big] + \mathbb{E}_{z\sim p(z)}\big[\log\big(1-D(G(z))\big)\big]. \] (3.1)

Decode it term by term. The discriminator wants \(D(x)\to1\) on real data (first term large) and \(D(G(z))\to0\) on fakes (second term large), so it maximises \(V\). The generator wants the opposite — \(D(G(z))\to1\) — so it minimises \(V\) through the only term it touches, the second. Goodfellow’s theorem shows that for a fixed, optimal \(D\), minimising \(V\) over \(G\) is equivalent to minimising the Jensen–Shannon divergence between the data and generated distributions, with the global optimum at \(p_G=p_\text{data}\). At that point the discriminator cannot do better than a coin flip: \(D(x)=\tfrac12\) everywhere, accuracy \(\approx 0.5\).

The non-saturating fix. Early in training \(G\) is terrible, so \(D(G(z))\approx0\) and the generator’s term \(\log(1-D(G(z)))\) is flat — its gradient vanishes just when \(G\) needs to learn most. In practice we instead have \(G\) maximise \(\log D(G(z))\) (“make fakes look real”), which gives the same fixed point but strong gradients early. Our code uses this non-saturating form.

🛠️ From scratch: two MLPs, two backward passes

Our gan.py builds \(G\) and \(D\) as small ReLU MLPs and hand-derives backprop for both. Each step trains \(D\) on a real batch (label 1) and a fake batch (label 0), then trains \(G\) to push its fakes toward “real.” The logic, condensed: 

# --- train D: real -> 1, fake -> 0  (binary cross-entropy) ---
fake = gen_forward(z)
d_real = disc_forward(real);  d_fake = disc_forward(fake)
grad_D  = bce_grad(d_real, 1) + bce_grad(d_fake, 0)
adam_update(D, grad_D)

# --- train G: make D say "real" about the fakes (non-saturating) ---
fake = gen_forward(z)
d_fake = disc_forward(fake)
dx = bce_grad(d_fake, 1)          # target = 1: "look real"
grad_G = backprop_through_D_into_G(dx)   # gradient passes through frozen D
adam_update(G, grad_G)

Notice the generator’s gradient is computed by backpropagating the discriminator’s “you look fake” signal through \(D\) and into \(G\)’s weights — \(D\) acts as a learned, moving loss function for \(G\). That is the whole trick, and it is why the two updates must stay roughly balanced.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Two networks, one game: \(\min_G\max_D\ \mathbb{E}_{p_\text{data}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{p(z)}[\log(1-D(G(z)))]\).
  • No likelihood: a GAN only learns to sample; it never defines \(p(x)\), so you cannot score data by likelihood.
  • Global optimum is \(p_G=p_\text{data}\), where \(D\equiv\tfrac12\) and accuracy \(\approx 0.5\) — the balance to watch for.
  • Non-saturating generator loss (maximise \(\log D(G(z))\)) fixes vanishing gradients early in training.
  • Sharp but unstable: GANs produce crisp samples but suffer mode collapse and delicate training — the trade for dropping likelihood.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. During healthy GAN training, the discriminator’s accuracy trends toward 0.5. Why is that a good sign?

  • It means the discriminator has stopped learning.
    A frozen discriminator would be bad. Here it is still learning — it simply cannot separate real from fake because the fakes are now excellent.
  • It means the generator has collapsed to one output.
    Mode collapse is a failure, and it usually lets \(D\) win (accuracy rises), not settle at 0.5. Accuracy 0.5 is the success signature.
  • The generator’s samples have become indistinguishable from real data, so the best \(D\) can do is guess.
    Correct. At the optimum \(p_G=p_\text{data}\), so \(D(x)=\tfrac12\) everywhere and accuracy \(\approx0.5\). The detective is reduced to a coin flip because the forgeries are perfect.
  • The learning rate has decayed to zero.
    Accuracy near 0.5 reflects the game’s equilibrium, not the optimiser’s schedule.

Q2. A key difference between a GAN and a VAE is that a GAN…

  • does not define or optimise a likelihood \(p(x)\); it only learns to sample.
    Correct. The VAE optimises the ELBO (a likelihood bound); the GAN optimises an adversarial game and never produces a density you can evaluate.
  • cannot use neural networks for the generator.
    The generator is a neural network — that is the whole point. This is simply false.
  • requires the chain-rule factorization.
    That is the autoregressive recipe (Lesson 1). GANs use no such factorization.
  • always produces blurrier samples than a VAE.
    The opposite tendency: GANs are prized for sharper samples than VAEs.

Q3. Why replace the generator’s \(\min\log(1-D(G(z)))\) with \(\max\log D(G(z))\)?

  • It changes the location of the optimum to something easier.
    It keeps the same fixed point (\(p_G=p_\text{data}\)); only the gradient behaviour changes.
  • It makes the discriminator unnecessary.
    \(D\) is still required — the new objective still routes gradients through it. It only reshapes \(G\)’s loss surface.
  • It turns the game into a likelihood objective.
    No likelihood appears; it is still adversarial. The change is purely about gradient strength.
  • Early on \(D(G(z))\approx0\), where \(\log(1-D(G(z)))\) is flat; the non-saturating form gives strong gradients when \(G\) is still bad.
    Correct. The original loss vanishes exactly when the generator most needs signal; maximising \(\log D(G(z))\) provides a steep gradient there, fixing slow early training.

Q4. “Mode collapse” in a GAN refers to…

  • the discriminator memorising the training set.
    That is discriminator overfitting, a different issue. Mode collapse is about the generator’s diversity.
  • the generator producing only a few distinct outputs, ignoring much of the data’s variety.
    Correct. \(G\) finds a handful of samples that reliably fool \(D\) and stops covering other modes — high quality on those few, but little diversity. Watching for it (and remedies like minibatch features or Wasserstein loss) is central to GAN practice.
  • the loss becoming exactly zero.
    GAN losses do not go to zero at equilibrium; they hover. Collapse is about output diversity, not loss value.
  • the latent prior shrinking to a point.
    The prior \(p(z)\) is fixed (e.g. \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)). Collapse is a property of the mapping \(G\), not the prior.

✍️ Practice

1. In gan.py, train the discriminator twice per generator update. What tends to happen, and why?

Show solution

A stronger discriminator often makes training less stable for the generator, not more. If \(D\) gets too good too fast, it pushes \(D(G(z))\to0\), and even the non-saturating gradient for \(G\) weakens — \(G\) receives little useful signal and may stall or collapse to a few modes. GAN training is a balancing act: neither player should dominate. In practice people tune the update ratio, lower \(D\)’s learning rate, or switch to losses (e.g. Wasserstein) that stay informative even when \(D\) is strong. Try it and watch the discriminator accuracy spike toward 1.0 — the telltale of an unbalanced game.

2. Switch \(G\)’s loss back to the saturating form \(\log(1-D(G(z)))\) and compare early-training progress.

Show solution

You should see noticeably slower or stalled early learning. With a fresh \(G\), fakes are obvious, so \(D(G(z))\approx0\); there, \(\tfrac{d}{dD}\log(1-D)\) is small, so the gradient passed to \(G\) is weak and it barely improves. The non-saturating loss \(\log D(G(z))\) has a large gradient in exactly that regime, which is why it is the default. Both share the same equilibrium, but the path there is far smoother with the non-saturating version — a concrete example of how loss shape, not just its minimiser, governs trainability.

3. Conceptual: you have a trained GAN and a trained autoregressive model. A colleague asks “which assigns higher probability to this real image?” What do you tell them?

Show solution

Only the autoregressive model can answer: it defines an explicit normalised \(p(x)\), so you can evaluate the image’s likelihood directly. The GAN has no density — it is an implicit model that knows only how to sample, so “the probability it assigns” is undefined. This is the fundamental trade GANs make: superb samples, but no likelihood for scoring, comparison, or anomaly detection. Evaluating GANs therefore relies on sample-based metrics (e.g. FID) rather than likelihood.
