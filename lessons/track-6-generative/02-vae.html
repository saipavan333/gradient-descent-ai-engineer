Variational autoencoders

Compress data to a small, well-behaved latent code and learn to decode it back. Make that code probabilistic and you get a generative model you can sample from — trained with one of the most elegant objectives in machine learning, the ELBO.

Track 6 · Lesson 2 Concept + derivation + code ~30 min Builds on: Lesson 0, Tracks 1, 4

Autoregressive models generate one slow coordinate at a time. The variational autoencoder (VAE) takes a different route: learn a compact latent space, so that sampling a whole data point is one quick decode of a random code. Getting there requires a genuinely beautiful piece of mathematics — the evidence lower bound — and one clever trick to make randomness differentiable. We derive both, then watch a from-scratch VAE generate.

💡 Concept: a probabilistic bottleneck

Start with an ordinary autoencoder: an encoder network squeezes input \(x\) into a low-dimensional code \(z\), and a decoder rebuilds \(x\) from \(z\). Trained to reconstruct, it learns to pack the essential factors of variation into \(z\). But it is not generative: if you pick a random \(z\) and decode it, you usually get garbage, because the encoder scattered codes wherever was convenient and left holes between them.

The VAE fixes this by making the bottleneck probabilistic and regularized. Introduce a latent variable \(z\) with a fixed, simple prior \(p(z)=\mathcal{N}(0,I)\). The decoder defines \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\): how to turn a code into data. The encoder defines \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\): a guess at which codes could have produced \(x\), output as a mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) rather than a single point. Training pulls the encoder’s codes toward the prior, so that afterwards we can sample \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\), decode, and land on realistic data.

📐 Derivation: the evidence lower bound (ELBO)

We would like to maximise the likelihood the model assigns to real data, \(\log p_\theta(x)\), where \(p_\theta(x)=\int p_\theta(x\mid z)\,p(z)\,dz\). That integral runs over every possible code and is intractable. The trick is to bring in the encoder \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and turn the intractable integral into something we can optimise. Multiply and divide by \(q_\phi\):

\[ \log p_\theta(x) = \log \int q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\frac{p_\theta(x\mid z)\,p(z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\,dz = \log \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\frac{p_\theta(x\mid z)\,p(z)}{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\right]. \] (2.1)

Now apply Jensen’s inequality — for the concave \(\log\), \(\log\mathbb{E}[u]\ge\mathbb{E}[\log u]\) — to pull the log inside the expectation. The result is a quantity we can compute, the evidence lower bound (ELBO):

\[ \log p_\theta(x) \;\ge\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z\mid x)}\!\big[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)\big]}_{\text{reconstruction}} \;-\; \underbrace{\mathrm{KL}\!\big(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z)\big)}_{\text{regularizer}} \;=\; \mathcal{L}(\theta,\phi;x). \] (2.2)

Read the two terms. The reconstruction term rewards decoders that make the real \(x\) probable from its own encoded \(z\) — “rebuild the input.” The KL term (the divergence from Lesson 0) pulls each encoded distribution \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) toward the prior \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) — “keep the codes tidy.” Maximising the ELBO does both at once. The amount we gave up in Jensen’s step is exactly \(\mathrm{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p_\theta(z\mid x))\ge0\), so the bound is tight when the encoder matches the true posterior — which is why a better encoder gives a tighter objective.

With a Gaussian encoder \(q_\phi(z\mid x)=\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\) and standard-normal prior, the KL term has a clean closed form you can differentiate by hand:

\[ \mathrm{KL}\!\big(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\,\|\,\mathcal{N}(0,1)\big) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_j\big(\sigma_j^2 + \mu_j^2 - 1 - \log \sigma_j^2\big). \] (2.3)

📐 The reparameterization trick

One obstacle remains. The reconstruction term is an expectation over \(z\sim q_\phi(z\mid x)\), and we need gradients to flow back through that sampling step into the encoder’s \(\mu,\sigma\). But “draw a random \(z\)” is not a differentiable operation — you cannot backprop through a dice roll. The fix is to move the randomness off the gradient path: instead of sampling \(z\) directly, write it as a deterministic function of \(\mu,\sigma\) plus fixed external noise,

\[ z = \mu + \sigma \odot \varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I), \] (2.4)

where \(\odot\) is elementwise multiplication. Now \(z\) is differentiable in \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), and \(\varepsilon\) is just a constant injected from the side — its randomness no longer blocks the chain rule. This is the single idea that makes VAEs trainable by ordinary backprop (Track 4).

🛠️ From scratch: a VAE in NumPy, gradients by hand

Our vae.py implements exactly Equations (2.2)–(2.4): an encoder MLP outputs \(\mu\) and \(\log\sigma^2\), we reparameterize, decode, and minimise reconstruction error plus the closed-form KL — every gradient derived by hand. The core of the training step: 

he = relu(x @ We + be)            # encoder hidden layer
mu = he @ Wmu + bmu               # mean of q(z|x)
lv = he @ Wlv + blv               # log-variance (we predict log sigma^2)
eps = rng.normal(size=mu.shape)
z  = mu + np.exp(0.5*lv) * eps    # (2.4) reparameterization trick
hd = relu(z @ Wd + bd)
xo = hd @ Wo + bo                 # decoder reconstruction

# loss = reconstruction MSE + KL( q(z|x) || N(0,I) )   -- Eqs (2.2),(2.3)
recon = np.mean((xo - x)**2)
kl    = np.mean(-0.5*np.sum(1 + lv - mu**2 - np.exp(lv), axis=1))
# ... hand-derived backprop sends gradients through BOTH terms ...

After training, generating is trivial and fast: draw \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) — no encoder needed — and decode. 

z = rng.normal(size=(n, latent_dim))   # sample the prior directly
samples = decode(z)                     # one forward pass = n new points

🔑 Key results to remember

  • ELBO: \(\log p_\theta(x)\ge \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x\mid z)] - \mathrm{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\|\,p(z))\) — reconstruction minus a regularizer.
  • The gap between \(\log p(x)\) and the ELBO is \(\mathrm{KL}(q_\phi\,\|\,\text{true posterior})\ge0\); a better encoder tightens the bound.
  • Reparameterization: \(z=\mu+\sigma\odot\varepsilon,\ \varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) makes sampling differentiable so backprop works.
  • Gaussian KL has the closed form \(\tfrac12\sum(\sigma^2+\mu^2-1-\log\sigma^2)\).
  • Sampling is fast (one decode of \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)), but outputs tend to be blurry — the VAE’s main weakness.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. Why does the VAE objective include the KL term that pulls \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) toward \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)?

  • To make reconstructions sharper.
    The KL term actually trades against reconstruction sharpness. Its purpose is organising the latent space, not sharpening outputs.
  • So the latent space matches the prior, letting us sample \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) and decode to realistic data.
    Correct. Without it, codes scatter and leave holes; decoding a random \(z\) gives garbage. The KL packs the codes against the prior so the prior is a valid place to sample from.
  • To prevent the encoder weights from exploding.
    That is what weight decay / normalisation handle. The KL constrains the distribution of codes, not weight magnitudes.
  • Because the chain rule requires it.
    The KL comes from the ELBO derivation, not the chain rule (which underlies autoregressive models). It is a regularizer toward the prior.

Q2. The reparameterization trick exists to…

  • speed up sampling at test time.
    Sampling speed is unaffected; the trick is about training. You still draw one \(\varepsilon\) either way.
  • remove the need for a decoder.
    The decoder is still essential — it turns \(z\) into \(x\). The trick only changes how \(z\) is sampled.
  • make the prior non-Gaussian.
    The prior stays \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\). The trick rewrites the sampling, not the prior.
  • let gradients flow back through the sampling of \(z\) into \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\).
    Correct. Writing \(z=\mu+\sigma\varepsilon\) puts \(\mu,\sigma\) on a differentiable path and pushes the randomness into the side input \(\varepsilon\), so ordinary backprop trains the encoder.

Q3. The ELBO is a lower bound on \(\log p_\theta(x)\). What is the gap between them?

  • \(\mathrm{KL}\) between the encoder \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\) and the true posterior \(p_\theta(z\mid x)\).
    Correct. Algebra shows \(\log p(x) = \mathcal{L} + \mathrm{KL}(q_\phi\,\|\,p_\theta(z\mid x))\). Since KL \(\ge 0\), \(\mathcal{L}\) is a bound, tight exactly when the encoder equals the true posterior.
  • Always zero; the ELBO equals the likelihood.
    Only in the ideal case where the encoder matches the true posterior. In general there is a positive gap.
  • The reconstruction error.
    Reconstruction is one term inside the ELBO, not the gap above it.
  • The learning rate times the batch size.
    The gap is a property of the model and encoder, not optimisation hyperparameters.

Q4. VAE samples are often criticised as “blurry.” Which explanation best fits?

  • The latent space is discrete, so it quantises outputs.
    The latent is continuous (\(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)). Blur is not a quantisation artefact.
  • The encoder is too small to memorise the data.
    Capacity affects fidelity generally, but blur persists even with large encoders — it is intrinsic to the objective, not just size.
  • Averaging under a Gaussian decoder plus KL pressure favour smooth, “average” reconstructions over sharp details.
    Correct. A Gaussian likelihood penalises squared error, whose optimum is a mean; combined with the KL pulling codes together, the decoder hedges toward smooth averages. GANs and diffusion target exactly this weakness.
  • Because the KL term is computed in closed form.
    Having a closed-form KL is a convenience and is exact; it does not cause blur. The cause is the averaging behaviour of the reconstruction objective.

✍️ Practice

1. In vae.py, scale the KL term by a weight \(\beta\) (this is the “\(\beta\)-VAE”). Train with \(\beta=0\) and with \(\beta=4\). Describe what happens to reconstructions and to samples drawn from the prior.

Show solution

With \(\beta=0\) the model is a plain autoencoder: reconstructions sharpen, but the codes ignore the prior, so sampling \(z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) and decoding produces junk — there is no pressure to fill the latent space. With \(\beta=4\) the KL dominates: codes collapse tightly onto the prior (sometimes “posterior collapse,” where the encoder ignores \(x\)), samples look prior-like and over-smoothed, and reconstructions degrade. The default \(\beta=1\) is the honest ELBO; tuning \(\beta\) trades reconstruction fidelity against latent-space regularity, and larger \(\beta\) is sometimes used deliberately to encourage disentangled latent factors.

2. Derive the Gaussian KL in Equation (2.3) for one dimension, starting from \(\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)=\mathbb{E}_q[\log q-\log p]\) with \(q=\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)\), \(p=\mathcal{N}(0,1)\).

Show solution

Write the log-densities: \(\log q = -\tfrac12\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \tfrac{(z-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\) and \(\log p = -\tfrac12\log(2\pi) - \tfrac{z^2}{2}\). Subtract: \(\log q-\log p = -\tfrac12\log\sigma^2 - \tfrac{(z-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} + \tfrac{z^2}{2}\). Take \(\mathbb{E}_q\), using \(\mathbb{E}_q[(z-\mu)^2]=\sigma^2\) and \(\mathbb{E}_q[z^2]=\mu^2+\sigma^2\): the middle term gives \(-\tfrac12\), the last gives \(\tfrac{\mu^2+\sigma^2}{2}\). Collecting, \(\mathrm{KL}=\tfrac12(\sigma^2+\mu^2-1-\log\sigma^2)\), exactly (2.3). Summing over independent dimensions gives the vector form.

3. Set the latent dimension to 1 and retrain. Walk \(z\) from \(-2\) to \(2\) and decode each value. What does the resulting path of points tell you about what the single latent coordinate has learned?

Show solution

Decoding a smooth sweep of \(z\) traces a continuous curve through data space — a learned 1-D coordinate along the data manifold. On two-moons, the single latent tends to parameterise position along the crescents: small \(z\) at one end, large \(z\) at the other, passing smoothly through the shape. This is the VAE’s real prize — a compact, continuous code where nearby \(z\) decode to similar data — and it is why VAEs are prized for representation learning and interpolation, even when their raw sample sharpness lags GANs and diffusion.
