Diffusion models

Destroy data with noise in many small, fixed steps — then train a network to reverse one step of that destruction. Generation becomes a walk from pure noise back to data. This is the engine behind today’s best image generators, and it trains with a loss as simple as “predict the noise.”

Track 6 · Lesson 4 Concept + derivation + code ~32 min Builds on: Lessons 0–3, Track 4

Autoregressive models were exact but slow; VAEs were fast but blurry; GANs were sharp but unstable. Diffusion models get sharp, diverse samples from stable training — the combination that made them the backbone of modern image, audio, and video generation. The idea is almost paradoxically simple: learn to remove a little noise, and apply it many times. We build a working denoising diffusion model from scratch and watch noise turn into data.

💡 Concept: break creation into many tiny denoising steps

Diffusion has two processes. The forward process is fixed and requires no learning: starting from a real data point \(x_0\), add a small amount of Gaussian noise, repeatedly, over \(T\) steps, until what remains is indistinguishable from pure noise \(x_T\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\). The reverse process is what we learn: a network that takes a noisy \(x_t\) and the step index \(t\), and undoes one step of noising. To generate, start from noise and run the learned reverse step \(T\) times.

A diffusion model is a deep, fixed-encoder VAE. Think of the forward process as a VAE “encoder” with no parameters that maps data to noise over \(T\) latent steps, and the learned reverse process as the “decoder.” Splitting generation into many small steps — instead of one big leap — is what makes the learning problem easy and the samples sharp.

📐 The forward process has a closed form

Each forward step adds noise with a small variance \(\beta_t\): \(q(x_t\mid x_{t-1})=\mathcal{N}\!\big(\sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,x_{t-1},\ \beta_t I\big)\). The \(\beta_t\) follow a fixed schedule (we use a line from \(10^{-4}\) to \(0.02\)). Composing many Gaussian steps is itself Gaussian, so we can jump straight from \(x_0\) to any step \(t\) in one shot. Writing \(\alpha_t=1-\beta_t\) and the running product \(\bar\alpha_t=\prod_{s=1}^{t}\alpha_s\),

\[ x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I). \] (4.1)

This closed form is what makes training efficient: to get a noisy sample at any step, we never simulate the whole chain — we draw one \(\varepsilon\) and apply (4.1). As \(t\) grows, \(\bar\alpha_t\to0\), so \(x_t\) smoothly loses the signal \(x_0\) and approaches pure noise \(\varepsilon\).

📐 Training objective: just predict the noise

Here is the surprise that makes diffusion practical. Rather than predict the cleaned data, we train a network \(\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\) to predict the noise \(\varepsilon\) that was added in (4.1). Ho et al.’s denoising diffusion (DDPM) showed that the full variational bound simplifies — dropping some weighting terms — to a plain mean-squared error between the true and predicted noise:

\[ \mathcal{L}_\text{simple} = \mathbb{E}_{x_0,\,t,\,\varepsilon}\Big[\big\lVert \varepsilon - \varepsilon_\theta(x_t,\,t)\big\rVert^2\Big], \qquad x_t \text{ from } (4.1). \] (4.2)

That is the entire loss: sample a data point, sample a random step \(t\), noise the point with a known \(\varepsilon\), and ask the network to recover \(\varepsilon\). No adversary, no likelihood bound to wrestle, no posterior collapse — just regression, which is why diffusion training is so stable.

To sample, invert one step at a time. Given the predicted noise \(\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\), the mean of the previous (less-noisy) sample is

\[ x_{t-1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}\Big(x_t - \frac{1-\alpha_t}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}\,\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\Big) + \sqrt{\beta_t}\,z, \quad z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\ (z=0 \text{ at } t=0). \] (4.3)

Start at \(x_T\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\), apply (4.3) for \(t=T,\dots,1\), and out comes a fresh data point. The small added \(\sqrt{\beta_t}\,z\) keeps the reverse process stochastic so it can produce variety rather than collapsing to one output.

🛠️ From scratch: a DDPM in NumPy

Our diffusion.py is Equations (4.1)–(4.3) in code. The denoiser is a small MLP that takes the point plus a few sinusoidal features of \(t\); training is the three lines at the heart of (4.2): 

t   = rng.integers(0, T, bs)              # random step per sample
eps = rng.normal(size=x0.shape)           # the noise we will add
x_t = np.sqrt(acp[t]) * x0 + np.sqrt(1-acp[t]) * eps   # (4.1)

pred = denoiser(x_t, t)                    # predict the noise
loss = np.mean((pred - eps)**2)            # (4.2): just MSE on noise
# ... hand-derived backprop updates the denoiser ...

Sampling walks the chain backward with (4.3), turning a cloud of noise into the data distribution: 

x = rng.normal(size=(n, 2))                # start from pure noise x_T
for t in reversed(range(T)):
    pred = denoiser(x, t)
    x = (x - (1-alpha[t])/sqrt(1-acp[t]) * pred) / sqrt(alpha[t])
    if t > 0: x += sqrt(beta[t]) * rng.normal(size=x.shape)   # (4.3)

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Forward = fixed noising; reverse = learned denoising. Only the reverse process has parameters.
  • Closed-form forward: \(x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\,x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\,\varepsilon\) lets you jump to any step in one draw.
  • Train by predicting the noise: \(\mathcal{L}=\mathbb{E}\lVert\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\rVert^2\) — a plain MSE, hence very stable.
  • Sample by starting at \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) and applying the reverse step (4.3) \(T\) times.
  • Best of both worlds — sharp, diverse samples with stable training — at the cost of many sequential steps per sample.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. In a diffusion model, which process is learned?

  • The forward noising process.
    The forward process is fixed by the \(\beta_t\) schedule and has no parameters — it just adds noise. Nothing is learned there.
  • The reverse denoising process.
    Correct. We train a network to undo one noising step; running it repeatedly turns noise into data. The forward direction is free.
  • Both processes are learned jointly.
    Only the reverse is learned. Keeping the forward process fixed and known is exactly what makes the training target (the added noise) available.
  • Neither — diffusion has no trainable parameters.
    The denoiser is very much trained; that is where all the parameters live.

Q2. The DDPM training loss \(\mathbb{E}\lVert\varepsilon-\varepsilon_\theta(x_t,t)\rVert^2\) asks the network to predict…

  • the clean data \(x_0\) directly.
    A reasonable guess, and an equivalent parameterisation exists — but the standard DDPM objective predicts the noise, which empirically trains better.
  • the next class label.
    There is no classification here; diffusion is unconditional generation in this lesson. It regresses on noise.
  • the noise \(\varepsilon\) that was added to make \(x_t\).
    Correct. Because \(x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\varepsilon\), knowing \(\varepsilon\) is equivalent to knowing \(x_0\); predicting \(\varepsilon\) gives a clean MSE target and stable training.
  • a probability between 0 and 1 that \(x_t\) is real.
    That is a GAN discriminator’s job. The diffusion network outputs a noise vector, not a realness score.

Q3. Why can we train on any timestep \(t\) without simulating all the steps before it?

  • The forward process has a closed form, \(x_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}x_0+\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\varepsilon\), so we jump straight to step \(t\).
    Correct. Composing Gaussian steps stays Gaussian, giving a one-shot formula for \(x_t\) from \(x_0\). We sample a random \(t\), noise once, and train — no chain simulation needed.
  • Because the reverse process is deterministic.
    The reverse process is actually stochastic (it adds \(\sqrt{\beta_t}z\)). The shortcut comes from the forward closed form, not reverse determinism.
  • Because \(T\) is small enough to ignore.
    \(T\) is often large (tens to thousands of steps). The efficiency comes from the closed form, not a small \(T\).
  • Because the noise schedule is learned.
    The schedule \(\beta_t\) is fixed, not learned. The key is the analytic jump-to-\(t\) formula.

Q4. Compared with a GAN, diffusion’s main practical advantage and disadvantage are:

  • Advantage: no neural network needed. Disadvantage: blurry samples.
    Diffusion very much needs a network, and its samples are sharp, not blurry. Both halves are wrong.
  • Advantage: exact likelihood. Disadvantage: adversarial instability.
    Diffusion does not optimise an exact likelihood (it uses a simplified bound), and it has no adversary, so “adversarial instability” is a GAN trait.
  • Advantage: single-step sampling. Disadvantage: mode collapse.
    Backwards: diffusion needs many steps to sample, and it largely avoids mode collapse. These describe GANs’ profile, not diffusion’s.
  • Advantage: stable training with sharp, diverse samples. Disadvantage: slow, many-step sampling.
    Correct. Diffusion trades the GAN’s instability for a simple regression loss and strong sample quality, but pays with \(T\) sequential network evaluations per sample — the motivation for later fast samplers (DDIM, distillation).

✍️ Practice

1. In diffusion.py, remove the timestep features so the denoiser sees only \((x,y)\). Train and sample. Why does quality collapse?

Show solution

The correct amount of noise to remove depends heavily on where you are in the chain: at large \(t\) the input is almost pure noise and the network should make a bold move toward the data; at small \(t\) it should barely adjust. Without knowing \(t\), one network must average the right behaviour across all noise levels, which is inconsistent and washes out the result. Conditioning on \(t\) (here via sinusoidal features, echoing the positional encodings of Track 5) lets a single network behave differently at each step — essential for diffusion to work.

2. Increase \(T\) from 50 to 200 (keep the same \(\beta\) range). Predict the effect on sample quality and on sampling time, then test it.

Show solution

More steps generally means each reverse step removes less noise, so the approximation in (4.3) is more accurate and samples get a little cleaner — up to a point of diminishing returns. The cost is linear: sampling now takes 200 network evaluations instead of 50, roughly 4× slower, while training cost per step is unchanged (you still noise to a single random \(t\)). This quality-vs-speed dial is why a major research thread builds fast samplers (e.g. DDIM, consistency models) that match quality in far fewer steps.

3. Conceptual: relate diffusion back to the VAE of Lesson 2. In what sense is a diffusion model a hierarchical VAE, and why might splitting generation into many steps help?

Show solution

Treat the noisy variables \(x_1,\dots,x_T\) as a chain of latent variables. The fixed forward process is a parameter-free encoder \(q(x_t\mid x_{t-1})\); the learned reverse process is a decoder \(p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid x_t)\); and the DDPM objective is a (re-weighted) ELBO for this deep latent-variable model. A standard VAE must leap from \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) to data in one decode — a hard, blur-prone jump. Diffusion breaks that leap into many gentle steps, each an easy denoising regression, so errors stay small and detail survives. Decomposing one hard problem into many easy ones is the recurring theme of this whole track — from the chain rule in Lesson 1 to here.
