Clustering & unsupervised learning

⏱ 25 min Lesson 3.10 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsNot all data comes labeled. Clustering finds structure on its own — customer segments, topic groups — and k-means is the workhorse. Knowing its assumptions and how to choose k is core unsupervised skill.
By the end, you can
  • Explain the k-means assign/update loop
  • Choose k with the elbow and silhouette
  • Know why to standardize first
  • Recognize k-means limits and alternatives

Concept Learning without an answer key

Every model so far was supervised — it learned from labeled examples. Now we drop the labels. In unsupervised learning you have only the inputs $X$ and want to discover structure that's already there, with nobody telling you the "right" answer. The most common form is clustering: grouping examples so that points in the same group are similar and points in different groups are not. Think segmenting customers by behavior, grouping documents by topic, or compressing colors in an image — all with no labels to learn from.

The catch that makes unsupervised learning genuinely different: there's no test-set accuracy to check, because there are no true labels. Success is fuzzier — "are these groups meaningful and useful?" — which makes evaluation and judgment a bigger part of the job.

Concept k-means: the workhorse clustering algorithm

The most-used clustering method is k-means. You tell it how many groups to find, $k$, and it discovers their centers. A centroid is the center of a cluster — the mean of its points. The algorithm is a beautifully simple two-step loop, repeated until it settles:

That's the whole algorithm: assign points to the nearest center, update each center to the average of its assigned points, and repeat. Each round can only lower the total spread of points around their centers, so it's guaranteed to settle (converge). Because it relies on distances and averages, you should standardize features first (Lesson 2.5) — otherwise a large-scale feature dominates the distance and the clusters form along it alone.

Concept How many clusters? Choosing k

k-means needs you to pick $k$ up front, but you usually don't know the true number of groups. Two tools help. The elbow method plots the within-cluster spread (called inertia — the total squared distance of points to their centers) against $k$: it always falls as $k$ grows, but there's often a "knee" where the improvement sharply flattens, suggesting a natural $k$.

A more quantitative tool is the silhouette score, which measures how well each point sits in its cluster versus the nearest other cluster, averaged over all points. It ranges from −1 to 1, and you pick the $k$ that maximizes it — higher means tighter, better-separated clusters.

Worked example Finding the right number of clusters

We generate data with three genuine blobs, then run k-means for $k = 2, 3, 4$ and look at both inertia (lower = tighter clusters, but always falls with $k$) and silhouette (higher = better-separated, with a real peak). A good method should point us to the true answer, 3.

Watch both signals agree on 3. Inertia drops massively from k=2 to k=3 (819.6 → 367.6) but only a little from k=3 to k=4 (367.6 → 310.3) — that flattening is the elbow, pointing at 3. Silhouette peaks at k=3 (0.557) and falls on either side — its clearest possible vote for 3. Together they recover the true number of groups with no labels at all. Note inertia alone would tempt you to keep raising $k$ (it never stops falling), which is exactly why the elbow shape, or the silhouette's peak, is what you read — not the raw number.

★ Key points to remember
  • Unsupervised learning finds structure in unlabeled data; clustering groups similar points.
  • k-means loops: assign points to the nearest centroid, then move centroids to the mean; repeat.
  • Pick k with the elbow (inertia's knee) or the silhouette score's peak.
  • Standardize features first — k-means uses distances, so scale matters.
  • k-means assumes roughly round, similar-sized clusters; other methods (DBSCAN, hierarchical) relax that.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1In the worked example, inertia kept dropping (819.6 → 367.6 → 310.3) but silhouette peaked at k=3. Explain why silhouette is the more trustworthy signal for choosing k here.
Show solution & reasoning

Inertia measures only how tight clusters are, and tightness always improves as you add centers — splitting a real cluster in two still lowers inertia, so inertia can't tell you when you've gone too far. Its drop from k=2 to k=3 is huge and from k=3 to k=4 is small (the elbow), which hints at 3, but the signal is qualitative.

Silhouette measures something better: how much closer each point is to its own cluster than to the next-nearest cluster — it rewards tightness and separation. Over-splitting (k=4) puts a boundary through a real group, so points sit near a neighboring cluster and silhouette drops. That gives silhouette a true peak at the right k (0.557 at k=3), making it a more decisive, quantitative criterion than eyeballing an elbow.

2You run k-means twice with different random starts and get two different clusterings. Why does this happen, and how do you reduce it?
Show solution & reasoning

k-means starts by placing centroids at random, and the assign/update loop only finds a local optimum — so a bad starting position can converge to a worse clustering. Different random starts can therefore land in different solutions.

The standard fix is to run it several times from different initializations and keep the best (lowest inertia) — which is exactly what scikit-learn's n_init=10 does (10 restarts). A smarter initialization called k-means++ spreads the initial centers out, making good convergence far more likely, and it's the default. Together these make k-means reliably reproducible despite its random heart.

3You cluster customers and get 5 groups. A stakeholder asks "is this clustering correct?" Why is that question harder to answer than for a supervised model, and how would you respond?
Show solution & reasoning

It's harder because there are no ground-truth labels to check against — unlike a classifier, where you compare predictions to known answers and compute accuracy. "Correct" isn't well defined for clustering; there's no single right grouping.

A good response shifts from "correct" to "useful and coherent." You can offer: internal metrics (silhouette score for separation), stability checks (do the clusters persist across resamples?), and — most importantly — interpretation: do the groups correspond to recognizable, actionable customer types (e.g., "bargain hunters," "loyal high-spenders")? Clustering's value is judged by whether it produces meaningful, decision-supporting structure, which always involves human domain judgment, not just a number.

★ Deep dive: DBSCAN, hierarchical clustering, and the shape of "similar"▸

k-means has three built-in assumptions that its cousins relax. It assumes you know $k$, that clusters are roughly round and similar-sized, and that every point belongs to some cluster. DBSCAN drops all three: it groups points that are densely packed together, discovers the number of clusters on its own, follows arbitrary shapes (crescents, spirals), and labels sparse points as noise rather than forcing them into a group. The trade is that it has its own knobs (a neighborhood radius and a minimum density) and struggles when clusters have very different densities. Hierarchical clustering takes yet another view, building a tree (dendrogram) by repeatedly merging the closest groups; you then "cut" the tree at whatever level gives the number of clusters you want, and you get a whole nested family of clusterings to inspect.

Underneath every clustering method is one deep choice: what does "similar" mean? k-means uses straight-line (Euclidean) distance, which is why it makes round clusters. Swap in a different distance — cosine similarity for text (Lesson 1.3), or a graph distance — and the same algorithms find very different structure. This is why feature engineering and scaling matter so much in unsupervised learning: you are literally defining the geometry in which "close" is measured, and the clusters are only as meaningful as that geometry. Clustering doesn't find the truth; it finds the structure implied by your chosen notion of similarity — a subtle but important humility about what these methods do.

Clustering groups points; the other great unsupervised task is simplifying them. Next, in 3.11 Dimensionality reduction, we squeeze many features down to a few — for visualization, speed, and noise removal — with PCA, t-SNE, and UMAP.
