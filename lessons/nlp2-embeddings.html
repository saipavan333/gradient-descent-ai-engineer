Word embeddings

⏱ 25 min Lesson 5.2 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsAn ID is a meaningless label; an embedding is a learned vector where similar words sit close and directions encode meaning. Embeddings turn language into geometry — the substrate attention operates on, and the engine of semantic search.
By the end, you can
  • Explain embeddings as learned dense vectors
  • Use cosine similarity to compare words
  • Understand analogies as vector arithmetic
  • Know how word2vec/GloVe learn from context

Concept From meaningless IDs to vectors that carry meaning

Tokenization gave us integer IDs, but an ID like 2543 is an arbitrary label — there's nothing about the number 2543 that says "cat," and "cat" (2543) is no closer to "kitten" (8001) than to "tractor" (4112). We need a representation where similar words are actually close. That representation is the embedding: a learned dense vector (a list of, say, 768 numbers) assigned to each token. The model stores them in an embedding table — one row per vocabulary entry — and "embedding a token" is just looking up its row.

The magic is that these vectors are learned so that meaning becomes geometry. After training on lots of text, words used in similar ways end up with similar vectors — "cat" and "kitten" point in nearly the same direction, while "cat" and "tractor" point apart. We measure closeness with cosine similarity (Lesson 1.3): the cosine of the angle between two vectors, which is +1 for same-direction, 0 for unrelated, −1 for opposite.

Concept Directions encode relationships

Embeddings capture more than similarity — directions in the space encode relationships. The famous example: take the vector for "king," subtract "man," add "woman," and you land almost exactly on "queen." The model learned a consistent "royalty" direction and a "gender" direction, so vector arithmetic does analogies.

The classic methods that first showed this — word2vec and GloVe — learn embeddings from the principle "you shall know a word by the company it keeps": words appearing in similar contexts get similar vectors. word2vec trains a small network to predict a word's neighbors; GloVe factorizes a word-co-occurrence matrix. Both produce a single fixed vector per word — useful, but with a limitation we'll fix shortly.

Worked example Similarity and analogies with vectors

Let's use tiny 3-dimensional embeddings (real ones have hundreds of dimensions) where the axes loosely mean [royalty, female, animateness]. We'll compute cosine similarities and do the king−man+woman analogy by finding the nearest word.

"king" and "queen" are highly similar (cosine 0.848 — they share royalty and animateness), while "king" and "apple" are dissimilar (−0.309 — pointing different ways). And the vector arithmetic king − man + woman lands closest to queen: subtracting "man" removes the male component, adding "woman" supplies the female one, and the royalty stays — so you arrive at the female royal. This is the same dot-product/cosine machinery from Lesson 1.3, now measuring meaning. Real embeddings do this across hundreds of dimensions capturing far subtler relationships, all learned from text.

★ Key points to remember
  • An embedding is a learned dense vector per token; "embedding a token" = looking up its row in the embedding table.
  • Embeddings are learned so meaning becomes geometry: similar words have similar vectors.
  • Closeness is measured by cosine similarity (Lesson 1.3); directions encode relationships (king − man + woman ≈ queen).
  • word2vec / GloVe learn embeddings from context ("a word by the company it keeps").
  • These give one fixed vector per word — a limitation contextual models (transformers) remove.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Two vectors: $\mathbf{a} = [1, 0]$ and $\mathbf{b} = [0, 1]$. Compute their cosine similarity and interpret it. Then for $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{c} = [2, 0]$.
Show solution & reasoning

cos($\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}$) $= \frac{(1)(0)+(0)(1)}{1 \cdot 1} = \mathbf{0}$ — perpendicular vectors, so the words are unrelated in this space (no shared direction).

cos($\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{c}$) $= \frac{(1)(2)+(0)(0)}{1 \cdot 2} = \frac{2}{2} = \mathbf{1.0}$ — same direction, so maximally similar, even though $\mathbf{c}$ is twice as long. That's the key property of cosine: it measures direction, ignoring magnitude (Lesson 1.3), which is why it's the right tool for embeddings — we care that two words point the same way, not how long their vectors happen to be.

2You build a search engine that should match a query to documents by meaning, not exact words (so "car" matches a doc about "automobiles"). How do embeddings make this possible?
Show solution & reasoning

Embed the query and each document into the same vector space, then rank documents by cosine similarity to the query vector. Because "car" and "automobile" have nearly identical embeddings (similar usage), a query for "car" lands close to documents about automobiles even with zero shared words — this is semantic search, and it beats keyword matching, which would miss the synonym entirely.

This is exactly the foundation of Track 7 (RAG & Vector Search): represent everything as embeddings, then retrieve by nearest-neighbor in embedding space. The same king−man+woman geometry that does analogies also powers "find me things that mean something similar" — one of the most useful applications of embeddings in production.

3A word2vec model trained on web text encodes "doctor" closer to "he" and "nurse" closer to "she." What's happening, and why is it a problem?
Show solution & reasoning

The embeddings absorbed a bias present in the training text. Because the corpus statistically associated "doctor" with male contexts and "nurse" with female ones, the learned geometry encodes that association — the "gender direction" that does the king/queen analogy also picks up doctor − he ≈ nurse − she. The model faithfully learned a societal bias from the data.

It's a problem because any system built on these embeddings (search, resume screening, translation) can propagate and amplify the bias — e.g., ranking male candidates higher for "doctor." Embeddings are a mirror of their training data, including its prejudices. Mitigations include debiasing the vectors, curating data, and auditing outputs — and recognizing it is the first step. This is a recurring theme in responsible AI (Track 11): models learn what's in the data, fairness included or not.

★ Deep dive: static vs contextual embeddings, and embedding everything▸

word2vec and GloVe give each word one fixed vector — which breaks on words with multiple meanings. "bank" gets a single embedding that awkwardly averages "river bank" and "savings bank," and "play" blends the verb and the noun. These are static embeddings: context-free. The breakthrough of transformers (this whole track) is contextual embeddings — the vector for "bank" is computed on the fly from the surrounding words, so "bank" near "river" and "bank" near "money" get different vectors. Attention (Lesson 5.4) is the mechanism that mixes in context, which is why a transformer's word representations are so much richer than a lookup table. The embedding table is still there at the input, but the network refines it layer by layer using context.

The idea generalizes far beyond words. You can embed sentences (for semantic search and clustering), images (so similar pictures are close — and, with models like CLIP, so an image and its text caption land near each other in a shared space), code, users and products (for recommendations, Track 10), and more. The unifying idea — "represent anything as a vector in a space where distance means similarity" — is one of the most powerful in all of machine learning, because once things are vectors you can use the entire toolkit of Tracks 1 and 3 on them: measure similarity, cluster, reduce dimensions, search. Embeddings are the bridge between messy real-world objects and the geometry that ML understands, and they reappear in every track ahead — most directly in Track 7, where embedding-based retrieval is the core of giving a language model your own knowledge.

Now each token is a meaningful vector. But how does a model actually use a sequence of them to predict and generate text? Next, in 5.3 Language modeling basics, we meet the simple objective — predict the next token — that underlies every model from autocomplete to GPT.
