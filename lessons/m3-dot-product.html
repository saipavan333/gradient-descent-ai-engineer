Dot products, similarity & projection

⏱ 26 min Lesson 1.3 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsSearch, recommendations, attention in transformers, embeddings — the way a machine decides 'how similar are these two things?' is almost always a dot product. It is the most important small operation in modern AI.
By the end, you can
  • Compute a dot product and state the two things it measures
  • Use cosine similarity to compare direction regardless of size
  • Understand projection: how much of one vector lies along another
  • Connect dot products to similarity search and attention

Concept The question behind half of modern AI

"Here's a song you liked — find more like it." "Here's a search box — find the most relevant pages." "Here's a word — which earlier words should I pay attention to?" Recommendations, search, and the attention mechanism inside every modern language model are all answering one question: how similar are these two vectors?

To answer it, a machine needs to turn the fuzzy idea of "similar" into a single number it can compare and rank. The tool that does this — cheaply, and with a lovely geometric meaning — is the dot product. You already used its mechanic in Lesson 1.2 (each row of a matrix was "combined with" a vector). Now we give it a name, understand what it actually means, and watch it power real systems.

Concept The computation: multiply pairs, then add

The dot product of two vectors of the same length works in two steps: (1) multiply their matching entries together (first×first, second×second, …), and (2) sum those products — "sum" just means "add them all into one total." The result is a single number (a scalar), not a vector. We write it with a centered dot: $a \cdot b$ (read "a dot b").

$$ a \cdot b = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \cdots + a_n b_n. $$

(The subscripts just label the positions: $a_1$ is the first entry of $a$, $b_1$ the first of $b$, and so on.)

Let's do the numbers from the picture, step by step. For $a = (2, 5, 1)$ and $b = (3, 1, 4)$:

$$ a \cdot b = (2)(3) + (5)(1) + (1)(4) = 6 + 5 + 4 = 15. $$

Two times three is six; five times one is five; one times four is four; add them, $6+5+4 = 15$. That's the entire computation. (And notice: this is precisely "row meets column" from matrix multiplication — a matrix multiply is really just a grid full of dot products.)

Concept What the number means: alignment

A single number from a sum of products doesn't obviously mean anything — until you learn the identity that makes the dot product matter. For any two vectors, it turns out that

$$ a \cdot b = \|a\|\,\|b\|\,\cos\theta. $$

Let's unpack every symbol. You know $\|a\|$ and $\|b\|$ — those are the lengths (norms) from Lesson 1.1. The new symbol $\theta$ (the Greek letter "theta") is the angle between the two arrows — how "open" the corner is between them, measured in degrees (a quarter turn is 90°, a straight line is 180°). And $\cos\theta$ (the cosine of that angle) is a number that smoothly ranges from $+1$ down to $-1$ and acts as an "alignment meter": it's $+1$ when the arrows point the exact same way, $0$ when they're at a right angle, and $-1$ when they point exactly opposite. You don't need to compute cosines by hand — just read $\cos\theta$ as "how aligned, on a scale from $-1$ to $+1$."

So the identity says, in plain English: the dot product is the two lengths multiplied together, then scaled by how aligned the directions are. Same lengths, but the more the arrows agree in direction, the bigger the dot product.

The sign of the dot product alone already tells a story, because $\cos\theta$ controls it:

If the vectors…Angle $\theta$$\cos\theta$$a\cdot b$ is…Plain meaning
point the same way$0^\circ$$+1$large positivestrongly similar
are at a right angle$90^\circ$$0$exactly $0$unrelated / independent
point opposite ways$180^\circ$$-1$large negativestrongly opposed

That middle row is worth memorizing: "a dot product of zero means the vectors are at a right angle." Vectors at right angles are called orthogonal (a fancy word for "perpendicular"), and the idea returns constantly — in PCA, in error terms, all over ML.

Concept Cosine similarity: compare direction, ignore size

There's a snag in using the raw dot product as a similarity score: it mixes length with alignment. A longer document shares more words with everything, so its dot products are big just for being long — not for being relevant. To compare direction only, we divide the lengths back out. Rearranging the identity above to solve for $\cos\theta$ gives cosine similarity:

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{a \cdot b}{\|a\|\,\|b\|}, \qquad \text{always between } -1 \text{ and } +1. $$

Because we divided away the lengths, only direction remains: $+1$ means "same direction / very similar," $0$ means "unrelated," $-1$ means "opposite." This is the default way to compare embeddings (the learned vectors that represent words, images, or products) in search, recommendations, and RAG (Track 8). A short query and a long document can still get a fair similarity score because length has been removed from the comparison.

Concept Projection: how much of one vector lies along another

One more idea drops straight out of the dot product. The projection of $a$ onto $b$ is the "shadow" $a$ casts on $b$'s direction — imagine shining a light straight down onto the line that $b$ lies on; the shadow of $a$ is how far along $b$ you get if you keep only the part of $a$ that points $b$'s way. Its length is

$$ \text{projection length} = \|a\|\cos\theta = \frac{a \cdot b}{\|b\|}. $$

A concrete check: with $a = (4, 3)$ and $b = (1, 0)$ (the horizontal direction), $a \cdot b = 4$ and $\|b\| = 1$, so the projection length is $4$ — exactly the horizontal part of $a$. Projection is the seed of how linear regression finds its "best fit" (it projects the targets onto what the features can reach) and how PCA finds its axes. You're quietly collecting the pieces.

Concept The payoff: this is how attention works

Here's where it all lands. In a transformer — the architecture behind modern language models — every word produces a query vector (what it's looking for) and a key vector (what it offers). To decide how much one word should "pay attention to" another, the model takes the dot product of the query with each key. Bigger dot product → more aligned → more attention. Those scores are then squeezed into weights that add up to 1 (by a step called softmax, which just turns a list of scores into positive fractions summing to 1), and the model blends information using those weights.

You won't build this until Track 6, but pause on what just happened: the tiny operation from this lesson — multiply matching entries and add — is the exact mechanism a state-of-the-art model uses to decide what's relevant. Learn the dot product well and a surprising amount of modern AI stops being mysterious.

Worked example A tiny search engine with cosine similarity

Let's rank documents by relevance to a query — the heart of search and RAG — using nothing but the dot product. We represent each document as a word-count vector over a small five-word vocabulary [data, model, train, cook, recipe] (each slot counts how many times that word appears).

The query ranks ML note highest, ML article next, and the unrelated Recipe blog at exactly 0 — they share no words, so the vectors are orthogonal (right angle, $\cos = 0$). Notice "ML article" scores lower than "ML note" even though it also contains the query's words: it's a longer vector, and cosine fairly discounts that extra length. With raw dot products both ML docs would tie at 2, hiding the real ranking — which is exactly why cosine, not the bare dot product, is the similarity of choice.

PyTorch ships this as a one-liner, and it's what vector databases compute at scale:

import torch.nn.functional as F
F.cosine_similarity(q, d, dim=0)     # same number, GPU-ready

TensorFlow: -tf.keras.losses.cosine_similarity(q, d) (Keras returns the negative by convention, so flip the sign).

★ Key points to remember
  • The dot product $a\cdot b = \sum_i a_i b_i$ multiplies matching entries and sums them into one number.
  • Its meaning: $a\cdot b = \|a\|\,\|b\|\cos\theta$ — lengths times alignment. Positive = similar direction, zero = right angle (orthogonal), negative = opposed.
  • Cosine similarity $\dfrac{a\cdot b}{\|a\|\,\|b\|} \in [-1, 1]$ compares direction only, ignoring length — the standard for embeddings, search, and RAG.
  • Projection length $= \dfrac{a\cdot b}{\|b\|}$ is how much of $a$ points along $b$ — the basis of best-fit and PCA.
  • Attention scores words by query·key dot products — the same operation, at the heart of transformers.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Compute the cosine similarity between $a = (1, 1)$ and $b = (1, 0)$, step by step. What angle does it correspond to, and does the number feel right?
Show solution & reasoning

Dot product: $a\cdot b = 1(1) + 1(0) = 1$. Lengths: $\|a\| = \sqrt{1^2+1^2} = \sqrt{2}$, $\|b\| = 1$. So $\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}\cdot 1} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt 2} \approx 0.707$.

That's $\cos 45^\circ$ — and indeed $a=(1,1)$ points up at a $45^\circ$ diagonal while $b=(1,0)$ points straight along the horizontal, so the angle between them is exactly $45^\circ$. A similarity of $0.71$ feels right: clearly related, but not identical. Checking a formula against a picture you can see is a habit worth keeping.

2A user's taste vector is $u = (5, 1, 0)$ (loves action, neutral on comedy, no romance). Two movies are $m_1 = (4, 0, 0)$ and $m_2 = (0, 1, 5)$. Using the raw dot product, which movie is recommended — and does it match intuition?
Show solution & reasoning

$u\cdot m_1 = 5(4) + 1(0) + 0(0) = 20$. $u\cdot m_2 = 5(0) + 1(1) + 0(5) = 1$.

$m_1$ wins by a wide margin (20 vs 1), which matches intuition: the user loves action and $m_1$ is pure action, while $m_2$ is mostly romance — a genre the user has zero interest in. This is, in miniature, how a recommender ranks items against a taste profile. (Follow-up: try cosine — it would still pick $m_1$, but the gap narrows because cosine ignores how strong the action signal is.)

3If two vectors are each already unit length (norm exactly 1), how does cosine similarity relate to the plain dot product? Why is this a useful trick in practice?
Show solution & reasoning

A unit vector is one whose length is 1. When $\|a\| = \|b\| = 1$, the denominator $\|a\|\|b\| = 1$, so $\cos\theta = a\cdot b$. For unit vectors, the dot product is the cosine similarity.

Why it's useful: real systems normalize embeddings to unit length once, up front (divide each vector by its own norm). Afterward, a single cheap dot product gives the cosine similarity directly — no per-comparison division. Vector databases searching millions of items rely on exactly this shortcut. It's a neat case of doing a little work early to save a lot of work later.

★ Deep dive: cosine vs. straight-line distance, and the mysterious $\sqrt{d_k}$ in attention▸

Why cosine and not Euclidean distance for text/embeddings? Picture two documents about the exact same topic, but one is twice as long, so its word-count vector is roughly double the other: $b \approx 2a$. They point the same direction, so cosine similarity is $1$ — "identical topic," which is what we want. But the straight-line distance $\|a - b\| = \|a - 2a\| = \|a\|$ is large, falsely declaring them different. Direction often carries the meaning; raw magnitude often carries irrelevant scale. Cosine keeps the former and discards the latter.

The attention scaling factor. When you reach transformers you'll see attention scores written as $\dfrac{q \cdot k}{\sqrt{d_k}}$, not just $q\cdot k$. Here's intuition you can already appreciate: if $q$ and $k$ each have $d_k$ entries of typical size around 1, their dot product is a sum of $d_k$ random-ish products, and that sum tends to grow as the vectors get longer (its spread scales like $\sqrt{d_k}$). For $d_k = 64$ that's a swing of roughly $\pm 8$ before the model has learned anything. Feeding numbers that large into softmax makes it "saturate" — one weight near 1, the rest near 0 — which stalls learning. Dividing by $\sqrt{d_k}$ rescales the scores back to a sane range so training stays healthy. You don't need the full story yet; just notice that a detail in a cutting-edge model is, at root, a statement about the typical size of a dot product — which you now understand.

Connection back to projection. The numerator of cosine similarity, $a\cdot b$, is also what defines the projection of $a$ onto $b$. Similarity and projection are two faces of the same operation: asking "how much does $a$ agree with the direction of $b$?" That single question, asked billions of times per second on GPUs, is a large part of what modern AI actually does.
