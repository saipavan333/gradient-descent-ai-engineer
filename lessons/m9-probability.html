Probability essentials: uncertainty, distributions, Bayes

⏱ 29 min Lesson 1.9 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsReal data is noisy, so models speak probability: classifiers output confidences, generative models sample from distributions. Bayes' rule is how evidence updates belief — the backbone of countless ML methods.
By the end, you can
  • Use probability to describe uncertainty
  • Recognize Bernoulli, categorical, and Gaussian distributions
  • Apply conditional probability and Bayes' rule
  • Explain why classifiers output probabilities, not hard labels

Concept Why models speak in probabilities

The real world is noisy and the future is uncertain, so a good model rarely says "this email is spam." It says "this email is spam with probability 0.92." That hedge is a feature, not a weakness: it lets you set sensible cut-offs, weigh risks, and combine evidence. Probability is simply the mathematical language for uncertainty — for talking precisely about things that might or might not happen — and it underpins classification, generative models (which literally roll dice to create things), and the loss functions you'll train with.

The starting vocabulary is small. An outcome is one possible result of some situation (rolling a 4). The sample space, written $\Omega$ (the Greek letter "omega"), is the set of all possible outcomes (for a die, $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$). An event is any group of outcomes you care about ("rolled an even number" = the group $\{2,4,6\}$). And the probability of an event is the fraction of the sample space it covers — a number between $0$ (impossible) and $1$ (certain). For a fair die, "even" covers 3 of the 6 outcomes, so its probability is $3/6 = 0.5$.

Concept Random variables and distributions

A random variable is a number whose value depends on a random outcome — for example, the result of a die roll, or "is this email spam?" coded as 1 (yes) or 0 (no). A distribution is the rule that says how probable each possible value is. Two rules always hold: every probability is at least 0, and they add up to exactly 1 (something must happen).

A few distributions appear constantly in ML, so learn their names now:

  • Bernoulli — one yes/no event with probability $p$ of "yes" (a single coin flip; "is it spam?"). It's what a two-class classifier outputs.
  • Categorical — one outcome out of $k$ choices, each with its own probability (which of 10 digits is this image?). It's what a multi-class classifier outputs, produced by a step called softmax (which turns a list of raw scores into positive numbers that sum to 1).
  • Gaussian (also called normal) — the famous bell curve for continuous quantities, described by a mean $\mu$ ("mu," the center) and a standard deviation $\sigma$ ("sigma," how spread out it is). We'll formalize mean and spread in Lesson 1.10; for now, mean = center, $\sigma$ = width.

Concept Conditional probability and Bayes' rule

Most useful probabilities are conditional: the probability of $A$ given that $B$ has happened, written $P(A \mid B)$ (the vertical bar reads "given"). "Probability of spam given the word 'free' appears." Evidence changes belief, and Bayes' rule is the exact recipe for that update:

$$ P(A \mid B) \;=\; \frac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)}. $$

Three named pieces, in plain words. $P(A)$ is the prior — how likely $A$ was before you saw any evidence (the base rate). $P(B \mid A)$ is the likelihood — how well $A$ explains the evidence you saw. And the left side $P(A\mid B)$ is the posterior — your updated belief after combining the two. (The bottom, $P(B)$, just rescales things so the probabilities still add to 1.) The classic, counterintuitive example is medical testing — and it carries a lesson that fools even doctors.

!
The base-rate trap

When a condition is rare, even a very accurate test gives mostly false alarms, because there are so many more healthy people to misclassify. Ignoring the prior (the base rate) is one of the most common reasoning errors in all of data science. Bayes' rule forces you to account for it.

Concept Why classifiers output probabilities, not labels

A classifier doesn't just guess a class — it estimates $P(\text{class} \mid \text{input})$, the probability of each class given the input. That probability is far more useful than a bare label: you can choose a cut-off based on the cost of mistakes (flag a transaction as fraud only if $P > 0.9$ to avoid annoying customers, or as low as $P > 0.3$ if missing fraud is catastrophic), rank predictions by confidence, and combine several models. The final softmax layer of a neural classifier exists precisely to turn raw scores into a valid probability distribution over the classes.

Worked example The medical test, computed — and a coin finding its fairness

Plug the diagram's numbers into Bayes' rule. Disease rate (prior) $P(\text{sick}) = 0.01$; test sensitivity $P(+\mid\text{sick}) = 0.99$ (it catches 99% of sick people); false-positive rate $P(+\mid\text{well}) = 0.05$ (5% of healthy people test positive anyway). What's the chance you're actually sick given a positive test?

p_sick = 0.01
p_pos_given_sick = 0.99
p_pos_given_well = 0.05

# P(+) = the two ways to test positive: truly sick, OR healthy false-positive
p_pos = p_pos_given_sick * p_sick + p_pos_given_well * (1 - p_sick)
posterior = (p_pos_given_sick * p_sick) / p_pos
print("P(sick | positive test):", round(posterior, 3))   # 0.167
OutputP(sick | positive test): 0.167

Only 16.7% — despite a "99% accurate" test! Why so low? Imagine 10,000 people: about 100 are sick (1%), and the test catches ~99 of them. But among the 9,900 healthy people, 5% — about 495 — also test positive. So a positive result is 99 true alarms drowned in 495 false ones: $99 / (99 + 495) \approx 17\%$. This is why a single positive screening test is usually followed by a second, independent test (which updates the posterior again). Now a simulation showing probability emerge from data — flip a fair coin and watch the observed fraction settle toward the true $0.5$:

import numpy as np
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
flips = rng.integers(0, 2, size=10000)        # 0 or 1, each with probability 0.5
for n in [10, 100, 1000, 10000]:
    print(f"after {n:5d} flips, fraction heads = {flips[:n].mean():.3f}")
Output (varies slightly by seed)after 10 flips, fraction heads = 0.400 after 100 flips, fraction heads = 0.530 after 1000 flips, fraction heads = 0.508 after 10000 flips, fraction heads = 0.499

With few flips the estimate is jumpy; with many it homes in on the true $0.5$. That convergence — more data gives a more reliable estimate — is the bridge to statistics (Lesson 1.10) and the reason large test sets give trustworthy performance numbers.

★ Key points to remember
  • Probability measures uncertainty: an event's probability is the fraction of the sample space it covers, between 0 and 1.
  • A distribution assigns probabilities to a random variable's values; they're ≥ 0 and sum to 1.
  • Key distributions: Bernoulli (yes/no), categorical (one of $k$, via softmax), Gaussian (bell curve, mean & std).
  • Bayes' rule updates belief with evidence: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior. Never ignore the prior (base rate).
  • Classifiers output probabilities so you can set cost-based cut-offs, rank, and combine models.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Two fair six-sided dice are rolled. What is the probability the sum is 7? (Hint: count the favorable outcomes out of the 36 total.)
Show solution & reasoning

There are $6 \times 6 = 36$ equally-likely outcomes. The pairs summing to 7 are $(1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)$ — six of them. So $P(\text{sum}=7) = 6/36 = 1/6 \approx 0.167$.

The reasoning is the definition of probability in action: count the slice of the sample space your event covers, divide by the whole. Seven is the most likely sum precisely because it has the most ways to occur — a fact casinos are built on.

2A spam filter sees the word "lottery." Given $P(\text{lottery}\mid\text{spam}) = 0.10$, $P(\text{lottery}\mid\text{not spam}) = 0.001$, and a prior $P(\text{spam}) = 0.3$, compute $P(\text{spam}\mid\text{lottery})$.
Show solution & reasoning

Numerator (likelihood × prior): $0.10 \times 0.3 = 0.030$.

Bottom $P(\text{lottery}) = 0.10(0.3) + 0.001(0.7) = 0.030 + 0.0007 = 0.0307$.

Posterior: $P(\text{spam}\mid\text{lottery}) = 0.030 / 0.0307 \approx 0.977$, about 98%.

The word "lottery" is so much more common in spam than in real mail that one occurrence pushes belief from a 30% prior up to 98%. This is exactly how a Naive Bayes spam classifier works — multiply such updates across many words. You just built the core of one of the first practical ML systems.

3A friend says: "This face-recognition system is 99.9% accurate, so if it flags someone at the airport, they're almost certainly the wanted person." Using base-rate reasoning, explain why that's wrong.
Show solution & reasoning

The wanted person is extraordinarily rare in the crowd — maybe 1 in 1,000,000 travelers. Even at 99.9% accuracy, the 0.1% false-positive rate applied to a million innocent people produces about 1000 false flags, versus the single true match. So a flag means roughly $1/1001 \approx 0.1\%$ chance of being the right person — not "almost certainly."

The reasoning is the base-rate trap again: when the prior is tiny, the sheer number of innocent people guarantees false positives swamp the true ones, no matter how accurate the system sounds. This is why "accuracy" alone is a misleading metric for rare-event detection — a theme that returns in Track 3 (precision/recall) and Track 11 (responsible AI).

★ Deep dive: independence, likelihood vs probability, and why we use log-probabilities▸

Independence. Two events are independent if one tells you nothing about the other, in which case their probabilities multiply: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A)\,P(B)$. "Naive" Bayes is "naive" precisely because it assumes features are independent given the class (clearly false — "free" and "money" co-occur), yet the approximation works remarkably well. Recognizing when independence holds (or is a tolerable lie) is a real modeling skill.

Likelihood vs probability. They use the same formula but ask opposite questions. Probability fixes the model and asks about data: "given a fair coin, how probable is the sequence HHTH?" Likelihood fixes the data and asks about the model: "given that I observed HHTH, how likely is each possible coin bias?" Flipping the question this way — choosing the model parameters that make the observed data most likely — is maximum likelihood estimation, the principle behind most loss functions, which is the heart of the next lesson.

Why logs. Multiplying many probabilities together (as Naive Bayes does across words, or as a language model does across thousands of tokens) gives numbers so tiny they "underflow" to zero in a computer — $0.01^{300}$ is unrepresentable. The fix is to work with log-probabilities: because $\log(ab) = \log a + \log b$, products turn into sums, which are numerically stable and faster. (A logarithm, "log," answers "what power do I raise the base to?"; the key fact here is just that it turns multiplication into addition.) This is why you'll constantly see log_softmax, negative log-likelihood, and log-loss in ML code — the same probabilities, in a form computers can handle. It's also why cross-entropy loss (Track 3) is written with logarithms.
