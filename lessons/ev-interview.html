Track 11 · Evals, Safety & Responsible AI

Interview check: Evals & Safety

⏱ 15 min Lesson 11.8
Why this existsEvals & safety interviews test whether you can be trusted to deploy AI responsibly: metrics as proxies, offline vs online, LLM-as-judge, fairness, safety, and accountability. This checkpoint rehearses those with model answers.
By the end, you can
  • Evaluate a model across quality and safety
  • Answer 'is 95% accuracy good?'
  • Reason about fairness and proxies
  • Discuss safety, red-teaming, and accountability

Concept What an evals & safety interview tests

Questions about evaluation and safety separate engineers who can build a model from those who can be trusted to deploy one responsibly. Interviewers probe whether you understand that metrics are proxies to be scrutinized, that offline results must be validated online, that generative systems need new evaluation methods, and that fairness, safety, privacy, and accountability are engineering concerns — not someone else's job. The signal they want is rigor and responsibility: an instinct to ask "how do I know this is actually good?" and "how could this cause harm?" The model answers below show that mindset.

Model answer "How would you evaluate a new model before shipping it?"

"I'd evaluate in layers, because no single method is enough. Offline first: on a held-out test set that's representative (including hard and rare cases) and leak-free, using metrics that match the task and error costs — precision/ recall or F1 rather than raw accuracy on imbalanced data, ranking metrics for retrieval, and for generation, human eval or a validated LLM-as-judge since there's no single correct output. I'd slice results by subgroup to catch failures overall metrics hide. Then online: because offline is a proxy that often doesn't transfer, I'd A/B test on real traffic, checking the primary metric and guardrail metrics (that engagement gains don't come at the cost of retention, satisfaction, or fairness), with proper statistical significance and no peeking. Safety and fairness: I'd test for bias (outcome rates by group, disparate impact), red-team for harmful outputs and jailbreaks, and check privacy (no PII leakage). Only if it's better offline, confirmed online, and safe would I ship — ideally via a gradual canary rollout with monitoring. The theme is that 'better' has to be proven across quality, safety, and fairness, not asserted from one number."

Strong close: metrics are proxies, so triangulate — offline, online, and human — and never trust a single number.

Model answer "A model is 95% accurate. Is it good?"

"I can't say without more context — 95% accuracy can be excellent or worthless. First, the class balance: if the positive class is rare (say 3%), a model predicting the majority always scores 97% while catching nothing — the accuracy paradox — so I'd want precision and recall on the class that matters, not accuracy. Second, the error costs: which is worse, a false positive or a false negative? For fraud or disease, missing a case is catastrophic, so recall matters most; for spam, a false alarm is costly, so precision does. Third, how it was measured: on what data, how representative, any leakage, and does it hold across subgroups (95% overall could hide 70% for one demographic — a fairness problem). Fourth, does the metric even capture the goal? For an open-ended or business task, accuracy may be the wrong measure entirely. So my answer is a set of questions, because 95% is a proxy whose meaning depends on the balance, the costs, the measurement, and the real objective. Reflexively accepting a headline accuracy is exactly the trap this field teaches you to avoid."

Model answer "How do you evaluate an LLM's output quality at scale?"

"The challenge is that generative outputs have no single correct answer, so I can't just compare to a label. The gold standard is human evaluation, but it doesn't scale, so the practical approach is LLM-as-judge: have a capable model score outputs against a clear rubric, returning a score and its reasoning. The non-negotiable step is validating the judge against human labels — measuring agreement (exact and within-1) on a sample — before trusting it, because the judge is itself a biased model. I'd watch for known judge biases: position bias (randomize and average over orderings), verbosity bias (control for length), and self-preference. I'd keep humans in the loop for the high-stakes and subjective judgments and as the periodic anchor that keeps the automated judge honest — because there's a real circularity risk in using LLMs to judge LLMs, where they share blind spots. So it's a hybrid: the judge for scale and continuous evaluation, human eval for validation and the hardest cases. And I'd be honest that evaluating capable generative systems well is genuinely hard and partly unsolved — which is exactly why it's so important to invest in."

Model answer "Your model shows disparate impact against a group, though you never used a protected attribute. Explain and fix."

"Removing the protected attribute doesn't remove the bias, because of proxy features — other inputs correlated with the attribute (zip code, school, employment gaps, word choice) that let the model discriminate indirectly. And if the training labels encode historical bias, the model learns to reproduce it through those proxies. 'Fairness through unawareness' — just dropping the attribute — is a common and insufficient fix. To address it: measure the disparate impact (outcome rates by group, the four-fifths rule) — which means keeping the protected attribute available for auditing even if it's not a feature. Diagnose which proxy features drive the gap. Mitigate with re-weighting or re-sampling the data, removing strong proxies, fairness constraints in training, or post-processing — accepting the trade-offs, including that fairness definitions conflict so I must choose one deliberately, and that there may be an accuracy cost. And I'd interrogate the labels: if past decisions were biased, the real fix is rethinking the target, not just the model. The key insight is that bias lives in correlations and history, so genuine fairness work is measuring, hunting proxies, and correcting data — not the false comfort of 'we didn't use gender.'"

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • Why is eval hard? Metrics are proxies for the goal; systems fail in the gap (Goodhart's law).
  • Is 95% accuracy good? Depends on class balance, error costs, measurement, and whether accuracy is even the right metric.
  • Precision vs recall? Precision = of flagged, how many right; recall = of actual positives, how many caught; choose by error costs.
  • Offline vs online? Offline is a fast proxy; online A/B is ground truth — offline wins often don't transfer.
  • A/B pitfalls? Peeking, too-short tests, no significance, single-metric (ignore guardrails).
  • Evaluate generation? Human eval (gold, unscalable) + validated LLM-as-judge (scalable, biased) — triangulate.
  • Fairness? Measure outcome rates by group; definitions conflict; removing the attribute doesn't remove bias (proxies).
  • Safety? Guardrails (input/output filters), red-teaming, jailbreaks; defense in depth, ongoing cat-and-mouse.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"You're building an AI system to screen job applications. Walk me through how you'd evaluate it — for quality AND responsibility." (Full answer.)
Show a strong answer

"Quality: I'd define success carefully — probably not 'predicts who we hired' (that encodes past bias) but a validated measure of job performance, and I'd be skeptical the labels are clean. Offline, I'd measure precision/recall on the outcome that matters, sliced by subgroup, on representative, leak-free data. Since a résumé screener is high-stakes, I wouldn't rely on offline alone. Responsibility — this is where I'd spend most time: Fairness is paramount — I'd measure disparate impact (outcome rates by protected group, four-fifths rule), knowing that even without using protected attributes, proxy features can discriminate, so I'd hunt for proxies and audit the labels for historical bias. I'd choose a fairness definition deliberately with stakeholders and legal, and accept the accuracy trade-offs. Transparency: applicants and regulators may require explanations for decisions, so I'd need interpretability and documentation (model cards). Human oversight: for a decision this consequential, the AI should assist humans, not autonomously reject people, with a clear appeals path. Privacy: résumés are PII — minimize, secure, and get consent. Accountability: clear ownership and audit trails. And I'd genuinely ask whether this system should be fully automated at all. The theme is that for a system affecting people's livelihoods, responsibility isn't a checkbox after quality — it's the core of the evaluation."

Leading with fairness, questioning the labels, insisting on human oversight and accountability, and even asking whether to build it — that's exactly the responsibility-first judgment a high-stakes AI evaluation demands, and what distinguishes a mature answer from a metrics-only one.

2"Our chatbot occasionally produces harmful content when users try hard enough. Leadership wants it '100% safe.' How do you respond?"
Show a strong answer

"I'd reset the expectation honestly while showing a rigorous plan. '100% safe' isn't achievable for an open-ended generative system facing adversarial users — jailbreaks are an ongoing cat-and-mouse, and anyone promising perfect safety misunderstands the problem. What I can commit to is driving harmful outputs as low as possible and ensuring that when failures happen, they can't cause serious damage. Concretely: defense in depth — safety training plus independent input/output guardrails (classifiers, not just keyword lists) so a jailbroken model's harmful output still gets caught on the way out; continuous red-teaming to find and patch new jailbreaks before users do, treating it as ongoing work, not a one-time gate; limiting capabilities so even a compromised bot can't take dangerous actions or access sensitive data (least privilege); monitoring live outputs to detect and respond to failures fast; and a clear incident process for when something slips through. I'd set measurable targets (harmful-output rate on a red-team suite, time-to-patch) and report against them. The honest framing is that safety is a continuous posture, not a solved state — so I'd deliver rigorous, layered, monitored defenses and rapid response, and I'd push back on the false promise of perfection, because believing you're '100% safe' is itself dangerous."

Pushing back on an impossible mandate with honesty and a concrete, layered, measurable safety program — rather than either over-promising or being dismissive — is precisely the mature safety judgment interviewers are testing, and it shows you understand adversarial robustness as a process.

3"Why do you think evaluation has become such a central challenge in modern AI?" (Tests depth of understanding.)
Show a strong answer

"Because AI capability has outrun our ability to measure it, and you can't reliably improve or safely deploy what you can't measure. A few reasons converge. Generative models broke the old paradigm: a classifier had a labeled test set and clear metrics, but open-ended generation has no single correct answer and multi-dimensional, subjective quality, so 'compare to the label' doesn't work — hence human eval, LLM-as-judge, and their difficulties. Capability makes evaluation harder, not easier: the more capable a model, the more expert judgment it takes to evaluate — assessing PhD-level outputs needs PhD-level evaluators — so the evaluation gap widens as models improve. The stakes rose: as AI makes consequential decisions and takes actions affecting millions, evaluating not just quality but safety, fairness, and harm became essential, and those are even harder to measure than accuracy. And metrics are proxies that get gamed (Goodhart) — benchmarks saturate and get contaminated, so measured progress can diverge from real progress. The result is that evaluation is now the bottleneck: it gates how fast we can improve models and how safely we can deploy them, which is why so much of the field's effort — and so much of what makes a team effective — is really about building trustworthy evaluation. My takeaway is that investment in evaluation is investment in the ability to make progress at all, and that treating it as a first-class discipline, not a formality, is what separates teams that improve reliably from teams optimizing in the dark."

Connecting the evaluation challenge to the shift to generative AI, the capability-evaluation gap, the rise in stakes, and Goodhart — and drawing the strategic conclusion that evaluation gates progress — demonstrates exactly the depth of understanding that marks a candidate who has truly internalized this track.

★ Deep dive: the evaluation-and-responsibility mindset, end to end▸

The strongest candidates on evaluation and safety share a single disposition: reflexive skepticism paired with responsibility. Where a beginner sees a number and accepts it, they ask "what does this metric actually capture, what does it miss, and how could it mislead?" — because they've internalized that every measurement is a proxy with blind spots (Lesson 11.1), from a headline accuracy to a benchmark to an LLM judge's score. This skepticism isn't cynicism; it's rigor. It shows up as instincts: check the class balance before trusting accuracy; slice by subgroup before trusting an aggregate; validate offline gains online before trusting they transfer; validate a judge against humans before trusting it at scale; look for proxy features before trusting 'we didn't use the sensitive attribute.' Each is the same move — don't take the convenient number at face value; triangulate and probe for what it hides. When you reason about any evaluation question this way, you demonstrate that you understand the deepest truth of the field: that knowing whether a system is good is often harder than making it, and that the ability to evaluate rigorously is what makes all the building meaningful.

The second half of the disposition is treating responsibility as inseparable from engineering. The mature engineer doesn't ask only 'does it work?' but 'is it fair? safe? private? accountable? should it exist?' — and treats those as their job, not a compliance afterthought. This connects evaluation and safety to everything in the curriculum: it's the discipline that decides whether a classifier's accuracy is trustworthy (Track 3), whether an LLM's fluent output is actually correct and safe (Track 6), whether a RAG answer is grounded (Track 7), whether an agent's actions are constrained (Track 8), whether a deployed system stays healthy (Track 9), and whether a designed system meets its real requirements (Track 10). Evaluation and responsibility are the connective tissue that turns capability into trustworthy capability. And they reflect where the field is going: as AI grows more powerful and more embedded in society, the decisive questions shift from 'can we build it?' to 'can we evaluate it, govern it, and ensure it serves people well?' The engineers who will matter most are those who hold both halves — the technical capability of the earlier tracks and the evaluative rigor and ethical seriousness of this one. That union of skill and judgment, capability wed to responsibility, is what this entire curriculum has been building toward, and carrying it into everything you build is what makes you not just a capable AI engineer, but a trustworthy one."
