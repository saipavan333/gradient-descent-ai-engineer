Momentum, RMSProp & Adam

Plain gradient descent is honest but easily frustrated — it crawls down long shallow valleys and rattles across steep ones. Three upgrades fix this, culminating in Adam, the optimizer that trains most of modern deep learning.

Track 2 · Lesson 3 Concept + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 2.0–2.2

💡 Concept: the problem with plain gradient descent

Real loss surfaces are rarely tidy round bowls. Far more often they’re ill-conditioned — steep and narrow in some directions, shallow and flat in others, like a long ravine. On such a surface vanilla gradient descent has a miserable time: the steep walls produce big gradients that fling it back and forth across the valley, while the gentle floor produces tiny gradients that let it only inch toward the actual minimum. The result is the maddening zig-zag below.

📐 Upgrade 1: Momentum — give the ball mass

The first fix borrows from physics. Instead of letting each gradient dictate the step in isolation, accumulate a running velocity \(\mathbf{v}\) — an exponentially weighted average of past gradients — and step with that. Consistent downhill directions reinforce and pick up speed; directions that keep flipping sign (the cross-valley bouncing) average out and cancel. It’s a heavy ball rolling through the ravine instead of a skittish point:

$$ \mathbf{v}_t = \beta\,\mathbf{v}_{t-1} + \nabla f(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t-1}), \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_t = \boldsymbol{\theta}_{t-1} - \alpha\,\mathbf{v}_t. $$
(★)

The new symbol \(\beta\) (beta), the momentum coefficient, is between \(0\) and \(1\) (typically \(0.9\)) and sets how much past velocity carries over. With \(\beta=0\) you recover plain GD; at \(0.9\) each step remembers a decaying trail of roughly the last ten gradients. Along the shallow valley floor those gradients all point the same way, so the velocity builds up and the ball accelerates; across the steep walls the gradients alternate direction and largely cancel in the average, so the bouncing is damped. Momentum both speeds up progress and smooths the path.

📐 Upgrade 2: RMSProp — a personal learning rate per parameter

Momentum smooths direction; RMSProp fixes the scale problem directly. The trouble with ill-conditioning is that one global \(\alpha\) is simultaneously too big for the steep directions and too small for the shallow ones. RMSProp gives each parameter its own effective learning rate by dividing its step by the recent typical size of its gradient — a running root-mean-square. Where gradients have been large (steep), it takes small steps; where they’ve been small (flat), it takes large ones, automatically equalizing progress across directions:

$$ \mathbf{s}_t = \rho\,\mathbf{s}_{t-1} + (1-\rho)\,\big(\nabla f\big)^2, \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_t = \boldsymbol{\theta}_{t-1} - \frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{\mathbf{s}_t}+\varepsilon}\,\nabla f. $$

Here \((\nabla f)^2\) is squared element-wise (so \(\mathbf{s}\) tracks each parameter’s typical squared gradient), \(\rho\approx 0.9\) is the averaging rate, and \(\varepsilon\) (epsilon, \(\sim 10^{-8}\)) is a tiny constant guarding against division by zero. The division \(\frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{\mathbf{s}}+\varepsilon}\) is element-wise too: every parameter gets a step rescaled to its own history. Steep and shallow directions now advance at comparable rates — exactly the cure for the ravine.

🎯 Upgrade 3: Adam — momentum and RMSProp together

Why choose? Adam (“adaptive moment estimation”) keeps both a momentum-style average of the gradient (the first moment \(\mathbf{m}\)) and an RMSProp-style average of its square (the second moment \(\mathbf{v}\)), and combines them. It also adds a small bias correction: since both averages start at zero, they’re biased toward zero early on, so we divide by \((1-\beta^t)\) to undo that:

$$ \mathbf{m}_t = \beta_1\mathbf{m}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1)\nabla f, \qquad \mathbf{v}_t = \beta_2\mathbf{v}_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2)(\nabla f)^2, $$

$$ \hat{\mathbf{m}}_t = \frac{\mathbf{m}_t}{1-\beta_1^{\,t}}, \quad \hat{\mathbf{v}}_t = \frac{\mathbf{v}_t}{1-\beta_2^{\,t}}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta}_t = \boldsymbol{\theta}_{t-1} - \frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t}+\varepsilon}\,\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t. $$

The defaults \(\beta_1=0.9\), \(\beta_2=0.999\), \(\varepsilon=10^{-8}\) work so reliably that Adam is the out-of-the-box optimizer for the vast majority of deep-learning models. It inherits momentum’s acceleration and RMSProp’s per-parameter scaling, so it sails through ill-conditioned ravines where plain GD stalls:

🛠️ From scratch: all three optimizers

Each is only a few lines. We run them on the same ill-conditioned quadratic and watch Adam and momentum leave plain GD behind.

code/track-2/optimizers.py
import numpy as np

# Ill-conditioned bowl: very steep in y, shallow in x. Minimum at (0,0).
A = np.array([1.0, 100.0])
grad = lambda p: 2 * A * p          # gradient of A0*x^2 + A1*y^2
loss = lambda p: np.sum(A * p**2)

def optimize(kind, steps=80):
    p = np.array([-4.0, 1.0]); v = np.zeros(2); s = np.zeros(2); m = np.zeros(2)
    b1, b2, eps = 0.9, 0.999, 1e-8
    for t in range(1, steps + 1):
        g = grad(p)
        if kind == "gd":
            p = p - 0.009 * g
        elif kind == "momentum":
            v = 0.9 * v + g;                p = p - 0.0018 * v
        elif kind == "rmsprop":
            s = 0.9 * s + 0.1 * g * g;      p = p - 0.1 * g / (np.sqrt(s) + eps)
        elif kind == "adam":
            m = b1*m + (1-b1)*g;            v = b2*v + (1-b2)*g*g
            mh = m / (1 - b1**t);           vh = v / (1 - b2**t)
            p = p - 0.25 * mh / (np.sqrt(vh) + eps)
    return loss(p)

for kind in ["gd", "momentum", "rmsprop", "adam"]:
    print(f"{kind:9s} final loss: {optimize(kind):.5f}")
# gd stays highest; momentum, rmsprop, adam reach far lower loss
🤖 What you'll actually type

In a real project you won’t hand-roll these — you’ll write torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4) and move on. But now you know exactly what that line does on every step: maintain two running averages of the gradient, bias-correct them, and take a per-parameter scaled, momentum-smoothed step. The famous default lr=3e-4 is just \(\alpha\) in the equations above.

🔑 Key results to remember

Lock these in

  1. Problem: ill-conditioned ravines make a single learning rate both too big (steep) and too small (shallow) — GD zig-zags and crawls.
  2. Momentum: accumulate velocity \(\mathbf{v}=\beta\mathbf{v}+\nabla f\), step \(-\alpha\mathbf{v}\). Accelerates consistent directions, damps oscillation (\(\beta\approx0.9\)).
  3. RMSProp: per-parameter learning rate by dividing by \(\sqrt{\text{running mean of }(\nabla f)^2}\). Equalizes steep vs flat directions.
  4. Adam = momentum + RMSProp with bias correction. Defaults \(\beta_1{=}0.9,\beta_2{=}0.999,\varepsilon{=}10^{-8}\); the standard deep-learning optimizer.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. How does momentum reduce the zig-zag across a ravine’s steep walls?

  • By using a smaller learning rate automatically.
    Momentum doesn’t shrink \(\alpha\); it changes the direction stepped, by averaging gradients over time.
  • The alternating cross-valley gradients cancel in the running average, while consistent ones accumulate.
    Correct. Gradients that flip sign each step average toward zero (damping the bounce); gradients pointing the same way reinforce (building speed along the floor).
  • By computing the Hessian.
    That’s second-order (Newton, Lesson 2.4). Momentum is purely first-order — it only ever uses gradients.
  • By ignoring the gradient entirely.
    Momentum very much uses the gradient — it accumulates gradients into a velocity. It just doesn’t use each one in isolation.

Q2. What problem does RMSProp’s division by \(\sqrt{\mathbf{s}}\) solve?

  • It removes the need for a gradient.
    RMSProp still needs the gradient \(\nabla f\) in the numerator; it only rescales it.
  • It makes the loss convex.
    No optimizer changes whether the loss is convex — that’s a property of the function, not the update rule.
  • It gives each parameter its own step size, equalizing steep and flat directions.
    Correct. Dividing by each parameter’s recent gradient magnitude shrinks steps in steep directions and enlarges them in flat ones — directly fixing ill-conditioning.
  • It guarantees convergence to the global minimum.
    No such guarantee exists for non-convex problems; RMSProp improves conditioning, not global optimality.

Q3. Adam combines which two ideas?

  • Newton’s method and gradient descent.
    Adam is entirely first-order; it never forms or inverts a Hessian, so Newton isn’t involved.
  • Convexity and regularization.
    Those are properties/penalties, not the moving parts of Adam. Adam is about how to use gradients.
  • Mini-batching and learning-rate decay.
    Those are useful but separate techniques; they aren’t what the Adam update is.
  • Momentum (1st moment of the gradient) and RMSProp (2nd moment).
    Correct. Adam tracks a momentum-style average \(\mathbf{m}\) and an RMSProp-style average \(\mathbf{v}\), bias-corrects both, and combines them.

Q4. Why does Adam include the bias-correction terms \(1-\beta^t\)?

  • The moment averages start at zero, biasing them low early; dividing by \(1-\beta^t\) corrects this.
    Correct. With \(\mathbf{m}_0=\mathbf{v}_0=0\), the early averages underestimate the true values; the factor \(1-\beta^t\) (which is small early, \(\to 1\) later) rescales them to be unbiased.
  • To make the learning rate decay over time.
    Bias correction isn’t a schedule; it actually boosts early steps (dividing by a small number), the opposite of decay.
  • To guarantee convergence.
    It improves early-step accuracy but provides no global convergence guarantee, especially on non-convex losses.
  • To make Adam identical to plain GD.
    Adam is deliberately different from GD; bias correction doesn’t collapse it to GD.

✍️ Practice

Problem 1 · reasoning

With momentum coefficient \(\beta = 0.9\), roughly how many past gradients meaningfully contribute to the current velocity? (Hint: weights decay like \(\beta^k\).)

Show solution

The gradient from \(k\) steps ago carries weight \(\beta^k = 0.9^k\). A common rule of thumb is the effective window \(\approx \frac{1}{1-\beta} = \frac{1}{0.1} = 10\) steps. (Check: \(0.9^{10}\approx 0.35\), \(0.9^{20}\approx 0.12\) — contributions fade over roughly ten to twenty steps.) Higher \(\beta\) means a longer memory and more smoothing; \(\beta=0.99\) averages ~100 steps.

Problem 2 · code

Run the four optimizers from the lesson and print the final losses. Confirm the ordering: Adam ≤ RMSProp/Momentum < GD.

Show solution

Running code/track-2/optimizers.py prints something like: gd 0.87, momentum 0.02, rmsprop 0.01, adam 0.018. Plain GD is stuck near \(0.87\) because the steep \(y\)-direction caps its learning rate, leaving the shallow \(x\)-direction barely moving; the other three reach two orders of magnitude lower. Exact numbers depend on step counts and hyperparameters, but GD being far worse is robust — that’s the whole point of the ravine.

Problem 3 · derivation

Show that without bias correction, Adam’s first-moment estimate \(\mathbf{m}_1\) after one step (starting from \(\mathbf{m}_0 = 0\)) underestimates the gradient by a factor of \((1-\beta_1)\). Then verify the correction fixes it.

Show solution

At \(t=1\), \(\mathbf{m}_1 = \beta_1\mathbf{m}_0 + (1-\beta_1)\mathbf{g}_1 = (1-\beta_1)\mathbf{g}_1\). With \(\beta_1=0.9\), that’s \(0.1\,\mathbf{g}_1\) — a tenfold underestimate of the actual gradient \(\mathbf{g}_1\). The bias-corrected version divides by \(1-\beta_1^{\,1} = 1-\beta_1 = 0.1\): \(\hat{\mathbf{m}}_1 = \mathbf{m}_1/(1-\beta_1) = (1-\beta_1)\mathbf{g}_1/(1-\beta_1) = \mathbf{g}_1\). Exactly the true gradient. \(\blacksquare\) Without correction, Adam would take tiny, timid first steps; with it, early steps have the right scale.

Problem 4 · reasoning

SGD with momentum sometimes generalizes better than Adam on image tasks, even though Adam trains faster. Give a plausible reason this trade-off exists.

Show solution

Adam’s per-parameter rescaling lets it dive quickly into whatever minimum is nearby — often a sharp one that fits the training data aggressively. Plain SGD (with momentum) takes more uniform, noisier steps and tends to settle in flatter minima, which empirically generalize better (they’re more robust to small shifts between train and test data). So Adam optimizes the training loss faster, but the solution it finds can generalize slightly worse. This is an active, somewhat unsettled research area — the practical upshot is that the “best” optimizer is task-dependent, and you should measure generalization (Track 3 & 9), not just training loss. It’s a good example of why faster optimization isn’t automatically better learning.
