Track 7 · RAG & Vector Search

Why RAG exists

⏱ 22 min Lesson 7.1 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsLLMs have frozen knowledge, hallucinate, and do not know your private data. RAG fixes all three by retrieving relevant documents at query time and grounding the answer in them — the most-deployed LLM technique in production.
By the end, you can
  • Explain why RAG exists (frozen knowledge, hallucination, private data)
  • Describe the retrieve-then-generate loop
  • Compare RAG vs fine-tuning vs long context
  • Assemble a grounded prompt

Concept The problem RAG solves

Track 6 ended on three hard limits of even the best LLM. Its knowledge is frozen at training time — it can't know today's news or anything after its cutoff. It hallucinates — confidently inventing plausible-sounding falsehoods. And it has never seen your private data — your company's documents, your codebase, your customer records. Scaling the model (Lesson 6.7) fixes none of these. The fix is to stop relying on the model's memory and instead give it the relevant information at question time.

That's Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): when a question comes in, retrieve the most relevant documents from a knowledge source, paste them into the prompt as context, and have the model generate its answer from that context. The model becomes a reasoning engine over documents you supply, rather than an oracle answering from frozen memory.

This single idea addresses all three limits at once. Frozen knowledge → retrieve fresh documents, updated anytime. Hallucination → ground the answer in real, provided text (and cite it, so users can verify). Private data → retrieve from your documents, which never touched the model's training. RAG is the most important and widely-deployed LLM technique in production, precisely because it turns a generic model into one that knows your world.

Concept RAG vs fine-tuning vs long context

RAG isn't the only way to give a model knowledge it lacks, and choosing among the options is a core design decision (Lesson 6.2 introduced this):

The contrast: fine-tuning (Lesson 6.2) changes the model's behavior but is unreliable for injecting specific facts and can't easily be updated. Long context — just pasting all your documents into a huge prompt — works for a handful of pages but is expensive (you pay per token, Lesson 5.1), hits the context limit, and dilutes the model's attention across irrelevant text. RAG retrieves only the few relevant chunks per query, so it scales to millions of documents, updates instantly (just change the document store), and is cheap. When the question is "how do I make the model know my data?", RAG is usually the answer.

Worked example The retrieve-then-generate loop, minimally

RAG's core loop is short: score documents for relevance to the query, take the best, and assemble a grounded prompt. Here's the whole pattern with a tiny corpus and naive keyword scoring (real systems use embeddings — Lesson 7.3 — but the loop is identical).

corpus = {
  "doc1": "Our return policy allows refunds within 30 days of purchase.",
  "doc2": "The headquarters is located in Seattle, Washington.",
  "doc3": "To reset your password, click Forgot Password on the login page.",
}
query = "what is the return policy?"

# 1) RETRIEVE: score each doc by word overlap with the query, pick the best
score = lambda text: len(set(query.lower().replace("?","").split()) & set(text.lower().split()))
best_id, best_text = max(corpus.items(), key=lambda kv: score(kv[1]))
print("retrieved:", best_id)

# 2) GENERATE: build a prompt that grounds the answer in the retrieved context
prompt = f"Answer using ONLY this context.\nContext: {best_text}\nQuestion: {query}\nAnswer:"
print(prompt)
Outputretrieved: doc1 Answer using ONLY this context. Context: Our return policy allows refunds within 30 days of purchase. Question: what is the return policy? Answer:

The retriever found doc1 (it shares "return" and "policy" with the query), and we built a prompt that hands the model that exact text and instructs it to answer only from it. The model would then respond "Refunds are allowed within 30 days of purchase" — grounded in a real document, not its memory. Two things to notice. First, the "Answer using ONLY this context" instruction is what reduces hallucination — it tells the model to stick to the provided facts. Second, this naive keyword scoring is brittle: a query like "how do I get my money back?" shares no words with "refund," so it would fail to retrieve doc1 — which is exactly why real RAG uses semantic embeddings (Lesson 7.3) that match meaning, not just words. But the skeleton — retrieve relevant text, ground the prompt in it — is the entirety of RAG, and everything in this track makes each step better.

★ Key points to remember
  • LLMs have frozen knowledge, hallucinate, and don't know your private data — scale fixes none of these.
  • RAG retrieves relevant documents and puts them in the prompt, so the model answers from provided text, not memory.
  • This makes answers current (update docs anytime), grounded (less hallucination, citable), and private-aware.
  • Versus alternatives: fine-tuning changes behavior not facts; long context is costly and capacity-limited; RAG scales and updates cheaply.
  • The loop is just: retrieve → assemble prompt → generate; this track improves each step.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A customer-support bot built on a base LLM (no RAG) confidently tells a user "your refund will arrive in 3 days," but your actual policy is 30 days. Explain both failures and how RAG fixes them.
Show solution & reasoning

Two failures combine. The model doesn't know your specific policy (it's private data never in its training), and instead of saying "I don't know" it hallucinated a plausible number (3 days). The result is a confident, wrong, brand-damaging answer.

RAG fixes both: retrieve your actual policy document ("refunds within 30 days") and put it in the prompt, instructing the model to answer only from it. Now the model reads the real policy and responds correctly, and you can show the cited source so the user (and your compliance team) can verify. This is the canonical RAG use case — grounding a model in an organization's real, authoritative documents so it stops making things up about your business.

2For each, say whether RAG, fine-tuning, or long context fits best: (a) answer questions over a 50,000-page legal library; (b) always respond in your company's specific brand voice; (c) summarize a single 10-page PDF the user just uploaded.
Show solution & reasoning

(a) 50,000-page library → RAG. Far too large for any context window, and you only need the few relevant pages per question — retrieve them. (b) Brand voice → fine-tuning. That's a consistent behavior/style, not knowledge — bake it into the weights (Lesson 6.2). (c) Single 10-page PDF → long context. It's small enough to paste entirely into the prompt for a one-off task; setting up a retrieval system would be overkill.

The decision rule: large or changing knowledge → RAG; consistent behavior/style → fine-tune; small one-off material → long context. Often you combine them (a fine-tuned-voice model using RAG over a document store). Matching the tool to the need — knowledge vs behavior vs one-off — is exactly the judgment from Lesson 6.2, now with RAG as the knowledge specialist.

3The naive keyword retriever in the worked example would fail for the query "how do I get my money back?" against a document about "refunds." Why, and what does this motivate?
Show solution & reasoning

Keyword overlap is lexical, not semantic: "money back" and "refund" share no words, so the overlap score is zero and the relevant document is never retrieved — even though they mean the same thing. Keyword search misses synonyms, paraphrases, and related concepts.

This motivates embedding-based (semantic) retrieval (Lesson 7.3): represent the query and documents as vectors that capture meaning (Lesson 5.2), so "money back" and "refund" land close together and the right document is retrieved despite zero shared words. The quality of RAG hinges on the quality of retrieval — if you don't fetch the relevant chunk, the model can't answer from it — which is why embeddings, vector search, and reranking (the next lessons) are the heart of making RAG actually work.

★ Deep dive: grounding, citations, and the limits of RAG▸

A major practical benefit of RAG beyond correctness is attribution. Because the answer is generated from specific retrieved chunks, you can show which sources it used — citations the user can click to verify. This is transformative for trust and adoption: in legal, medical, and enterprise settings, an answer you can't trace to a source is often useless, whereas "here's the answer, and here are the three documents it came from" is actionable. It also enables a powerful failure mode for safety: if retrieval finds nothing relevant, a well-designed RAG system can say "I don't have information on that" rather than hallucinate — grounding gives the model a principled way to abstain. Building citation and abstention into the prompt and pipeline is part of what separates a toy RAG demo from a production-grade one.

RAG is powerful but not magic, and knowing its limits prevents disappointment. Garbage in, garbage out: if your documents are wrong, outdated, or contradictory, RAG will faithfully serve wrong answers — it grounds in your sources, for better and worse. Retrieval is the bottleneck: if the relevant chunk isn't retrieved (bad chunking, weak embeddings, an ambiguous query), the model can't use it, and most RAG failures are actually retrieval failures, not generation failures (which is why Lesson 7.7 evaluates retrieval separately). It doesn't add reasoning: RAG supplies facts, but synthesizing them, handling multi-hop questions ("compare the policies in docs A and B"), and resolving conflicts still depend on the model's capabilities and the prompt. And it adds latency and complexity: every query now involves a retrieval step and a larger prompt. The mature view is that RAG is the right tool for grounding a model in a knowledge base, used alongside good prompting (Track 6), and for agentic systems, tools (Track 8) — and that getting each step of the pipeline right (chunking, embedding, search, reranking, evaluation) is what this entire track teaches, because a RAG system is only as good as its weakest link. Done well, RAG is what makes LLMs genuinely useful on real, proprietary, ever-changing knowledge — which is most of the valuable work in the world.

RAG starts by breaking your documents into retrievable pieces — and how you cut them quietly decides answer quality. Next, in 7.2 Chunking strategies, we learn to split documents so the right context can actually be found.
