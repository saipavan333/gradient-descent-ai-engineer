Track 11 · AI System Design

Caching, sharding & cost control

⏱ 23 min Lesson 11.7 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsAt scale, cost is a first-class constraint. Caching (savings scale with hit rate) and sharding (split what's too big for one machine) are the core levers. The unifying principle: spend expensive computation only where it adds value.
By the end, you can
  • Use layered caching (exact + semantic)
  • Compute cache savings from hit rate
  • Explain sharding and its trade-offs
  • State the spend-where-it-pays principle

Concept Cost is a first-class design goal

An AI system that works but costs too much to run is a failed design. At scale — millions of requests, GPU inference, huge indexes — cost isn't an afterthought; it's a primary constraint you design against, alongside latency and correctness. This lesson covers the cross-cutting techniques that keep large AI systems fast and affordable, the two most important being caching (avoid repeated work) and sharding (split work that's too big for one machine). These aren't tied to any one system — they apply to recommenders, search, RAG, and LLM platforms alike — which is why they're gathered here as the general cost-and-scale toolkit.

Caching is the highest-leverage cost technique: store the results of expensive computations and reuse them when the same (or similar) request recurs, instead of recomputing. It works because real traffic is repetitive — popular queries, common inputs, and trending items recur constantly — so a cache can absorb a large fraction of requests before they ever reach the expensive model. AI systems cache at many layers: an exact-match cache (identical request → stored answer), a semantic cache (a similar request, judged by embedding similarity → reuse a near-answer), plus caching of embeddings, retrieval results, and features. Each layer catches requests the model would otherwise have to compute.

Concept Sharding: when one machine isn't enough

The other pillar is sharding: splitting data (or work) that's too big for one machine across many, each holding one shard (a partition of the whole). When your vector index is 500 GB and no single machine has that much RAM, you split it into, say, 10 shards of 50 GB across 10 machines. A router (or coordinator) receives each query, sends it to all shards in parallel, and merges their partial results into a final answer. Sharding is how systems scale horizontally past the limits of one machine — for storage (a huge index), for throughput (spread load across shards), or for model size (split a model too large for one GPU across several, Lesson 10.6). It's the standard answer to "this doesn't fit / can't keep up on one box."

Worked example What a cache hit rate is worth

Caching's payoff scales directly with the hit rate — the fraction of requests served from cache. Here, a 60% hit rate means only 40% of requests reach the expensive model:

requests, hit_rate, cost_miss = 1_000_000, 0.60, 0.002
without_cache = requests * cost_miss
with_cache    = requests * (1 - hit_rate) * cost_miss
print(f"without cache        : ${without_cache:,.0f}")
print(f"with {hit_rate:.0%} hit rate    : ${with_cache:,.0f}")
print(f"saved                : ${without_cache - with_cache:,.0f} ({hit_rate:.0%})")
Outputwithout cache : $2,000 with 60% hit rate : $800 saved : $1,200 (60%)

Without a cache, all 1 million requests hit the model at $0.002 each: $2,000. With a 60% cache hit rate, only 400,000 requests reach the model: $800 — a $1,200 (60%) saving. The saving is exactly the hit rate: a cache that serves 60% of traffic cuts model cost by 60% (and cuts latency too, since a cache hit returns almost instantly versus running the model). This is why caching is the first cost lever to reach for — the savings are proportional to how repetitive your traffic is, and much real traffic is very repetitive (a small number of popular queries make up a large share of volume). It also shows why raising the hit rate matters so much: going from 60% to 80% would cut model cost by another half again. A semantic cache boosts the hit rate further by catching similar (not just identical) queries — "how do I reset my password" and "I forgot my password" can share an answer. The trade-off is staleness: a cached answer can go out of date if the correct answer changes, so caches use expiry (TTLs) and invalidation, tuned to how fast answers change. But for the large fraction of requests whose answer is stable, caching is close to free throughput and cost reduction — the single highest-leverage move in scaling an AI system affordably.

★ Key points to remember
  • Cost is a first-class design constraint at scale, alongside latency and correctness.
  • Caching reuses results for repeated/similar requests — savings scale with the hit rate (a 60% hit rate cuts model cost 60%).
  • Cache in layers: exact-match, semantic (similar queries via embeddings), plus embeddings/retrieval/features.
  • Caching's risk is staleness — managed with expiry (TTL) and invalidation.
  • Sharding splits data/work too big for one machine across nodes; a router queries all shards and merges — horizontal scaling for storage, throughput, or model size.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1For which of these would caching help a lot, and for which would it be risky or useless: (a) an LLM FAQ bot, (b) a stock-price predictor, (c) a "generate a unique poem" feature? Explain.
Show solution & reasoning

(a) LLM FAQ bot — caching helps a lot. Users ask the same common questions repeatedly ("how do I reset my password?"), and the answers are stable, so a high cache hit rate is achievable and the cached answers stay correct. A semantic cache helps further by catching paraphrases. This is the ideal caching case: repetitive requests with stable answers. (b) Stock-price predictor — caching is risky/limited. The correct answer changes constantly (prices move by the second), so a cached prediction goes stale almost immediately; caching for more than a tiny window would serve wrong answers. You could cache for a few seconds at most, but the staleness risk is severe — this is a case where answers change fast, so caching is mostly inappropriate. (c) "Generate a unique poem" — caching is useless (and wrong). The whole point is a unique output each time; serving a cached poem defeats the feature. Requests may be identical ("write me a poem") but the desired behavior is fresh, non-deterministic output, so caching would break the product.

The reasoning framework is two questions: do requests repeat? and is the correct answer stable (and is a repeated answer desired)? Caching pays off only when both are yes — repetitive traffic and stable/reusable answers (case a). When answers change fast (b), caching risks staleness and needs very short TTLs or none. When fresh/unique output is the point (c), caching is semantically wrong regardless of repetition. This shows that caching isn't universally applicable — it's a tool whose fit depends on the request and answer characteristics, and recognizing when it applies (and when it would silently serve wrong or inappropriate results) is as important as knowing how it works. A designer who caches indiscriminately will ship stale stock prices or identical "unique" poems; one who reasons about repetition and answer-stability applies it exactly where it wins.

2Sharding a vector index across 10 machines improves storage and throughput, but what new problems does it introduce?
Show solution & reasoning

Sharding buys scale but adds distributed-systems complexity. Fan-out latency: every query now goes to all 10 shards and must wait for all to respond before merging — so the query is as slow as the slowest shard (the tail-latency problem, Lesson 11.2, amplified: with 10 shards, the chance that at least one is slow on any given query goes up). Coordination overhead: the router must fan out, collect, and merge results correctly, which is extra work and a potential bottleneck or single point of failure. Result merging: combining partial results (e.g., top-k from each shard into a global top-k) must be done right, and some operations are harder to shard than others. Fault tolerance: if one shard's machine dies, do you lose that slice of the index? You typically need replication (each shard duplicated), which adds cost and consistency concerns. Load balance / hot shards: if data isn't partitioned evenly, one shard may get disproportionate traffic and become a bottleneck. Operational burden: more machines to deploy, monitor, and keep in sync.

The reasoning point is that sharding is not free scaling — it trades single-machine simplicity for distributed-systems problems (tail latency, coordination, fault tolerance, rebalancing) that you must then design for. This is why you don't shard until you have to: as long as the data fits and one machine keeps up, a single node is far simpler and more reliable. When you cross the threshold where you must shard, you take on these problems deliberately — adding replication for fault tolerance, hedging or timeouts for tail latency, careful partitioning for load balance, and monitoring for hot shards. Recognizing that horizontal scaling introduces its own class of failures (rather than being a clean win) is exactly the distributed-systems maturity that separates a naive "just add machines" answer from a real design. It echoes the general lesson that every scaling technique in this track — replicas, sharding, caching — solves one problem while introducing new ones you must then manage.

3Step back across the whole track: name the recurring techniques for scaling AI systems affordably, and the single principle that unifies them.
Show solution & reasoning

The recurring techniques form a compact toolkit. Replication + load balancing + autoscaling (Lesson 10.6): many copies behind a balancer, scaled to load, for throughput. The two-stage funnel (Lessons 10.3–10.4): a cheap wide stage then an expensive narrow one, so the costly model runs on few items. Offline/online split (Lessons 10.3–10.5): precompute the heavy work (indexes, embeddings, training) so the per-request path is light. Batching (Lesson 11.6): pack work into efficient passes to maximize hardware utilization. Caching (this lesson): reuse results for repeated work. Sharding (this lesson): split what's too big across machines. Model compression / quantization (Lesson 11.6): make each inference cheaper. Together these are the standard levers you assemble in any large AI system design.

The unifying principle is don't do expensive work you can avoid, and when you must do it, do it as efficiently as possible — or more sharply, match the cost of the computation to the value and frequency of the request. Every technique is an instance: the funnel avoids running the expensive model on items that a cheap filter can reject; offline/online avoids redoing heavy work per request; caching avoids recomputing repeated requests; batching and compression avoid wasting hardware on each computation; sharding avoids the impossibility of one machine. You spend expensive resources (big models, GPUs, precise computation) only where they add value — on the genuinely hard, genuinely new, genuinely important requests — and use cheap resources (filters, caches, precomputation, approximations) everywhere else. This is also the through-line of the whole track and its link back to MLOps (Track 10): a great AI system design is one that meets the requirements (accuracy, latency, availability) at the lowest cost, by intelligently allocating expensive computation only where it's warranted. Internalizing this single principle — spend compute where it pays, avoid it everywhere else — turns the grab-bag of techniques into one coherent strategy, and it's the mental model that lets you design an affordable system for almost any AI problem, which is exactly what the interview check consolidates next.

★ Deep dive: the caching hierarchy, cost monitoring, and designing for a budget▸

Production AI systems cache at a hierarchy of layers, each catching work at a different stage. Closest to the user, an answer/response cache (exact and semantic) returns finished results for repeated queries, skipping everything. Deeper, an embedding cache avoids re-embedding the same text, a retrieval cache stores the retrieved chunks for a query, and a feature cache holds computed features. Even inside the model, the KV cache (Lesson 11.6) avoids recomputing attention over prior tokens. The design skill is placing caches where the traffic repeats and the results are reusable, and choosing each cache's eviction and expiry policy — how big it is (memory cost vs hit rate), how long entries live (TTL, traded against staleness), and when to invalidate (when underlying data changes). A well-designed caching hierarchy can cut both cost and latency dramatically, and it's one of the highest-return investments in a scaled system — but each layer adds complexity and a staleness surface, so you add the ones that pay.

Underlying all of this is a discipline that ties the track together: designing to a budget. Just as you set a latency budget (Lesson 11.2), mature teams set a cost budget — a target cost per request or per month — and design the system to hit it, monitoring actual cost as a first-class production metric (Lesson 10.7) with alerts on spend, per-feature cost attribution, and dashboards, because at scale an inefficiency is real recurring money. This reframes design as a constrained optimization: meet the accuracy, latency, and availability requirements at or under the cost budget, using the toolkit (funnel, offline/online, batching, caching, sharding, compression) to spend expensive computation only where it earns its keep. It's why the best AI system designers think like economists as much as engineers — every architectural choice has a cost, and the art is buying the required quality and speed for the least money. This completes the picture the whole track has been assembling: an AI system is a set of learned and infrastructural components, composed under real requirements (functional and non-functional), scaled and made affordable with a standard toolkit, and kept healthy by the operational lifecycle — all reasoned about explicitly and traded off against each other. Being able to carry a design from a vague prompt through requirements, architecture, the ML, scaling, and cost, while reasoning out loud about every trade-off, is the capstone skill of applied AI engineering — and the interview check next turns it into the questions and habits that let you demonstrate it.

That completes the design toolkit — you can architect AI systems that meet their requirements at scale and stay affordable. Next, 10.8 — System Design: interview & key takeaways consolidates the whole track into the questions you'll be asked and the mental models that make your answers stand out.
