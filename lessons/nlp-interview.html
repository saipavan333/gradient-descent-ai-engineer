Interview check: NLP & Transformers

⏱ 15 min Lesson 6.9
Why this existsTransformer interviews probe attention from the inside: walk through self-attention, justify √d scaling, pick an architecture. This checkpoint rehearses those questions with model answers that show the reasoning.
By the end, you can
  • Walk through self-attention and shapes
  • Justify √dₖ scaling and multi-head
  • Choose encoder vs decoder for a task
  • Explain why transformers replaced RNNs

Concept What an NLP / transformers interview tests

Transformer interviews probe whether you understand attention from the inside — can you walk through the math, justify the design choices, and pick the right architecture? These questions come up in nearly every modern ML interview, because transformers underpin everything. Each model answer below shows the reasoning interviewers listen for.

Model answer "Walk me through self-attention."

"Each token produces three vectors by learned linear projections of its embedding: a Query, a Key, and a Value. To compute attention, I take the dot product of every token's Query with every token's Key — that gives an n×n matrix of relevance scores. I divide by √dₖ for stability, apply a softmax to each row so the weights sum to 1, and use those weights to take a weighted sum of the Values. The result is each token's new representation: a blend of all tokens' values, weighted by relevance. In one line it's softmax(QKᵀ/√dₖ)V. The intuition is a soft dictionary lookup — the Query asks, Keys advertise, Values deliver — and because it's all dot products and a softmax, it's fully differentiable and trainable by backprop. 'Self'-attention just means Q, K, and V all come from the same sequence."

Strong close: mention multi-head — several of these in parallel, each learning a different relationship.

Model answer "Why divide by √dₖ?"

"For gradient stability. The dot product of two dₖ-dimensional vectors with unit-variance components has variance proportional to dₖ — so in high dimensions the raw scores get large in magnitude. Large scores push the softmax into a saturated, nearly one-hot regime where its gradient is almost zero, so learning stalls. Dividing by √dₖ rescales the scores back to roughly unit variance, keeping the softmax in a responsive range with healthy gradients. It's a small constant, but without it deep attention trains poorly — it's the 'scaled' in scaled dot-product attention."

Model answer "Why multi-head attention instead of one big attention?"

"A single attention computes one weighted average, which can only capture one kind of relationship at a time and tends to blur multiple signals together. Multi-head attention splits the representation into several subspaces and runs an independent attention in each, with its own learned Q/K/V projections. So different heads can specialize — one tracks syntactic dependencies, another coreference, another local word order — and you capture several relationships in parallel within one layer. Their outputs are concatenated and mixed by an output projection. The total compute is similar to one large head, but the model gets multiple 'views,' which empirically matters a lot. Interpretability studies even find heads with clear, distinct jobs."

Model answer "Encoder-only vs decoder-only — when do you use which?"

"It comes down to whether you need to understand or generate, which corresponds to bidirectional vs causal attention. Encoder-only (BERT) attends in both directions, so every token sees full left-and-right context — best for understanding tasks: classification, sentiment, named-entity recognition, and producing embeddings for retrieval. Decoder-only (GPT) uses a causal mask so each token sees only the past — required for left-to-right generation, and the basis of modern LLMs. Encoder–decoder (T5) reads an input bidirectionally and generates an output causally with cross-attention — natural for transformation tasks like translation. Mechanically the only difference between bidirectional and causal is a mask that sets future scores to −∞ before the softmax. In practice, large decoder-only models now handle most tasks via prompting, but encoders remain the efficient choice for embeddings."

Model answer "Why did transformers replace RNNs?"

"Two main reasons. Parallelism: an RNN is sequential — each step depends on the previous hidden state — so it can't parallelize across the sequence, which is slow on GPUs. A transformer processes all positions at once. Long-range dependencies: attention connects any two positions directly in a single step, so there's no signal that must survive a long chain of multiplications — it sidesteps the vanishing-gradient problem that limited RNNs. LSTMs/GRUs mitigated vanishing with gated memory, but transformers removed the bottleneck entirely and added massive parallelism, which is what let models scale to billions of parameters and trillions of tokens. The trade-off is that attention is O(n²) in sequence length, which is the cost of every token seeing every token."

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • Attention formula? softmax(QKᵀ/√dₖ)V — scores, scale, softmax, weighted sum of values.
  • Q, K, V? Learned linear projections of the embedding: Query asks, Key advertises, Value delivers.
  • Why positional encoding? Attention is order-blind (permutation-equivariant); position is added so order matters.
  • Attention complexity? O(n²) in sequence length — the long-context bottleneck.
  • Subword tokenization? Spell any word from a fixed vocab (BPE/WordPiece) — no out-of-vocabulary problem.
  • Causal mask? Set future scores to −∞ before softmax → lower-triangular attention → generation-safe.

Q1. "Write the scaled dot-product attention equation." The correct answer is:

  • A$Q + K + V$
  • B$\text{softmax}(Q + K)\,V$
  • C$\text{softmax}(QK^\top / \sqrt{d_k})\,V$
  • D$Q K^\top V$ with no softmax
Attention isn't a sum of Q, K, V.
Scores come from a dot product $QK^\top$, not a sum, and the scaling is missing.
Correct. Dot-product scores, scaled by √dₖ, softmaxed into weights, times the values.
The softmax (turning scores into a weight distribution) is essential.

Q2. An interviewer asks why a plain transformer can't tell "dog bites man" from "man bites dog." Best answer:

  • AIt has too few parameters
  • BSelf-attention is permutation-equivariant (order-blind); without positional encoding both have the same token set
  • CThe tokenizer failed
  • DThe learning rate was wrong
Parameter count isn't the issue; order-blindness is.
Correct. Attention treats input as a set, so order must be injected via positional encoding — otherwise the two sentences are identical to the model.
Both tokenize fine; the issue is the absence of position information.
Unrelated to learning rate.

Q3. "What's the computational complexity of self-attention in sequence length n, and why does it matter?"

  • AO(1) — constant
  • BO(log n)
  • CO(n) — linear
  • DO(n²) — every token attends to every token, which limits context length
Computing all pairwise scores is far from constant.
It's quadratic, not logarithmic.
Every-to-every scoring is quadratic, not linear.
Correct. The n×n score matrix makes attention O(n²) in time and memory — the central reason long context is expensive and a major research target.

Q4. "You need to generate text, conditioned only on the past." Which attention pattern?

  • ACausal (masked) attention — set future positions to −∞ before softmax
  • BBidirectional attention
  • CNo attention
  • DRandom masking
Correct. Causal masking ensures each token sees only itself and earlier tokens — exactly what generation requires.
Bidirectional would let the model peek at future tokens — cheating for generation.
Generation still uses attention, just masked.
The mask is structured (future = −∞), not random.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"Whiteboard the shapes through one attention layer: a batch of 2 sequences, 5 tokens each, model dimension 64, 8 heads. What are the key intermediate shapes?"
Show a strong answer

"Input is (2, 5, 64) — batch × tokens × dim. The Q/K/V projections keep dimension 64, so Q, K, V are each (2, 5, 64). For multi-head with 8 heads, I reshape to (2, 8, 5, 8) — splitting the 64 into 8 heads of dimension 8. The scores $QK^\top$ are (2, 8, 5, 5) — a 5×5 attention matrix per head per sequence. After softmax (over the last dim) and multiplying by V (2, 8, 5, 8), the per-head output is (2, 8, 5, 8). I concatenate the heads back to (2, 5, 64) and apply the output projection, returning (2, 5, 64) — same as the input, so it can feed the next block."

Tracking shapes confidently — especially the n×n attention matrix per head and the same-shape output — signals you've implemented this, not just read about it. Note dₖ here is 8 (the per-head dim), so the scaling is √8.

2"Your transformer handles 2K-token inputs fine but runs out of memory at 16K. Explain why, and name two mitigations."
Show a strong answer

"Self-attention is O(n²) in sequence length — the attention matrix has n² entries. Going from 2K to 16K is 8× the length but 64× the attention memory and compute, which blows the memory budget. Mitigations: FlashAttention, which computes exact attention without materializing the full n×n matrix in memory (tiling the computation), giving big memory savings; sparse or sliding-window attention, where each token attends to only a local window or a subset rather than all tokens, reducing the cost toward linear; and linear-attention approximations. I'd also check whether I actually need full global attention or whether a retrieval approach (Track 8) would let me use a shorter context."

This connects the architectural fact (O(n²)) to a concrete production problem and a current, named toolkit — exactly the applied depth interviewers want.

3"Explain to a non-expert why a transformer trained only to predict the next word can write coherent essays."
Show a strong answer

"The model is trained on a simple game: given some text, guess the next word, over and over, on basically all the text on the internet. To get good at that game, it has to pick up on patterns — grammar, facts, how arguments flow, the style of different writing — because all of those help it guess better. The attention mechanism lets it look back at everything written so far and focus on the words that matter for the next one, so it keeps track of context over long passages. To write an essay, it just plays the game repeatedly: predict a word, add it, predict the next, building the text one word at a time, each one informed by everything before it. So 'predict the next word, really well, at huge scale' turns out to require — and produce — a lot of what looks like understanding."

The skill here is explaining a technical system in plain language while staying accurate (next-token prediction + attention for context + autoregressive generation). Communicating to non-experts is a real, tested competency, especially for senior roles.

★ Deep dive: reasoning about transformers, end to end▸

The most impressive transformer answers trace the whole pipeline from text to prediction and locate any specific question within it: text → subword tokens → embeddings → +positional encoding → (Q/K/V → scaled dot-product attention → multi-head → add & norm → FFN → add & norm) × N blocks → output head → next-token distribution. When an interviewer asks about any one piece — "what does layer norm do here?", "why three projections?", "what's the mask for?" — you can answer it and say how it fits the whole. That end-to-end fluency is what separates someone who memorized "attention is softmax(QKᵀ/√d)V" from someone who understands the architecture.

Three habits make transformer reasoning sharp. First, always be ready to track shapes — the n×n attention matrix, the same-shape block output, the per-head splits — because shape questions are the fastest way to test real understanding, and getting them right is unmistakably credible. Second, connect each design choice to a problem it solves: √d scaling → softmax gradients; positional encoding → order-blindness; residuals/layer norm → deep-stack training (Track 4); causal mask → no peeking during generation. Every piece exists for a reason, and naming the reason shows depth. Third, link to the bigger picture — attention replaced recurrence to fix vanishing gradients and enable parallelism (Track 4); the same architecture scales into LLMs (Track 7); attention embeddings power retrieval (Track 8). The transformer isn't an isolated trick; it's the convergence point of the whole course. You built it from a single neuron, through backprop and sequence models, to attention and the full block — which means you can reason about it from first principles rather than reciting facts. That is exactly the foundation Track 7 stands on, where these models are scaled, aligned, and turned into the assistants and tools you actually use."

That completes Track 6 — you understand the transformer from tokens to architectures, inside and out. Next, Track 7 (LLMs & Generative AI) scales this architecture into the large language models that power modern AI: pretraining, fine-tuning, alignment, decoding, prompting, and more.
