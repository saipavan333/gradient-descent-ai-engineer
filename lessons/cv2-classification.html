Image classification & CNN architectures

⏱ 40 min Lesson 14.2 ⬇ Runnable notebook
Why this existsImage classification is the base capability all of vision builds on. Understanding convolution, why architectures like ResNet work, and transfer learning is what lets you actually train a working image model — usually without starting from scratch.
By the end, you can
  • Compute a 2-D convolution by hand and explain weight sharing
  • Explain the CNN feature hierarchy (edges to textures to parts to objects)
  • Say why ResNet skip connections let networks go very deep
  • Use transfer learning: fine-tune a pretrained backbone on a small dataset

Concept Convolution: the operation that sees

Image classification — "what is in this picture?" — is the foundational vision task, and it's powered by convolution. A convolution slides a small filter (also called a kernel — a tiny grid of weights, say 3×3) across the image; at each position it multiplies the filter by the pixels it covers and sums the result, producing one number. Doing this everywhere yields a feature map that highlights wherever the filter's pattern appears. A filter tuned to detect vertical edges lights up at vertical edges; one tuned for a texture lights up on that texture. Crucially, the same filter is used at every position (weight sharing), so it detects its pattern anywhere in the image — and the filter's weights are learned, so the network discovers which patterns matter.

Worked example Convolution from scratch — detecting edges

Here's a real convolution: sliding a 3×3 edge-detection filter over a small image (a solid square on a background). The filter responds to changes in intensity, so it lights up at the square's edges and stays flat (zero) in the uniform interior:

import numpy as np
img = np.array([[0,0,0,0,0],
                [0,9,9,9,0],
                [0,9,9,9,0],
                [0,9,9,9,0],
                [0,0,0,0,0]], float)
kernel = np.array([[-1,-1,-1],[-1,8,-1],[-1,-1,-1]], float)   # edge detector
H,W,k = *img.shape, 3
out = np.zeros((H-k+1, W-k+1))
for i in range(H-k+1):
    for j in range(W-k+1):
        out[i,j] = (img[i:i+k, j:j+k] * kernel).sum()          # multiply + sum
print("feature map:\n", out.astype(int))
Outputfeature map: [[45 27 45] [27 0 27] [45 27 45]]

The filter slides over the 5×5 image and produces a 3×3 feature map. Look at the pattern: the center is 0 — there the filter sits entirely inside the uniform block of 9s, and the edge detector (which sums to zero over any constant region: 8·9 − 8·9 = 0) correctly reports "no edge here, it's flat." The corners and edges are large (45, 27) — there the filter straddles the boundary between the block and the background, detecting the change. So this single small filter has located the square's edges purely by arithmetic, and it would find edges anywhere in a larger image because the same filter slides everywhere. That's the essence of convolution and why it's perfect for vision: it detects local patterns (here, edges) in a translation-invariant way with very few parameters (just the 9 filter weights). A real CNN stacks many such layers — early layers learn simple filters (edges, colors), and deeper layers combine them into complex patterns (textures, shapes, object parts, whole objects) — a hierarchy of features built from this one operation. And the filters aren't hand-designed like this edge detector; they're learned by gradient descent (Track 4), so the network discovers exactly which patterns are useful for the task. This tiny multiply-and-sum, repeated across the image and stacked into layers, is how a computer learns to see.

Concept Architectures: from LeNet to ResNet and beyond

A CNN architecture is a specific arrangement of convolutional and other layers, and the field's progress is largely a story of better architectures. Early CNNs (LeNet, AlexNet) stacked convolutions and pooling layers. The pivotal breakthrough was ResNet, which introduced residual (skip) connections — a path that lets a block's input skip past its layers and add directly to the output. This solved the problem that very deep networks were hard to train (vanishing gradients, Track 4): the skip gives gradients a clear path backward, so networks of hundreds of layers became trainable, and depth is what gives CNNs their power. Later architectures (EfficientNet and others) refined how to scale networks efficiently. You rarely design these from scratch — you use a proven architecture.

★ Key points to remember
  • Convolution slides a small learned filter over the image (multiply + sum) to produce a feature map highlighting where a pattern appears.
  • Weight sharing (same filter everywhere) gives translation invariance and few parameters.
  • Stacked conv layers build a hierarchy: edges → textures → parts → objects; the filters are learned, not hand-designed.
  • ResNet's skip connections made very deep networks trainable (gradients flow through the skip) — the key architecture breakthrough.
  • You use proven architectures (ResNet, EfficientNet) rather than designing from scratch — often with transfer learning.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You need an image classifier for a custom task but only have a few thousand images. What's the standard, effective approach, and why?
Show solution & reasoning

The standard approach is transfer learning (Track 4, Capstone 1): take a CNN (or Vision Transformer) pretrained on a massive image dataset, freeze its convolutional backbone, and train only a small new classification head on your few thousand images. This works because the pretrained backbone has already learned the general visual features — edges, textures, shapes, object parts — that are useful for almost any image task (the hierarchy this lesson described), so you don't need millions of images or huge compute to relearn how to see; you only need enough data to teach the small head your specific classes. A few thousand images is far too few to train a deep CNN from scratch (it would overfit badly), but plenty to adapt a pretrained one.

The reasoning is the key insight that the expensive part of vision — learning good features — transfers across tasks, so you reuse it. You'd load a proven architecture with pretrained weights (a ResNet or EfficientNet from a model library), replace its final layer with one sized to your number of classes, freeze the backbone, and train the head; if you have enough data and need more accuracy, you can then fine-tune the top backbone layers at a low learning rate. You'd combine this with data augmentation (previous lesson) to stretch your limited data further, and evaluate honestly on a held-out set (Track 11). This is not just a shortcut — it's how essentially all real-world custom image classifiers are built, because training from scratch is rarely worth it. Recognizing that the answer to "small data, custom vision task" is "pretrained backbone + trained head + augmentation," and explaining why (transferable hierarchical features), is exactly the practical judgment a vision role wants — and it's the same pretrain-then-adapt paradigm that runs through the whole curriculum.

2What is pooling (e.g., max pooling), and why do CNNs use it between convolutional layers?
Show solution & reasoning

Pooling downsamples a feature map by summarizing small regions — max pooling takes the maximum value in each little window (say 2×2), so a feature map is halved in height and width, keeping the strongest response in each region. CNNs use it for several reasons. Reduce spatial size (and thus computation and memory) as you go deeper, so the network is efficient. Add translation robustness: by keeping the max in a region, small shifts of a feature don't change the output much — the network cares that a pattern is present in this area, not its exact pixel. Enlarge the receptive field: after pooling, each later neuron "sees" a larger portion of the original image, letting deep layers integrate information over the whole picture and detect big, composite patterns.

The reasoning connects to the hierarchy idea: convolution detects local patterns, and pooling progressively coarsens the spatial resolution while the channel/feature richness grows, so the network moves from "where exactly is this edge" (fine spatial detail, many positions) toward "what objects are present" (coarse spatial detail, rich semantic features) — exactly the progression from pixels to meaning that classification needs. By the final layers, the spatial grid is small and the features are highly abstract, ready to be flattened and classified. Alternatives to pooling exist (strided convolutions do the downsampling within the convolution itself, and some modern designs use less pooling), but the concept — reduce spatial resolution while building abstraction and gaining shift-robustness — is fundamental to how CNNs turn a detailed pixel grid into a compact representation of what's in the image. Understanding pooling as the mechanism that trades spatial precision for semantic abstraction as you go deeper shows you grasp how a CNN actually distills an image into a classification, not just that it has layers.

3Explain why a CNN can recognize a cat whether it's in the top-left or bottom-right of the image, but a plain fully-connected network struggles with this.
Show solution & reasoning

The property is translation invariance, and the CNN gets it for free from weight sharing. A convolutional filter is the same set of weights applied at every position as it slides across the image, so a filter that detects "cat-ear texture" fires wherever that texture appears — top-left, bottom-right, anywhere. The network learns the pattern once and detects it everywhere. Pooling adds further robustness to small shifts. So a cat's features are recognized regardless of position, and the network needs to learn each pattern only once.

A fully-connected network struggles because every input pixel connects to every neuron with its own separate weight, so there's no sharing across positions: a pattern in the top-left is processed by completely different weights than the same pattern in the bottom-right. To recognize a cat anywhere, it would have to independently learn "cat" for every possible location — a huge waste of parameters and, critically, of data (it would need to see cats in every position to generalize). It has no built-in notion that a pattern means the same thing wherever it appears. This is the crux of why CNNs revolutionized vision: they bake in the correct assumption — that visual patterns are the same regardless of location (translation invariance) — through weight sharing, giving them far better generalization on images with far fewer parameters. The reasoning skill is seeing that this isn't a minor efficiency tweak but a fundamental inductive bias matched to how images work (Track 4): objects can appear anywhere, so the architecture should recognize them anywhere by construction. That single idea — shared filters sliding everywhere — is what makes convolution the right tool for vision, and articulating why (weight sharing → translation invariance → learn-once-detect-everywhere) demonstrates real understanding of the architecture, not just its name.

★ Deep dive: the ImageNet moment and how architectures evolved▸

Modern computer vision has a clear origin story worth knowing. In 2012, a deep CNN (AlexNet) won the ImageNet image-classification competition by a huge margin, dramatically beating the hand-engineered computer-vision methods that came before — the moment that ignited the deep learning revolution and showed that learned hierarchical features beat human-designed ones. From there, architectures evolved rapidly along a few themes. Deeper: VGG showed that stacking many small (3×3) convolutions worked well, pushing depth. Trainably deeper: ResNet's residual connections solved the vanishing-gradient barrier so networks could go from tens to hundreds of layers, a decisive leap in accuracy — and residual connections became a universal building block, used far beyond vision (transformers rely on them too, Track 5). Efficient: architectures like MobileNet and EfficientNet focused on getting the most accuracy per unit of computation, crucial for running vision on phones and at scale (the cost concerns of Track 10). Each step was a better way to arrange the same basic ingredients — convolutions, nonlinearities, normalization, pooling, and skips — into a network that learns better features.

The deeper lessons for a practitioner are two. First, you stand on this evolution rather than reinventing it: in practice you pick a proven architecture with pretrained weights and adapt it (transfer learning), because these designs and their trained features are the accumulated result of years of research and enormous compute you can't replicate — the same pretrain-then-adapt economics that define modern AI (Track 6, Capstones 1–2). Second, the CNN's reign is no longer absolute: Vision Transformers (next lessons but one) have shown that the transformer architecture — with its more general, less image-specific inductive bias — can match or beat CNNs given enough data, echoing the broader shift toward attention-based models across AI. So while convolution remains a workhorse and CNNs are still excellent (especially with limited data, where their built-in image assumptions help), the field is in a moment where CNNs and transformers coexist and compete for vision, much as they did for language before transformers won there. Understanding convolution deeply — what it computes, why weight sharing gives translation invariance, how stacking builds a feature hierarchy, and why skip connections unlocked depth — gives you the foundation to reason about both the CNNs that dominated the last decade and the Vision Transformers reshaping vision now, which is where this track heads after covering the tasks (detection, segmentation) that build on classification. Classification is the base; everything else in vision extends it.
