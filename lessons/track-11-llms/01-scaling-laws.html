Scaling laws & the training recipe

Why did the field bet billions on making models bigger? Because loss falls with scale in a startlingly predictable way — a power law you can fit on cheap small models and extrapolate. This is the equation behind “just scale it up.”

Track 11 · Lesson 1 Concept + code ~24 min Builds on: Track 3, Lesson 0

Bigger models, more data, and more compute reliably lower the loss — but the surprise, discovered empirically around 2020, is how reliably. The relationship is a smooth power law spanning many orders of magnitude. That predictability turned model-building from guesswork into forecasting: measure a few small models, fit the curve, and know what a model 100× larger will do before you build it.

💡 Concept: loss follows a power law in scale

Let \(N\) be the number of model parameters. Across a huge range, the test loss follows

\[ L(N) \;=\; E \;+\; A\,N^{-\alpha}, \] (1.1)

with three constants: \(E\) is the irreducible loss (the noise floor you can never beat, from the inherent unpredictability of language); \(A\) sets the scale; and \(\alpha\) is the scaling exponent — how fast loss falls as the model grows. On a log–log plot, the reducible part \(A N^{-\alpha}\) is a straight line of slope \(-\alpha\). The same form holds separately for dataset size \(D\) and for compute \(C\).

The exponent \(\alpha\) is typically small (≈0.05–0.35), which has a sobering implication: because the loss falls as \(N^{-\alpha}\), you need exponential increases in size for linear improvements in loss. Diminishing returns are baked in — but they’re predictable diminishing returns, and that is what makes scaling a rational bet.

🛠️ From scratch: fit a scaling law and extrapolate

Our scaling_laws.py generates noisy loss measurements from a hidden law, fits \((E,A,\alpha)\), and forecasts a model far larger than any measured. Fitting is easy once you notice that after subtracting \(E\), the law is linear in log-space: \(\log(L-E)=\log A-\alpha\log N\). We grid-search \(E\) and do a linear fit for the rest: 

for E in candidate_irreducible_losses:      # try each noise floor
    y = log(L - E); x = log(N)              # log(L-E) = logA - alpha*logN  (a line)
    slope, intercept = polyfit(x, y, 1)     # linear regression in log space
    keep the E with the smallest fit error
# -> recovers E, A, alpha
Compute-optimal training (Chinchilla). Given a fixed compute budget \(C\approx 6ND\) (parameters × data), should you spend it on a bigger model or more data? Scaling laws answer this. The 2022 “Chinchilla” result showed most large models were under-trained — for compute-optimal training, parameters and tokens should grow in roughly equal proportion (≈20 tokens per parameter). It’s why a well-trained smaller model can beat a bigger, data-starved one. Scaling isn’t just “make \(N\) huge”; it’s balancing \(N\) and \(D\) under a compute constraint.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Scaling law: \(L(N)=E+A\,N^{-\alpha}\) — a power law in model size (also in data \(D\) and compute \(C\)).
  • \(E\) is the irreducible loss (noise floor); \(\alpha\) is the scaling exponent — a straight line of slope \(-\alpha\) on log–log axes.
  • Small \(\alpha\) means exponential scale buys linear loss reduction: predictable diminishing returns.
  • Fit on cheap small models, extrapolate to forecast large ones before spending the compute.
  • Compute-optimal (Chinchilla): under a budget \(C\approx6ND\), grow parameters and data together (~20 tokens/param).

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. In \(L(N)=E+A\,N^{-\alpha}\), what does \(E\) represent?

  • The loss at \(N=1\).
    At \(N=1\) the loss is \(E+A\); \(E\) alone is the limit as \(N\to\infty\).
  • The learning rate.
    \(E\) is a property of the task and data, not an optimizer setting.
  • The irreducible loss — the floor no amount of scaling can beat.
    Correct. As \(N\to\infty\), \(A N^{-\alpha}\to0\) and \(L\to E\). It reflects the inherent unpredictability in the data (e.g. genuine ambiguity in language) that no model can remove.
  • The number of training epochs.
    Epochs aren’t in this law; \(E\) is the asymptotic loss floor.

Q2. On log–log axes, the reducible part of the loss \(A\,N^{-\alpha}\) appears as…

  • a straight line with slope \(-\alpha\).
    Correct. \(\log(A N^{-\alpha}) = \log A - \alpha\log N\), which is linear in \(\log N\) with slope \(-\alpha\). This straightness is exactly what makes scaling laws so easy to fit and extrapolate.
  • a parabola.
    Power laws are lines (not parabolas) in log–log space.
  • a flat horizontal line.
    Flat would mean no dependence on \(N\); the slope is \(-\alpha\neq0\).
  • a random scatter.
    The whole point is that it’s strikingly non-random — a clean line.

Q3. Why can labs justify enormous training runs before doing them?

  • Because loss always reaches zero eventually.
    Loss approaches the irreducible floor \(E\), not zero.
  • Because bigger models are guaranteed to be useful.
    Scaling predicts loss, not downstream usefulness directly; the justification is the predictable loss curve.
  • Because scaling is cheap.
    It’s the opposite — it’s expensive, which is exactly why forecasting matters.
  • Because the power law fit on small models extrapolates, predicting the big model’s loss in advance.
    Correct. The smooth, fittable power law lets you forecast the loss of a 100×-larger model from cheap small runs — turning a costly bet into a calculated one.

Q4. The Chinchilla finding is that, for a fixed compute budget, many large models were…

  • too small.
    They were large in parameters; the problem was on the data side, not model size.
  • under-trained on too little data — parameters and tokens should scale together.
    Correct. Compute-optimal training balances \(N\) and \(D\) (~20 tokens/param); giant models trained on too few tokens waste compute, and a smaller, well-fed model can beat them.
  • trained with the wrong activation function.
    Chinchilla is about the compute allocation between size and data, not activations.
  • using too large a vocabulary.
    Vocabulary (Lesson 0) is unrelated to the compute-optimal size/data balance.

✍️ Practice

1. Run scaling_laws.py, then remove the two largest training points before fitting. Does the extrapolation get worse? What does that teach about fitting scaling laws?

Show solution

With fewer and smaller points, the fit (especially the irreducible loss \(E\)) becomes less certain and the far extrapolation drifts. The lesson: extrapolating a power law is only as trustworthy as the range of scales you fit over — the more decades of \(N\) you measure, the more reliable the forecast, particularly for pinning down \(E\), which only reveals itself as you approach the floor. This is why scaling-law papers measure many model sizes across several orders of magnitude before extrapolating, and treat the far end with appropriate caution.

2. If \(\alpha=0.3\), roughly how much larger must a model be to halve the reducible loss \(A N^{-\alpha}\)?

Show solution

Halving \(A N^{-\alpha}\) means \((N_2/N_1)^{-\alpha}=\tfrac12\), so \(N_2/N_1 = 2^{1/\alpha} = 2^{1/0.3}\approx 2^{3.33}\approx 10\). You need roughly a 10× larger model to halve the reducible loss. That’s the quantitative face of diminishing returns: each halving of the gap-to-the-floor costs an order of magnitude more scale (and, since compute \(\approx 6ND\), far more compute). It also explains why the last increments of quality are so expensive — and why efficiency techniques (the rest of this track) matter so much.

3. Conceptual: scaling laws predict loss, but we care about capabilities (reasoning, coding). Why is the link between them subtle?

Show solution

Loss is a smooth average over all next-token predictions, and it falls predictably. But specific capabilities can appear more abruptly as loss crosses certain thresholds — sometimes called “emergent” behaviors — because a capability may require getting many tokens right in sequence, a nonlinear function of the smooth per-token loss. So while the aggregate loss curve is reliable, which capabilities a given loss unlocks is much harder to forecast, and whether such jumps are truly emergent or artifacts of how we measure them is actively debated. The practical takeaway: scaling laws are a strong guide for loss and a weaker, still-evolving guide for downstream ability — which is why evaluation (Track 9) remains essential alongside scaling.
