Fine-tuning & LoRA

You can’t retrain a billion-parameter model on your laptop — but you can adapt one. LoRA freezes the giant pretrained weights and learns a tiny low-rank update, matching full fine-tuning at a fraction of the cost. It’s how most custom LLMs are actually built.

Track 11 · Lesson 2 Concept + code ~26 min Builds on: Tracks 1, 4, Lesson 1

Pretraining builds a general model; fine-tuning adapts it to a specific task or style. But full fine-tuning updates every weight — impossible on consumer hardware for large models, and it produces a full-size copy per task. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) makes adaptation cheap by exploiting a simple fact: fine-tuning updates are approximately low-rank. You’ll build it from scratch and watch it match full fine-tuning.

💡 Concept: the update is low-rank, so factor it

Full fine-tuning replaces a pretrained weight matrix \(W_0\) (size \(d\times d\)) with \(W_0+\Delta W\), learning all \(d^2\) numbers of \(\Delta W\). The key insight: the change \(\Delta W\) needed to adapt a model tends to have low rank — it lives in a small subspace (recall rank from Track 1). So instead of learning the full matrix, factor it as a product of two thin matrices:

\[ \Delta W = B\,A, \qquad B \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times r},\; A \in \mathbb{R}^{r\times d}, \qquad r \ll d. \] (2.1)

Now you train only \(A\) and \(B\) — \(2dr\) parameters instead of \(d^2\). The pretrained \(W_0\) stays frozen. At inference the layer computes \(h = W_0x + BAx\); you can even fold \(BA\) back into \(W_0\) afterward, so LoRA adds zero extra latency once merged. Initialize \(B=0\) so training starts exactly at the pretrained model and departs smoothly.

🛠️ From scratch: LoRA matches full fine-tuning

Our lora.py sets up a task whose true weight change is genuinely low-rank, then fine-tunes two ways. LoRA’s gradients flow through the product \(BA\) by the chain rule (Track 4): 

A = small_random(r, d); B = zeros(d, r)         # start: BA = 0 (= pretrained model)
for step in range(steps):
    W = W0 + B @ A                              # effective weight (W0 frozen)
    gW = grad_of_loss_wrt(W)                    # error signal at the layer
    gB = gW @ A.T;  gA = B.T @ gW               # chain rule through B·A
    B -= lr * gB;   A -= lr * gA                # train ONLY A and B

The result, on a 32×32 layer whose task shift is rank 2:

  • Full fine-tune: final loss ≈ 0, training 1024 parameters.
  • LoRA (r=2): final loss ≈ 0, training 128 parameters — just 12% — for the same result.
Why this is transformative in practice. On a real LLM with \(d\) in the thousands, LoRA trains well under 1% of the parameters — small enough to fine-tune on a single consumer GPU. And because \(W_0\) is untouched, one frozen base model can host many tiny LoRA “adapters” (one per task/customer), swapped in at will, instead of storing a full model copy each. This is the backbone of modern parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) and of the LoRA-adapter ecosystems around open models.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • LoRA: freeze \(W_0\), learn \(\Delta W=BA\) with rank \(r\ll d\); train \(2dr\) params instead of \(d^2\).
  • Works because fine-tuning updates are approximately low-rank — they live in a small subspace.
  • Init \(B=0\) so training begins at the pretrained model; merge \(BA\) into \(W_0\) for zero added latency.
  • Matches full fine-tuning on suitable tasks at a fraction of the trainable parameters (12% here; <1% on real LLMs).
  • One frozen base + many small adapters — cheap to train, store, and swap per task.

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. In LoRA, which parameters are actually trained?

  • The full weight matrix \(W_0\).
    \(W_0\) is frozen — that’s the whole point. Training it would be full fine-tuning.
  • Only the two small matrices \(A\) and \(B\) in \(\Delta W = BA\).
    Correct. \(W_0\) stays fixed; gradient descent updates only \(A\) (\(r\times d\)) and \(B\) (\(d\times r\)), a tiny fraction of the parameters.
  • The embeddings only.
    LoRA adapts weight matrices (e.g. attention projections), not just embeddings; and it trains \(A,B\), not the originals.
  • None — LoRA is training-free.
    LoRA absolutely trains; it just trains far fewer parameters.

Q2. LoRA trains \(2dr\) parameters instead of \(d^2\). For \(d=4096\) and \(r=8\), the fraction trained is about…

  • 50%.
    Far too high — LoRA’s savings are dramatic at large \(d\).
  • 10%.
    Still much too high for these numbers.
  • 1%.
    Close in spirit but still an overestimate; compute it exactly.
  • about 0.4%.
    Correct. \(2dr/d^2 = 2r/d = 16/4096 \approx 0.0039\), i.e. ~0.4%. This is why LoRA fits on modest hardware — you optimize a tiny sliver of the weights.

Q3. Why initialize \(B=0\) (so \(BA=0\)) at the start of LoRA training?

  • So training begins exactly at the pretrained model, then departs smoothly.
    Correct. With \(BA=0\), the initial effective weight is just \(W_0\) — the adaptation starts as a no-op and gradient descent moves it gradually, avoiding a disruptive random jump away from the good pretrained solution.
  • To make \(A\) untrainable.
    \(A\) is still trained; only the product starts at zero. \(A\) is given small random values so gradients can flow.
  • Because zero matrices are faster to multiply.
    The speed reason is the low rank, not the zero init; and \(B\) becomes nonzero quickly.
  • To reduce the vocabulary size.
    Vocabulary is unrelated to LoRA’s initialization.

Q4. A big practical advantage of keeping \(W_0\) frozen is that…

  • the model runs at lower precision automatically.
    Freezing weights doesn’t change their precision; that’s quantization (next lesson).
  • it removes the need for a tokenizer.
    Tokenization is still required regardless of how you fine-tune.
  • one base model can host many small swappable adapters, one per task, instead of a full copy each.
    Correct. Since the base is unchanged, each task is just a tiny \(A,B\) pair you load on top — cheap to store and hot-swap, versus keeping a full fine-tuned model per task.
  • it guarantees zero training loss.
    Loss depends on the task and capacity; freezing \(W_0\) doesn’t guarantee perfect fit.

✍️ Practice

1. In lora.py, make the true task shift full-rank (change r_true to d) but keep LoRA’s rank at 2. What happens to LoRA’s final loss, and why?

Show solution

LoRA can no longer reach zero loss — a rank-2 update \(BA\) cannot represent a full-rank \(\Delta W\), so it fits only the best rank-2 approximation of the needed change and leaves residual error. This exposes LoRA’s core assumption: it works because real fine-tuning updates are approximately low-rank. When the required change genuinely isn’t, you must raise \(r\) (more capacity, more parameters) or fall back to fuller fine-tuning. Empirically, for adapting pretrained LLMs, small ranks (4–64) suffice — which is the finding that made LoRA so useful.

2. Sweep LoRA’s rank \(r = 1, 2, 4, 8\) on the rank-2 task. Where does adding rank stop helping?

Show solution

Performance improves up to \(r=2\) (matching the true rank of the task shift) and then plateaus — ranks 4 and 8 don’t help because there’s no additional structure to capture; they just add parameters that the extra directions drive toward zero. This mirrors choosing any capacity hyperparameter (Track 3): match it to the complexity of what you’re modeling. In practice you don’t know the true rank, so you pick \(r\) by validation, starting small; the sweet spot is usually far below \(d\), which is the empirical basis for LoRA’s efficiency.

3. Conceptual: LoRA lets you merge \(BA\) into \(W_0\) after training. Why is that useful, and what do you give up by merging?

Show solution

Merging computes \(W_0' = W_0 + BA\) once, so inference runs a single matrix multiply with zero extra latency or memory versus the original layer — ideal for deploying one specialized model. What you give up is modularity: once merged, you can’t cheaply swap adapters or keep the base shared across tasks, because the base has been altered. So the choice is deployment-shaped: merge when serving one fixed task for lowest latency; keep adapters separate when you need to host many tasks on one base or update adapters independently. Both are used in production.
