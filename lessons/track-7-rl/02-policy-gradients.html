Policy gradients (REINFORCE)

Q-learning learned values and acted greedily. Policy gradients skip the middleman and optimize the policy itself with gradient ascent — the family that scales to continuous actions and underlies how modern language models are fine-tuned.

Track 7 · Lesson 2 Concept + derivation + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 0–1, Track 4

Value-based methods choose actions indirectly, through a learned \(Q\). But many problems have huge or continuous action spaces where \(\max_a Q\) is hopeless, and sometimes the best policy is genuinely random. Policy-gradient methods optimize a parameterized policy \(\pi_\theta\) directly by gradient ascent on expected return. We derive the policy gradient theorem, implement REINFORCE from scratch, and watch it learn.

💡 Concept: optimize the policy itself

Let the policy be a differentiable function \(\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\) with parameters \(\theta\) — for example a softmax over neural-network scores. Define the objective as the expected return of trajectories \(\tau=(s_0,a_0,s_1,a_1,\dots)\) the policy generates:

\[ J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\big[R(\tau)\big], \qquad R(\tau)=\sum_t \gamma^t r_{t+1}. \] (2.1)

We want to climb \(J\) by gradient ascent, \(\theta\leftarrow\theta+\alpha\nabla_\theta J\). The obstacle: the expectation is over trajectories whose distribution depends on \(\theta\), and the environment’s dynamics are unknown and non-differentiable. The policy gradient theorem shows we can estimate \(\nabla_\theta J\) anyway, using only quantities we can compute.

📐 The policy gradient theorem (log-derivative trick)

Write the objective as an integral over trajectories, \(J(\theta)=\int p_\theta(\tau)R(\tau)\,d\tau\), and differentiate. The trick is the identity \(\nabla_\theta p_\theta = p_\theta \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta\) (since \(\nabla\log p = \nabla p / p\)), which turns the gradient back into an expectation:

\[ \nabla_\theta J = \int p_\theta(\tau)\,\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(\tau)\,R(\tau)\,d\tau = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi_\theta}\!\big[\nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(\tau)\,R(\tau)\big]. \] (2.2)

Now the magic: the trajectory probability factors as \(p_\theta(\tau)=p(s_0)\prod_t P(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t)\,\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\). Taking the log turns the product into a sum, and the environment terms \(p(s_0)\) and \(P(\cdot)\) do not depend on \(\theta\), so their gradient is zero. Only the policy survives:

\[ \nabla_\theta\log p_\theta(\tau) = \sum_t \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t). \] (2.3)

Substituting back gives the estimator we can actually compute — no model of the environment needed, just the ability to sample trajectories and differentiate the policy:

\[ \nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[\sum_t \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\,G_t\right], \] (2.4)

where \(G_t\) is the return following step \(t\). Read it as a weighted maximum-likelihood update: increase the log-probability of each action, weighted by how good the return that followed it was. Good actions get reinforced; bad ones get suppressed — hence the name REINFORCE.

🛠️ From scratch: REINFORCE on GridWorld

Our reinforce.py uses a softmax policy with a table of preferences \(\theta[s,a]\). For softmax, the gradient of the log-probability has a tidy closed form: \(\nabla_{\theta[s,\cdot]}\log\pi(a\mid s) = \mathbf{1}_a - \pi(\cdot\mid s)\) — push up the taken action, push down all actions in proportion to their current probability. The loop is Equation (2.4): 

traj = run_episode(theta)                 # sample a trajectory from pi_theta
G = 0; returns = []                        # compute discounted returns G_t
for (s, a_i, r) in reversed(traj):
    G = r + GAMMA * G; returns.append(G)
returns.reverse()
for (s, a_i, _), Gt in zip(traj, returns):
    p = softmax(theta[s])
    grad = -p; grad[a_i] += 1.0            # d log pi(a|s) = 1_a - pi
    theta[s] += alpha * (Gt - b) * grad    # (2.4); b is an optional baseline
The baseline trick. You can subtract any function \(b(s)\) that doesn’t depend on the action from \(G_t\) without changing the gradient’s expectation — because \(\mathbb{E}[\nabla\log\pi\cdot b(s)]=0\). But a good baseline (like the average return) dramatically reduces variance: actions are now judged as better or worse than typical, not by their raw, noisy return. This single idea leads straight to actor–critic.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Policy gradient theorem: \(\nabla_\theta J = \mathbb{E}\big[\sum_t \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\,G_t\big]\).
  • Log-derivative trick turns \(\nabla\) of an expectation into an expectation of \(\nabla\log\pi\) — and the unknown environment dynamics drop out.
  • REINFORCE = sample trajectories, weight each action’s \(\nabla\log\pi\) by the return that followed. Weighted maximum likelihood.
  • Strengths: handles continuous/large action spaces and stochastic optimal policies; optimizes what you care about directly.
  • Weakness: high variance. A baseline \(b(s)\) reduces variance without adding bias.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. In the policy gradient \(\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a\mid s)\,G_t]\), why don’t the environment’s transition probabilities appear?

  • Because the environment is assumed deterministic.
    It can be stochastic; the transitions still vanish for a different reason.
  • Because we approximate them as uniform.
    No approximation is made — they disappear exactly.
  • Because \(\log p_\theta(\tau)\) splits into environment terms (independent of \(\theta\), gradient zero) plus policy terms.
    Correct. Taking \(\nabla_\theta\) of the log-trajectory-probability kills every factor that doesn’t depend on \(\theta\), leaving only \(\sum_t\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta\). This is why policy gradients are model-free.
  • Because \(\gamma=1\).
    The cancellation holds for any \(\gamma\); the discount is irrelevant to it.

Q2. What problem are policy-gradient methods especially better at than value-based methods like Q-learning?

  • Continuous or very large action spaces, where \(\max_a Q\) is intractable.
    Correct. A parameterized policy outputs actions directly (e.g. a Gaussian over a continuous control), avoiding the maximization over actions that value-based methods require.
  • Problems with only two actions.
    Tiny discrete action sets are exactly where value-based methods are easiest; policy gradients don’t have a special edge there.
  • Problems where the model \((P,R)\) is fully known.
    With a known model, dynamic programming is ideal; policy gradients shine when sampling is the only option and actions are continuous.
  • Problems with no reward.
    Without reward there is nothing to optimize for either family.

Q3. The defining weakness of vanilla REINFORCE is…

  • it is biased and converges to the wrong policy.
    The estimator is unbiased; its problem is variance, not bias.
  • it requires the transition model.
    It is model-free — that’s a strength, not a weakness.
  • it cannot represent stochastic policies.
    It naturally represents stochastic policies (e.g. softmax); that’s another of its strengths.
  • high variance, because each update uses the full noisy return \(G_t\).
    Correct. Monte-Carlo returns swing a lot from episode to episode, so the gradient is noisy and learning is slow/unstable — motivating baselines and actor–critic.

Q4. Subtracting a state-dependent baseline \(b(s)\) from \(G_t\) in the update…

  • introduces bias, trading accuracy for speed.
    It does not bias the gradient — that’s the whole point of choosing \(b\) to depend only on the state.
  • leaves the gradient’s expectation unchanged but reduces its variance.
    Correct. Since \(\mathbb{E}[\nabla\log\pi\cdot b(s)]=0\), subtracting \(b(s)\) keeps the estimator unbiased while judging actions relative to the state’s average — which lowers variance.
  • only works if \(b(s)\) is the true optimal value.
    Any action-independent \(b(s)\) preserves unbiasedness; the optimal-value baseline is merely a very good variance-reducing choice.
  • requires a model of the environment.
    The baseline is just a learned or computed number per state; no model is needed.

✍️ Practice

1. Run reinforce.py with the baseline on and off (the baseline flag). Compare how smooth and fast the learning curves are.

Show solution

With the baseline on, the curve is noticeably smoother and usually reaches good performance sooner. The baseline (a running average of returns) recenters the weighting so that an action is reinforced only if it did better than usual from that state, instead of being reinforced merely because the episode’s raw return was positive. The expected gradient is identical, but its variance is smaller, so each step points more reliably uphill. This is the cleanest demonstration of why every modern policy-gradient method uses some baseline or critic.

2. Derive the softmax score gradient \(\nabla_{\theta_a}\log\pi(a^\star\mid s) = \mathbf{1}[a=a^\star]-\pi(a\mid s)\) for \(\pi(a\mid s)=\frac{e^{\theta_a}}{\sum_b e^{\theta_b}}\).

Show solution

\(\log\pi(a^\star\mid s)=\theta_{a^\star}-\log\sum_b e^{\theta_b}\). Differentiate with respect to \(\theta_a\): the first term gives \(\mathbf{1}[a=a^\star]\); the second gives \(-\frac{e^{\theta_a}}{\sum_b e^{\theta_b}}=-\pi(a\mid s)\). So \(\nabla_{\theta_a}\log\pi(a^\star\mid s)=\mathbf{1}[a=a^\star]-\pi(a\mid s)\). Intuition: raising the taken action’s score increases its log-prob by 1 unit, but raising any score also inflates the normaliser, subtracting that action’s current probability. This is exactly the grad = -p; grad[a_i] += 1 line in the code.

3. Conceptual: how is REINFORCE on a sequence of tokens related to fine-tuning a language model with a reward (Lesson 4)?

Show solution

Treat the language model as a policy \(\pi_\theta(\text{token}_t\mid \text{tokens}_{<t})\) — exactly the autoregressive model from Track 5. A generated response is a trajectory; a scalar reward (say, a human-preference score) plays the role of the return. The policy gradient \(\mathbb{E}[\nabla\log\pi_\theta(\text{tokens})\cdot \text{reward}]\) then increases the probability of high-reward responses — this is the core of RLHF. Real systems use a smarter, lower-variance variant (PPO with a value baseline and a KL penalty), but the engine is the policy gradient you just derived.
