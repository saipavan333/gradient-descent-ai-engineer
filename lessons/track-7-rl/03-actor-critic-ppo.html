Actor–critic & PPO

Combine the two ideas you’ve built: a policy that acts and a value function that judges. Actor–critic slashes the variance that plagued REINFORCE, and PPO adds the one safeguard that made policy gradients reliable enough to train ChatGPT.

Track 7 · Lesson 3 Concept + code ~28 min Builds on: Lessons 1–2

REINFORCE pointed the right way but trembled with variance; Q-learning was stable but indirect. Actor–critic fuses them: an actor (a policy, Lesson 2) chooses actions, and a critic (a value function, Lesson 1) supplies a low-variance baseline that tells the actor whether each action beat expectations. We build it from scratch, then meet PPO, the algorithm that made large-scale policy optimization — including RLHF — practical.

💡 Concept: an actor guided by a critic

Recall the baseline trick from Lesson 2: subtracting a state-value baseline from the return reduces variance without bias. Actor–critic takes this to its conclusion. Rather than wait for the full Monte-Carlo return \(G_t\), the critic estimates the value \(V(s)\) and we form the advantage from a single step — the TD error of Lesson 1:

\[ A_t \;=\; \underbrace{r_{t+1} + \gamma V(s_{t+1})}_{\text{what actually happened}} \;-\; \underbrace{V(s_t)}_{\text{what we expected}}. \] (3.1)

The advantage answers a sharp question: was this action better or worse than the state’s average? Positive means the action beat expectations (make it more likely); negative means it underperformed (make it less likely). The actor updates with the policy gradient using \(A_t\) in place of the noisy \(G_t\), while the critic updates toward its own TD target. Two networks, learning together.

\[ \theta_{\text{actor}} \mathrel{+}= \alpha\, A_t\, \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t), \qquad V(s_t) \mathrel{+}= \beta\, A_t. \] (3.2)

🛠️ From scratch: actor–critic, and the variance win

Our actor_critic.py keeps a softmax actor \(\theta[s,a]\) and a tabular critic \(V[s]\), updating both online at every step with Equation (3.2): 

p = softmax(theta[s]); a_i = sample(p)
ns, r = step(s, A_LIST[a_i])
adv = r + GAMMA * (0 if terminal(ns) else V[ns]) - V[s]   # (3.1) TD advantage
V[s] += a_critic * adv                                     # critic learns value
grad = -p; grad[a_i] += 1.0
theta[s] += a_actor * adv * grad                           # actor uses advantage

📐 PPO: taking big steps safely

One danger remains. A policy gradient step can change the policy so much that it lands in a bad region and never recovers — training collapses. We would like to reuse a batch of experience for several updates (sample-efficient) without letting the policy lurch too far. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) solves this by clipping. Define the probability ratio between the new and old policies, \(\rho_t(\theta)=\dfrac{\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a_t\mid s_t)}\), and optimize

\[ L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t\Big[\min\big(\rho_t A_t,\ \operatorname{clip}(\rho_t,\,1-\epsilon,\,1+\epsilon)\,A_t\big)\Big]. \] (3.3)

The clip caps how much the objective rewards moving the ratio beyond \([1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]\) (typically \(\epsilon=0.2\)). If an action was good (\(A_t>0\)), you gain by raising its probability — but only up to \(1+\epsilon\); past that the gradient flattens, removing the incentive to overshoot. This keeps each update proximal — close to the old policy — giving the stability of small steps with the efficiency of reusing data. PPO’s reliability is why it became the default for continuous control and for the RL stage of RLHF.

The whole track, assembled. PPO is built from parts you now own: the policy gradient (Lesson 2) provides the direction, the critic/advantage (this lesson) lowers variance, and the clip bounds the step. Lesson 4 plugs this engine into a language model.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Actor–critic = policy (actor) + value function (critic); the critic supplies a low-variance advantage baseline.
  • Advantage \(A_t = r + \gamma V(s') - V(s)\): did the action beat the state’s expected value?
  • Updates: actor ascends \(A_t\nabla\log\pi\); critic descends the squared TD error. Both learn online.
  • PPO clips the policy-ratio objective to \([1-\epsilon,1+\epsilon]\), allowing safe multi-step reuse of each batch.
  • Why it matters: PPO’s stability made large-scale policy optimization — robotics and RLHF — practical.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. What does the “critic” contribute in actor–critic?

  • It selects the actions to take.
    That’s the actor’s job. The critic evaluates, it doesn’t choose.
  • A value estimate \(V(s)\) used to form a low-variance advantage that guides the actor.
    Correct. The critic’s \(V(s)\) turns the raw return into an advantage \(A_t=r+\gamma V(s')-V(s)\), giving the actor a cleaner, lower-variance learning signal than REINFORCE’s full return.
  • A model of the environment’s transitions.
    The critic learns a value function, not a transition model; actor–critic is still model-free.
  • The exploration noise.
    Exploration comes from the stochastic actor, not the critic.

Q2. A positive advantage \(A_t>0\) for action \(a_t\) means the update will…

  • decrease the probability of \(a_t\).
    Backwards — positive advantage means the action was better than expected, so we make it more likely.
  • leave the policy unchanged.
    A nonzero advantage produces a nonzero gradient; the policy moves.
  • increase the probability of \(a_t\) in state \(s_t\).
    Correct. The actor update \(A_t\nabla\log\pi\) pushes up the log-probability of actions whose advantage is positive — those that beat the state’s expected value.
  • update only the critic, not the actor.
    The same advantage trains both; the actor very much responds to it.

Q3. What problem does PPO’s clipping specifically prevent?

  • The critic overfitting the value function.
    Clipping acts on the policy ratio, not the critic’s fit.
  • The reward being too sparse.
    Reward sparsity is a separate issue; clipping doesn’t address it.
  • Exploration stopping too early.
    That’s an exploration-schedule concern; clipping is about step size, not exploration.
  • A single update moving the policy so far it lands in a bad region and collapses.
    Correct. By capping the ratio at \(1\pm\epsilon\), PPO removes the incentive to take huge steps, keeping the new policy close to the old one so you can safely reuse the batch for several updates.

Q4. In the ratio \(\rho_t=\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)/\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t\mid s_t)\), what does \(\rho_t>1\) indicate?

  • The updated policy makes that action more likely than the old policy did.
    Correct. \(\rho_t>1\) means the new policy assigns higher probability to \(a_t\) than the old one; PPO clips how far this is allowed to be rewarded so the policy doesn’t move too aggressively.
  • The action had negative advantage.
    The ratio measures probability change, not advantage sign; they are independent quantities.
  • The critic’s value was exactly correct.
    The ratio says nothing about critic accuracy.
  • The episode has terminated.
    Termination is unrelated to the probability ratio.

✍️ Practice

1. Run actor_critic.py and reinforce.py several times. Which reaches good performance more consistently, and why?

Show solution

Actor–critic is typically faster and steadier across runs. Both follow the same expected gradient direction, but actor–critic’s advantage signal has much lower variance than REINFORCE’s full Monte-Carlo return, so individual updates are more reliable and less prone to the occasional huge, destabilising step. The trade-off is a small bias: the critic’s \(V\) is only an estimate, so the advantage is slightly biased early in training (the classic bias–variance trade-off from Track 3, now in an RL setting). In practice the variance reduction wins handily.

2. In PPO’s clipped objective, suppose \(A_t>0\) and the ratio \(\rho_t\) grows to \(5\). What does the \(\min/\)clip do, and why is that desirable?

Show solution

With \(A_t>0\) and \(\epsilon=0.2\), the clipped term caps \(\rho_t\) at \(1.2\), so \(\operatorname{clip}(\rho_t,0.8,1.2)A_t = 1.2A_t\), while the unclipped term is \(5A_t\). The \(\min\) selects the smaller, \(1.2A_t\), so beyond a 20% increase in probability there is no further gain — the gradient flattens. This is desirable because a ratio of 5 means the policy has moved drastically from the data-collecting policy; rewarding that would encourage reckless, unstable updates. Clipping says “you’ve moved far enough; stop.”

3. Conceptual: actor–critic needs a critic \(V(s)\). For a language-model policy (Lesson 4), what is the “state,” and what might the critic be?

Show solution

The state is the prompt plus the tokens generated so far; the action is the next token. The critic \(V(s)\) is a value head — usually a small linear layer on top of the transformer’s hidden state — trained to predict the expected final reward of the response from that partial sequence. In RLHF, this value head is trained alongside the policy during PPO, and the advantage \(A_t\) tells the model which tokens improved the expected human-preference score. So the same actor–critic machinery you built scales, essentially unchanged, to aligning billion-parameter models.
