RLHF: aligning language models

The technique that turned a raw text predictor into a helpful assistant. RLHF learns a reward from human preferences, then uses the policy optimization you just built to steer a language model toward what people actually want.

Track 7 · Lesson 4 Concept + code ~26 min Builds on: Lessons 2–3, Track 5

A pretrained language model (Track 5) predicts likely text — but “likely” is not the same as “helpful, honest, and harmless.” Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) closes that gap. It is the capstone of this track because it assembles everything: the transformer as a policy, a learned reward, and PPO. We build a miniature RLHF end to end and watch the central alignment trade-off appear in real numbers.

💡 Concept: optimize for preferences you can’t write down

We cannot write a formula for “a good answer.” But humans can reliably say which of two answers is better. RLHF turns those comparisons into a trainable objective in three stages, shown below: start from a pretrained/supervised model, learn a reward model from human preference comparisons, then optimize the language-model policy against that reward with reinforcement learning.

📐 Stage 1: a reward model from preferences

Collect comparisons: for a prompt, show two model responses \(x_i,x_j\) and record which a human preferred. Model the probability that \(x_i\) is preferred with the Bradley–Terry model, where a learned reward \(r_\phi(x)\) sets each option’s “strength”:

\[ \Pr(x_i \succ x_j) = \sigma\big(r_\phi(x_i) - r_\phi(x_j)\big) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-(r_\phi(x_i)-r_\phi(x_j))}}. \] (4.1)

Train \(r_\phi\) by maximizing the likelihood of the observed preferences — exactly logistic regression (Track 3) on reward differences. The reward is only identifiable up to an additive constant (only differences matter), which is fine: the policy stage cares only about relative reward. In our toy, the reward model recovers the hidden human values almost perfectly. 

for _ in range(n_pairs):
    i, j = sample_pair()
    y = human_prefers_i(i, j)                 # 1 if human picked i
    p = sigmoid(r[i] - r[j])                   # Bradley–Terry prediction (4.1)
    g = (y - p)                                # logistic-regression gradient
    r[i] += lr * g;  r[j] -= lr * g

📐 Stage 2: optimize the policy, but stay close to home

Now improve the policy \(\pi\) to earn high reward. If we only maximized \(r_\phi\), the policy would exploit the reward model’s errors and drift into gibberish that scores high but reads terribly — reward hacking. The fix is a KL penalty that holds the policy near the trusted reference \(\pi_{\text{ref}}\) (the pretrained model):

\[ \max_{\pi}\ \mathbb{E}_{x\sim\pi}\big[r_\phi(x)\big] \;-\; \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\pi \,\|\, \pi_{\text{ref}}\big). \] (4.2)

This is optimized with PPO (Lesson 3), treating the language model as the policy and \(r_\phi\) as the reward. The coefficient \(\beta\) sets the trade-off: small \(\beta\) chases reward hard but drifts far from fluent language; large \(\beta\) stays safe but barely improves. For our toy the KL-regularized objective even has a closed-form optimum, \(\pi(x)\propto\pi_{\text{ref}}(x)\,e^{r_\phi(x)/\beta}\), which lets us trace the trade-off exactly.

Reward models are proxies, not truth. The reward model is itself a learned approximation of messy, inconsistent human judgments. Optimize it too hard and you maximize its flaws (Goodhart’s law: “when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure”). The KL penalty is a guardrail, not a cure — which is why evaluation, red-teaming, and newer methods like direct preference optimization (DPO) remain active research.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • RLHF = three stages: pretrain/SFT → reward model from preferences → RL (PPO) policy optimization.
  • Reward model: Bradley–Terry, \(\Pr(x_i\succ x_j)=\sigma(r_\phi(x_i)-r_\phi(x_j))\); trained like logistic regression on comparisons.
  • Policy objective: \(\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_\pi[r_\phi] - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}(\pi\|\pi_{\text{ref}})\); the language model is the policy, optimized by PPO.
  • The KL penalty prevents reward hacking by keeping the policy near the trusted reference; \(\beta\) tunes reward-vs-drift.
  • Reward is a proxy — over-optimization triggers Goodhart’s law; alignment is not “solved” by RLHF alone.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. Why train a reward model from comparisons instead of asking humans to score each response directly?

  • Comparisons require no humans.
    They very much require humans — humans make the comparisons. The advantage is elsewhere.
  • Absolute scores are always more accurate.
    The opposite motivates RLHF: absolute numeric scores are noisy and inconsistent across people and time.
  • Relative “A is better than B” judgments are far more consistent and reliable than absolute numeric scores.
    Correct. People disagree wildly on “rate this 1–10” but agree much more on pairwise preferences. Bradley–Terry converts those stable comparisons into a continuous reward.
  • Comparisons let you skip the policy-optimization stage.
    You still need the RL stage to act on the reward; comparisons only improve how the reward is learned.

Q2. What is the role of the KL penalty \(\beta\,\mathrm{KL}(\pi\|\pi_{\text{ref}})\) in the policy objective?

  • It keeps the optimized policy close to the trusted reference, preventing reward hacking.
    Correct. Without it, the policy drifts to exploit the reward model’s errors, producing high-scoring nonsense. The KL term anchors it near fluent, sensible language.
  • It increases the reward model’s accuracy.
    The reward model is already trained; the KL term constrains the policy, not the reward model.
  • It removes the need for PPO.
    PPO is how the objective is optimized; the KL term is part of the objective, not a replacement for the optimizer.
  • It makes training fully supervised.
    The RL stage is still reinforcement learning; the KL penalty is a regularizer, not a switch to supervised learning.

Q3. In RLHF, what plays the role of the “policy” being optimized by RL?

  • The human labelers.
    Humans provide preference data for the reward model; they are not the policy.
  • The reward model.
    The reward model supplies the reward signal; it is not the thing being optimized to act.
  • A separate game environment.
    There is no external game; the “environment” is essentially the reward model scoring generated text.
  • The language model itself, generating tokens as actions.
    Correct. The transformer is the policy \(\pi_\theta(\text{token}\mid\text{context})\); generating a response is a trajectory, and PPO adjusts \(\theta\) to earn more reward while staying near the reference.

Q4. “Reward hacking” / Goodhart’s law in RLHF means…

  • the reward model becomes perfectly accurate.
    It’s the opposite concern — the policy exploits the reward model’s imperfections.
  • over-optimizing an imperfect reward proxy maximizes its flaws, so true quality can fall even as measured reward rises.
    Correct. The reward model only approximates human preference; pushed too hard (small \(\beta\)), the policy finds high-reward outputs the model mistakenly likes. Hence the KL guardrail and careful evaluation.
  • the KL penalty grows without bound.
    Reward hacking is about exploiting the reward, not about the KL term diverging.
  • the policy stops generating text.
    It keeps generating — just text that games the reward rather than genuinely helping.

✍️ Practice

1. Run rlhf.py and read the \(\beta\) sweep. As \(\beta\) decreases from 2.0 to 0.1, describe what happens to expected reward and to KL, and what that means for a real assistant.

Show solution

Expected reward rises monotonically as \(\beta\) falls (the policy is allowed to chase reward harder), but KL from the reference also climbs steeply. For a real assistant, a too-small \(\beta\) means the model maximizes the reward model’s quirks — verbose, sycophantic, or formulaic answers that score well but help less — while drifting from fluent, calibrated language. Practitioners pick \(\beta\) (and watch held-out human evals) to sit at the “knee” of this curve: most of the reward gain, before the quality starts degrading. The toy reproduces this trade-off exactly because its KL-regularized optimum is closed-form.

2. The reward model is identifiable only up to an additive constant. Why doesn’t that constant affect the optimized policy?

Show solution

Adding a constant \(c\) to every reward, \(r_\phi(x)\to r_\phi(x)+c\), shifts the objective \(\mathbb{E}_\pi[r_\phi]\) by the same \(c\) for every policy, so it cannot change which policy is best. Equivalently, in the closed-form optimum \(\pi(x)\propto\pi_{\text{ref}}(x)e^{r_\phi(x)/\beta}\), the factor \(e^{c/\beta}\) is constant across \(x\) and cancels in the normalization. Bradley–Terry training reflects this: it only ever sees reward differences \(r(x_i)-r(x_j)\), so the overall level is never pinned down — and never needs to be.

3. Conceptual: DPO (direct preference optimization) skips the explicit RL loop. Given the closed-form optimum \(\pi^\star(x)\propto\pi_{\text{ref}}(x)e^{r(x)/\beta}\), sketch why a preference objective could train the policy directly.

Show solution

Invert the optimum: \(r(x)=\beta\log\frac{\pi^\star(x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(x)}+\text{const}\). So the reward is implicitly encoded by how much the optimal policy raises a response’s probability over the reference. Substitute this expression into the Bradley–Terry preference likelihood (4.1), and the reward model disappears — you get a loss directly in terms of the policy and the reference on preferred vs dispreferred pairs. Minimizing it makes preferred responses more likely than dispreferred ones relative to the reference, achieving RLHF’s goal with a single supervised-style objective and no reward model or PPO loop. That is DPO, and it follows entirely from the KL-regularized optimum you saw here.
