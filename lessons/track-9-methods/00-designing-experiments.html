Designing experiments

A result you can trust starts long before the code runs — in the design. A good experiment isolates one cause, predicts an outcome in advance, and is powerful enough to detect the effect it’s looking for.

Track 9 · Lesson 0 Method + code ~24 min Builds on: Tracks 3, 8

Track 8 taught you to read and critique others’ work; Track 9 teaches you to produce work that survives the same scrutiny. It starts with experimental design — the part beginners skip and experts obsess over. A sloppy design can’t be rescued by clever analysis; a clean one makes the analysis almost trivial.

💡 Concept: change one thing, predict the outcome

An experiment tests a hypothesis — a specific, falsifiable claim like “adding component X improves metric Y.” To test it cleanly you must distinguish three kinds of variable: the independent variable you deliberately change (X), the dependent variable you measure (Y), and everything else, which you must hold fixed as controls. If you change two things at once, you cannot attribute the result to either.

The pre-registration habit. Writing down what result would confirm or refute your hypothesis before running is the single cheapest way to do honest science. Without it, the brain’s pattern-matching will find a story in any result — a phenomenon Track 8’s cherry-picking demo made concrete. A prediction made in advance turns an ambiguous outcome into a clear yes/no.

📐 Statistical power: will you even see the effect?

Suppose your method truly helps. Will your experiment detect it? The probability of detecting a real effect is the experiment’s power. It depends on three things: the effect size (how big the true improvement is), the noise (run-to-run standard deviation), and the sample size (number of seeds). An under-powered experiment — too few seeds for the effect — will frequently miss real improvements and is also prone to reporting noise as signal. Convention treats power \(\ge 0.8\) as adequate. 

def power(effect, sigma=0.03, n_seeds=10, trials=2000):
    hits = 0
    for _ in range(trials):
        A = rng.normal(0.0,    sigma, n_seeds)     # baseline
        B = rng.normal(effect, sigma, n_seeds)     # method (true gain = effect)
        se = sqrt(var(A)/n + var(B)/n)
        hits += abs((B.mean()-A.mean())/se) > 1.96  # significant?
    return hits / trials                            # = power

🔑 Key results to remember

  • Hypothesis → variables → prediction → run → analyze. Change one independent variable; hold controls fixed.
  • Predict before you run. A pre-stated outcome prevents rationalizing whatever you happen to see.
  • Power = probability of detecting a real effect; rises with effect size and seeds, falls with noise.
  • Size the experiment first: small effects need many seeds (power \(\ge 0.8\)); under-powered studies miss real effects and amplify noise.
  • Confounds kill attribution: if two things changed, the result belongs to neither.

🧩 Quiz

🧩 Check your understanding
Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. You change your model’s architecture and its learning rate, then see an improvement. What’s the problem?

  • Nothing — an improvement is an improvement.
    You can’t publish or build on a result you can’t attribute; the “why” matters as much as the “what.”
  • You can’t attribute the gain to either change — they’re confounded.
    Correct. With two independent variables changed at once, the improvement could be from either or their interaction. Change one thing at a time to attribute the effect.
  • The learning rate is always irrelevant.
    Learning rate often matters a lot; that’s precisely why changing it alongside the architecture muddies attribution.
  • Architecture changes can’t affect results.
    They very much can — which is another reason to isolate the variable you’re testing.

Q2. What is an experiment’s “power”?

  • The compute it consumes.
    That’s resource cost, unrelated to statistical power.
  • The size of the true effect.
    Effect size influences power but isn’t the same thing; power also depends on noise and sample size.
  • The training accuracy of the model.
    Accuracy is an outcome, not the probability of detecting an effect.
  • The probability of detecting a real effect, given its size, the noise, and the number of seeds.
    Correct. Power answers “if the effect is real, how likely am I to find it significant?” You raise it with more seeds or by reducing noise.

Q3. Your true effect (0.01) is smaller than the run-to-run noise (0.03), and you run 5 seeds. What’s likely?

  • The experiment is under-powered — it will usually fail to detect the real effect.
    Correct. With a tiny effect buried in larger noise and few seeds, power is low (the figure shows ~0.12 here). You’d frequently miss the real gain, and any “significant” result is likely noise.
  • You’ll reliably detect the effect.
    No — power is very low in this regime; reliable detection needs many more seeds.
  • The effect size will increase with more runs.
    The true effect is fixed; more runs only sharpen your estimate, they don’t change the effect.
  • Noise is irrelevant when the effect is small.
    Backwards — noise matters most when the effect is small relative to it.

Q4. Why predict the outcome before running the experiment?

  • It makes the code run faster.
    Prediction is about scientific honesty, not performance.
  • It’s required by Python.
    It’s a methodological discipline, not a language feature.
  • It prevents rationalizing whatever result appears as if you expected it (hindsight bias).
    Correct. Stating in advance what would confirm or refute the hypothesis stops you from fitting a story to noise after the fact — the same self-deception behind cherry-picking.
  • It guarantees the hypothesis is true.
    Nothing guarantees truth; prediction just makes the test honest by fixing the success criterion ahead of time.

✍️ Practice

1. Run power_analysis.py. You expect a true gain of about 0.02 with noise 0.03. How many seeds do you need for power ≥ 0.8?

Show solution

From the table, an effect of 0.02 reaches ~0.83 power at 40 seeds (and only ~0.33 at 10 seeds). So you’d budget roughly 40 seeds per method to be reasonably confident of detecting a true 0.02 improvement. The practical takeaway: decide this before running, because discovering after a 10-seed run that you had only a 1-in-3 chance of seeing your effect means the experiment was never going to answer the question. Power analysis turns “how many seeds?” from a guess into a calculation.

2. Design an experiment to test “dropout improves test accuracy on dataset D.” Name the independent variable, dependent variable, and three controls.

Show solution

Independent variable: dropout (on vs off), ideally swept over a few rates. Dependent variable: test accuracy on D. Controls to hold fixed: the architecture, the optimizer and learning-rate schedule, the number of training epochs, the data splits and preprocessing, and the set of random seeds — varying only dropout. You’d run several seeds for each condition and compare mean test accuracy with confidence intervals. The subtlety: dropout changes effective capacity, so a scrupulous design also checks that the no-dropout baseline was tuned (e.g. with weight decay) so you’re not just beating an under-regularized strawman — a Track-8 red flag.

3. Conceptual: why can a single very large, expensive experiment be worse evidence than several small, controlled ones?

Show solution

A single big experiment usually changes many things at once (bigger model, more data, longer training, new tricks), so even a clear improvement can’t be attributed to any one cause — it’s confounded. It also typically runs once (no seeds), giving no estimate of noise. Several small, controlled experiments each isolate one variable, can be repeated across seeds for error bars, and together build a causal picture: “the gain comes from component X, not the extra compute.” This is why strong papers pair a headline result with a battery of controlled ablations (next lesson) rather than resting on one giant run.
