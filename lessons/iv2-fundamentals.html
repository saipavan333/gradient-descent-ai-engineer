ML fundamentals — questions & model answers

⏱ 26 min Lesson 12.2
Why this existsFundamentals questions test whether your ML knowledge rests on solid ground. Bias-variance, overfitting, precision/recall, imbalanced data, and the offline/online gap recur in nearly every interview — with follow-ups that reward understanding over memorization.
By the end, you can
  • Explain the bias-variance trade-off
  • Diagnose and prevent overfitting
  • Choose precision vs recall by error cost
  • Debug offline-good-but-prod-bad

Concept The questions that anchor every ML interview

ML fundamentals questions test whether you understand why machine learning works, not just which library to call. They recur in nearly every interview because they reveal the depth of your mental model — and interviewers probe with follow-ups, so memorized definitions collapse while genuine understanding holds. This lesson rehearses the classic fundamentals questions with model answers that show the reasoning interviewers want. The topics cluster into a few areas, and knowing the cluster helps you anticipate the follow-ups.

Model answer "Explain the bias–variance trade-off."

"Bias–variance decomposes a model's error into two sources. Bias is error from wrong assumptions — a model too simple to capture the pattern (underfitting); a linear model on curved data has high bias. Variance is error from sensitivity to the specific training data — a model so flexible it fits the noise (overfitting), which then fails to generalize. The trade-off: as you make a model more complex, bias falls but variance rises, so total error is U-shaped — there's a sweet spot of complexity that minimizes error on unseen data. You diagnose it by comparing train and validation error: high error on both means high bias (underfitting), while low train error but high validation error means high variance (overfitting). You reduce bias with a more expressive model or better features; you reduce variance with more data, regularization, or a simpler model. The whole goal of ML — generalization — is managing this trade-off so the model learns the signal, not the noise." Strong follow-up: "Modern deep learning complicates the classic picture — very large over-parameterized models can generalize well despite huge capacity, which the 'double descent' phenomenon describes, so the simple U-curve is a starting intuition, not the whole story."

Model answer "What is overfitting, and how do you prevent it?"

"Overfitting is when a model learns the training data too well — including its noise and quirks — so it scores high on training data but poorly on new data; it memorized instead of generalizing. You detect it by the gap between train and validation performance. To prevent it: more data (the best fix — harder to memorize a large, varied dataset); regularization (L1/L2 penalties that discourage large weights, dropout in neural networks, early stopping); a simpler model (less capacity to memorize); cross-validation to tune honestly; and data augmentation to synthetically enlarge the data. The unifying idea is to constrain the model's freedom to fit noise, or give it so much data that the signal dominates. It's the direct manifestation of high variance in the bias–variance trade-off." Follow-up they'll ask: "How does regularization actually help? — It adds a penalty for complexity to the loss, so the model only uses capacity that genuinely reduces error, trading a little training fit for better generalization."

Model answer "Precision vs recall — explain and when you'd prioritize each."

"Both measure a classifier on the positive class. Precision = of the items we predicted positive, how many actually were (TP/(TP+FP)) — it penalizes false alarms. Recall = of the actual positives, how many we caught (TP/(TP+FN)) — it penalizes misses. They trade off: a stricter threshold raises precision but lowers recall. Which to prioritize depends on the cost of each error. Prioritize recall when a miss is catastrophic — fraud detection, disease screening — where you'd rather investigate false alarms than let a real case through. Prioritize precision when a false alarm is costly — spam filtering (don't trash a real email), or recommending expensive interventions. F1, the harmonic mean, balances them when both matter. And crucially, on imbalanced data, accuracy is misleading — a 99%-accurate model can catch zero fraud — so precision and recall are the honest metrics." Why it lands: tying the choice to error costs, and flagging the accuracy trap, shows you evaluate models by what actually matters.

Model answer "How would you handle an imbalanced dataset (1% positives)?"

"First, use the right metrics — never accuracy (which a majority-class predictor games); use precision/recall, F1, and the precision-recall AUC on the minority class. Then, techniques at three levels. Data: resample — oversample the minority (or use SMOTE to synthesize examples) or undersample the majority — to balance what the model sees. Algorithm: use class weights to make errors on the rare class cost more, so the model attends to it. Threshold: tune the decision threshold to the cost trade-off rather than defaulting to 0.5. I'd also make sure the evaluation reflects reality — enough minority examples in the test set for stable estimates, and stratified splits. The key insight is that imbalance breaks naive training and evaluation, so you address it in how you measure, how you sample or weight, and where you set the threshold — matched to the real cost of missing the rare class." Follow-up: "Is oversampling always good? — It can cause overfitting to duplicated minority points, so class weights or careful synthesis are often preferable, and you always validate on untouched, real data."

Model answer "Your model does great in training but poorly in production. Walk me through diagnosing it."

"Great-in-training, poor-in-production, with the model unchanged, points at the data or evaluation, not the model's architecture, so I'd check in order. Overfitting / leaked evaluation: was the great 'training' number actually on a proper held-out set, or did I leak test data (tuning on it, or a feature that leaks the label)? A falsely high offline score is common. Distribution shift: does production data match training data, or has it drifted (different inputs than I trained on)? Train/serve skew: are features computed the same way in production as in training — a classic silent killer where the model sees subtly different inputs live. The metric: did I optimize a proxy (offline accuracy) that doesn't match the real production goal? To diagnose, I'd inspect real production inputs and predictions, compare production feature distributions to training, verify the feature pipeline is identical, and check for leakage in the offline setup. The discipline is to not assume the model is broken — the model is the same, so something about the data it's fed or how it was evaluated changed." Why it lands: a structured, data-first diagnosis (leakage, shift, skew, metric) signals real production experience.

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • Bias vs variance? Bias = too-simple (underfit); variance = too-sensitive (overfit); complexity trades one for the other.
  • Overfitting fix? More data, regularization, simpler model, cross-validation, augmentation.
  • Precision vs recall? Of predicted-positive how many right vs of actual-positive how many caught; choose by error cost.
  • Generative vs discriminative? Generative models P(x,y) (can generate data); discriminative models P(y|x) (just classify) — discriminative usually wins at pure classification.
  • Supervised vs unsupervised? Learn X→y from labels vs find structure in unlabeled X.
  • Why not accuracy on imbalanced data? A majority-class predictor scores high while catching none of the rare class.
  • L1 vs L2? L1 (lasso) drives weights to zero (feature selection, sparse); L2 (ridge) shrinks them smoothly.
  • What is cross-validation? Rotate which fold is validation to estimate performance robustly and tune without leaking the test set.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"Why might a random forest outperform a single decision tree? Explain the mechanism."
Show a strong answer

"A single decision tree is high variance — small changes in the data produce very different trees, and a deep tree overfits by carving the training data into memorized regions. A random forest is an ensemble of many trees whose predictions are averaged (or voted), and it reduces that variance through two sources of randomness: bagging (each tree trains on a bootstrap sample of the data) and random feature subsets (each split considers only a random subset of features). This makes the trees diverse and decorrelated, so their individual errors are somewhat independent and cancel out when averaged — the wisdom-of-crowds effect. The result keeps the low bias of trees (they can fit complex patterns) while slashing the variance that makes a single tree overfit, so the forest generalizes far better. The key mechanism to name is variance reduction through averaging decorrelated models, and the reason the randomness matters is that averaging only helps if the models make different mistakes." This answer lands because it explains the why (variance reduction via decorrelation) rather than just asserting "forests are better," and connects to the bias–variance framing interviewers love.

2"Explain gradient descent to me as if I were a smart non-expert, then tell me what the learning rate does."
Show a strong answer

"Imagine you're on a foggy hillside trying to reach the lowest point, and you can only feel the slope under your feet. The sensible strategy is to take a step downhill — in the direction the ground drops most steeply — then feel the slope again and repeat, until the ground is flat and you've reached the bottom. Gradient descent is exactly this for a model: the 'hill' is the error (loss) as a function of the model's parameters, the 'slope' is the gradient (which way error increases), and the model repeatedly nudges its parameters in the opposite direction of the gradient to reduce error, over and over, until the error stops improving. That loop — compute the slope, step downhill, repeat — is how essentially every model learns, from linear regression to GPT. The learning rate is the size of each step. Too small and learning is painfully slow (tiny steps take forever to reach the bottom); too large and you overshoot the minimum, bouncing around or even diverging (leaping across the valley and up the other side). So it's a crucial knob you tune — big enough to make progress, small enough to converge — and a lot of practical training is about getting it right." This answer works because the analogy makes it intuitive, it names the real mechanism (step opposite the gradient to reduce loss), and it explains the learning rate through the same picture — exactly the "explain it simply then go deeper" the prompt asks for.

3"When would you choose a simple model (like logistic regression) over a complex one (like a deep neural network)?"
Show a strong answer

"I'd choose the simpler model in several common situations, because 'more complex' is not 'better' by default. Small data: a deep network needs lots of data and will overfit on a small dataset, where logistic regression's low variance generalizes better. Interpretability matters: logistic regression's coefficients are explainable (essential in regulated domains — credit, healthcare — where you must justify decisions), while a deep net is a black box. Latency/cost constraints: the simple model is faster and cheaper to train and serve, which can be decisive at scale. The relationship is roughly linear or simple: if a simple model already captures the pattern, a complex one adds cost and overfitting risk for no gain. Baseline and iteration speed: I'd almost always start simple as a baseline — it's fast to build, sets the bar a complex model must beat, and often is good enough. I'd move to a deep network when there's enough data, the pattern is genuinely complex (images, text, rich interactions), the accuracy gain justifies the cost and opacity, and interpretability isn't a hard requirement. The principle is match model complexity to the data, the problem, and the constraints — the best model is the simplest one that meets the requirements, not the fanciest one available." This lands because it frames model choice as a requirements-driven trade-off (data, interpretability, cost, problem complexity) rather than a reflex toward deep learning — exactly the judgment senior interviews reward.

★ Deep dive: what fundamentals questions really test▸

Fundamentals questions are deceptively simple on the surface — "what is overfitting?" sounds like a definition to recite — but interviewers use them to probe depth of understanding through relentless follow-ups. The pattern is: you give a definition, and they ask "why?", "how does that work?", "what if...?", "how would you detect/fix it?", drilling until they find the edge of your real understanding. Someone who memorized "overfitting is fitting the training data too well" stalls at "why does regularization help?", while someone who understands can explain that regularization penalizes complexity so the model only uses capacity that genuinely reduces error, connect it to the variance term in bias–variance, and relate it to more data and early stopping. This is why the curriculum's insistence on defining every term and explaining the intuition and the "why" is exactly the right preparation — the questions reward the mental model, not the vocabulary. The best move in the interview is to give a clear answer and volunteer the next level of depth (the mechanism, the trade-off, the connection), signaling you understand rather than recite.

The deeper reason these questions dominate ML interviews is that the fundamentals are load-bearing — everything else is built on them. Bias–variance underlies every modeling decision; precision/recall and proper evaluation underlie every claim that a model works; the notion of generalization (learning signal, not noise, and having it hold on new data) is the entire point of ML and reappears as distribution shift and drift in production. So an interviewer testing fundamentals is really testing whether your advanced knowledge rests on solid ground or on memorized surface patterns that will crumble on a novel problem. This connects fundamentals to the whole rest of this interview track and curriculum: the deep learning, LLM, and system design questions all ultimately reduce to fundamentals applied in a specific setting — an LLM hallucinating is a generalization/evaluation problem; a RAG system's retrieval failing is a metrics problem; a production model degrading is distribution shift. Candidates who have truly internalized the fundamentals can reason about any of these from first principles, which is why solidifying them is the highest-leverage interview preparation there is. Master the fundamentals deeply, and the rest of the interview becomes applying them fluently rather than recalling disconnected facts — which is exactly the difference between a candidate who has read about ML and one who understands it.
