Track 5 · Computer Vision

Vision-language & multimodal models

⏱ 40 min Lesson 5.7 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsCLIP put images and text in one shared space, unlocking zero-shot classification and seeding the vision-language models inside modern assistants. This lesson closes the loop between vision and language — the defining convergence of current AI.
By the end, you can
  • Explain how CLIP aligns images and text via contrastive learning
  • Do zero-shot classification by matching image and text embeddings
  • Distinguish captioning and visual question answering (VQA)
  • Describe how modern VLMs feed image tokens into a language model

Concept One shared space for images and text

Until now, vision and language have been separate worlds. Multimodal models join them — a modality is a type of data (images, text, audio), and a multimodal model works across more than one. The model that opened this door is CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-training), and its idea is elegant: train an image encoder and a text encoder so that a picture and its caption land at nearly the same point in a shared embedding space (a space where each image and each piece of text becomes a vector, and closeness means "these mean similar things"). After training, a photo of a dog and the text "a photo of a dog" produce almost-identical vectors, while unrelated pairs land far apart. Vision and language now live in one coordinate system, and you can compare across them directly — measure how well any image matches any text just by checking whether their vectors are close.

How is this trained? By contrastive learning: show the model a big batch of (image, caption) pairs and train it so each image's vector is pulled close to its own caption's vector and pushed apart from all the other captions in the batch — "match the true pairs, separate the mismatches." Do this over hundreds of millions of image-text pairs scraped from the web (captions, alt-text) and the encoders learn a rich, shared understanding of visual and linguistic concepts — no manual labels required, because the caption is the supervision. The stunning payoff is zero-shot classification: to classify an image into categories you never explicitly trained on, just write each category as text ("a photo of a cat," "a photo of a dog"), embed them, and pick whichever caption-vector is closest to the image's vector. The model classifies into brand-new categories described in words alone — no fine-tuning, no labeled examples.

Concept From matching to describing and answering

CLIP matches images and text, but the same "join the modalities" idea powers models that generate language about images. Image captioning takes an image and produces a sentence describing it (image → text). Visual question answering (VQA) takes an image and a question and produces an answer ("How many people are in this photo?" → "Three"). Modern vision-language models (VLMs) — the image-understanding ability inside assistants like GPT-4V, Claude, and Gemini — generalize all of this: you show them an image and converse about it in natural language, and they can describe, answer, reason, read text in the image, and more.

Architecturally, today's VLMs follow a common recipe that reuses everything you've learned: a vision encoder (often a ViT, sometimes CLIP's image encoder) turns the image into a set of visual tokens, a projection maps those into the same token space a language model understands, and a large language model (Track 7) then treats the image tokens just like text tokens — attending to them alongside the words of your question to generate an answer. In other words, an image becomes "words" the LLM can read (echoing the ViT patches-as-tokens idea), and the LLM's reasoning does the rest. This is why the frontier of vision is increasingly the frontier of language too: the strongest visual reasoners are language models that have been taught to see.

Worked example Zero-shot matching with cosine similarity

Once images and text share a space, matching is just measuring vector closeness — with cosine similarity (how aligned two vectors are: 1.0 = same direction, 0 = unrelated). Here one image embedding is compared against three caption embeddings; the closest caption wins — that's zero-shot classification in five lines:

The image vector [0.9, 0.1, 0.8, 0.2] is compared to each caption vector by cosine similarity. It scores 0.996 against "a photo of a cat" (nearly identical direction — a strong match), but only 0.365 and 0.316 against "dog" and "bicycle" (very different directions — weak matches). Taking the highest score, the model classifies the image as "a photo of a cat" — and here is the remarkable part: the model was never trained on a "cat vs dog vs bicycle" task. We invented those three categories on the spot by writing them as text, embedded them with the text encoder, and compared. That's zero-shot classification: because images and text share one space, you can classify into any categories you can describe in words, with no labeled training examples and no fine-tuning — just embed the candidate labels and pick the nearest. Change the label set to "a chihuahua / a husky / a poodle" and you have a dog-breed classifier, instantly, from the same model. Real CLIP uses ~512-dimensional vectors from encoders trained on hundreds of millions of web image-text pairs, but the operation is exactly this: embed the image, embed the candidate texts, take the most similar. This one trick — comparison in a shared space — is also how image search (embed a text query, find the nearest image vectors), deduplication, and retrieval for multimodal RAG (Track 8) all work. Aligning modalities into a shared space turns "understanding across vision and language" into simple vector arithmetic.

★ Key points to remember
  • Multimodal models work across data types (modalities); CLIP aligns images and text into one shared embedding space where a picture and its caption land close together.
  • It's trained by contrastive learning on hundreds of millions of web image-text pairs — pull true pairs together, push mismatches apart; the caption is the supervision (no manual labels).
  • The shared space enables zero-shot classification: describe categories as text, embed them, and pick the caption closest to the image — classify into new categories with no training examples.
  • Captioning (image→text) and VQA (image+question→answer) generate language about images; matching, describing, and answering all connect pixels to language.
  • Modern VLMs feed image tokens (from a ViT) into a large language model, so the image becomes "words" the LLM reasons over — the strongest visual reasoners are language models taught to see.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1You need to classify product photos into 500 fine-grained categories, but you have almost no labeled examples. Why might CLIP zero-shot be a better first move than training a CNN classifier?
Show solution & reasoning

Training a 500-class CNN classifier requires labeled examples per class — realistically dozens to hundreds each, so tens of thousands of labels — which you don't have. CLIP zero-shot sidesteps the labeling problem entirely. Because CLIP aligns images and text in a shared space, you can classify by simply writing each of the 500 categories as text ("a photo of a <category>"), embedding those 500 label-texts once, embedding each product photo, and assigning each photo to the nearest label-vector. No labeled training images, no fine-tuning — you get a working 500-way classifier immediately, out of a model that already learned visual and linguistic concepts from hundreds of millions of web pairs. That makes it the ideal first move: it's essentially free to set up, gives you a baseline in an afternoon, and tells you how far "understanding described in words" alone gets you on your specific categories. For many category sets it's surprisingly strong, especially when the categories are describable in natural language (which product taxonomies usually are).

The reasoning skill is matching the method to the data constraint: when labels are the bottleneck (as here), prefer an approach whose "training" is writing text, not collecting labeled images. The mature plan is staged. Start with CLIP zero-shot as a no-label baseline. Then, if accuracy is insufficient on the hard/fine-grained categories, improve incrementally — better label wording (prompt engineering the class names, e.g., "a close-up photo of a <category> product"), or collect a small labeled set and do few-shot or light fine-tuning on top of CLIP's features (which are already excellent, so a little data goes far — transfer learning again). This ladder — zero-shot → prompt-tuned → few-shot → fine-tuned — lets you spend labeling effort only where it's actually needed, guided by where the baseline fails. Recognizing that CLIP converts a labeling problem into a describing problem — and using it as the cheap first rung before investing in labels — is exactly the practical, cost-aware judgment (echoing the data-is-the-bottleneck theme from segmentation and Track 2) that gets a working classifier shipped fast.

2Explain how the "patches as tokens" idea from the ViT lesson connects to how modern VLMs let a language model reason about an image.
Show solution & reasoning

The connection is that both turn an image into tokens so a transformer can process it — VLMs just take one more step. Recall the ViT insight: an image can be cut into patches, each flattened into a vector (a token), and then a transformer processes the sequence exactly as it processes words. A modern VLM builds directly on this. Its vision encoder (usually a ViT) turns the image into a set of visual tokens — the same patches-as-tokens move. Then comes the extra step: a projection layer maps those visual tokens into the same embedding space the language model uses for word tokens, so from the LLM's perspective the image tokens are just more tokens in its vocabulary's space. The LLM then does what it always does — attention over a sequence of tokens — but now that sequence contains both the image's visual tokens and the words of your question. Because attention lets every token attend to every other, the LLM's answer-generation can freely relate the question's words to the image's regions, which is exactly what "reasoning about the image" requires. So the image becomes "words the LLM can read," and the LLM's existing language reasoning does the visual reasoning.

The reasoning principle — and the through-line of the whole modern-AI story — is that the transformer is a universal token processor, so bringing a new modality to it means (1) tokenize that modality and (2) align its tokens with the space the model already uses. ViT solved step 1 for images (patches → tokens); CLIP and VLM projections solve step 2 (map those image tokens into a text-compatible space). This is why the pieces across this track and Track 6/6 snap together: patches-as-tokens (ViT), a shared image-text space (CLIP), and image-tokens-fed-to-an-LLM (VLM) are three uses of the same core move. Being able to trace that single idea — represent everything as tokens in a shared space, then let attention relate them — from ViT through CLIP to multimodal assistants demonstrates that you understand modern AI as one coherent architecture rather than a pile of separate tricks. That unifying view is precisely what makes the difference between memorizing model names and actually understanding why the field looks the way it does.

3CLIP learned from hundreds of millions of image-text pairs scraped from the web. What capabilities does that scale unlock, and what risks does it carry?
Show solution & reasoning

The web-scale training is the source of both CLIP's power and its problems. On capabilities: training on hundreds of millions of diverse, naturally-captioned images gives CLIP astonishingly broad coverage — it has "seen" a huge range of objects, scenes, styles, and the words people use for them, which is exactly why it generalizes zero-shot to categories no one curated a labeled dataset for. The breadth of the web is the breadth of its competence; you get a model that recognizes concepts from "golden retriever" to "art deco building" to "a diagram of a cell" because all of those appeared, captioned, somewhere in its training data. This scale-driven generality — capability emerging from massive, weakly- labeled data rather than careful curation — is the same force behind large language models (Track 7), here applied to vision- language. On risks: the web is uncurated, so the model inherits the web's biases and gaps. It absorbs societal stereotypes present in captions and their associations (which can surface as biased classifications or associations across gender, race, and culture — a fairness problem in the sense of Track 12); it reflects what the web over- and under-represents (strong on internet-common concepts, weak on rare or under-documented ones, and skewed toward the languages and cultures that dominate web text); and the training data raises consent and copyright questions, since images and captions were scraped without their creators' explicit permission.

The reasoning skill is recognizing the double-edged nature of web-scale training: the same indiscriminate ingestion that grants broad zero-shot capability also ingests the web's biases, imbalances, and rights issues — you cannot get one without confronting the other. This has concrete practical consequences: you should evaluate CLIP-based systems for bias before deploying them (especially for consequential classification of people), expect weaker performance on concepts and communities the web underrepresents, and be aware of the legal and ethical debate around scraped training data. It connects the exciting capability of this lesson straight back to the responsible-AI discipline of Track 12: a model's competence and its harms often flow from the same source — its training data — so understanding where a model learned from is inseparable from understanding both what it can do and how it can fail. Holding capability and risk together, and tracing both to the data, is exactly the mature, responsible perspective that distinguishes an engineer who can deploy these systems safely from one who only marvels at what they can do.

★ Deep dive: the convergence of vision and language, and where the field is heading▸

This lesson marks a convergence that defines the current era: vision and language are merging into unified systems. For most of deep learning's history they were separate fields with separate architectures, conferences, and experts. CLIP was a hinge point — by putting images and text in one space, it showed the two could be learned together, and it immediately became a component in other systems (it guides diffusion models' text conditioning, powers multimodal retrieval, and seeds VLM vision encoders). The trajectory since has been steadily toward generality: from CLIP (align two modalities) to captioning/VQA (generate language about images) to today's vision-language models that hold a fluent conversation about any image, read text within it, reason over charts and documents, and increasingly handle video and audio too. The frontier assistants — GPT-4V, Claude, Gemini — are natively multimodal: a single model that sees, reads, and reasons, because (as the practice problem traced) an image can be tokenized and fed into a language model's attention just like words. The practical upshot for an aspiring engineer is that "computer vision" and "NLP" are no longer separate careers so much as facets of one skill set built on the transformer — which is exactly why this curriculum taught them as connected tracks rather than silos.

Standing at the end of the Computer Vision track, look at how the whole thing coheres. You began with the most basic fact — an image is a grid of numbers (a tensor) — and built up: CNNs learned to extract visual features; the task hierarchy (classification → detection → segmentation) showed increasing spatial granularity over those features; Vision Transformers re-expressed images as token sequences so the language-model architecture could process them; diffusion models flipped analysis into generation; and now multimodal models fuse vision with language into unified reasoners. The connective tissue is the themes this curriculum has hammered throughout: transfer learning and pretraining (don't start from scratch — reuse learned features), the transformer as a universal token processor (tokenize a modality and attention handles the rest), scale and data as the engines of capability (and the sources of bias), and responsible evaluation (capability and harm often share a root in the data). Computer vision is no longer an island; it's one province of a unified landscape of AI, connected to language, generation, and reasoning — and you now have the map of the whole territory. The final lesson is a short interview check to consolidate it; after that, you can carry this integrated understanding of how machines see, create, and reason about the visual world into real work.
