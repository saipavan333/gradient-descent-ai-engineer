Vectors: the language of data

⏱ 25 min Lesson 1.1 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsBefore a model can learn anything, your data has to become numbers it can do arithmetic on. That representation is the vector — it is the atom of all of machine learning.
By the end, you can
  • Explain what a vector is and why ML represents data as vectors
  • Read and write vectors, and compute their length (norm)
  • Add and scale vectors, and picture what those operations do
  • Compute all of this in NumPy
i
Before we start — what "math foundations" really means

You do not need to be "good at math" to do this. We will build every idea from the ground up, define every word the first time it appears, and never assume you remember a formula from school. If a sentence ever uses a term you don't know, that's a bug in the lesson — every term gets explained. Take it slowly; re-read anything twice. That's normal here.

Concept Why we have to turn things into numbers

Let's start with what a machine-learning model actually is. Strip away the buzzwords and a model is just a recipe made of arithmetic: it takes some numbers in, multiplies and adds them in particular ways, and produces a number out (a prediction). That is all it can do — arithmetic on numbers. It cannot read a word, look at a photo, or understand a house the way you do. It only ever sees numbers.

This creates one unavoidable first problem. The things we want to predict about — a house's price, whether an email is spam, what a photo shows — are not numbers. So before any learning can happen, we have to translate each real-world thing into a list of numbers the model can chew on. That list of numbers is called a vector, and it is the single most basic object in all of machine learning. Everything else is built on top of it.

Let's define the words carefully, because we'll use them constantly:

  • A feature is one measurable fact about a thing. For a house, "size in square feet" is a feature. "Number of bedrooms" is another feature. A feature is always a single number (or something we turn into a number).
  • A vector is an ordered list of numbers. "Ordered" means the position matters and never changes: the first slot is always size, the second is always bedrooms, and so on, for every house. When the numbers in a vector are the features of one thing, we call it a feature vector.
  • The dimension of a vector is simply how many numbers are in the list. A list of 4 numbers is a 4-dimensional vector. (Here "dimension" just means "count of slots" — nothing spookier than that.)

Here is one house being translated into one vector:

We write that vector as $x = (1800,\ 3,\ 12,\ 350)$. A few notes on this notation (notation just means "the agreed way of writing something down"):

  • The letter $x$ is just a name for the whole list, so we can refer to it without rewriting all four numbers every time. Letters used as stand-in names for numbers (or lists of numbers) are called variables.
  • The numbers are wrapped in parentheses and separated by commas — that's the convention for "this is one vector."
  • Because this list has 4 numbers, we say it "lives in 4-dimensional space" and write $x \in \mathbb{R}^4$. Let's decode that symbol by symbol: $\in$ means "is in" or "belongs to." $\mathbb{R}$ (a fancy capital R) stands for the real numbers — that's just the ordinary numbers you already know: whole numbers, decimals, and negatives, like $3$, $0.5$, $-12$, $1800$. The small raised $4$ means "an ordered list of four of them." So $x \in \mathbb{R}^4$ reads, in plain English, as "x is an ordered list of four ordinary numbers." That's it.

Why does this matter beyond houses? Because the same trick encodes everything. A small grayscale photo that is 28 pixels wide and 28 pixels tall has $28 \times 28 = 784$ brightness values, so it becomes a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{784}$ — a list of 784 numbers. A word inside a modern language model becomes a vector of, say, 768 numbers, $\mathbb{R}^{768}$. The recipe never changes: list the numbers, fix the order. Once something is a vector, the model can finally do arithmetic on it.

i
A note on how vectors are written

You'll see vectors written two ways: lying down as a row, $(1800, 3, 12, 350)$, or standing up as a column (the same numbers stacked vertically). They mean the same list. The column form just makes some later math (in Lesson 1.2) line up neatly on the page. Don't let the rotation confuse you — it's the same vector.

Concept The second way to see a vector: an arrow

The "list of numbers" picture is correct, but there's a second picture that unlocks the geometry (the shapes-and-space view) behind machine learning. A vector can also be seen as an arrow that starts at a fixed center point and points to a location.

To draw this we need a couple of basic words. A number line is just a straight line with numbers marked along it, like a ruler. An axis (plural: axes) is one such number line used as a reference direction — typically we draw a horizontal axis and a vertical axis crossing each other. The point where they cross, where everything is zero, is the origin. A coordinate is the position of something measured along an axis ("3 units along the horizontal axis").

With that vocabulary: the 2-number vector $v = (3, 2)$ is the arrow that starts at the origin and reaches the point that is 3 units along the horizontal axis and 2 units up the vertical axis. (We use a 2-number vector here only because a flat page has 2 directions to draw in — the idea works for any number of dimensions, we just can't sketch more than 3.)

Seeing a vector as an arrow lets us ask two natural questions that turn out to matter enormously later: how long is the arrow? and which way does it point? Let's nail down "how long."

How long is a vector? The norm

The length of a vector's arrow is called its norm, written with double bars: $\|v\|$ (read "the norm of v" or "the length of v"). To compute it we borrow one fact from geometry, the Pythagorean theorem. Here it is in plain words, because we'll lean on it: in a right triangle (a triangle with one square 90° corner), the longest side — the slanted one, called the hypotenuse — is related to the two shorter sides (the "legs") by

$$ (\text{leg}_1)^2 + (\text{leg}_2)^2 = (\text{hypotenuse})^2. $$

Two more words used there: to square a number means to multiply it by itself ($3$ squared, written $3^2$, is $3 \times 3 = 9$). The square root of a number, written $\sqrt{\;}$, is the reverse question — "what number, multiplied by itself, gives this?" ($\sqrt{9} = 3$, because $3 \times 3 = 9$).

Now look back at the arrow for $v = (3, 2)$. It is exactly the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs are 3 (across) and 2 (up). So its length is:

$$ \|v\| = \sqrt{3^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9 + 4} = \sqrt{13} \approx 3.61. $$

Let's read that left to right, slowly: square the 3 to get 9; square the 2 to get 4; add them to get 13; take the square root of 13, which is about 3.61. (The symbol $\approx$ means "approximately equal to" — $\sqrt{13}$ is a never-ending decimal, so we round.) For a vector with any number of components $v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n$, the same idea generalizes: square every component, add them all up, take the square root:

$$ \|v\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}. $$

That single formula is the engine behind the question "how far apart are two things?" To compare two houses (or two users, or two images), we'll measure the vector between them and take its norm. Search, recommendations, and clustering all rest on this.

Concept The only two things we ever do to vectors

Almost every operation in linear algebra is built from just two simple moves. Learn these two and the rest stops being scary.

Move 1 — Addition (combine two vectors)

To add two vectors, you add them element-wise. "Element-wise" means position by position: add the first numbers together, add the second numbers together, and so on. For example $(2, 1) + (1, 2) = (2{+}1,\ 1{+}2) = (3, 3)$. The two lists must be the same length, since each slot needs a partner.

In the arrow picture, addition is "walk, then keep walking": travel along the first arrow, and from wherever you land, travel along the second arrow. The sum is the single arrow from your starting point to your final position. People call this tip to tail (the tail of the second arrow is placed on the tip of the first).

Move 2 — Scaling (stretch, shrink, or flip one vector)

To scale a vector, you multiply every number in it by the same single plain number. That single plain number is called a scalar (it's called a "scalar" precisely because it scales — resizes — the vector). For example $1.5 \times (2, 1) = (1.5{\times}2,\ 1.5{\times}1) = (3, 1.5)$.

What scaling does to the arrow: a scalar bigger than 1 makes it longer (stretch); a scalar between 0 and 1 makes it shorter (shrink); a negative scalar flips it to point the exact opposite way. Crucially, the arrow stays on the same line through the origin — only its length and which-way-along-that-line change.

★
Why these two tiny moves are a big deal

Combine them — scale a few vectors and add the results, like $2u + 3v$ — and you get what's called a linear combination (a "combination" built only from scaling and adding). That phrase will keep coming back: a linear combination is the skeleton of a neuron, a linear model, and a single layer of a neural network. You are literally three lessons away from seeing why. Everything heavy is built from these two light moves.

Worked example Houses as vectors, in NumPy — line by line

Let's make this concrete with real code. We'll use NumPy, the standard Python library (a library is a bundle of ready-made tools you can import and use) for working with lists of numbers fast. NumPy's core object is the array, which is its name for a vector (or a grid of numbers). We'll encode two houses, measure how different they are, and rescale a feature. Read the comments (the text after #, which Python ignores) — they explain each line.

Walk through the important line, the distance. Subtracting the two houses gives the difference vector $[850, 1, -28, 140]$ — each number says how far apart the houses are on that one feature (850 sq ft bigger, 1 more bedroom, 28 years newer, $140k pricier). Its norm, $\approx 862$, squashes all four differences into a single "how different overall" number.

Now notice something important hiding in that 862: it is almost entirely driven by size (850) and price (140), while the 1-bedroom and 28-year gaps barely register. Why? Simply because those features use bigger numbers — size is in the hundreds-to-thousands, age is in the tens. The distance can't tell "important" from "just numerically large." This is a real and famous trap, and the fix (rescaling features so they're comparable) is a Lesson 2.5 topic — but you can already see why it will be needed, which is the whole point of learning the foundation first.

The same operations in PyTorch (the deep-learning library we'll use later) look nearly identical — its "tensors" are arrays with extra superpowers (they can run on a GPU and track gradients, both covered later):

import torch
a = torch.tensor([1800., 3, 12, 350]); b = torch.tensor([950., 2, 40, 210])
print(torch.linalg.norm(a - b))          # tensor(861.9...)

And the TensorFlow version is tf.norm(a - b). Different spellings, identical idea — which is exactly why we learn the idea, not the spelling.

★ Key points to remember
  • A model only does arithmetic on numbers, so we first encode each real thing as a vector — an ordered list of numbers.
  • Each slot is a feature (one measurable fact); the count of slots is the dimension; $\mathbb{R}^4$ just means "an ordered list of 4 ordinary numbers."
  • A vector is both a list of numbers and an arrow (a direction and a length).
  • The norm $\|v\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}$ is the arrow's length (Pythagoras), and it powers "how far apart are two things."
  • Every operation is built from element-wise addition (tip-to-tail) and scaling (multiply every entry by one scalar); together they make a linear combination.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1By hand, compute $\|(5, 12)\|$ step by step. Then compute $2 \cdot (5, 12)$ and its norm, and explain the relationship between the two lengths.
Show solution & reasoning

Length: square each component ($5^2 = 25$, $12^2 = 144$), add ($25 + 144 = 169$), square-root ($\sqrt{169} = 13$). So $\|(5,12)\| = 13$ (a 5-12-13 right triangle).

Scaled: $2\cdot(5,12) = (10, 24)$, and $\|(10,24)\| = \sqrt{100 + 576} = \sqrt{676} = 26$.

Reasoning: 26 is exactly $2 \times 13$. Scaling a vector by a number $c$ multiplies its length by $|c|$ (the size of $c$, ignoring sign). Worth remembering as $\|c\,v\| = |c|\,\|v\|$ — doubling a vector doubles its arrow.

2Two users' ratings of three films are $u = (4, 0, 5)$ and $w = (1, 0, 2)$ (0 means "not seen"). Find the difference vector and the distance between them. Which film contributes most to the distance, and which contributes nothing?
Show solution & reasoning

Difference: $u - w = (4{-}1,\ 0{-}0,\ 5{-}2) = (3, 0, 3)$. Distance: $\sqrt{3^2 + 0^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{18} \approx 4.24$.

Reasoning: films 1 and 3 each add $3^2 = 9$ to the squared distance; film 2 adds $0$ because the users agree there. Distance only "feels" disagreement, and here the users differ equally on films 1 and 3. This is the seed of how recommendation systems decide two users are similar.

3In NumPy, X is a grid where each row is one house's feature vector. Write one line that returns the "average house" (the mean of each feature across all houses), and explain what axis=0 does.
Show solution & reasoning
X.mean(axis=0)

Reasoning: axis=0 means "go down the rows," averaging each column (each feature) on its own. So you get one vector holding the mean size, mean bedrooms, mean age, and mean price — the center of the cloud of houses. (If you used axis=1 you'd instead average across each row, mixing size with bedrooms, which is meaningless here.) Choosing the right axis is a constant source of bugs, so the habit to build now is: the axis you name is the direction you collapse. We'll meet axes again in Lesson 2.3.

★ Deep dive: "length" is a choice — meet other norms▸

The length we computed, $\|v\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_i v_i^2}$ (the little "2" labels which norm; the $\sum$ symbol, "sigma," just means "add up all the terms"), is the L2 or Euclidean norm — straight-line distance, the everyday kind. But "distance" is actually a choice, and a few other choices appear by name in real machine learning:

NormHow you compute it for $v=(v_1,\dots,v_n)$Picture / meaningWhere it shows up
L1 (Manhattan)add up the sizes (absolute values): $|v_1| + \cdots + |v_n|$city-block distance — you can only travel along streets, no diagonalsLasso regularization (Track 3), which pushes many features to exactly 0
L2 (Euclidean)$\sqrt{v_1^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}$straight-line, "as the crow flies"most distances; Ridge regularization; k-means clustering
L∞ (max)the single biggest size, $\max_i |v_i|$"how bad is the worst component?"worst-case and robustness analysis

("Absolute value," written $|{-}5| = 5$, just means the number's size ignoring its minus sign.) For $v = (3, -4)$: L1 $= 3 + 4 = 7$; L2 $= \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$; L∞ $= \max(3, 4) = 4$. Same vector, three different "lengths," each handy for a different job. The deeper point: when you later choose between L1 and L2 regularization, you're really choosing which notion of size to penalize — and that choice changes what your model learns. NumPy gives you all three: np.linalg.norm(v, 1), np.linalg.norm(v) (L2 is the default), and np.linalg.norm(v, np.inf).
