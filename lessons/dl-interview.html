Track 4 · Deep Learning

Interview check: Deep Learning

⏱ 15 min Lesson 4.10
Why this existsDeep-learning interviews test mechanics: derive backprop, diagnose vanishing gradients, justify an architecture. This checkpoint rehearses those questions with model answers that show the reasoning.
By the end, you can
  • Explain backprop and vanishing gradients crisply
  • Justify ReLU, Adam, batch norm, and CNN sharing
  • Diagnose training failures from symptoms
  • Walk through a small-data plan end to end

Concept What a deep-learning interview round tests

Deep-learning interviews probe whether you understand the mechanics — can you explain backprop, diagnose a training failure, justify an architecture? — not just name techniques. The questions below recur constantly; each model answer shows the reasoning an interviewer is listening for, building on the mental map of the whole track.

Model answer "Walk me through backpropagation."

"Backprop computes the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight, so gradient descent can update them. It's the chain rule applied to the network's computational graph. There are two passes: the forward pass computes each layer's output up to the scalar loss; the backward pass starts from $\partial L/\partial L = 1$ and moves right-to-left, and at each node multiplies the incoming gradient by that node's local derivative to get the gradient flowing out. Because each layer's gradient reuses the gradient already computed for the layer after it, the whole thing costs about one extra forward pass rather than one pass per weight. In code I just build the forward computation and call loss.backward() — autograd records the graph and applies the chain rule automatically."

Strong follow-up: mention reverse-mode autodiff is ideal because the network has many inputs (weights) and one output (the loss).

Model answer "Why do vanishing gradients happen, and how do you fix them?"

"To reach an early layer, the gradient is a product of the local slopes of all the layers after it. If those slopes are below 1 — which sigmoid and tanh guarantee in their saturated regions — the product shrinks exponentially with depth, so early layers get almost no signal and stop learning. In RNNs it's worse because a long sequence is like a very deep network in time. Fixes: use ReLU (slope 1 for positive inputs, so it doesn't shrink the gradient), proper initialization (He/Xavier, to keep the per-layer scale near 1), normalization layers (batch/layer norm), and residual connections, which give gradients a direct skip path around layers. For RNNs specifically, LSTMs/GRUs add a gated cell-state highway so gradients flow across many steps. The mirror problem, exploding gradients, is handled by gradient clipping."

Model answer "Why does batch normalization help training?"

"It normalizes each layer's activations to roughly mean 0, variance 1 within a mini-batch, then applies a learnable scale and shift. The practical effect is that activations stay in a healthy range across depth, so you can train deeper networks faster and with higher learning rates, and it's more robust to imperfect initialization. The original motivation was reducing 'internal covariate shift' — stopping the input distribution to each layer from drifting as earlier layers update — though later work suggests its bigger benefit is actually smoothing the loss landscape. It also adds mild regularization from the batch-to-batch noise. One caveat: it behaves differently in train vs eval mode (batch stats vs running stats), so you must call model.eval() at test time; and for transformers, layer norm is preferred because batch statistics are awkward there."

Model answer "When would you use a CNN, and why is it efficient for images?"

"CNNs are for data with grid/spatial structure — images, spectrograms. Their efficiency comes from two ideas. Parameter sharing: a small filter slides across the whole image reusing the same weights, so you detect a pattern anywhere with a handful of parameters instead of millions — and you get translation tolerance for free. Locality: each filter looks at a small neighborhood, matching how visual features are local; stacking layers grows the receptive field so deep neurons integrate global context. Compared to a fully-connected net, a conv layer can have a thousand times fewer weights on an image while generalizing better. For non-grid tabular data I wouldn't use a CNN — gradient-boosted trees usually win there (Track 3)."

Model answer "Feature extraction vs fine-tuning — how do you choose?"

"Both start from a pretrained model. Feature extraction freezes the backbone and trains only a new head — fast, data-efficient, low risk; I use it when my dataset is small or very similar to the pretraining data. Fine-tuning unfreezes some or all of the backbone and trains it at a low learning rate so the features adapt — I use it when I have more data or a domain that differs from the original. The low learning rate matters: the pretrained weights are valuable, and a freshly-initialized head produces large early gradients that could wreck them, so I often warm up the head first (frozen backbone) and then unfreeze gradually. Rule of thumb: small/similar → freeze; larger/different → fine-tune."

★ Rapid-fire answers to have ready
  • Why ReLU? Slope 1 for positive inputs (no vanishing), cheap, sparse activations; sigmoid/tanh saturate.
  • Why zero_grad()? PyTorch accumulates gradients; you must clear them each step or they pile up.
  • Adam vs SGD? Adam = momentum + per-parameter step sizes; robust default. Tuned SGD+momentum can generalize slightly better.
  • Loss is NaN? LR too high, exploding gradients, or bad init/normalization — lower LR, clip gradients, check scaling.
  • Overfitting fixes? Dropout, weight decay, early stopping, data augmentation, more data, smaller model.
  • First debugging step? Overfit a tiny batch to ~0 loss to separate bugs from tuning.

Quiz Rapid-fire check

Q1. "Our 60-layer network's loss won't drop at all, and the early layers' gradients are ~0." Best first hypothesis?

  • AThe learning rate is too high
  • BThere's too much data
  • CVanishing gradients — fix with ReLU, better init, normalization, or residual connections
  • DThe batch size is too small
A too-high LR causes spiky/NaN loss, not near-zero early gradients.
More data doesn't zero out early-layer gradients.
Correct. Near-zero gradients in early layers of a very deep net is the vanishing-gradient signature; the fixes are ReLU/init/normalization/residuals.
Batch size affects noise, not this systematic depth-wise decay.

Q2. An interviewer asks why a deep stack of purely linear layers is pointless. The crisp answer:

  • ALinear layers are too slow
  • BA composition of linear maps is itself linear, so the whole stack equals a single linear layer
  • CLinear layers can't be initialized
  • DThey use too much memory
Speed isn't the issue; expressiveness is.
Correct. Without nonlinear activations the layers collapse to one matrix — no more powerful than linear regression. Activations are what make depth meaningful.
They initialize fine; that's irrelevant.
Memory isn't the conceptual problem.

Q3. "Why is Adam a reasonable default optimizer?" A complete answer mentions:

  • AIt guarantees the global minimum
  • BIt removes the learning rate
  • CIt needs no gradients
  • DIt combines momentum with per-parameter adaptive step sizes, so it's robust with little tuning
No optimizer guarantees the global minimum on a non-convex loss.
It still has a learning rate (default ~1e-3); it adapts per-parameter scaling on top.
It very much uses gradients.
Correct. Momentum + adaptive per-weight steps make Adam converge fast and forgivingly across many problems.

Q4. "You have 300 images for a new task." The best answer leads with:

  • ATransfer learning from a pretrained model (freeze backbone, train a new head) plus data augmentation
  • BTrain a 100-layer CNN from scratch
  • CUse a single linear layer on raw pixels
  • DCollect no more data and accept low accuracy
Correct. With tiny data, reuse pretrained features (freeze + new head) and augment — exactly the data-efficient recipe.
300 images can't train a deep net from scratch — it would overfit immediately.
Raw-pixel linear models ignore spatial structure and underperform badly on images.
Transfer learning is precisely the tool for small data — don't give up accuracy.

Practice Mock interview prompts

1"Your model trains to 99% train accuracy but 78% validation. Walk me through how you'd diagnose and fix it." (2-minute answer.)
Show a strong answer

"The 21-point gap is classic overfitting — high variance. First I'd confirm it on the loss curves (train falling, validation turned up). Then I'd attack variance in order of leverage: data augmentation and more data first, since making the dataset effectively larger is the most reliable fix; then regularization — dropout, weight decay, and early stopping at the validation minimum; then reduce model capacity if it's oversized. I'd tune the regularization strength on the validation set and re-check the gap. I'd also sanity-check there's no leakage inflating the train score. I'd avoid just training longer, which would widen the gap."

This shows the diagnosis (gap → variance), an ordered toolkit (augmentation → regularization → capacity), and the discipline of validating fixes — exactly the reasoning from Lessons 3.5 and 4.5.

2"Implement a single training step in PyTorch from memory, and explain each line."
Show a strong answer

"optimizer.zero_grad() — clear last step's gradients, because PyTorch accumulates them. pred = model(x) — the forward pass. loss = loss_fn(pred, y) — measure error with the task-appropriate loss (cross-entropy for classification, MSE for regression). loss.backward() — backprop; autograd fills every parameter's .grad via the chain rule. optimizer.step() — apply the update rule ($w \leftarrow w - \eta\,\nabla L$, or Adam's adaptive version) to every parameter."

The four-beat rhythm — zero_grad → forward/loss → backward → step — plus a one-line reason for each is exactly what's expected. Bonus points for noting the common bug: forgetting zero_grad() makes gradients accumulate and training diverge, and forgetting model.train()/eval() mishandles dropout/batchnorm.

3"Why did transformers replace RNNs for most sequence tasks?"
Show a strong answer

"Two core reasons. Parallelism: an RNN is inherently sequential — step $t$ needs step $t-1$ — so it can't parallelize across the sequence, making it slow to train. A transformer processes all positions at once, which is far more efficient on modern hardware and is why they scale to huge data. Long-range dependencies: attention connects any two positions directly in a single step, regardless of distance, so it sidesteps the vanishing-gradient problem that plagues RNNs over long sequences — where the signal has to survive multiplication through many time steps. LSTMs/GRUs mitigated vanishing with gated memory, but transformers eliminated the bottleneck entirely. The trade-off is that attention is quadratic in sequence length, which is its own active research area."

This connects Lesson 4.7's vanishing-gradient theme to the architecture of Track 6, showing you understand why the field moved on, not just that it did.

★ Deep dive: how to reason out loud about deep learning▸

Deep-learning interviews reward a specific habit: connecting symptoms to mechanisms to fixes. When asked about a failure, narrate the causal chain — "the loss is NaN, which means a numerical blow-up, which usually comes from too-high a learning rate or exploding gradients, which I'd address by lowering the LR and clipping gradients." That structure (observation → cause → remedy) shows you can actually debug, not just recite. Always quantify the trade-off too: ReLU vs sigmoid, Adam vs SGD, freeze vs fine-tune, batch norm vs layer norm — each has a "use X when…, but the cost is…" that signals real experience.

Two meta-tips. First, tie deep learning back to fundamentals: backprop is the chain rule (Track 1), weight decay is L2 regularization (Track 3), a neuron is logistic regression (Track 3), transfer learning is the freeze-and-adapt principle behind foundation models (Track 7). Interviewers love seeing the unifying threads, because it proves you understand rather than memorize. Second, when you're unsure, reason from the loss-minimizing loop: almost every deep-learning question reduces to "what is the gradient doing, and is it flowing well?" — vanishing/exploding gradients, initialization, normalization, residual connections, optimizers, and learning rates are all answers to that one question. Hold that thread and you can derive an answer to most questions live, which is far more impressive than a memorized fact. That mindset — everything is the gradient-descent loop, scaled and stabilized — is the real takeaway of Track 4, and it carries straight into the transformers and large language models ahead."

That completes Track 4 — you can build, train, debug, and transfer deep networks, and explain every piece.
