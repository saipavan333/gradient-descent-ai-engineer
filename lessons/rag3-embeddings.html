Embeddings for retrieval

⏱ 24 min Lesson 8.3 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsRetrieval finds relevant chunks by meaning, not words. Embeddings turn text into vectors where semantic neighbors are close, so cosine similarity retrieves the right chunks even with zero shared words.
By the end, you can
  • Explain retrieval as nearest-neighbor search
  • Use cosine similarity to rank chunks
  • Know why embeddings beat keyword search
  • Use one embedding model for docs and queries

Concept Retrieval = nearest neighbors in embedding space

To retrieve the chunks relevant to a question, we need to compare meaning, not match words. The tool is the embedding from Lesson 6.2: a learned vector where similar meanings have similar vectors. The recipe is exactly the semantic search teased in Lesson 6.2's practice. Offline: embed every chunk and store its vector. At query time: embed the question, then find the chunk vectors closest to the query vector — the nearest neighbors — and return them. Closeness is measured by cosine similarity (Lesson 1.3): the cosine of the angle between vectors, which is high when they point the same way regardless of length.

This is the heart of RAG, and it's why embeddings matter so much: the model can only answer from chunks the retriever finds, and the retriever finds them by vector closeness. A query and a chunk that mean the same thing will have nearby vectors even if they share no words — which is the decisive advantage over keyword search.

Concept Embedding models and the details that matter

The vectors come from an embedding model — usually a transformer (often an encoder, Lesson 5.8) trained specifically so that semantically related texts get nearby vectors. You don't train your own; you pick one (many open and commercial options) based on quality, speed, dimension, and cost. A few practical details: normalization (scaling vectors to unit length) is common so cosine similarity reduces to a simple dot product; the embedding dimension (often 384–1536) trades quality against storage and search speed; and the embedding model must be the same for indexing chunks and embedding queries, or the vectors won't be comparable.

Worked example Ranking chunks by cosine similarity

Let's retrieve by similarity. We embed a small set of documents and a query, then rank documents by cosine similarity to the query. (We use TF-IDF vectors here so it runs without a heavy neural model — TF-IDF is lexical, but the cosine-ranking mechanism is identical to neural retrieval; a real embedding model would additionally capture synonyms, as the diagram shows.)

The query about refunds and returns retrieved exactly the two relevant documents — the refund policy (0.203, highest) and the return policy (0.117) — while the unrelated password and office documents scored 0. The mechanism is precisely nearest-neighbor by cosine: embed everything with the same vectorizer, compute the cosine between the query and each document, and sort. This is the entire retrieval step of RAG. With a real neural embedding model, the only difference is the vectors are richer — they'd also rank a document about "getting your money back" highly despite it sharing no words with "refund," which lexical TF-IDF can't do (as the worked example in Lesson 8.1 showed). Swap TF-IDF for a neural embedder and you have production-grade semantic retrieval; the ranking-by-cosine skeleton you just wrote stays exactly the same.

★ Key points to remember
  • Retrieval embeds chunks and the query into one vector space and returns the chunks closest to the query (nearest neighbors).
  • Closeness = cosine similarity (Lesson 1.3): high when vectors point the same way, regardless of length.
  • Embeddings beat keywords by matching meaning — synonyms and paraphrases land close even with zero shared words.
  • Use a trained embedding model (you pick one); the same model must embed both chunks and queries.
  • Details: normalize vectors (cosine → dot product), and the dimension trades quality vs speed/storage.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1Two unit vectors $\mathbf{a} = [0.6, 0.8]$ and $\mathbf{b} = [0.8, 0.6]$. Compute their cosine similarity (which, for unit vectors, is just the dot product). Are these "chunks" fairly similar or unrelated?
Show solution & reasoning

Cosine $= \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = (0.6)(0.8) + (0.8)(0.6) = 0.48 + 0.48 = \mathbf{0.96}$. That's very close to 1, so the two chunks are highly similar in meaning — their vectors point in nearly the same direction.

Note that because both vectors are unit length ($0.6^2 + 0.8^2 = 1$), cosine similarity equals the plain dot product — which is why systems normalize embeddings to unit length: it makes the most common similarity computation a single fast dot product (Lesson 1.3), and dot products are exactly what vector databases (next lesson) are optimized to compute at scale. A similarity of 0.96 here would mean "retrieve this chunk"; a value near 0 would mean "irrelevant."

2Your RAG system retrieves chunks that are topically related to the query but never quite contain the specific answer. The embeddings are fine. Where else might the problem be?
Show solution & reasoning

Likely chunking (Lesson 8.2), not embeddings. If the answer-bearing text was split badly — the specific fact separated from its context, or chunks too large so the precise answer is diluted in a long passage — then even perfect embeddings retrieve the right topic but not the right span. Retrieval can only return what the chunks contain.

Diagnose by inspecting the retrieved chunks: do they actually contain the answer? If not, revisit chunk size and boundaries (smaller or structure-aware chunks for precise facts, or the small-to-big pattern from Lesson 8.2's deep dive). Other culprits: the query is phrased very differently from the document (consider query rewriting, Lesson 8.5), or you need reranking (Lesson 8.5) to surface the truly-best chunk from a topically-relevant set. The habit of asking "is this a chunking problem, an embedding problem, or a ranking problem?" is how you debug RAG systematically — and it's why the track separates these steps.

3You're choosing between a 384-dimensional and a 1536-dimensional embedding model. What's the tradeoff, and how would you decide?
Show solution & reasoning

Higher dimension (1536) generally captures more nuance, so retrieval quality is often better — but each vector takes 4× the storage and searching is slower and more memory-hungry (more numbers to compare per vector, across millions of vectors). Lower dimension (384) is cheaper and faster but may miss subtle distinctions. It's the quality-vs-cost tradeoff that recurs throughout ML.

Decide empirically (Track 3's model-selection mindset): build an evaluation set of real queries with known relevant chunks (Lesson 8.7), measure retrieval quality (recall@k) for each model, and weigh the quality gain against the storage/latency/cost increase at your scale. Often a good 384-dim model is plenty and far cheaper to run; sometimes the larger model's quality justifies its cost. Don't assume bigger is better — measure it on your data, just as with model size in Track 7. Many teams also test several embedding models, since quality varies a lot by domain (general text vs code vs medical).

★ Deep dive: how embedding models are trained, and their limits▸

Retrieval embedding models are trained with contrastive learning: show the model pairs of texts that should be close (a question and its answer, a query and a relevant document, two paraphrases) and pairs that should be far (a query and an irrelevant document), and train the vectors so positives are near and negatives are far (often with an InfoNCE/contrastive loss — conceptually, "pull relevant pairs together, push irrelevant ones apart"). This is why a good retrieval embedder maps a question and its answer close together even though they're worded completely differently — it was explicitly trained on question→answer pairs to do exactly that. It's different from the word2vec embeddings of Lesson 6.2 (which captured word similarity) and from a language model's internal embeddings (optimized for next-token prediction, not retrieval) — retrieval embedders are purpose-built for "find the relevant text," which is why you use a dedicated embedding model rather than, say, pulling vectors out of a chatbot. These models are typically bi-encoders: they embed the query and each document independently, so you can pre-compute all the document vectors offline and only embed the query at runtime — fast and scalable. The price of that independence is that the model never sees the query and document together, which limits how finely it can judge relevance — the gap that cross-encoders and reranking fill (Lesson 8.5).

It's worth knowing embeddings' limitations so you deploy them wisely. They can struggle with exact matches that matter — specific names, codes, IDs, or numbers, where a slight semantic blur is harmful and old-fashioned keyword search actually does better (which is why hybrid search, Lesson 8.5, combines both). They inherit biases and domain gaps from training: a general-purpose embedder may poorly distinguish technical or legal nuances it wasn't trained on, so domain-specific embedders or fine-tuned ones can help. They have a fixed input length (another reason for chunking). And similarity is only as meaningful as the training objective — two texts can be "similar" in a way that's irrelevant to your task (same topic, opposite stance). The practical wisdom mirrors the whole track: embeddings are a powerful, learned similarity function, not a perfect one, so you measure retrieval quality on real queries (Lesson 8.7), combine semantic with keyword search where exactness matters (Lesson 8.5), and treat the embedding model as a tunable component, not a black box. Get the embedding step right and the rest of RAG has a solid foundation; get it wrong and no amount of clever prompting downstream can recover the chunks you failed to retrieve.

Computing cosine similarity against millions of chunk vectors for every query would be far too slow if done naively. Next, in 7.4 Vector databases & ANN search, we see how specialized indexes make this near-instant.
