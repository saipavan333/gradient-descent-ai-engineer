k-Nearest Neighbors

Before deep learning, a whole toolbox of classical models solved — and still solves — most real-world ML problems. We start with the simplest idea of all: to classify something, look at what’s nearby.

Track 12 · Lesson 0 Concept + code ~20 min Builds on: Tracks 1, 3

Deep networks dominate images and language, but on the tabular data most organizations actually have, classical models — trees, forests, boosting, SVMs — are still the workhorses, often winning outright. This track builds them from scratch. We begin with k-nearest neighbors, the model with no training at all, because it makes the core ideas of the whole field vivid.

💡 Concept: classify by neighbourhood vote

k-NN could not be simpler. To predict the label of a new point, find the \(k\) closest points in the training set — closeness measured by Euclidean distance (Track 1) — and take a majority vote of their labels. There is no training phase and no learned parameters; the model is the training data. It’s a non-parametric, instance-based method: it makes no assumption about the shape of the boundary and simply lets the data speak locally. 

def knn_predict(Xtrain, ytrain, x, k):
    d = sum((Xtrain - x)**2, axis=1)      # squared distance to every training point
    nn = argsort(d)[:k]                    # indices of the k closest
    return majority_vote(ytrain[nn])       # their most common label
Everything costs somewhere. k-NN pushes all its cost to prediction: training is instant (just store the data), but each prediction must compare against every training point — slow and memory-heavy at scale, and it degrades in high dimensions (the “curse of dimensionality,” where distances become uninformative). The models in the rest of this track invert this: they spend effort at training time to build a compact rule that predicts fast. That trade — when to compute — is a theme across all of ML.

🔑 Key results to remember

  • k-NN: label a point by the majority vote of its \(k\) nearest training neighbours. No training, no parameters.
  • \(k\) is the bias–variance dial: small \(k\) = flexible/noisy (high variance); large \(k\) = smooth (higher bias). Tune on validation.
  • Non-parametric: makes no assumption about the boundary shape; the data is the model.
  • Cost is at prediction time (compare to all training points) and it suffers in high dimensions.
  • Feature scaling matters: distances are dominated by large-scale features unless you standardize (Track 1).

Answer from memory, then read why.

Q1. How does k-NN “train”?

  • By gradient descent on a loss.
    There’s no loss and no optimization; k-NN doesn’t fit parameters.
  • By building a tree of splits.
    That’s a decision tree (next lesson). k-NN has no model structure to build.
  • It doesn’t — it just stores the training data and does the work at prediction time.
    Correct. k-NN is instance-based: “training” is memorizing the data; all computation happens when a query point asks for its neighbours.
  • By estimating a Gaussian per class.
    That’s a generative Gaussian classifier (Track 6). k-NN makes no distributional assumption.

Q2. Decreasing \(k\) toward 1 tends to…

  • increase variance — the boundary hugs individual points and can fit noise.
    Correct. With \(k=1\) each prediction depends on a single nearest point, so the boundary is jagged and sensitive to noise — classic high variance / low bias.
  • increase bias and underfit.
    That’s the large-\(k\) direction; small \(k\) is more flexible, not more biased.
  • have no effect on the boundary.
    \(k\) strongly shapes the boundary, as the two panels show.
  • make training slower.
    There’s no training; \(k\) affects the prediction rule, not a training cost.

Q3. Why must you usually standardize features before using k-NN?

  • To make training converge.
    There’s no training to converge; the reason concerns distances.
  • To reduce the number of classes.
    Scaling doesn’t change classes; it changes how distance is computed.
  • Because k-NN can’t handle negative numbers.
    Negatives are fine; the issue is the relative scale of features.
  • Because a feature on a large numeric scale dominates the Euclidean distance, drowning out others.
    Correct. Distance sums squared differences across features; a feature ranging in thousands overwhelms one ranging in fractions unless you standardize them to comparable scales.

Q4. A key weakness of k-NN at scale is…

  • it can only draw straight boundaries.
    The opposite — its boundaries are highly flexible and non-linear.
  • prediction is slow because each query compares against all training points.
    Correct. With no compact model, every prediction scans the whole dataset (and it worsens in high dimensions), which is costly for large data — unlike the trees and boosters that build a fast rule at training time.
  • it requires labelled data for training only, not prediction.
    It uses the labelled data at prediction time; that’s exactly why it’s slow.
  • it always underfits.
    With small \(k\) it can overfit badly; underfitting isn’t its characteristic failure.

✍️ Practice

1. Run knn.py and sweep \(k\) from 1 to 101. Plot test accuracy vs \(k\). Where is the sweet spot, and what happens at both extremes?

Show solution

You’ll see a hump: accuracy is a bit lower at \(k=1\) (fitting noise), rises to a broad plateau for moderate \(k\) (e.g. 5–25), then falls as \(k\) grows too large and the vote includes far-away, irrelevant points (underfitting — eventually every query just predicts the majority class). The sweet spot is the plateau, chosen by validation. This is the bias–variance trade-off (Track 3) made tangible in a single, tunable number — the same shape you’d see for tree depth or any capacity knob.

2. Add a third, pure-noise feature (random values unrelated to the label). What happens to k-NN’s accuracy, and why?

Show solution

Accuracy typically drops, because the irrelevant feature adds noise to every distance computation — two points that are close in the meaningful features can be pushed apart by the random one, corrupting who counts as a “neighbour.” This is a face of the curse of dimensionality: as you add uninformative dimensions, Euclidean distance becomes less discriminative and k-NN degrades. It’s why feature selection and scaling matter so much for distance-based methods, and a reason tree-based models (which can ignore useless features by never splitting on them) often handle messy tabular data more gracefully.

3. Conceptual: k-NN gets ~94% on two-moons with essentially no code. Why do we bother with the more complex models in the rest of this track?

Show solution

k-NN shines on small, low-dimensional, well-scaled data, but it scales poorly (slow predictions, large memory), degrades in high dimensions, gives no insight into which features matter, and can’t easily incorporate feature interactions or importance. Trees and their ensembles train a compact, fast model, handle mixed feature types and irrelevant features gracefully, and expose feature importance — which is why gradient boosting, not k-NN, wins most tabular competitions. Each model trades simplicity, speed, accuracy, and interpretability differently; knowing the whole toolbox lets you pick the right one, which is the point of learning them all.
