Track 5 · Computer Vision

Image generation with diffusion

⏱ 45 min Lesson 5.6 ▶ Open in Colab
Why this existsDiffusion models power Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, and Midjourney. The idea — learn to reverse noise, then denoise from static into a new image — is beautiful and, once seen clearly, demystifies how machines generate images from a text prompt.
By the end, you can
  • Explain the forward (add noise) and reverse (learned denoise) processes
  • Describe generation as repeated denoising starting from pure noise
  • Explain text conditioning and how a prompt steers the output
  • Say why latent diffusion (compress, then diffuse) makes it fast

Concept Generate by learning to undo noise

Every vision task so far has analyzed an image — labeled it, boxed it, segmented it. Now we flip the problem: generative models create brand-new images that never existed. The technology behind Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, and Midjourney is the diffusion model, and its central idea is beautiful and almost paradoxical: to learn to create images, first learn to destroy them, then run the destruction backward. There are two processes. The forward process takes a real training image and adds a tiny bit of random noise (small random numbers) over and over — hundreds of steps — until the image is pure static, indistinguishable from random noise. This part involves no learning at all; it's just "gradually smear the image into noise," and we control it exactly. The reverse process is where the model lives: a neural network is trained to undo one step of noising — given a noisy image, predict the noise that was added so it can be subtracted, yielding a slightly cleaner image.

Here is the payoff. Once the network can reliably remove a step of noise, you can generate from nothing: start with a fresh sheet of pure random noise (not derived from any image) and apply the learned denoising step hundreds of times. Each step nudges the static a little closer to something image-like, and after all the steps a brand-new, coherent image emerges — one the model was never shown, sampled from the distribution of images it learned. That's the whole trick: generation is repeated denoising, starting from noise. It's stable to train (each step is a simple, well- posed "predict the noise" regression problem) and it produces stunning quality, which is why diffusion displaced earlier generative approaches like GANs for most image generation.

Concept Steering it with text, and doing it cheaply in latent space

Two additions turn this from "generate a random image" into "generate the image I asked for, fast." First, conditioning: to make the output follow a text prompt like "a cat astronaut," the text (encoded by a language model, often a CLIP text encoder from the next lesson) is fed into the denoising network at every step, so the denoising is steered toward images matching the words. The model doesn't just remove noise toward "any image" but toward "an image consistent with this caption" — that's text-to-image generation. Second, and crucial for practicality, latent diffusion (the "Stable Diffusion" design): running hundreds of denoising steps on full-resolution pixels is enormously expensive, so instead an autoencoder compresses the image into a much smaller latent representation, the whole diffusion process runs in that small latent space (far cheaper), and a decoder expands the final latent back into a full image.

Latent diffusion is the breakthrough that made text-to-image accessible: by diffusing in a compressed latent (perhaps 48× smaller in area) rather than raw pixels, it cut the compute enough to run on a single consumer GPU while keeping quality high — the decoder restores the fine detail at the end. Put the pieces together and you have modern text-to-image: encode to a small latent, denoise many steps while conditioning on the prompt, decode back to pixels. Every term here is one you've met — noise, neural network regression, encoders/decoders (the U-Net from segmentation is the usual denoiser), text embeddings — recombined into a system that turns a sentence into a picture.

Worked example One forward-noising step (and undoing it)

The heart of diffusion is one noising step and its inverse. The standard forward step mixes the clean signal x0 with noise using a factor alpha_bar (how much signal is retained): x_t = √(alpha_bar)·x0 + √(1−alpha_bar)·noise. If the model can predict that noise, subtracting it recovers the original. Here it is on a 4-pixel "image":

Follow the mechanics. With alpha_bar = 0.64, the mixing weights are √0.64 = 0.8 on the signal and √0.36 = 0.6 on the noise. So the noisy image x_t is 0.8·x0 + 0.6·noise — the clean pixels [0.8, 0.2, 0.5, 0.9] get partly buried under scaled noise, becoming [1.698, 0.4, 0.987, 2.065]. That's one forward step: signal shrunk toward 0.8× and noise mixed in. Now the reverse: the network's only job is to look at x_t and predict what noise was added. In this toy we cheat and hand it the true noise (pred_noise = noise), then run the algebra backward — subtract the scaled noise and divide by 0.8 — and it recovers x0 exactly: [0.8, 0.2, 0.5, 0.9]. That is the entire principle in miniature. A real diffusion model does exactly this, except (1) it doesn't get the true noise — it learns to predict it, trained on millions of (clean image, noised image) pairs; (2) it repeats the step hundreds of times; and (3) to generate, it starts x_t from pure random noise (no x0 at all) and denoises step by step until an image appears. The "predict the noise, then subtract it" loop you just saw is how Stable Diffusion turns static into a picture — everything else is scale, learning, and repetition.

★ Key points to remember
  • A diffusion model learns to generate by learning to reverse noising: the forward process smears an image into pure noise; the reverse process (a trained network) removes noise step by step.
  • The network's job each step is simple: predict the noise in a noisy image so it can be subtracted — a well-posed regression that's stable to train.
  • To generate, start from pure random noise and run the learned denoiser many times — a brand-new image emerges.
  • Text conditioning feeds an encoded prompt into every denoising step, steering output toward the words (text-to-image).
  • Latent diffusion (Stable Diffusion) runs the whole process in a small compressed latent (encoder→diffuse→decoder), making high-quality text-to-image fast enough to run widely.

Practice Build the reasoning yourself

1A friend says: "Diffusion is magic — it makes images out of nothing." Explain, without magic, how starting from random noise can produce a coherent image.
Show solution & reasoning

There's no magic — the "nothing" (random noise) is just the starting point, and all the structure comes from the learned denoising network. Here's the demystified account. During training, the model saw millions of real images, each progressively noised, and learned one narrow skill: given a noisy image, predict the noise that was added. Learning that skill well means the model has, implicitly, learned what real images look like — because to know what's "noise" in a picture, you must know what's "signal" (a plausible image). Now to generate: we hand the model a sheet of pure random noise and ask it to do its one skill — predict the noise and remove a bit. The model has never seen this particular noise, but it responds as trained: it nudges the static a hair toward "what a real image would look like." Repeat hundreds of times, and each nudge accumulates — random static → vague blobs → rough shapes → a coherent image. The image emerges because every step pulls toward the distribution of real images the model learned. So the coherence doesn't come from the noise; it comes from the millions of images baked into the denoiser's weights, applied step by step.

The reasoning skill is locating where the information actually lives: not in the random seed, but in the learned model. This dissolves the apparent paradox ("something from nothing") — the noise supplies randomness (so you get a different image each time, which is why it's generative rather than deterministic), while the network supplies all the structure (learned from data). It also clarifies a real limitation people miss: a diffusion model can only generate things consistent with what it learned, so its outputs reflect its training data — including its gaps and biases. And it frames the ethical issues correctly: because the structure comes from training images, questions of memorization (occasionally reproducing a training image too closely), copyright (what was in the training set), and misuse (deepfakes) are all really questions about the data and the learned model, not the noise. Being able to explain generation as "repeated learned denoising pulling noise toward the data distribution" — no magic — is exactly the clear mental model that lets you reason about what these systems can and can't do, and where their outputs really come from.

2Diffusion needs many denoising steps (often 20–1000) to make one image, while a classifier needs one forward pass. Why the difference, and what's the practical consequence?
Show solution & reasoning

The difference is intrinsic to the two problems. Classification is a one-shot mapping: image in, label out — a single forward pass suffices because you're reading off an answer that's directly present. Generation by diffusion is an iterative refinement: turning pure noise into a coherent image is too hard to do in one jump, so the model breaks it into many small, each-one-easy steps ("remove a little noise"), and the image is built up gradually across all of them. It's the difference between recognizing a finished drawing (one glance) and creating one (many strokes). Each denoising step is one forward pass of the network, so generating an image costs N forward passes where N is the number of steps — making generation fundamentally slower and more compute-heavy than classification. That's the practical consequence: latency and cost scale with the number of steps, so text-to-image is far more expensive per output than recognition, and reducing the step count is a major goal.

This directly motivates two big engineering thrusts you should know. First, faster samplers: clever numerical methods (DDIM and successors) that get good images in far fewer steps (e.g., 20–50 instead of 1000), and distillation techniques that train a student model to take giant steps — some can generate in just 1–4 steps. Second, latent diffusion (this lesson): making each step cheaper by working in a compressed space, which multiplies with fewer steps to make the whole thing practical. The reasoning principle is recognizing that iterative generation trades speed for quality and stability — many small well-posed steps are easier to learn than one impossible leap, but you pay in compute per image. Understanding this "N forward passes per image" cost structure explains why generation feels slow, why step-count and sampler choice are the knobs users and engineers tune, and why so much research targets fewer steps. It's the Track 11 efficiency mindset applied to generative models: know your cost model (here, cost ∝ steps), then attack the dominant term.

3Latent diffusion inserts an encoder (image→latent) and decoder (latent→image) around the diffusion process. What does each buy you, and what's the risk of compressing too aggressively?
Show solution & reasoning

The encoder/decoder pair exists to make diffusion affordable by shrinking what the expensive part operates on. The encoder compresses a full-resolution image into a much smaller latent (say reducing a 512×512×3 image to a 64×64 latent — dramatically fewer numbers), and this is the payoff: the hundreds of denoising steps then run on the small latent instead of full pixels, cutting compute and memory by a large factor — enough to turn "needs a datacenter" into "runs on a consumer GPU." The decoder does the inverse at the very end, expanding the final denoised latent back into a full-resolution image and restoring fine detail. So the division of labor is elegant: the autoencoder (encoder+decoder) handles the expensive job of moving between pixels and a compact code, and the diffusion model does its iterative generation cheaply in that compact code. This is why "latent" diffusion was the unlock for widely-usable text-to-image — the quality stayed high while the cost dropped by roughly the compression factor.

The risk of compressing too aggressively is loss of detail and fidelity. The latent is a lossy compression: if you squeeze too hard, the encoder throws away information the decoder can't recover, so fine textures, small text, sharp edges, and precise details get blurred or hallucinated — no amount of good denoising in the latent can restore detail the encoding already discarded. So there's a fidelity-vs-efficiency trade-off in the compression ratio: more compression means cheaper diffusion but a coarser ceiling on achievable detail; less compression preserves detail but costs more. The reasoning skill is seeing latent diffusion as a two-stage system where the autoencoder sets a quality ceiling and the diffusion model works beneath it — so the compression ratio is a deliberate design choice balancing cost against the finest detail you need. This is a recurring systems pattern: put the expensive computation in a compressed representation, but respect that compression is lossy and bounds your quality. Recognizing that the encoder's compression is both the source of the speedup and the source of a fidelity limit — and can be pushed too far — is exactly the trade-off-aware thinking that distinguishes understanding a system from just naming its parts.

★ Deep dive: diffusion vs GANs, and why "predict the noise" won▸

It's illuminating to contrast diffusion with the approach it largely displaced: the GAN (Generative Adversarial Network). A GAN pits two networks against each other — a generator that tries to produce realistic images and a discriminator that tries to tell real from fake — training them in competition until the generator's fakes fool the discriminator. GANs can produce sharp images in a single forward pass (fast generation, a real advantage), but the adversarial training is notoriously unstable and finicky: it can fail to converge, oscillate, or collapse to producing a few repetitive outputs (mode collapse), and getting a GAN to train well was something of a dark art. Diffusion won most of image generation for the opposite virtues: its training is stable and simple — there's no adversary, just a straightforward regression ("predict the noise") with a clear loss that reliably decreases — and it produces excellent, diverse quality that covers the full data distribution rather than collapsing. The price is slow, multi-step generation (the previous practice problem), which is why the whole ecosystem of faster samplers and distillation exists. The interview-ready contrast: GANs — one-shot and fast but unstable to train and prone to mode collapse; diffusion — multi-step and slower but stable to train, easy to scale, and higher fidelity/diversity. The shift from GANs to diffusion is a case study in how trainability and stability can matter more than raw generation speed when you're pushing quality and scale.

Zoom out and diffusion ties the whole track together. Its denoising network is almost always a U-Net — the exact encoder-decoder-with-skips architecture from the segmentation lesson — because denoising, like segmentation, is a dense image-to-image task (in at full resolution, out at full resolution), so the same architecture that predicts a per-pixel mask is ideal for predicting per-pixel noise. The text conditioning uses embeddings from a CLIP-style text encoder, the subject of the very next lesson, and increasingly the denoiser itself uses transformer blocks (diffusion transformers) rather than pure convolutions — the ViT influence from the last lesson, reaching generation. So a modern text-to-image model is a synthesis of nearly everything you've learned: an autoencoder for compression, a U-Net or transformer denoiser, attention for conditioning, text embeddings for the prompt, and the noise-prediction training objective that makes it all stable. That synthesis is the point of this track — the analytical tasks (classify, detect, segment), the architectures (CNN, ViT), and now generation are not separate topics but one interconnected toolkit. The final lesson closes the loop by joining vision and language directly (CLIP and multimodal models), completing the picture of how machines both understand and create in the visual world.

Diffusion already borrowed a text encoder to follow prompts. Next, vision-language models like CLIP make that image-text connection the main event — learning a shared space where a picture and its caption line up.
