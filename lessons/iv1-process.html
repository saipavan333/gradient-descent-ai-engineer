How AI/ML interviews are structured (this year)

⏱ 22 min Lesson 13.1
Why this existsAI/ML interviews are a funnel — recruiter screen, technical coding, ML/domain round, then a multi-hour onsite. Knowing the format (and what changed this year: AI-assisted coding, eval-as-system-design, GenAI everywhere) lets you prepare deliberately.
By the end, you can
  • Map the interview funnel and stages
  • Know what each round tests
  • Understand 2026 shifts (AI coding, eval focus)
  • Prepare strategically and think out loud

Concept Know the game you're playing

Before drilling questions, understand how AI/ML interviews are actually structured — because knowing the stages, what each tests, and what's changed recently lets you prepare deliberately instead of blindly. The process is a funnel: a sequence of increasingly demanding stages that progressively filter candidates, so a strong showing at each stage earns you the next. The whole loop typically runs 6–8 weeks and ends in an onsite (now often virtual) that is the real test.

The stages, in order. The recruiter screen is a non-technical chat about your background and the role. The technical coding screen tests programming and problem-solving (data structures, algorithms, and increasingly practical coding). The ML / domain round probes machine-learning knowledge — fundamentals, deep learning, LLMs — through conceptual questions and discussion. The onsite loop is the crux: a 4–6 hour block of several interviews covering coding, ML system design, behavioral questions, and usually a deep-dive on a past project. Each of the next lessons in this track drills one of these areas.

Concept What's different this year

AI/ML interviewing has shifted meaningfully in the last couple of years, and preparing for the current reality — not the interview of five years ago — matters. Three changes stand out.

First, AI-assisted coding interviews are emerging. Several major companies now let candidates use an approved AI assistant during the coding round — a change that reflects how engineers actually work — so the skill being tested is shifting from "can you produce code" to "can you judge and integrate code": prompt well, spot the assistant's mistakes, and build something correct and coherent. The pragmatic response is to practice both modes: enough unaided problems to be genuinely fluent, and enough AI-assisted building to be fast and discerning with a tool. Second, evaluation has become the new system design: for GenAI system design rounds, interviewers now care at least as much about how you'd measure quality, control cost and latency, and add guardrails and monitoring as about the architecture diagram itself — the central challenge is balancing quality, cost, latency, and safety, and explaining the trade-offs. Third, GenAI is everywhere: RAG, LLMs, prompting, and agents are now standard interview topics across many ML roles, not a niche specialty. If you've worked through this curriculum, you're prepared for all three — but go in expecting the modern format.

★ Key points to remember
  • The interview is a funnel: recruiter screen → technical coding → ML/domain round → onsite loop → offer, over ~6–8 weeks.
  • The onsite loop (4–6 hours) is decisive — coding, ML system design, behavioral, and a past-project deep-dive.
  • AI-assisted coding rounds are emerging; the tested skill is judging/integrating code — practice both with and without an assistant.
  • Evaluation is the new system design: cost, latency, guardrails, and monitoring now weigh as much as the architecture.
  • GenAI (RAG, LLMs, agents) is now a standard topic across ML roles, not a niche.

Practice Prepare strategically

1You have four weeks before an ML engineer onsite. How would you structure your preparation across the areas the loop tests?
Show a strong approach

Map your prep to the loop's components and your weakest areas. Coding (ongoing, daily): practice problems to stay fluent — data structures/algorithms and practical implementation — in both unaided and AI-assisted modes, since either format may appear; aim for communicating your reasoning out loud, not just solving. ML fundamentals & depth (steady): review the conceptual areas — bias-variance, metrics, deep learning, transformers, LLMs, RAG — using this curriculum's interview checkpoints; be able to explain each clearly and handle follow-ups. ML system design (heavy focus): practice designing systems end to end (recommenders, search, RAG assistants, LLM platforms), and — reflecting the current emphasis — rehearse reasoning about evaluation, cost, latency, and guardrails, not just the architecture. Behavioral & projects (don't neglect): prepare STAR-structured stories and a crisp deep-dive on your strongest project, since the project discussion is often decisive.

The strategy is to allocate time by both importance and your gaps: everyone needs coding and fundamentals sharp, but system design and the behavioral/project rounds are where many strong coders under-prepare and lose offers. I'd do a little coding daily to stay warm, block dedicated sessions for system design (the highest-variance, most-coachable area), rehearse project stories out loud until they're crisp, and do at least one full mock interview per area to practice under pressure. The meta-point is that the onsite tests several distinct skills, so preparation must be balanced across all of them rather than over-investing in the one you enjoy — a brilliant coder who can't discuss trade-offs in system design or articulate their project impact will still struggle. Deliberate, gap-driven, balanced prep across the loop's components is what converts knowledge into offers.

2Why do interviewers consistently ask you to "think out loud," and how does that change how you should approach every round?
Show a strong approach

Interviewers ask you to think out loud because they're evaluating your reasoning process, not just your final answer — in real work, how you approach ambiguous problems, weigh options, and communicate matters as much as the solution, and the interview is a proxy for that. A correct answer with no visible reasoning tells them little about how you'd perform on novel problems; a well-reasoned approach (even to a problem you don't fully solve) shows exactly the thinking they're hiring for. This is especially true now that AI can produce code and answers — the differentiating human skill is judgment, which is only visible when you externalize it.

This changes your approach across every round. In coding, narrate your plan before implementing, explain trade-offs of your approach, and talk through your debugging — a silent solve, even if correct, scores worse than a communicated one. In system design, the entire value is in reasoning out loud: state assumptions, justify each choice by requirements, and openly weigh trade-offs (this is the answer, since there's no single right design). In ML/conceptual rounds, don't just give the textbook definition — explain the intuition and the "why," and connect ideas. In behavioral, structure your stories so your reasoning and decisions are clear. The unifying practice is to treat the interviewer as a collaborator you're thinking with, making your mental process visible and inviting their input — which also lets them give hints and see you incorporate feedback. Candidates who go quiet and emerge with an answer, or who treat the interview as a test to be silently aced, systematically underperform those who make their reasoning a shared, spoken process. Given that the field increasingly values judgment over rote production, thinking out loud isn't just interview etiquette — it's how you demonstrate the exact skill being hired for.

3The job market and interview bar for AI/ML roles are demanding. How do you think about standing out beyond just answering questions correctly?
Show a strong approach

Standing out comes from demonstrating the qualities that correct answers alone don't reveal: judgment, communication, depth, and genuine engagement with trade-offs and responsibility. Many candidates can recite that RAG retrieves-then-generates or that precision trades off with recall; fewer can reason crisply about when to use each, what breaks in production, and how they'd measure and safeguard it. So beyond correctness: show depth by going a level deeper than asked (mention the failure mode, the trade-off, the way you'd evaluate it) — signaling you've actually built and debugged these systems, not just read about them. Lead with trade-offs in every design discussion, since balancing quality, cost, latency, and safety is explicitly what modern interviews reward. Communicate clearly — structure your answers, define terms, and make your reasoning easy to follow, which is itself a hireable skill. Show responsibility — raising evaluation, fairness, and safety unprompted marks you as someone trustworthy to deploy real systems.

The deeper reframing is that interviews test whether you'd be a good colleague and engineer, not a trivia database — and increasingly so as AI handles more rote production. Your projects are a huge lever here: a candidate who can deep-dive a real project, explaining the problem, their specific decisions and trade-offs, what went wrong and what they learned, demonstrates applied judgment far more convincingly than any memorized answer. Curiosity and honesty stand out too: saying "I'm not sure, but here's how I'd reason about it" or "I'd want to measure X before deciding" reads as mature and real, whereas confident bluffing reads as risky. In a demanding market, the goal isn't to be a flawless answer machine — it's to be visibly the kind of thoughtful, communicative, trade-off-aware, responsible engineer that teams want to work with and trust with consequential systems. That's what this whole curriculum has been building, and the interview is your chance to show it.

★ Deep dive: preparing for the modern AI/ML interview▸

A few principles make preparation efficient and effective. First, practice out loud and under realistic conditions — do mock interviews (with peers, mentors, or platforms) rather than only solving silently, because the gap between "I know this" and "I can explain this clearly under time pressure to a stranger" is large and only closed by rehearsal. Verbalizing also surfaces the shallow spots in your understanding. Second, go for depth on the fundamentals over breadth of memorized answers: interviewers probe with follow-ups ("why?", "what if?", "how would that fail?"), and genuine understanding lets you handle any follow-up, while memorized answers collapse at the first "why." The curriculum's emphasis on defining every term and explaining the intuition is exactly this depth. Third, prepare your projects as rigorously as the technical topics — for each significant project, be able to explain the problem and constraints, the key technical decisions and their alternatives, the trade-offs you made, what went wrong, and what you'd do differently; the project deep-dive is often where senior candidates most differentiate themselves, and vague project answers undermine otherwise-strong candidates.

Fourth, calibrate to the specific role and company: an ML researcher role, an ML engineer role, an LLM/applied-AI role, and an ML infrastructure role weight these areas differently, so research the role and emphasize accordingly (though the fundamentals are universal). Fifth, stay current, given how fast the field and its interviews move — the shifts toward AI-assisted coding, evaluation-centric system design, and GenAI ubiquity are recent, so verify the current format for your target companies rather than assuming the interview of a few years ago. Finally, and most importantly, remember that the interview is testing whether you can do the job, so the best long-term preparation is genuinely learning to build, evaluate, and reason about AI systems — which is precisely what this curriculum has done. The interview-specific practice (mocks, project prep, knowing the format) sits on top of real competence; it polishes and reveals what you know, but can't substitute for it. Approach interview prep as the final, communication-focused layer over a foundation of actual understanding, and you'll walk in able to demonstrate not just that you know the answers, but that you can think — which, in an era where answers are cheap and judgment is scarce, is exactly what gets you hired.
